# Chapter 34: Structuring Deals with Missing-Heir Risk

*Escrow, Insurance, Indemnification, and Contingent Structures*

## 34.1 The Residual-Risk Problem

Chapters 30 through 33 give you the tools to eliminate uncertainty. Chapter 30 finds everyone findable. Chapter 31 serves everyone reachable. Chapter 32 handles the heirs who cannot sign for themselves. Chapter 33 binds the unknown. Together, those tools can reduce missing-heir risk to near zero. But "near zero" is not the same as zero — and in many deals, the cost and timeline of reducing risk to near zero exceed what the deal economics can bear. That gap between what's legally possible and what's economically sensible is where this chapter lives.

This chapter teaches you how to close deals when missing-heir risk or capacity risk cannot be fully eliminated. The deal has to happen — a seller is ready now, a buyer is waiting, the window is finite — and the legal process to achieve mathematical certainty would take twelve months and cost twenty-five thousand dollars the deal cannot support. So instead of eliminating risk, you manage it. You quantify it. You shift it. You insure it. You hold money back against it. You structure the transaction so that if the worst happens, the worst is survivable.

The tools in this chapter are **escrow holdbacks**, **title insurance endorsements**, **indemnification agreements**, **contingent purchase structures**, **fractional acquisitions that accept residual co-tenancy**, and the **layered combinations** of those tools. Used correctly, they let you close deals that would otherwise be dead. Used carelessly, they paper over problems that become catastrophic later. The difference between the two is understanding what each tool actually does and when to use which one.

This chapter closes out Part VI of the Codex. Everything you learned in Chapters 30 through 33 — the skip-tracing, the service by publication, the guardian ad litem work, the unknown-heir litigation — feeds directly into the structuring decisions this chapter teaches. If you have not read those chapters, read them first. The tools here assume you already know what you tried and why it was not enough.

## 34.2 When Residual Risk Is Acceptable

Not every deal with residual risk is one you should close. Before reaching for the structuring tools, ask whether the underlying deal makes sense with this particular risk attached. The analysis is not complicated, but it requires honest math and honest self-assessment.

### Factors That Make Residual Risk Acceptable

1. The residual risk is small — a fractional interest you have been unable to account for represents **1/32 or less** of the total title
2. The **statute of limitations clock** has already run, or will run within a predictable window, making later claims progressively harder to assert
3. The **unknown heir population**, if it exists at all, is likely small — a single possible ghost, not a family tree of dozens
4. **Title insurance is available** with manageable exceptions or endorsements
5. The deal has **enough margin** to absorb a later payout if one becomes necessary
6. The **buyer and seller both understand** the residual risk and have priced it into the transaction
7. A later-surfacing claimant, if they appear, can reasonably be **bought out at pro-rata value** without destroying the deal economics

### Factors That Make Residual Risk Unacceptable

1. The gap is large — **1/4 or more** of the title is uncertain
2. **Multiple possible ghost branches** exist — a prior marriage AND non-marital children AND international descendants
3. **Title insurance will not issue** at all
4. Title insurance will issue **only with exclusions** that leave the buyer materially exposed
5. **Deal margins are thin** — a later buyout would put the deal underwater
6. The property has **unique characteristics** — development-ready land, entitlements, specific use — that make a later partition action destructive
7. The **other party is not fully informed** or would refuse the transaction if they were

### The Buyer-Side vs. Seller-Side Distinction

This chapter is written primarily from the perspective of the fractured-equity investor as buyer — acquiring interests from heirs in a property with residual uncertainty. Many of the same tools work in reverse when the investor later resells to a downstream buyer. The structural difference: when you are buying, you want the risk shifted **toward the seller** — escrow holdback funded from the purchase price, indemnification running from the seller. When you are selling, you want the risk shifted **toward the buyer or toward insurance** — title insurance endorsements priced into the deal, warranty limitations. The tool choices flip depending on which side of the table you occupy.

## 34.3 Escrow Holdbacks

The most straightforward tool. A portion of the purchase price is held in escrow — by the title company or by an independent escrow agent — against the possibility that a later-surfacing claimant must be paid. If the risk does not materialize within a defined period, the escrow releases to the seller. If a claim surfaces, the escrow funds the resolution.

The beauty of the escrow holdback is its simplicity. Both parties understand it. The title company can administer it without additional infrastructure. The economics are transparent. The weakness is equally transparent: the escrow has a fixed duration, and a claim that surfaces after the escrow releases falls entirely on the buyer.

### How an Escrow Holdback Works

1. **Purchase agreement** specifies that a portion of the purchase price will be held in escrow at closing
2. **Escrow agent** — typically the title company, but can be an independent escrow agent — receives the holdback at closing
3. **Escrow agreement** governs the terms — how long, what triggers release, what triggers payout
4. If no claim surfaces within the defined period, **escrow releases to the seller automatically**
5. If a claim surfaces, **escrow funds negotiate or litigate the claim**, with any remainder released per the agreement

### Sizing the Holdback

The right holdback size depends on what the residual risk actually represents. Work it out mathematically:

1. Identify the **maximum possible fractional interest** of any unaccounted heir population
2. Calculate **pro-rata fair market value** of that fractional interest
3. Add a reasonable premium for the **cost of defending a claim** — attorney fees, expert witnesses, time
4. That number is the **minimum holdback**; add headroom as negotiations allow

**Example:** $400,000 property, potential unknown-heir gap of up to 1/16, equals $25,000 pro-rata. Add $10,000 for legal-defense contingency. Holdback should be at least $35,000 — more if negotiations allow. A seller who pushes for a smaller holdback is asking you to personally bear the risk that the holdback was designed to address. Resist that.

### Holdback Duration

How long the escrow runs is a negotiation between the risk profile and the seller's need for their money. The seller wants the shortest possible holdback. You want the holdback to run at least as long as the most dangerous limitations period for likely claims. Typical ranges:

1. **One year** — minimum meaningful period, useful when the risk is low and immediate surfacing is unlikely
2. **Two to four years** — matches the statute of limitations window for many challenge claims
3. **Five years** — matches common adverse-possession and bill-of-review windows in Texas
4. **Ten years** — covers most long-tail claims; rarely achievable in negotiation
5. **Indefinite** — escrow until a quiet title judgment enters or a specific event occurs; sellers strongly resist this, but it is sometimes the right answer for high-stakes deals

### Release Triggers

The escrow agreement should specify exactly what causes funds to release. Vague language creates disputes. Typical release triggers:

1. **Passage of the holdback period with no claim having been asserted** — automatic release to seller, the most common trigger
2. **Entry of a final, non-appealable quiet title or determination-of-heirship judgment** — releases to seller regardless of time elapsed
3. **Title insurance claim paid** that resolves the underlying issue — release to seller, reduced by the claim amount if applicable
4. **Buyer's written certification** that diligent search has been completed with no adverse result — release to seller on the buyer's affirmation

### Payout Triggers

When do the escrow funds actually pay out to resolve a claim? The agreement must be specific:

1. A claimant has **asserted a written claim** of inheritance interest in the property
2. The claim **appears facially valid** — the claimant appears to be a real person with a real connection to the decedent
3. Buyer's title company or attorney has determined that **defending the claim without payout is not economically sensible**
4. A **settlement has been negotiated**, or a judgment has been entered

### Mechanics of Escrow Holdbacks

1. Escrow agent must be **truly independent** — not the buyer's attorney, not the seller's attorney
2. **Title company holdbacks** are common and efficient — the same company that insures the title can hold the escrow
3. A **separate escrow agreement** is required — not just contract language, which is unenforceable as an escrow instruction
4. **Interest on escrow funds** typically accrues to the seller — they are holding the money that would otherwise be theirs
5. **Escrow fees** are paid by the buyer, the seller, or split, depending on negotiation — usually buyer
6. **Tax treatment** — the seller generally recognizes the full purchase price as income in the year of sale, not when escrow releases; specific tax advice is required because this can be a painful surprise

## 34.4 Title Insurance — The Primary Risk-Shifting Tool

Title insurance is the single most powerful tool in this chapter. A title insurance policy transfers the risk of defects in title — including undiscovered heirs, defective service in prior proceedings, and other chain-of-title problems — from the insured to the insurance company. For a premium paid at closing, the insurer agrees to defend and indemnify against covered claims.

Unlike escrow, which holds your own money as a reserve, title insurance puts a regulated, capitalized institution's resources behind the defense of your title. When a claim surfaces two years after closing with a title insurance policy in place, the insurer hires the lawyers, bears the litigation cost, and pays the settlement or judgment. That is qualitatively different protection from an escrow account that may or may not be large enough.

### What Title Insurance Actually Covers

1. **Defects in title existing at the time of the policy** — this is the core coverage
2. **Encumbrances and liens** not disclosed in the policy
3. **Unmarketability of title** — defects that make the title legally unmarketable even without a formal claim
4. **Lack of a right of access**
5. **Certain specific risks** covered by endorsements negotiated at or before closing

Importantly, title insurance is **backward-looking, not forward-looking**. It covers defects that existed when the policy issued but were unknown. It does not cover events that happen after closing. A claimant who asserts an interest that was already in existence at closing is a covered risk. A claimant asserting a claim that arose from your actions after closing is not.

### Owner's Policy vs. Lender's Policy

Two primary title policy types exist, and understanding the distinction matters:

**Owner's policy.** Insures the owner's equity in the property against title defects. The policy amount equals the purchase price (or sometimes the property's fair market value at the time of claim). This is the critical policy for fractured-equity investors. If you are buying for cash or are the primary equity investor, the owner's policy is what you need.

**Lender's policy.** Insures a lender's security interest — usually issued to the lender in conjunction with a mortgage. Amount equals the loan amount. Does not protect the owner's equity beyond the lender's secured position.

Both policies insure against the same categories of defects. They differ in what they protect. If you are the equity investor and you borrow to fund the deal, you need both — the lender requires its own policy, and you need your own to protect your equity.

### The Exception Schedule

Every title policy includes a **schedule of exceptions** — risks the policy does not cover. Understanding the exceptions is as important as understanding the coverage. For fractured-equity transactions, common exceptions include:

1. Rights or claims of parties in possession not shown by public records
2. Matters that an accurate survey would disclose
3. Easements or claims of easements not shown by public records
4. Taxes or special assessments not yet due or payable
5. **Specific exceptions for known chain-of-title issues** — this is the critical category for fractured-equity deals

A title commitment that lists "rights of unknown heirs of [decedent], if any" as a **specific exception** leaves the buyer uninsured against the very risk the transaction is trying to address. That exception means: if an unknown heir surfaces and asserts a claim, the title company does not cover it. The deal has title insurance on paper but no protection where it matters. Getting that exception removed — or insured through an endorsement — is the heart of pre-closing negotiation with the title company.

### Endorsements That Address Missing-Heir Risk

Title insurance endorsements modify the standard policy to add coverage. The standard policy's exceptions are not negotiable in their standard form — but endorsements allow tailored additions. For fractured-equity work:

1. **Affirmative coverage endorsements** for specific identified risks — the insurer agrees to defend and indemnify against a specific named concern, converting an exception into covered territory
2. **Extended coverage endorsements** that remove standard exceptions — typically require additional documentation and premium
3. Environmental, zoning, access, and survey endorsements — less relevant to the heir issue but sometimes relevant to the underlying property

The critical move for fractured-equity deals is **negotiating the specific exception for unknown heirs to be either removed or converted into affirmative coverage through an endorsement**. This typically requires: the Chapter 30 diligent search documentation, the Chapter 33 judgment if one has been entered, affidavits of heirship completed by disinterested witnesses, the family tree diagram showing what is known and what remains uncertain, and sometimes a premium increase of 10-25% above the base rate.

Title companies vary significantly in their appetite for this kind of endorsement work. Some will not touch it without a judgment. Others will issue affirmative coverage if the diligent search is thorough and the residual risk is documented as small. Know your title company's underwriting standards before you get deep into a deal.

### Enhanced Owner's Policies

Some title insurers offer **enhanced owner's policies** that include broader coverage and fewer standard exceptions as part of the base policy. Premium costs more — often 15-25% above the standard rate — but coverage is more complete. For fractured-equity properties where residual risk matters, the enhanced policy is frequently worth the extra cost. The savings on endorsement negotiations alone can justify the premium difference.

### Premium Costs

Title insurance premiums in Texas are regulated by the Texas Department of Insurance. Standard owner's policy premiums follow a rate schedule based on the property's value — roughly **0.5% to 0.7% of the purchase price** for most residential and smaller commercial transactions. Enhanced coverage and endorsements add to the base premium. For a $500,000 fractured-equity property, expect a base premium of approximately $3,000–$3,500, plus endorsement costs. For a $1.2 million commercial property, the base premium is in the $6,000–$8,500 range before endorsements.

### The Title Company as a Partner

Title companies that handle fractured-equity deals regularly can be enormously helpful. They know which endorsements work for which risks, what documentation the underwriter will want to see, and when an issue is insurable versus when it isn't. A title company that has seen twenty determination-of-heirship endorsements knows what the underwriter at the home office needs. A title company that has never done one may refuse simply out of unfamiliarity.

**Developing relationships with one or two experienced title companies is essential infrastructure for fractured-equity work.** Treat them as partners, not as vendors. Bring them into the deal early — before you have a signed contract, not after. Their underwriting perspective will tell you whether the deal is insurable before you spend money negotiating purchase terms you may not be able to close.

## 34.5 Indemnification Agreements

An indemnification agreement is a contractual promise by one party to compensate another if a specified risk materializes. In fractured-equity transactions, the seller (or selling heirs) indemnifies the buyer against loss from missing-heir claims that arise from the chain of title being conveyed. The word "indemnification" sounds formal; the concept is straightforward — the seller is agreeing that if someone shows up later and their claim traces back to the seller's family tree, the seller will make the buyer whole.

### The Basic Structure

Indemnification clauses in real property transactions typically provide:

1. **The seller warrants** certain facts about the title — who the heirs are, that no other heirs exist to their knowledge
2. **The seller agrees to defend against and indemnify** the buyer for any claims arising from those facts being incorrect
3. **The seller's obligation** has a scope (what kinds of claims are covered), a duration (how long after closing), and sometimes a **cap** (maximum aggregate liability)

The scope, duration, and cap are all negotiating points. The buyer wants broad scope, long duration, and no cap. The seller wants narrow scope, short duration, and a low cap.

### When Indemnification Works Well

1. The indemnifying party **has meaningful assets** available to pay if a claim materializes — real property, investment accounts, something you can reach
2. The scope of the indemnity **specifically covers the risk** that actually worries the buyer — not generic language that the seller can argue excludes the actual claim
3. The duration is **long enough to cover the statute of limitations periods** for likely claims — five years minimum for most Texas real property claims
4. The indemnity is **backed up by other protections** — escrow holdback, personal guaranty, insurance — so that if the indemnitor becomes unable to pay, another layer catches the claim

### When Indemnification Is Nearly Worthless

1. **The indemnifying party has no assets** — an individual heir with a $45,000 interest and no other meaningful wealth cannot indemnify against a $200,000 claim. A promise without assets behind it is paper, nothing more.
2. The indemnity is **too short** to cover the actual limitations period — a two-year indemnity against a claim that can be asserted for four years leaves you exposed for two years with no recourse
3. The **scope is narrow** and excludes the specific risks the buyer worries about — "claims arising from the seller's own actions" does not cover unknown-heir claims arising from the decedent's family tree
4. **Finding the indemnifying party five years later** to enforce the indemnity is itself a skip-trace problem — the same heir who was hard to locate before closing may be impossible to locate when you need to enforce
5. The indemnity is **stripped of practical effect by procedural requirements** — mandatory arbitration in an inconvenient forum, notice and cure periods so long that the claim has already been lost by the time you can act

### The Seller Warranty Package

In fractured-equity deals, the seller-side warranties that are typically negotiated represent the factual foundation that the indemnity rests on:

1. **Seller has good and indefeasible title** to the interest being conveyed — the core warranty
2. **Seller is one of the legally recognized heirs** of the named decedent — establishing their authority to convey
3. **The affidavit of heirship accompanying the deed is true and correct** in all material respects
4. **Seller has no knowledge of additional heirs** not disclosed in the affidavit — knowledge-qualified representation
5. **Seller has not previously conveyed the interest** or encumbered it in any way
6. **No liens, claims, or encumbrances exist** against the interest except as specifically disclosed

Each of these warranties creates indemnification exposure if it turns out to be false. The seller who warrants that no other heirs exist and then an undisclosed child surfaces is exposed for breach of warranty — and that exposure is what makes the indemnity valuable. The warranty and the indemnity are two halves of the same protection.

### Affidavit of Heirship — The Most Important Single Document

In Texas, affidavits of heirship are governed by **Tex. Est. Code § 203.001** and related provisions. A properly executed affidavit of heirship, signed by two disinterested witnesses, is **prima facie evidence** of the matters stated in it after the affidavit has been on file in the real property records for **five years**. Before five years, the affidavit has evidentiary weight but is more vulnerable to challenge.

The affidavit identifies the decedent, the marriages, the children, the current heirs, and the family history supporting the heirship claims. When the affidavit is complete and accurate, it is a strong foundation for later title — the five-year clock ticks toward prima facie status, limitations periods run, and the affidavit accumulates legal weight over time.

When the affidavit **omits heirs, conceals facts, or contains knowing misstatements**, it creates legal exposure well beyond the transaction itself. A buyer who relies on a fraudulent affidavit of heirship and later faces a claim from the omitted heir has causes of action against the seller — but those causes of action are only valuable if the seller has assets to reach. And the title company that issued on the basis of the false affidavit has its own claims against the seller. A fraudulent affidavit of heirship is not just a civil problem; depending on the facts, it can be a criminal one.

The affidavit of heirship is also the document most commonly done carelessly. Witnesses who are not truly disinterested. Family members who fill in what they think is probably true without actually knowing. Spouses who omit their own prior children because the conversation is uncomfortable. These errors create real downstream problems. Insist on accuracy.

### Joint and Several Liability

Where multiple heirs sign a single transaction, the indemnification can be structured as **joint and several** — each heir individually liable for the full amount, not just their pro-rata share. If there are four heirs each selling a 25% interest, joint-and-several liability means each of them is individually on the hook for the full indemnification obligation, not just 25% of it.

Joint-and-several liability is stronger protection for the buyer, but heirs often resist it because any one of them could end up defending a claim that was caused by another heir's concealment. It is a negotiating point — sellers push for several-only liability (each responsible only for their proportionate share); buyers push for joint-and-several. In deals where one heir is clearly the most knowledgeable about the family history and the others are peripheral, joint-and-several on the knowledgeable heir with several-only on the others is a reasonable compromise.

### Personal Guaranty from Individual Heirs

Where the selling heir is selling through an LLC or trust, a **personal guaranty from the underlying individual** adds meaningful protection. Without the guaranty, the indemnifying party is limited to the entity's assets — which may be minimal or deliberately minimized. An LLC that holds a single fractional interest and nothing else cannot indemnify a large claim. The individual behind the LLC can, if they have personal assets. The personal guaranty bridges that gap.

Guarantors sometimes resist because they do not understand the risk they are taking, or because they prefer the liability shield of the entity. Explain the tradeoff: the guaranty is more likely to reduce friction if a claim arises than to result in actual payment, because the goal is to have enough layers of protection that no single layer is ever fully tested. A seller who understands that framing is more likely to sign.

## 34.6 Contingent Purchase Structures

A contingent structure makes part of the purchase price conditional on the resolution of some uncertainty. Different from escrow — where the money is set aside at closing and held against the risk — contingent structures shift the **timing of the payment itself**, often tying it to specific events rather than just the passage of time.

### Earn-Outs

The buyer pays a base price at closing and agrees to pay additional consideration later if specific events occur. For fractured-equity deals, typical earn-out triggers:

1. **Additional payment when a determination of heirship judgment enters** resolving the uncertainty — the seller benefits from cooperating with the judicial process
2. **Additional payment when the buyer resells** at a specified price — the seller participates in the upside if the deal works out
3. **Additional payment if no claim materializes** within a defined period — rewarding the seller's warranty holding true

Earn-outs align incentives: the seller has a financial reason to cooperate with title clearing, provide accurate information, and not obstruct later proceedings. The weakness is that earn-outs require ongoing administration, create disputes over whether triggers have been met, and may require litigation to enforce if the relationship sours.

### Graduated Payment Structures

Seller receives a **stream of payments over time** rather than a lump sum at closing. The structure provides the seller with ongoing cash flow while providing the buyer with **ongoing leverage** — if a claim surfaces, future payments can be reduced or suspended pending resolution. Graduated payments are most natural for income-producing properties where the buyer's cash flow can service the payment stream.

The documentation is more complex than a straight purchase — you effectively have a seller-financed note, with the added wrinkle of contingent offset rights if a claim surfaces. The note, the deed of trust securing the seller's interest, and the offset provisions all have to be carefully coordinated.

### Option Structures

The buyer acquires an **option to purchase** rather than an immediate purchase. Option payments are made over time. The option exercise is conditional on title clearing — or on the title being in a state the buyer is willing to accept. This structure is useful when the buyer wants to **control the property during the clearing process** without taking on all the risk upfront.

An option does not transfer title. The buyer has the right to purchase but has not purchased. This means the buyer cannot sell, cannot encumber the property, and may face limits on improvements. But it also means the buyer has not committed to a price until they see what the title actually looks like after the clearing work is done.

### Lease with Option to Purchase

For income-producing properties, the buyer can **lease the property while the title clears**, with an option to purchase once title is resolved. The lease generates cash flow for the seller — or for the buyer if they sublease. The option preserves the buyer's position. If title clears, the option is exercised. If not, the lease continues or terminates under its terms.

The lease-with-option structure keeps the property generating income during the clearing period while delaying the moment of full commitment. It is most useful when the clearing process is expected to take six to eighteen months and the property has tenants in place.

### The Contingent-Structure Tradeoffs

**Advantages:**
1. Aligns incentives — the seller has reason to cooperate with title clearing
2. Reduces the buyer's upfront risk
3. Can close deals where a straight purchase cannot because the buyer will not pay full price for unclear title

**Disadvantages:**
1. More complex to document and manage — ongoing administrative obligations for both sides
2. Sellers often prefer cash at closing and **discount contingent payments heavily** — the nominal price on paper may not reflect what the seller thinks they are actually getting
3. Creates ongoing administrative obligations and potential for dispute
4. Option and lease structures may limit what the buyer can do with the property during the interim period

## 34.7 Acquiring Fractional Interests Only

Sometimes the right structure is to **acquire only the interests you can acquire cleanly**, and to leave the unresolved interests alone. The buyer ends up as a fractional co-tenant with the unresolved — and possibly unknown — holders of the remaining interests. This is not always a bad outcome. Texas co-tenancy law gives certain rights to co-tenants regardless of the others. But it requires planning, because the path from fractional ownership to full ownership runs through a different set of tools than the purchase-side structuring this chapter has covered so far.

### What Fractional Acquisition Looks Like

The investor acquires, say, 75% of the property from identified, cooperative heirs. The remaining 25% remains in the hands of unresolved heirs — some known but uncooperative, some unknown. The investor is now a **75% co-tenant** in a Texas tenancy-in-common. That interest is fully valid, fully recorded, and fully owned. The unresolved 25% is someone else's problem — for now.

### Rights of a Co-Tenant Under Texas Law

1. **Possession** — any co-tenant has the right to possess the entire property, subject to the equal rights of other co-tenants. As the majority co-tenant, you have possessory rights from day one.
2. **Use** — co-tenants may use the property for its intended purpose, though one co-tenant may owe others accounting for certain profitable uses
3. **Income** — a co-tenant who collects rents must account to other co-tenants for their proportionate share. As the 75% co-tenant, you are entitled to 75% of the rents — but you must account for the remaining 25% to whoever eventually claims that interest
4. **Improvements** — a co-tenant who improves the property has rights to contribution but also bears the risk that the improvement is not recoverable in full
5. **Partition** — any co-tenant can force partition of the property, either in kind or by sale. This is the critical exit right.
6. **Sale of own interest** — a co-tenant can sell their own fractional interest to a third party, even without the consent of other co-tenants

### The Partition Path

When you acquire a majority fractional interest and cannot resolve the balance, **partition is often the exit**. A partition-by-sale proceeding forces a court-supervised sale of the entire property; proceeds are distributed to co-tenants according to their fractional interests. The unresolved minority interests receive notice through service and publication, making the proceeding binding on unknown heirs as well as known ones.

The partition path takes time — typically six to eighteen months for contested proceedings, somewhat less for uncontested ones — and costs money in legal fees and court costs. But it converts an uncertain fractional position into clean title or clean cash, whichever outcome the partition produces.

### Accepting the Long Hold

Some fractured-equity investors specifically **build portfolios of fractional interests** that they hold indefinitely, generating income and waiting for the remaining interests to become resolvable over time. As the majority co-tenant, you control use and management. Other interests that are truly unknown may never surface. The property generates income. Limitations periods run. After ten or twenty years, what was once an uncertain 25% minority interest may be practically unreachable by anyone.

This is a different business model than short-hold flipping, but it is a legitimate approach to the residual-risk problem. Rather than trying to clean up the mess before closing, you accept the fractional interest and operate accordingly, with partition as the safety valve if you ever need to force resolution. Patient capital and fractional equity are natural partners.

## 34.8 Layering Multiple Tools

The most sophisticated fractured-equity transactions combine several of the tools in this chapter. Each tool addresses a different facet of the risk; together they produce a structure with layered protection. This is the belt-and-suspenders approach to closing hard deals.

### A Typical Layered Structure

For a **$600,000 property with known unknown-heir risk of up to 1/8**:

1. **Title insurance** issued with specific endorsement addressing the identified heir risk — premium approximately $5,000 including endorsement cost
2. **Escrow holdback** of $50,000 for four years, releasing automatically if no claim surfaces
3. **Joint-and-several indemnification** from the selling heirs, with a five-year duration and a $75,000 aggregate cap
4. **Determination-of-heirship proceeding** filed within 90 days of closing, with the investor as applicant — converts the uncertain question into a judicial determination that binds unknown heirs through service and publication
5. **Seller warranties** covering the factual disclosures in the affidavit of heirship
6. **Personal guaranties** from the principal selling heirs
7. **General or special warranty deed** with warranty language matching the statutory form

### Why Layer Tools

Each tool has weaknesses. **Title insurance has exclusions** — the very risk may be excepted from coverage unless carefully negotiated. **Escrow has duration limits** — a claim that surfaces after the escrow period is entirely the buyer's problem. **Indemnification is only as good as the indemnitor's assets** — a judgment-proof indemnitor is worthless. **Contingent structures depend on future events** — if the trigger event never occurs, the protection never activates.

By layering, you protect against failure of any single tool:

- If title insurance has an exclusion for a specific claim, the **escrow may cover it**
- If the escrow period has expired, the **indemnification may cover it**
- If the indemnitor is judgment-proof, **title insurance may cover it**
- If title insurance and the escrow are both exhausted, the **determination-of-heirship judgment** may have resolved the underlying question before the problem arose

The layered structure is not about redundancy for its own sake. It is about recognizing that each tool has a failure mode, and stacking the tools so that no single failure mode sinks the deal.

### The Cost of Layering

Layering is not free. Each tool adds cost — insurance premiums, escrow fees, escrow agent fees, attorney time to negotiate and document each layer, and the carrying cost of capital held in escrow. A fully layered structure might add **3%–5% to the total transaction cost**. On deals where the margin is large enough, layer everything. On smaller deals where the cost of full layering would eat the margin, identify the **two or three most important tools** for the specific risk and accept residual risk on the others. The structuring cost should be proportionate to the deal's economics and to the magnitude of the risk being managed.

## 34.9 Documenting the Structure

The documentation matrix for a layered fractured-equity transaction includes several interlocking documents. Your attorney drafts them with consistent definitions and cross-references. Inconsistencies between documents create gaps — a payout trigger in the escrow agreement that does not match the indemnification trigger, or a deed warranty that is narrower than the seller's warranty in the purchase agreement. The documents have to fit together.

### The Purchase Agreement

The central document that binds the transaction:

1. Identifies the property, the interest being conveyed, the parties, and the purchase price
2. Contains the **seller warranties and representations** — the factual promises the indemnity enforces
3. Identifies **specific disclosed risks** — the "seller disclosures" schedule that forms the basis for the buyer's acknowledgment
4. Specifies **closing conditions** — what must happen before closing obligation triggers
5. Specifies the **indemnification terms** — scope, duration, cap, trigger events
6. Specifies the **escrow holdback terms** — amount, duration, release and payout triggers
7. Specifies the **title insurance requirements** — minimum coverage, required endorsements, acceptable exceptions
8. Includes **survival clauses** — which warranties and indemnities survive closing, and for how long

### The Deed

The instrument that actually conveys the interest. Under Texas law, three primary deed types are available:

- **General warranty deed** — seller warrants against all claims, from any source, at any time in history
- **Special warranty deed** — seller warrants against claims arising through the seller's own chain, not claims arising before the seller acquired the interest. This is the most common form in fractured-equity transactions.
- **Quitclaim deed** — conveys whatever interest the seller has, with no warranty whatsoever. Used when the seller will not or cannot make any warranty.

For fractured-equity transactions, **special warranty deeds are common** — the seller warrants against claims arising through themselves, but not against the pre-existing missing-heir issues in the chain. When the buyer has title insurance and an indemnification agreement, the special warranty provides meaningful protection without requiring the seller to warrant against the very uncertainty that makes the deal complicated.

### The Escrow Agreement

A separate, stand-alone document — not just language embedded in the purchase agreement:

1. Identifies the **escrow agent**, the holdback amount, and the duration
2. Specifies **release triggers** in precise, measurable terms
3. Specifies **payout triggers** with equal precision
4. Addresses **interest, fees, and tax treatment** — who receives interest, who pays the escrow agent's fees, what representations the parties are making about tax treatment
5. Provides for **dispute resolution** if buyer and seller disagree about whether a claim has materialized or whether a trigger has been satisfied

### The Affidavit of Heirship

1. Signed by **two disinterested witnesses** — individuals who have personal knowledge of the family history but no financial interest in the outcome
2. Identifies the **decedent, the marriages, the children, and the current heirs**
3. Recites the **family history** supporting the heirship claims in enough detail to be useful to a later court or title examiner
4. Recorded in the **real property records** of the county where the property is located
5. Becomes **prima facie evidence** after five years of recording under Tex. Est. Code § 203.001

### Personal Guaranties

1. Separate document(s) from **each individual** guaranteeing the indemnification obligations
2. Specifies the **scope, duration, and cap** — matching the terms of the underlying indemnification
3. Includes **waivers of suretyship defenses** where appropriate — preventing the guarantor from asserting defenses that the principal indemnitor could have asserted but did not
4. Survives the principal's death, insolvency, or disappearance

### Title Commitment and Policy

1. **Title commitment issued before closing** with the terms and exceptions the policy will contain — this is the document you negotiate from, not the final policy
2. **Buyer reviews and negotiates** the commitment — getting specific exceptions removed, endorsements added, coverage terms confirmed
3. **Policy issues at closing** on the terms confirmed in the final, negotiated commitment
4. **Endorsements issued at closing** — not after, because endorsements that are added after closing may not relate back to the policy date

### Closing Statement

1. Records the **flow of funds at closing** — who pays what to whom
2. Shows the **escrow holdback amount being retained** — not disbursed to the seller at closing
3. Shows **insurance premiums and endorsement costs** — paid from closing proceeds
4. Shows any other disbursements — guaranty fees, legal fees, recording costs

## 34.10 Disclosure Obligations

When you sell a fractured-equity interest to a downstream buyer — whether another investor, an end-user, or a developer — disclosure obligations matter. This is not the place for silence. The tools you used to manage the risk do not make the risk disappear; they transfer and limit it. Your downstream buyer inherits whatever the tools left behind. They are entitled to know that.

### What Must Be Disclosed

1. **Known material defects in title** — any chain-of-title issue that a reasonable buyer would consider material to the purchase decision
2. **Known potential missing-heir claims** — specifically including any issue you managed with escrow, insurance, or indemnification
3. **Status of any pending or threatened litigation** affecting the property
4. **Material facts about the chain of title** that a reasonable buyer would want to know
5. **Specific matters** that state or federal law requires to be disclosed in the sale of real property

### Why Full Disclosure Matters

1. **Failure to disclose material facts can give the buyer a rescission remedy** — the deal unwinds, and you are back to square one, possibly after the buyer has incurred improvement costs or carrying costs
2. **Misrepresentation in a transaction can create fraud liability** beyond the transaction itself — punitive damages, attorney fees, and loss of your real estate license if you are licensed
3. **Disclosure failures in one deal become known** in the industry — destroying the investor's reputation and deal flow with title companies, other investors, and future sellers
4. **Insurance and indemnification are structured on the assumption of honest disclosure** — hiding material facts from the title company can void the policy, leaving you with a piece of paper and no coverage

### The Buyer's Acknowledgment

When the buyer is sophisticated and the transaction prices the risk into the purchase price, a **buyer's acknowledgment of the disclosed risks** becomes part of the documentation. The buyer acknowledges that they are aware of the specific risks, that they have priced them into the purchase, and that the transaction is proceeding with eyes open. This acknowledgment is protection for the seller against later "you didn't tell me" claims — but it **only works if the disclosure was actually complete**. An acknowledgment that the buyer reviewed a disclosure schedule that was itself incomplete does not protect the seller.

## 34.11 Practical Negotiation

### What Sellers Care About

1. **Getting their money at closing**, or at least the maximum possible at closing — holdbacks are money they don't have in hand
2. **Limiting their ongoing obligations and liabilities** after closing — they want the deal to be done
3. **Certainty** — knowing the deal is complete and won't come back to create problems
4. **Tax treatment** — structuring the transaction to optimize their tax position
5. **Reputation within their family** — not looking like they sold out or cut someone out

Understanding what the seller cares about tells you how to negotiate the tools that matter most. A seller who desperately needs cash at closing may accept a longer indemnification period if you reduce the escrow holdback. A seller who fears family blowback may accept a comprehensive disclosure package if it protects them from being blamed for hiding something.

### What Buyers Care About

1. **Getting clean title** — or insured title that produces the same practical result
2. **Having recourse** if something goes wrong — not just paper promises but real assets behind them
3. **Not overpaying for known risks** — the purchase price should reflect the uncertainty
4. **Being able to resell or hold** with confidence
5. **Managing the total cost of risk mitigation** against the deal's margin

### The Negotiating Space

Most of the structuring in this chapter lives in the space where buyer and seller interests diverge:

- The buyer wants a **large escrow holdback for a long period**; the seller wants a small holdback for a short period
- The buyer wants **joint-and-several indemnification with personal guaranties**; the seller wants several-only liability with a cap
- The buyer wants the **seller to pay for all the risk tools**; the seller wants the buyer to bear those costs

Each of these is a negotiating point. The final structure reflects the leverage each side brings.

### Relative Leverage Considerations

1. When the seller is one of many heirs and **cannot force a partition alone**, the buyer often has leverage — the seller cannot extract value without cooperation
2. When the seller controls a **critical fractional interest** — the majority, or a blocking position — the seller has leverage
3. When there are **other willing buyers** for the same property, the seller has leverage
4. When the **property has unique characteristics** and the buyer specifically wants this one, the seller has leverage
5. When the **seller needs cash urgently**, the buyer has leverage
6. When **time is short for closing**, both sides compromise more

## 34.12 Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Full Layering, Large Deal

A **$1.2 million commercial tract in Travis County**. Chain of title has two unresolved potential-heir issues — an undocumented prior marriage in one branch and an international grandchild situation in another. The investor is acquiring interests from all known heirs in a single coordinated transaction.

**Structure:**

1. Base purchase price paid at closing: **$1.1 million**
2. Escrow holdback: **$100,000 for four years**
3. Title insurance: enhanced owner's policy with endorsements for the two identified risks — premium approximately **$7,000**
4. Joint-and-several indemnification from all selling heirs, five-year term, **$200,000 aggregate cap**
5. Personal guaranties from the three largest selling heirs
6. Determination-of-heirship proceeding filed by the investor within 60 days of closing at investor's cost — approximately **$18,000**
7. Published service on unknown heirs in both Travis County and the relevant Mexican state newspapers
8. Total cost of risk mitigation beyond purchase price: approximately **$125,000 over four years**

The deal is big enough to support the layered protection. Eighteen months after closing, the determination of heirship judgment enters; no unknown heirs surface. The escrow releases on schedule at month 48. Total cost of residual risk: the insurance premium, the litigation cost, and the time value of the held-back $100,000. The deal closes and holds.

The lesson from this example: the $125,000 in risk-mitigation costs on a $1.2 million transaction represents about 10% of the purchase price — a meaningful but justifiable investment. The deal would have been undoable without the layered structure, because no title company would have insured without the endorsement work and no knowledgeable buyer would have paid close to market without the escrow and indemnification backstops.

### Example 2 — Smaller Deal, Targeted Tools

A **$180,000 residential lot in Nueces County**. Single unresolved concern: one of the named heirs has a child from a prior relationship whose current whereabouts are unknown. Investor works Chapter 30 but does not pursue full Chapter 33 litigation because deal economics don't support it.

**Structure:**

1. Base purchase price at closing: **$172,000**
2. Escrow holdback: **$8,000 for two years**
3. Title insurance with specific exception for the known-missing-child issue — premium approximately **$1,100**
4. Written indemnification from the selling heir covering the missing-child risk, two-year term, capped at **$25,000**
5. Affidavit of heirship executed by the selling heir and two disinterested witnesses, describing the missing child's identity and the diligent search conducted

Cost of risk mitigation beyond purchase price: approximately **$10,000**. Deal still closes; investor accepts the residual risk tradeoff.

Nineteen months later, the escrow releases automatically — no claim has been asserted. The title insurance exception remains, but at this point the investor has decided to hold the property long-term rather than resell, and the exception is tolerable for a long-term hold. If the investor had intended to flip quickly, the uncovered exception would have been a problem at the downstream closing — a lesson about planning the exit at the time of purchase.

### Example 3 — When to Walk Away

A **$280,000 duplex in Bexar County**. Unresolved concerns: two different missing-heir branches, no clear documentation of the underlying family tree, and a specific claimant has already sent a letter asserting an interest without yet filing suit. Title company reviews the file and **declines to issue** without a determination of heirship judgment. The cost of obtaining the judgment is approximately $22,000 with a nine-month timeline. The seller cannot wait nine months.

The investor concludes that without title insurance and without the judgment, the residual risk is too high for the deal's margin. The investor offers a materially reduced purchase price reflecting the risk. The seller declines. The investor walks away.

Eight months later, the seller comes back — the existing claimant has filed suit. The investor, who walked away before getting entangled, is on the sidelines watching someone else's problem. The deal that looked like a missed opportunity turned out to be a bullet dodged.

This example illustrates the most important lesson in this chapter: **the tools exist to structure deals that are genuinely closeable, not to close deals that should not close**. When the gap is too large, when insurance won't touch it, when the margin is insufficient, the right answer is to walk. The discipline to walk is what separates operators who build durable portfolios from operators who blow up on a single bad deal.

### Example 4 — Fractional Acquisition as the Strategy

A **40-acre rural tract in Harris County**. Twelve known heirs, one clearly identified well-documented unknown-heir branch that likely represents 1/6 of the total title. Full Chapter 33 resolution would cost approximately $28,000 and take nine months, but the deal timeline is pressing.

**Structure chosen:**

1. Investor acquires fractional interests from the **ten cooperating heirs — approximately 83% of title**
2. Leaves 17% outstanding — the 1/6 unknown-heir branch and one uncooperative identified heir's interest
3. Investor is now a **fractional co-tenant** with valid, recorded title to 83%
4. Files **partition action within six months** of closing
5. Partition proceeding brings the remaining interests under court jurisdiction via service and publication
6. Partition ultimately resolves ownership through **partition by sale**, with proceeds distributed to co-tenants

The investor never tries to clean up the title before closing — instead accepts fractional ownership and uses partition to resolve the balance. Total elapsed time from initial purchase to resolution: approximately **fourteen months**. Total cost including partition litigation: approximately **$18,000** — less than the $28,000 the direct-resolution path would have cost, and with a working investment during the fourteen months.

The fractional-acquisition-to-partition path turned a nine-month delay into a parallel process: the investor held a working asset while the litigation resolved the title. The key insight is that partition does not require clean title going in — it produces clean title coming out.

## 34.13 Common Pitfalls

**Paper protections without asset backing.** An indemnification from a seller with no assets is worthless. Back the promise with something real — escrow, guaranty, insurance. A signed indemnification agreement is not protection; it is a lawsuit waiting to happen against a judgment-proof defendant.

**Title exceptions that swallow the deal.** A title commitment that lists the very risk you are worried about as a specific exception leaves the buyer uninsured. Get the exception removed, endorsed into affirmative coverage, or priced into the deal. Never assume that having a title insurance policy means you are covered against the specific risk — read the exceptions every time.

**Escrow periods too short.** A 12-month escrow may expire before the most likely claim even surfaces. Heirs have lives. News travels slowly through family networks. Overseas relatives may not learn of a sale for years. Match the period to the statute of limitations for the specific risk, not to what the seller found acceptable in negotiation.

**Incomplete affidavits of heirship.** An affidavit that omits a known heir is a problem that compounds — the buyer relies on it, the title company relies on it, the later litigation depends on it. Every person involved in the chain of reliance is harmed by an inaccurate affidavit. Complete honesty is both a legal obligation and a practical necessity.

**Relying on title insurance as a substitute for legal process.** Title insurance covers some risks, not all. The policy's exceptions matter more than the coverage. Read every exception in every commitment before closing. Do not assume coverage exists until the commitment confirms it specifically.

**Failing to coordinate the tools.** Escrow that pays on triggers the indemnification agreement does not recognize. Title insurance that excludes the very risk the indemnification is meant to cover. Each tool assumes the others cover what they do not. The tools have to fit together — not create gaps — and that requires an attorney who has read all of them together, not just individually.

**Ignoring jurisdiction-specific rules.** Texas law has specific rules about title warranties, affidavits of heirship, statutes of limitations, and real property transfers. The structuring tools used in other states may not map directly to Texas practice. Local counsel matters. Out-of-state investors who adapt structures from their home state without local legal review create problems that are invisible until they are catastrophic.

**Treating every deal the same.** The right structure for a $100,000 deal is different from the right structure for a $2 million deal. Scale the tools to the transaction. Full layering on a small deal eats the margin. Minimal structuring on a large deal leaves the investor exposed. The structure should be proportionate to the economics and to the magnitude of the specific risk.

**Underinvesting in the initial search.** The cheapest residual-risk tool is the Chapter 30 search that eliminates the risk before it matters. Investors who skip the diligent search to save money and then spend more on structuring tools to manage risks they could have resolved are paying twice. Do the search thoroughly. Then decide what structuring is needed for the risk that remains.

**Hiding risks from the downstream buyer.** When you resell, the risks you managed do not disappear — they are inherited by your buyer. Disclosure is both a legal obligation and a reputational one. The investor who hides known title issues from a downstream buyer is taking on fraud exposure, rescission exposure, and the long-term reputational cost of being known as someone who doesn't disclose.

**Letting the seller dictate unreasonable terms.** Sellers sometimes refuse meaningful protections because they expect the buyer to take all the risk without compensation. Walk away from those deals. Not every deal needs to close. The deals you don't close protect the ones you do.

**Assuming the problem will just go away.** Ghost heirs do not age out of existence. The family of a missing heir from 1965 may still have descendants with claims today. Time passes; the underlying interests persist. Structuring for today's risk also means structuring for tomorrow's descendants of the people who are missing now.

## 34.14 Key Takeaways

**Residual risk cannot always be eliminated — but it can almost always be managed.** The tools in this chapter shift, quantify, insure, and hold back risk that cannot be fully resolved through the earlier chapters' tools. The toolkit exists precisely because perfect title is not always achievable within real deal economics.

**Not every deal with residual risk should close.** When the gap is large, the margin is thin, the other side won't engage fairly, or insurance won't touch it — walk away. The discipline to walk is the most important risk-management tool in this chapter.

**Escrow holdbacks are the workhorse.** Straightforward, negotiable, and effective. Size to the risk, duration to the limitations period, release triggers that are precise and measurable. The escrow is only as good as its size relative to the actual maximum exposure.

**Title insurance is the single most powerful tool.** It transfers risk to a regulated, capitalized insurer. Work with experienced title companies. Negotiate exceptions and endorsements before closing, not after. Read every exception in every commitment.

**Indemnification is only as good as the indemnitor.** Back promises with assets — personal guaranties, escrow, insurance. A promise without backing is paper. The indemnitor who is judgment-proof when the claim surfaces provides no protection at all.

**Contingent structures align incentives.** Earn-outs, graduated payments, options. Useful when both sides have reason to want title cleared. The seller's financial interest in title clearing is the buyer's leverage.

**Fractional acquisition with partition is a legitimate path.** When the full resolution is too slow or expensive, acquire what you can and use co-tenancy tools to resolve the rest. The partition proceeding produces clean title whether or not the missing interests were ever identified.

**Layer the tools.** Each tool has weaknesses. Layered structures protect against the failure of any single element. The cost of layering — 3%–5% of the transaction — is the premium for knowing the deal holds even when one layer fails.

**Documentation must be consistent.** Purchase agreement, deed, escrow agreement, affidavit of heirship, personal guaranties, title policy — the pieces have to fit together. Inconsistencies create gaps that claims fall through.

**Disclosure is non-negotiable.** To your buyer, to the title company, to any court involved. Hiding risks is fraud, and fraud unwinds deals and ends careers. The buyer who is fully informed and signs anyway is a protected buyer. The buyer who was not fully informed has a rescission claim.

**Match the tool to the deal.** $100,000 deals and $2 million deals need different structures. Scale the cost of risk mitigation to the transaction's economics and to the magnitude of the specific risk.

**The cheapest risk tool is doing Chapter 30 thoroughly.** Eliminating risk before closing is cheaper than managing risk after closing. Do the search thoroughly, then decide what structuring is needed for what remains.

**Time is a risk tool.** Statutes of limitations run. Records accumulate. Affidavits of heirship become prima facie evidence after five years. Patient investors benefit from the passage of time, even without active intervention.

**The layered structure plus genuine diligence is nearly bulletproof.** A transaction backed by thorough Chapter 30 search, a Chapter 33 judgment where warranted, title insurance with endorsements, an escrow holdback, seller warranties, and indemnification is about as close to risk-free as fractured-equity work gets. Not zero — but close enough to close with confidence.
