# Chapter 33: Unknown Heir Litigation: Heirship Determinations, Quiet Title, and Binding Unknown Heirs

## 33.1 The Unknown-Heir Problem

Sometimes you don't know who the heirs are. Not that they're missing — they might be right down the street — but that you cannot be certain you have identified them all. The decedent died without a will. The family tree is known only to the living generation. A first marriage from the 1950s left behind a child that nobody in the current family ever met. A brother who left Texas in 1962 had children somewhere. The courthouse record is silent on who actually inherited. Your chain of title has a hole, and the hole is shaped like "unknown heirs, if any."

This chapter teaches the legal mechanisms that resolve that uncertainty. Determination of heirship under the Texas Estates Code. Quiet title actions that bind all claimants, known and unknown. Declaratory judgments that fix ownership with the force of a court order. Publication notice that brings unknown parties under the court's jurisdiction. The attorney ad litem framework that protects the absent. And the procedural framework that, when used correctly, produces a judgment that the title industry will actually insure and that later claimants cannot easily undo.

This is the chapter where you turn probability into certainty. When Chapter 30 has found everyone findable, when Chapter 31 has served everyone who exists but cannot be reached, when Chapter 32 has secured signatures from those who cannot sign themselves — Chapter 33 closes the remaining gap by binding whoever might exist whom we have not yet identified. A judgment here is what transforms "my best guess of the heirs" into "the court has declared who the heirs are."

## 33.2 Where Unknown Heirs Come From

Before we work through the tools, understand the common scenarios that produce unknown heirs. Each has its own diagnostic signature in the record.

### Intestate Death with an Unclear Family Tree

The most common scenario. Decedent died without a will. No probate was opened. No determination of heirship was ever conducted. Forty years pass. Today, we know the decedent had a sister and two brothers — but one of the brothers had children by a relationship nobody in the family acknowledged, and those children may or may not be heirs depending on Texas intestacy rules applied to facts nobody has ever reduced to judicial findings. The heirship is theoretical; it has never been adjudicated.

### Prior Undisclosed Marriages

The decedent had a first marriage decades before anyone in the current family was born. That marriage may have produced children. A 1948 marriage in another state that ended in a 1952 divorce with one child can produce an heir who shows up in your chain of title as a ghost — legally real, documentarily invisible. Until you dig, you don't know whether the ghost has a name.

### Pretermitted Children

A child born after a will was executed, whom the will does not mention. Texas has specific statutes on pretermitted children that may give them a forced share regardless of the will's silence. If the testator had a child late in life and never updated the will, that child can claim an interest the will never accounted for.

### Non-Marital Children

Children born outside of marriage have full inheritance rights under Texas law if paternity can be established under the Texas Family Code's paternity provisions. A decedent who fathered a child in 1975 — acknowledged, unacknowledged, or somewhere in between — has an heir whose existence may not appear in any document the family has ever produced.

### Adopted-Out and Adopted-In Children

Texas law on inheritance by adopted-out children has changed over time. Under current law, a child adopted out generally does not inherit from the biological parents — but transitional rules, adult adoption, and specific family circumstances complicate the analysis. Adopted-in children, by contrast, inherit from the adoptive parents as if biological. Reading the adoption records matters.

### Deceased Heirs With Unknown Descendants

The decedent's son died in 1998. Did the son leave children? Grandchildren? What were their names? Where do they live? If they exist, they inherited their father's share through per stirpes distribution. Until you identify them — or until a court binds them through the procedures in this chapter — your title has a hole where the son's share used to be.

### Out-of-State Heirs

A sibling who moved to California in 1965, married, had children, and died in 2005. Those California descendants are Texas heirs of the Texas decedent. Texas courts have jurisdiction over Texas land regardless of where the heirs live, but locating and serving them adds complication — and if they cannot be found despite diligent search, the tools in this chapter bring them under the court's jurisdiction anyway.

### International Heirs

Heirs who live in Mexico, Central America, the Caribbean, or elsewhere. Locating them requires the international-trail work from Chapter 30. Serving them requires compliance with the Hague Service Convention or the Inter-American Convention on Letters Rogatory, depending on the country. Binding them through publication when they cannot be served at all is possible but requires additional procedural care.

## 33.3 Determination of Heirship — The Core Tool

The Texas Estates Code provides a specific judicial proceeding called a determination of heirship. It is exactly what it sounds like: a court hearing at which the court determines, with the force of a binding judgment, who the heirs of a deceased person are. For fractured-equity work, the determination of heirship is the single most powerful tool in this chapter.

### Statutory Basis

Determinations of heirship are governed by **Chapter 202 of the Texas Estates Code**. The proceeding can be initiated by any person with a property interest that depends on the heirship determination, including:

1. A purported heir
2. A party claiming to own property by purchase from an heir
3. A creditor of the estate or of an heir
4. A party with a lien on property that the decedent or an heir owned
5. Any other person whose rights may be affected

A fractured-equity investor who has acquired a fractional interest from one heir has standing to seek a determination of heirship that binds all other heirs, known and unknown.

### Where to File

Determinations of heirship are filed in the probate court of the county where:

1. The decedent resided at the time of death (primary venue)
2. The decedent's property is located (if the decedent did not reside in Texas)
3. The decedent died, in limited circumstances

In Texas counties with statutory probate courts (the larger urban counties), those courts have dedicated probate jurisdiction. In other counties, the county court at law or the constitutional county court handles probate matters. Your attorney will identify the correct venue and the correct court within that venue.

### What the Application Contains

The application for determination of heirship must state, under oath, specific facts about the decedent and about the persons claiming to be heirs. **Tex. Est. Code § 202.005** sets forth the required contents:

1. Name and domicile of each applicant
2. Name, age, marital status, and address of decedent, and time and place of decedent's death
3. Names and addresses of the decedent's heirs, their relationship to the decedent, and whether they are adults or minors
4. A facts-based account of the family history on which the claimed heirship is based
5. Whether decedent was married, and if so, the name of the spouse, the date and place of marriage, and whether either spouse was previously married
6. Identification of any known children, including children from prior marriages and children born out of wedlock
7. Allegation that no administration is pending or necessary, or details of any pending administration
8. Description of the property to be distributed
9. Statement that the applicant has made a diligent search for heirs and disclosure of any heirs whose identity or whereabouts is unknown

### The "Unknown Heirs" Language

Critically, the application can identify not only known heirs by name but also **"unknown heirs" as a category of defendants**. This is the procedural mechanism that allows the court to bind people whose names you do not yet have. The application essentially says: "The following persons are known heirs, and in addition, there may be unknown heirs whose identities cannot be determined despite diligent search; the applicant asks the court to determine heirship as to all heirs, known and unknown."

### Service Requirements

Under **Tex. Est. Code §§ 202.051 through 202.056**, notice must be given to all known heirs by personal service, and notice must be given by publication to unknown heirs. Specifically:

1. Personal service on known heirs in the state
2. Service by certified mail, or other methods, on known heirs outside the state
3. Service by publication on unknown heirs — running in a newspaper of general circulation in the county where the proceeding is pending, for the statutorily required period
4. Posting at the courthouse, as a backup to publication
5. Service on any known heirs whose addresses are unknown, by publication along with the unknown heirs

### The Attorney Ad Litem Requirement

Under **Tex. Est. Code § 202.009**, the court must appoint an attorney ad litem to represent the interests of any heirs whose names or whereabouts are unknown. This is not optional. The ad litem:

1. Conducts independent investigation into the heirship facts
2. Reports to the court on the investigation
3. Raises objections on behalf of the unknown heirs if the record does not support the proposed determination
4. Is paid from the estate or by the applicant, depending on the court's order

Applicant-side attorneys who treat the ad litem as a formality to be checked off get bad results. The ad litem who feels respected and well-informed does the investigation, concurs with the applicant's proposed findings, and the court enters the judgment. The ad litem who feels obstructed flags concerns for the court, and the court either continues the matter for additional evidence or declines to enter the requested findings.

### The Evidence at the Hearing

Determination of heirship hearings are evidence-driven. The court needs to see proof of the facts alleged in the application. Evidence typically includes:

1. Death certificate of the decedent
2. Marriage certificates (or records establishing that no marriage occurred)
3. Divorce decrees (if prior marriages ended in divorce)
4. Birth certificates of the claimed heirs
5. Death certificates of any predeceased heirs (to establish cascading heirship)
6. Testimony of two disinterested witnesses who knew the decedent and the family — this is statutorily required in most cases
7. The affidavit of diligent search (from Chapter 30) documenting the effort to locate unknown heirs
8. Any additional evidence on family history, prior marriages, non-marital children, or adoptions that the court may require

### The Two-Witness Rule

**Tex. Est. Code § 202.151** requires the testimony of at least two disinterested and credible witnesses who knew the decedent and the family. These witnesses testify under oath about the decedent's marriages, children, and family circumstances. "Disinterested" means the witness does not stand to benefit from the determination — which usually excludes heirs themselves. Identifying two such witnesses, contacting them, and preparing them to testify (or to provide depositions or written declarations if the court permits) is a meaningful task on the applicant side.

### The Judgment

If the evidence supports the application, the court enters a judgment declaring the heirs of the decedent and their respective shares. The judgment:

1. Identifies the decedent
2. Lists each heir by name (or the category of "unknown heirs" if appropriate)
3. States each heir's relationship to the decedent
4. States each heir's fractional share under Texas intestacy law
5. Becomes binding on all parties who were served, including unknown heirs bound through publication
6. Becomes binding on later claimants subject to the challenge rules described later in this chapter

### The Four-Year Limitations Period

Under **Tex. Est. Code § 202.0025**, an application for determination of heirship can be filed at any time. There is no limitations period on the application itself. However, the statute of limitations on related claims — for example, a later heir who claims they were not bound by the judgment — has its own timing rules. Working with an attorney who handles heirship matters regularly is essential to navigate these timing questions correctly.

## 33.4 Quiet Title Actions

Quiet title is the complementary tool. Where determination of heirship identifies who the heirs are, quiet title confirms who owns the real property and extinguishes competing claims. Together, these two tools produce the kind of definitive title that the industry will insure and that later claimants cannot easily unwind.

### What Quiet Title Does

A quiet title action asks the court to declare that the plaintiff holds good title to specific real property, free of any adverse claim by the named defendants. It's called "quiet title" because it quiets — silences — the competing claims. A successful quiet title judgment extinguishes the defendants' claims and leaves the plaintiff with clean title.

### Statutory Basis

Quiet title in Texas is partly statutory and partly rooted in the court's general equitable jurisdiction. Relevant statutes include:

1. **Tex. Prop. Code § 22.001 through § 22.045** — trespass to try title, the traditional Texas action to resolve land ownership disputes
2. **Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code Chapter 37** — the Uniform Declaratory Judgments Act, used for declaratory relief regarding real property interests
3. General equitable jurisdiction of district courts over title disputes

### Trespass to Try Title vs. Declaratory Judgment

Texas actually has two related but distinct procedures for resolving title disputes:

**Trespass to try title.** The traditional statutory action under Tex. Prop. Code Chapter 22. Requires specific pleading elements, a particular burden of proof, and ends in a judgment that vests title. Historically the principal vehicle for title disputes in Texas.

**Declaratory judgment on title.** Under Chapter 37 of the Civil Practice and Remedies Code, parties can seek a declaratory judgment as to title interests. Sometimes overlaps with trespass to try title, sometimes is a more flexible alternative depending on the specific facts.

Texas courts have developed a body of case law on when each procedure is appropriate. The short version: for resolving actual ownership disputes with specific defendants, trespass to try title is often the cleaner vehicle; for broader declaratory relief or where the dispute is more about the scope of an interest than the ownership itself, declaratory judgment may fit better. Your attorney will select the right vehicle based on the specific facts.

### Binding Unknown Parties

A quiet title or trespass-to-try-title action can name defendants as "unknown heirs" or "unknown claimants" along with known defendants. Under **Texas Rules of Civil Procedure Rule 116** (service on unknown defendants) and the associated case law, notice by publication can establish jurisdiction over unknown parties. The court appoints an attorney ad litem to represent the unknown parties. The resulting judgment binds the named and the unknown defendants alike.

### Evidence Requirements

Quiet title or trespass-to-try-title requires evidence supporting the plaintiff's claim of title. This typically includes:

1. The chain of title documents — deeds, probate records, heirship determinations
2. Evidence of the decedent's death and family circumstances
3. The diligent search affidavit from Chapter 30
4. Testimony establishing the family history and chain of ownership
5. Evidence rebutting any specific adverse claims

### Combining Heirship and Quiet Title

Sophisticated fractured-equity practice frequently combines determination of heirship and quiet title in a single proceeding. The application alleges both: "determine who the heirs are, and based on that determination, quiet title in the plaintiff as to the shares acquired from the identified heirs." The court enters a judgment addressing both issues at once. This reduces cost and timeline compared to two sequential proceedings.

## 33.5 Service on Unknown Heirs — Procedural Detail

Binding unknown parties requires precise procedural compliance. Sloppy service produces judgments that later get unwound. This section walks through the specifics.

### Rule 109 — Service by Publication

**Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 109** authorizes service by publication on certain categories of defendants, including unknown defendants. The applicant's attorney swears an affidavit stating that the names or residences of the defendants are unknown despite diligent search, and the court may then authorize publication.

### Rule 116 — Unknown Parties

**TRCP 116** specifically addresses service on unknown parties. Once authorized, notice is published in a newspaper of general circulation in the county where the proceeding is pending. The publication typically runs once a week for four consecutive weeks. Content of the publication includes:

1. The court in which the proceeding is pending
2. The case name and number
3. A description of the nature of the proceeding
4. A description of the property at issue
5. The deadline by which the defendants must answer
6. Notice that failure to answer will result in judgment by default

### Newspaper Selection

The newspaper must be one of general circulation in the county. Large urban counties have dedicated legal-notice newspapers that handle most such publications. Rural counties may have only one or two newspapers, and the court may specify which one. Cost varies from under $100 to several hundred dollars depending on the publication, the length of the notice, and the number of weeks.

### Posting at the Courthouse

In addition to publication, notice is typically posted at the courthouse. The clerk handles this step; it is rarely a meaningful issue but confirming compliance in the file is important.

### The Diligent Search Affidavit

The foundation of publication service is the diligent search affidavit — the document produced by your Chapter 30 work. This affidavit:

1. States that the applicant has conducted a diligent search for the unknown heirs
2. Describes the specific steps taken in the search (databases searched, people interviewed, records reviewed, specialized trails pursued)
3. States the results of the search — names and addresses identified, if any, and that despite the search, the identities or whereabouts of additional heirs remain unknown
4. Is sworn under penalty of perjury

This affidavit is read carefully by the ad litem, by the court, and potentially by later challenging claimants. The quality of the affidavit — concrete, dated, sourced — is the difference between a bulletproof judgment and a vulnerable one.

### Due Process Considerations

Publication notice is a constitutionally acceptable substitute for personal service only when personal service is not reasonably possible. The leading case, ***Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank & Trust Co.*** (1950), holds that notice must be "reasonably calculated" to reach the defendants. Publication is acceptable when the defendants cannot be identified or located through reasonable effort — but it is not acceptable as a shortcut when defendants' identities and addresses are actually findable. If you knew or could have known where an heir lives and you published instead of serving, the judgment is vulnerable to attack on due process grounds.

## 33.6 The Attorney Ad Litem's Work

Chapter 32 introduced the attorney ad litem concept for guardianship cases. In unknown-heir litigation, the ad litem plays an analogous but distinct role — representing the unidentified and unserved people whose interests may be bound by the judgment.

### Appointment

The court appoints the ad litem at or near the beginning of the proceeding. Selection is typically from a list of local attorneys who handle these appointments regularly. The court sets an initial fee structure — often an hourly rate with a budget, or a flat fee for defined tasks.

### The Ad Litem's Investigation

The ad litem's job is to investigate whether the proposed findings are correct and whether all reasonable efforts have been made to identify additional heirs. Typical ad litem work:

1. Review the applicant's pleadings and evidence
2. Review the diligent search affidavit and the underlying documentation
3. Conduct independent investigation — sometimes running additional database searches, contacting witnesses, or reviewing historical records
4. Interview the applicant's witnesses to confirm the family history
5. Sometimes contact known family members who were not part of the applicant's evidence
6. Review any international or specialized search efforts
7. Write a report to the court summarizing the investigation and the ad litem's recommendations

### Cooperation with the Ad Litem

Cooperation is not optional, and it's not just a courtesy. The ad litem is going to investigate one way or another. Cooperating with the investigation — providing documentation proactively, answering questions promptly, connecting the ad litem with family witnesses, paying invoices on time — produces a shorter investigation and a more favorable report. Obstructing the investigation produces a longer investigation and a more skeptical report.

### What Makes the Ad Litem's Job Easy

1. A complete and specific diligent search affidavit with sources, dates, and outcomes
2. Organized chain-of-title documentation
3. A clear family tree with supporting documents (birth certificates, marriage certificates, death certificates)
4. Accessible family witnesses who can speak to family history
5. Proactive disclosure of anything that might be an issue — a prior marriage nobody wants to talk about, a child born out of wedlock, an adopted child

### What Makes the Ad Litem's Job Hard

1. Vague or boilerplate diligent search affidavits
2. Unexplained gaps in the family history
3. Witnesses who won't talk, or who give inconsistent accounts
4. Facts that have been hidden or minimized in the application
5. Resistance to paying the ad litem's reasonable fees

### The Ad Litem's Report

At the end of the investigation, the ad litem files a written report with the court. The report:

1. Describes the investigation conducted
2. Summarizes the evidence supporting or contradicting the proposed heirship findings
3. States the ad litem's conclusions — typically agreement with the proposed findings, but sometimes objections or requests for further investigation
4. Addresses the adequacy of the search for unknown heirs
5. Addresses the adequacy of the service on unknown heirs

A favorable ad litem report is the most valuable single piece of evidence at the hearing. A skeptical report can delay or defeat the application.

## 33.7 The Default Judgment Pathway

After service has been completed — personal service on known heirs, publication on unknown heirs — defendants have a period (typically forty-two days from publication under the applicable rules) to answer. If they do not answer, the plaintiff may take default judgment.

### Documenting Service

Before seeking default, the record must establish that service was completed properly:

1. Returns of personal service, showing who was served, when, and how
2. Publisher's affidavit showing the notice was published on the dates required
3. Certificate of posting at the courthouse, if applicable
4. Clerk's certification that no answer was filed within the answer period

### The Default Judgment Hearing

Some courts require a hearing for default judgments in heirship or quiet title cases; some proceed on submission. Whether by hearing or submission:

1. The applicant's evidence is presented — sworn testimony, documentary evidence, the diligent search affidavit
2. The ad litem's report is filed and considered
3. The two-witness testimony is presented if required
4. The court enters findings and judgment

### The Judgment's Scope

A properly entered judgment in an unknown-heir case binds:

1. The named defendants who were personally served
2. The named defendants who were served by publication because their addresses were unknown
3. The unknown defendants bound through publication
4. The successors in interest of any of the above

Subject to the collateral attack rules described later, this judgment is the authoritative determination of who owns what.

## 33.8 Attacks on the Judgment

Judgments binding unknown parties through publication are not immune from challenge. Understanding the attack vectors is essential for structuring a judgment that will hold up.

### Bill of Review

Under Texas law, a party who was bound by a judgment but did not actually have notice of the proceeding may be able to file a **bill of review** (also called equitable bill of review) to set the judgment aside. Requirements typically include:

1. A meritorious defense — the party has a real claim that would have changed the outcome
2. That the party was prevented from asserting the defense by fraud, accident, or official mistake unmixed with the party's own negligence
3. That the party acted with due diligence upon learning of the judgment

A bill of review filed by an unknown heir who later surfaces and claims the diligent search was inadequate is the most common attack vector. Strong documentation in the original file is the best protection.

### Direct Attack on Due Process Grounds

A later-surfacing heir may argue that publication notice did not satisfy due process because the applicant actually knew or could have known the heir's whereabouts. If the heir can show that a basic reasonable search would have found them and the applicant's affidavit was misleading, the judgment can fall.

### Time-Based Limitations

Texas has limitations periods on various collateral attacks. Most residual-ownership claims become increasingly difficult to assert after four years. After certain periods — often ten years or longer, depending on the claim theory — most challenges become essentially impossible. Title insurance underwriting reflects these timing windows, which is why older determination-of-heirship judgments generally become more insurable over time.

### How to Make the Judgment Bulletproof

**Genuine diligent search.** The Chapter 30 methodology actually executed, not summarized. Professional databases run, specialized trails triggered, family interviews conducted.

**Specific affidavit.** Dates, names of databases, names of family members interviewed, dates of letters mailed. Not boilerplate.

**Competent ad litem with favorable report.** The ad litem's concurrence is powerful evidence in any later challenge.

**Complete evidence package.** All family documents, all witness testimony, all specialized-search results in the record.

**Fair treatment of any heirs who later surface.** Sometimes an heir appears six months or two years after the judgment. How you respond matters. A willingness to negotiate a fair resolution with that heir often heads off a bill-of-review challenge that would unwind more.

## 33.9 International Parties and Hague Service

When unknown heirs may reside outside the United States, service obligations become more complex. Binding international parties through publication is possible but requires additional procedural care.

### The Hague Service Convention

The **Hague Convention on the Service Abroad of Judicial and Extrajudicial Documents** governs service in signatory countries. For unknown heirs — people whose identities and addresses are unknown — Hague service typically cannot be effected because there is no identified address to serve. In that case, publication notice, properly documented, usually suffices. For known heirs in Hague signatory countries whose addresses are known, Hague service is required.

### Inter-American Convention

For Latin American countries, the **Inter-American Convention on Letters Rogatory** is an alternative or complementary framework. Mexico, in particular, has specific procedural requirements for service on Mexican nationals. Your attorney will coordinate with counsel in the relevant country when known foreign heirs must be served.

### Publication for International Unknown Heirs

When unknown heirs may be anywhere in the world, publication in a Texas newspaper is clearly inadequate to actually reach them — but it is what Texas law requires, and the due process question shifts to whether the applicant has done what is reasonable under the circumstances. Documentation of international search efforts — Catholic parish records, hometown associations, consular inquiries where appropriate — strengthens the position that publication is the best available method.

## 33.10 Costs and Timeline

Unknown heir litigation is more expensive and slower than transactions with identified, cooperative heirs. Budget accordingly.

### Typical Cost Breakdown

| Line Item | Typical Range |
|---|---|
| Attorney fees — applicant (straightforward) | $5,000 – $15,000 |
| Attorney fees — applicant (complex) | $15,000 – $40,000 |
| Attorney ad litem fees | $1,500 – $5,000 typical; more for complex |
| Publication costs | $100 – $800 |
| Filing fees and court costs | $500 – $2,000 |
| Professional database costs (diligent search) | $500 – $3,000 |
| Private investigator (when needed) | $2,500 – $20,000 |
| Expert witnesses (genealogists, when required) | $2,000 – $10,000 |
| Title insurance and related closing costs | Variable |

**Total budget for a typical fractured-equity heirship determination with quiet title: $10,000–$30,000.** Complex cases run significantly higher.

### Typical Timeline

| Phase | Duration |
|---|---|
| Chapter 30 diligent search completed | 30 – 90 days |
| Application prepared and filed | 2 – 4 weeks |
| Service on known heirs | 2 – 6 weeks |
| Publication period | Approximately 28 days of actual publication |
| Ad litem investigation and report | 30 – 60 days |
| Hearing and entry of judgment | 30 – 60 days after ad litem report |
| **Total elapsed time (straightforward)** | **6 – 9 months from start to judgment** |
| Complex cases (international heirs or appellate issues) | 12 – 24 months |

### Coordinating with Deal Economics

The costs and timeline in this chapter are substantial — meaning unknown-heir litigation makes sense only when the deal economics support the expense. A property worth $200,000 with a 1/8 unknown-heir gap worth $25,000 generally does not justify $15,000 in litigation to resolve the 1/8. A property worth $600,000 where the unknown-heir gap covers multiple fractional interests and total uncertainty affects half the title is a different calculus. Know the math before filing.

## 33.11 Worked Examples

### Example 1 — The Three-Generation Intestacy

A 60-acre tract in Washington County. Original owner died intestate in 1952. Three children survived him; two are now dead, and their children (the grandchildren of the original owner) are scattered across Texas and California. One of the dead sons, Howard, had a son named Michael whom the family knows almost nothing about — Michael's mother left the area when Michael was an infant, and no one in the Texas family has seen or spoken to Michael in forty years.

The applicant — the fractured-equity investor who has acquired interests from the known heirs — files a combined application for determination of heirship and quiet title. The application names all known heirs by name and specifically names "unknown heirs of Howard, deceased, if any." Publication runs. An attorney ad litem is appointed, investigates, runs additional TLO searches, interviews the family, and concludes that Michael cannot be located despite diligent search. Two disinterested neighbors from the 1950s — still alive and still living in the area — testify about the family history. The court enters a judgment determining the heirs, including "Michael [last name], if living, and his descendants, if any" as a recognized category, and quiets title in the applicant as to the interests acquired.

**Total cost: approximately $12,000. Total time: seven months.** Title insurance issues without exception. The deal closes.

### Example 2 — The Prior Marriage

A 120-acre ranch in Webb County. The decedent, a woman who died in 2008, had been married twice — first to a man she divorced in 1961, second to a man who survived her. Her second-marriage children (three adults) are known. The first-marriage situation is murky: the family knew vaguely that there had been a prior marriage but believed it was childless. Chapter 30 research reveals that the first husband is still alive and has told a distant cousin that "there was a child." No further information is available.

The applicant files determination of heirship and quiet title. The application names the second-marriage children, the first husband, and "unknown heirs of the prior marriage, if any." Publication runs. The ad litem contacts the first husband, who — now in his 80s and willing to clear the record — provides an affidavit stating that a child was born of the first marriage in 1960, was relinquished for adoption at birth, and has never been in contact. Under Texas law, children adopted out do not inherit from biological parents (subject to specific statutory exceptions). The court, with the ad litem's concurrence, enters a judgment determining the second-marriage children as the sole heirs. The relinquished child is named as a bound defendant through the "unknown heirs of the prior marriage" category.

**Total cost: approximately $18,000. Total time: nine months.**

### Example 3 — The International Heir Gap

A 20-acre tract in Zavala County. Decedent, originally from Coahuila, Mexico, died in Texas in 1995 owning the tract. Known Texas heirs: three children and five grandchildren. Suspected unknown heirs: descendants of a sibling who remained in Mexico and died in the 1980s. The applicant executes a full Chapter 30 international search — hometown association, Catholic parish records, consular resources — and identifies two of the Mexican sibling's descendants by name but cannot establish whether additional descendants exist.

The applicant files determination of heirship and quiet title, naming all identified heirs (Texas and Mexican) and "unknown heirs residing in Mexico or elsewhere, if any." Hague service is completed on the two identified Mexican heirs. Publication covers the unknown category. The ad litem reviews the international search documentation — letter to the parish, response from the parish, hometown association records — and concludes that diligent search has been completed. The court enters judgment. The identified Mexican heirs ultimately agree to sell their interests; the unknown heir category is bound by the judgment.

**Total cost: approximately $22,000. Total time: twelve months** (additional time for international service).

### Example 4 — The Bill of Review That Didn't Happen

A Brazoria County tract cleared through heirship determination in 2018. Three years later, a woman contacts the investor claiming to be an unacknowledged daughter of one of the deceased heirs — someone who should have been identified in 2018 but was not mentioned in the application. She has a DNA test and documentation. A bill of review is potentially on the table.

The investor's counsel reviews the 2018 file: the diligent search was thorough, the ad litem investigation was complete, two witnesses testified to the family's composition as they knew it — but nobody in the 2018 process had heard of this woman. The investor, advised by counsel, opens a negotiation with her rather than fighting. The woman agrees to a buyout of her interest at pro-rata fair value. A stipulated modification of the prior judgment is entered. The bill of review is never filed. The title remains insurable.

The experience teaches the investor that even a well-executed heirship determination cannot eliminate all possible later claims — but that responding fairly to surprise claimants is usually cheaper than litigating them.

## 33.12 When Not to Pursue Unknown Heir Litigation

**The interest gap is small.** If the uncertain portion of the title is a 1/32 interest in a $100,000 property, the math rarely supports $10,000+ of litigation. Walk away or proceed without resolving the gap.

**The family is entirely intact.** If every heir is known, identified, and within reach, heirship determination is still available but may be overkill compared to voluntary conveyances supported by affidavits of heirship.

**The deal economics are thin.** Marginal deals cannot absorb $15,000–$25,000 of litigation cost. Only deals with meaningful upside justify this tool.

**A title company will insure without it.** If the title company will issue a commitment on the chain of title as-is, with affidavits of heirship and appropriate exceptions, you may not need a full judicial determination.

**Time pressure is extreme.** If the deal must close in 60 days, unknown heir litigation cannot happen in that timeframe. Different tools apply — insurance, escrow, indemnification — and Chapter 34 covers those.

## 33.13 Coordinating with Other Chapters

### With Chapter 30 (Breadcrumbing)

The Chapter 30 methodology is the foundation for the diligent search affidavit that supports publication service in Chapter 33 litigation. Strong Chapter 30 work produces a strong affidavit that produces a judgment that withstands challenge. Weak Chapter 30 work produces the opposite. Every hour invested in thorough genealogical and location research in Chapter 30 is an investment in the durability of the Chapter 33 judgment.

### With Chapter 31 (Service by Publication)

Chapter 31 taught the general mechanics of service by publication. Chapter 33 applies those mechanics specifically in the context of heirship and quiet title. The publication rules are the same; the substantive claims are different. The procedural precision required is identical — defective publication in either context produces a judgment that can be attacked on notice grounds.

### With Chapter 32 (Heirs Who Can't Sign for Themselves)

Some known heirs may be minors, under guardianship, or otherwise unable to sign for themselves. Chapter 32 tools apply to their signatures. Chapter 33 tools apply to unknown heirs who may exist alongside the known-but-vulnerable ones. Both problems can appear in the same deal, and both can be addressed in a single combined proceeding if the pleadings are structured to cover all gaps at once.

### With Chapter 34 (Deal Structuring)

Chapter 34 covers how to close deals when unknown-heir risk cannot be fully eliminated through litigation — escrow, insurance, indemnification, contingent structures. The tools in Chapter 33 eliminate risk through judicial determination; the tools in Chapter 34 manage the residual risk that remains. When time pressure or economics make Chapter 33 impractical, Chapter 34 provides the next best option.

## 33.14 Key Takeaways

**Unknown-heir litigation resolves the ghost problem.** When you cannot identify every heir, judicial process binds whoever may exist whom you have not identified.

**Determination of heirship is the core tool.** Tex. Est. Code Chapter 202. Court-declared identification of the decedent's heirs and their shares. Any person with a property interest depending on the heirship determination has standing to file.

**Quiet title and trespass to try title are the complementary tools.** They confirm ownership and extinguish competing claims in the real property itself. Together with heirship determination, they answer both questions that title companies and buyers need answered.

**Combining heirship and quiet title in one proceeding is often best.** Resolves both the family-tree question and the ownership question in a single judgment, reducing cost and timeline compared to two sequential proceedings.

**Service requirements are strict.** Personal service on known heirs, publication on unknown heirs, proper posting and filing of returns. Procedural precision is essential — sloppy service produces judgments that later get unwound.

**The diligent search affidavit is the foundation.** Built from Chapter 30 work. Specific, dated, sourced, comprehensive. The affidavit's quality determines the judgment's durability against bill-of-review and due-process attacks.

**The attorney ad litem is central.** Appointed by the court to represent unknown parties. Cooperation with the ad litem produces favorable reports and clean judgments. Obstruction produces skeptical reports and delayed or denied applications.

**The two-witness rule must be met.** Tex. Est. Code § 202.151. Two disinterested witnesses who knew the family testify to the heirship facts. Identifying and preparing them is a meaningful task.

**Judgments can be challenged.** Bill of review and due-process attacks are the main vectors. Strong original documentation is the best defense. Responding fairly to surprise claimants who surface post-judgment is usually cheaper than litigating the challenge.

**International heirs add complexity.** Hague Service Convention for known foreign heirs in signatory countries. Inter-American Convention for Latin American countries. Publication-only binding for truly unknown international parties, supported by documentation of international search efforts.

**Budget $10,000–$30,000 and 6–9 months.** Straightforward cases. Complex cases run substantially higher and longer. Know the math before you file.

**Deal economics must support the process.** Small interest gaps in small deals don't justify six-month proceedings and five-figure legal bills. Match the tool to the economics.

**Fair treatment of surprise claimants.** Heirs who surface after judgment are common. Negotiated resolution is usually cheaper than litigation. The investor in Example 4 avoided a bill of review by writing a check — a far better outcome than eighteen months of appellate briefing.

## 33.15 What's Next

Chapter 34 covers deal structuring when missing-heir risk and capacity risk cannot be fully eliminated through the tools in Chapters 30 through 33. Escrow holdbacks, title insurance endorsements, indemnification agreements, and contingent purchase structures that allow transactions to close with manageable residual risk.

Together, the Part VI chapters — 30 through 34 — provide the complete toolkit for situations where the ordinary voluntary-acquisition pathway does not apply. Finding the missing, proceeding when the search fails, working with heirs who cannot sign, binding unknown parties through litigation, and structuring deals around residual risk that cannot be eliminated. With these tools, fractured-equity work extends from the simple cases where every heir is known and cooperative to the complex cases where the legal system itself must resolve the uncertainties that the family could not resolve.
