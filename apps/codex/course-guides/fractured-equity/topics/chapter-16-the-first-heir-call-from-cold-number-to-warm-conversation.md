## 16.1 Why the First Call Determines Everything

You've found the deal. You've researched the heirs. You've underwritten the economics. Now you have to pick up the phone and make a call to someone who has never heard of you, doesn't know you're coming, and has every reason to hang up.

The first call determines whether the deal happens or not. An operator who makes good first calls converts 40–60% of their contacts into genuine conversations that lead to negotiation. An operator who makes bad first calls converts 5–10%. Same deal opportunities, wildly different outcomes.

Most investors are terrible at this. They call with pressure, lead with money, sound scripted, push for signatures. The heir hangs up. The operator concludes "nobody wants to sell" and moves on. Meanwhile the operator down the street who knows how to talk to people is signing that same family's papers next week.

This chapter teaches you the first-call methodology that actually works. Opening scripts. Rapport building. Handling the objections you'll hear over and over. Setting up follow-up conversations. Knowing when to back off and when to press.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to pick up a phone, call an heir you've never met, and either (a) set up a second conversation or (b) get enough information to know the deal isn't happening — all within 10–20 minutes.

## 16.2 Before You Dial — The Pre-Call Preparation

Never make a first call cold off the research. Always prepare.

### What to Have in Front of You

1. The full family tree and heir's specific relationship to the decedent
2. The property address and basic details
3. The decedent's name and approximate date of death
4. This specific heir's fractional share calculation
5. Your notes on why this property is fractured
6. Any known facts about this specific heir (occupation, location, family situation)
7. Your basic offer parameters (range, not specific number yet)

### What to Know About the Heir Specifically

1. How are they related to the decedent? (Child? Grandchild? Niece?)
2. Are they the oldest or youngest? (Oldest often have more information; youngest sometimes more receptive)
3. Have they been involved in the property? (Living there? Managing it? Or completely detached?)
4. Are they local or distant? (Distant heirs often more motivated to liquidate)
5. Any known life circumstances? (Retirement, divorce, financial distress — all affect motivation)

### Mental Preparation

1. You're calling a person, not a target
2. They may be grieving, confused, or suspicious
3. Your job is to have a human conversation, not close a deal
4. If you push too hard, you lose them permanently

### Timing of Call

1. Weekday evenings (6:00–8:30 PM local time): best for most heirs
2. Weekend mornings (10:00 AM–12:00 PM): second best
3. Avoid Monday mornings and Friday evenings
4. Avoid holiday weeks unless you have specific reason
5. For older heirs, mid-afternoon often works
6. For working professionals, evenings or weekends

### Calls Per Session

Make 3–5 first calls per session, then stop. First calls are emotionally demanding if done right. You need to be fully present for each one. Don't try to power through 15 calls in a row.

## 16.3 The Opening Script — First 30 Seconds

The first 30 seconds of the call determine whether you have a conversation or not. Here's the opening that works:

### The Basic Opening

> "Hi, is this [heir's full first name]? My name is [your full name], I'm calling from [your company name] here in [your city]. I'm calling about a property that I believe was owned by [decedent's full name], your [relationship — father, grandmother, etc.]. Do you have a few minutes to talk?"

**Four elements:**

1. Confirm who you're speaking with (first name, not last, sets a less formal tone)
2. Identify yourself and your company
3. State the specific reason — the property and the decedent
4. Ask permission for their time

### Why This Works

1. You're not hiding anything. The purpose is clear from the start.
2. Identifying their specific relationship to the decedent shows you've done research and are not a random telemarketer.
3. Asking for their time treats them as a person, not a prospect.
4. Using the decedent's full name tells them this is legitimate — you know specific details about their family.

### What NOT to Open With

1. "Are you interested in selling a property?" (Too transactional, triggers resistance)
2. "I buy houses for cash, fast and easy!" (Sounds like a scam)
3. "Your deceased father left some property..." (Assumes details you shouldn't assume)
4. "I have a question about your family..." (Evasive, triggers suspicion)

### Handling the Immediate Question "Who Is This?" or "How Did You Get My Number?"

Most heirs will interrupt the opening with one of these questions. Answer directly:

> "My company does real estate research. We look at county tax records for properties that have been in the same family for a long time without anyone managing them. Your [relationship]'s property came up in that research, which is how I found his name and then was able to find you through public records. Everything I'm looking at is public information."

Key points:

1. Don't hide the research process
2. Don't claim you got their info from a mutual friend (lie, will be caught)
3. Acknowledge that it might feel strange to get this call
4. Make it clear you're working from public records, not stolen data

## 16.4 The Rapport-Building Phase — Minutes 1–5

Once they've agreed to talk, you have maybe 5 minutes to establish enough rapport that they'll engage in a real conversation. This phase is about them, not about the deal.

### Start with Acknowledgment and Condolences (If Applicable)

> "Before we get into any of the details, I want to say — I'm sorry for the loss of your [relationship]. I know that can be complicated, especially when there's property still to sort out."

If the decedent died more recently (within 2–3 years), this matters a lot. If the decedent died 20 years ago, it matters less but still acknowledge.

### Show Your Research, Briefly

> "I've been researching this property. It looks like your [relationship] owned it since [approximate year], and I can see from the tax records that the taxes have been unpaid for the past [X] years. I'm not trying to surprise you with information you don't know — I just want to let you know what I've learned, so we're on the same page."

**Purpose:**

1. Signals that you've done homework
2. Establishes the factual baseline
3. Creates shared reality they can respond to
4. Does not pressure them

### Ask Open-Ended Questions

> "Can you help me understand the family's situation with this property? I don't want to assume anything — what's been happening with it?"

Listen to their answer. Really listen. The information they share here is worth gold:

1. Who's been managing it (or not)?
2. What other family members are involved?
3. What's their personal connection to the property?
4. What's their financial situation?
5. What have they tried in the past?
6. Who are the other heirs, and what are their relationships?

### Active Listening Techniques

1. Paraphrase back what they've said: "So it sounds like your uncle was the one handling the taxes but then he passed..."
2. Ask follow-up questions: "What happened after that?"
3. Show empathy for difficult situations: "That sounds really frustrating"
4. Don't interrupt
5. Let silence happen — people fill silence with more information

### By the End of the Rapport Phase, You Should Know

1. How engaged this heir is with the property (high, medium, low)
2. What their general attitude is (cooperative, wary, hostile, confused)
3. Who else in the family they're connected to
4. Whether they have decision-making influence over others
5. What their timeline and motivation pressure points are

## 16.5 The Transition — Moving From Rapport to Business

After 5–8 minutes of rapport, you transition to the actual business of the call. Don't rush this. If you feel like you're still building rapport, stay there.

### The Transition Script

> "What I wanted to share with you is that my company actually works with families in exactly this situation. Properties that have been tied up for years because taxes weren't paid, or because the estate was never probated, or because there are a lot of heirs and nobody could agree on what to do. We help families get these properties off their hands, clean up all the title issues, pay the back taxes, and give each of the heirs some money for their share."

**Key elements:**

1. Name the problem in terms they'll recognize
2. Introduce your role as a solver
3. Hint at compensation without mentioning numbers
4. Don't make it sound like a high-pressure sales pitch

### Continue to the Specifics

> "In your specific situation, it looks like [briefly describe what you see]. I've done research on the family, and based on what I see, there are roughly [N] heirs who would each have an inherited interest in the property. What I do is work with each of the heirs individually to acquire their share, and then I clean up the property, pay the taxes, and either fix it up to sell or sell it as-is. It usually takes [timeframe] from start to finish."

### The Critical First Question

> "Is this something you'd be open to hearing more about?"

Wait for their answer. Don't keep talking. This is where the conversation shifts from information-giving to opt-in.

### Three Possible Responses

**"Yes, tell me more."** Proceed to the deeper conversation (Section 16.6).

**"Maybe, but I have questions."** Answer questions honestly and simply. Questions are good — they mean engagement.

**"No, I'm not interested."** Don't push. Acknowledge it, leave a door open (Section 16.8).

## 16.6 The Deeper Conversation — Minutes 10–20

Once they've expressed openness, you go deeper. This is where you explore whether this deal actually makes sense for both of you.

### Establish What They Understand About Their Own Situation

> "Before I explain anything about what I do, can you help me understand what you already know? Have you and your family talked about this property? What have you been told about who owns it?"

Listen carefully. Most heirs have incomplete understanding of their legal position. Common misunderstandings:

1. "My mother left everything to us in her will" (but the will was never probated)
2. "We all inherited it together" (without understanding fractional shares)
3. "Our uncle owns it now" (but Grandma's estate was never settled)
4. "The taxes are being paid" (when they aren't)

Don't correct them yet. Just understand what they think is true.

### Explain the Legal Reality (Carefully and Plainly)

> "Here's what I can see from the records — and I want to be clear, I'm not a lawyer, and I'm going to recommend that you talk to one. But based on what the title company and my attorney have told me:
>
> When someone dies without a will that goes through probate, Texas law decides who inherits the property. The property doesn't automatically pass to one person. Each of the decedent's children — and if any of the children have already passed away, each of their children — ends up owning a fractional share. For this property, that means there are approximately [N] people, including you, who each legally own a piece of the property.
>
> That's why the property has been stuck. With [N] separate owners, none of them can sell or deal with the property alone. And with nobody actively paying taxes, the county is eventually going to foreclose."

### Explain the Path Forward

> "The way my company helps in this situation: I work with each of the heirs individually. I offer each of you a payment for your share. I handle all the legal paperwork — the title insurance, the probate or heirship documents, the back taxes. Each of you gets paid from your share of the property's value. At the end, I own the whole property and I can either sell it or develop it. That's how I make my money, and that's how the family gets relief from a property that's been sitting there with problems for years."

### The "What's In It For Me" Moment

> "For you specifically, your share of this property — based on [decedent's family structure] — would be about [fractional share, e.g., 1/6 or 1/8]. The property's current market value is approximately [ARV or range]. Before back taxes and other costs come out, your theoretical share would be around [theoretical entitlement]. My offer is typically [a range that works in your underwriting, e.g., $15,000–$25,000] depending on how we structure everything. I can't give you a final number until I've confirmed everything, but I want to give you a sense of what we're talking about."

### Important Framing

1. Use the word "offer" not "price" (price implies negotiation, offer implies a decision they make)
2. Give a range, not a specific number, on the first call
3. Explain why you're not giving a final number yet (research not complete, confirmation with other heirs)
4. Don't apologize for the discount from theoretical entitlement — the discount is the price of certainty and immediacy
5. Don't oversell — let the math speak

## 16.7 Handling the Common Objections

Certain objections come up on almost every first call. Know how to handle them.

### "How Did You Find Me?"

> "I used public records. Your [relationship]'s name was on the property's tax records. I was able to trace through obituaries and genealogical records to identify family members, and then I used a public records search service to find phone numbers. Everything I'm using is publicly available information."

### "This Property Is Worth a Lot More Than That. We've Been Waiting for It to Appreciate."

> "You're right that the property has value. The market value is approximately [X]. But there are a few things between that number and cash in your pocket: the back taxes, the legal costs to clean up the estate, the fact that the property has to be sold, and the fact that there are [N] people with inheritance claims who each need to get paid. My offer is net of all of those costs. If you all tried to sell it on the open market together, you'd have to agree on everything, clean up title, pay taxes and attorneys, and split what's left. My offer gives you certainty and a quick path, and it accounts for the work I'm doing."

### "I Need to Talk to the Other Heirs Before I Agree to Anything"

> "That makes complete sense. Family decisions like this should include the family. Would you want me to contact them, or would you rather talk to them yourself first? I'm happy to do either. The only thing I'd ask is — if you do decide to talk to them first, let's set up a follow-up conversation so we can move forward. These situations don't get easier if they sit."

### "Can You Send Me Something in Writing?"

> "Absolutely. I'm going to send you a letter that explains everything we just discussed, the approximate offer amount, and what the process would look like. I'll have it to you within [timeframe]. What's the best email address or mailing address to send it to?"

### "How Do I Know You're Not Scamming Me?"

> "Great question, and I would ask exactly the same thing in your position. Here's what I can do: I can give you references to other families I've worked with, my attorney's contact information, my title company's contact information, and my business records. I'd rather have you check me out thoroughly than worry about it. I'll send all of that with the written offer. Also, at the end of the day, nothing actually happens until you sign papers in front of a notary. You're in control of whether this deal happens."

### "I Need to Think About It"

> "That's completely fair. This isn't a decision to rush. What I'd suggest: let me send you the written materials. You take a week or two to look them over, talk to family if you want, consult an attorney if you want. Then we set up a follow-up call. No pressure from me — I'll be here when you're ready. When would be a good time for me to call you back? Two weeks? Three?"

### "Your Offer Is Too Low. I Want More."

> "I understand. Let me explain how I got to this range, and then we can talk about what specifically feels too low. [Walk through the math briefly.] If you think there are factors I'm not considering, I want to hear them. I'm not trying to lowball — I'm trying to give you a real offer based on real numbers. What do you think it should be?"

Listen to their counter. If it's within 20% of your walk-away, you can negotiate. If it's 2–3x what you can pay, you have a different problem.

### "I'm Angry. This Feels Like You're Taking Advantage of Us"

> "I hear you. Let me say — I'm not going to argue with you about how this feels. What I will say is, the property is in the situation it's in, with or without me. I didn't create the back taxes or the title problems. My business is helping families in this situation, and the offer I'm making is based on real numbers and real work on my end. If you don't want to work with me, that's absolutely your right, and I'll wish you well. But I'd rather you make that decision based on facts than based on how you're feeling in this moment. Would it help if I came back to you in a few days?"

### "I'm the Executor / Administrator / I Have Power of Attorney, So I Make the Decision"

> "That's good to know. It actually simplifies things significantly. If you have the legal authority to sign for the estate, we can potentially handle this whole transaction with just your signature instead of having to work with each of the heirs individually. Can you tell me more about your role and when you were appointed?"

This opens up the Chapter 18 single-signature opportunity. Research this claim carefully.

## 16.8 When the Call Ends Without a Commitment

Most first calls end without a signed commitment. That's fine. The goal of the first call is a second call, not a signed deal.

### Ending with a Positive Door-Open

> "I appreciate you taking the time to talk with me. Let me send you the written information we discussed. I'll call you back on [specific date]. If anything comes up for you before then, here's my number. I'm genuinely here to help your family get this handled, and I'll respect whatever you decide."

### Ending with a Clear Next Step

> "So here's what I'm going to do: I'll send you the written offer by [date]. I'll call you on [date] to follow up. In the meantime, you can reach me at [number] if you have any questions. Sound good?"

### Ending with a Hard No

> "I understand this isn't going to work for you, and I respect that. If anything changes — or if you have questions down the road — please don't hesitate to reach out. Here's my direct number. I wish you the best."

Don't push when they say no. Hard pushing converts 1% of "no" calls but alienates the other 99%. Leave the door open gracefully and move on.

### Document Everything

Right after the call ends, while it's fresh:

1. Date and time of call
2. Full duration
3. Who else was on the call (if anyone)
4. Key things they said
5. Their attitude and openness level
6. Next steps and dates
7. Any new information about other heirs
8. Your gut read on whether this person will engage

This documentation becomes your CRM. Every follow-up should start with a review of your prior notes.

## 16.9 The Follow-Up Sequence

One call rarely closes a deal. Most deals take 3–8 contacts before a signed agreement.

### Standard Follow-Up Cadence

1. **Day 1:** First call
2. **Day 2:** Written offer sent by email or mail
3. **Day 7:** Follow-up call (did they receive materials? any questions?)
4. **Day 14:** Second follow-up call (more time to think; decision check)
5. **Day 21:** Third follow-up (moving toward close or walking away)
6. **Day 30+:** Monthly check-ins if they're still thinking

### Follow-Up Call Scripts

**7-day follow-up:**
> "Hi [name], it's [your name]. I wanted to follow up on our conversation from last week. Did you get the written materials I sent? Have you had a chance to look them over? What questions do you have?"

**14-day follow-up:**
> "Hi [name], checking in again. I know these decisions take time. Where are you in your thinking? Have you talked to other family members? Is there anything I can do to make this easier?"

**21-day follow-up:**
> "Hi [name], I wanted to see where things stand. I'm happy to continue working with you on this, and I'm also happy to step back if it's not the right time. What's your sense?"

**30-day+:**
> "Hi [name], it's [your name]. I'm just checking in periodically on this one because I know sometimes life circumstances change. No pressure at all — just wanted you to know I'm still here if and when you're ready to move forward."

### When to Stop Following Up

After 3–4 unanswered calls, move the heir to a quarterly check-in schedule. If they've been clear that they're not interested, respect that and stop. Continuing to call becomes harassment.

## 16.10 Calling Multiple Heirs — The Parallel Strategy

You don't call heirs one at a time. You call multiple heirs in parallel, which allows you to:

1. Build momentum from early commitments
2. Avoid being hostage to any one heir
3. Discover which heirs are decision-makers
4. Gather information about heirs you haven't called yet

### The Standard Sequence

**Week 1:** Call the "easiest" heirs first — ones likely to be cooperative based on your research. Usually adult children who live away from the property, have their own financial situations, and have no strong emotional attachment to the property.

**Week 2:** Call the "middle" heirs — people who might be involved but aren't central to the decision-making.

**Week 3:** Call the "hard" heirs — people who may be hostile, who live on the property, who are emotionally attached, or who have influence over others.

### Why This Sequence

By the time you talk to the hard heirs, you already have:

1. Signed commitments from easier heirs, which creates social proof
2. Information about the family dynamics from prior conversations
3. Clear understanding of who has decision-making influence
4. Confidence from momentum

### What to Say About Other Heirs

> "I've been in contact with other members of the family. I'm not at liberty to share what they've specifically said, but I can tell you that several of them are moving forward with me. My goal is to bring this together so everyone gets taken care of at the same time."

Don't name specific heirs unless they've given permission or unless it's strategic.

## 16.11 Protecting Yourself in Early Conversations

Early conversations can cause problems if you're not careful. Some precautions:

### Don't Make Promises You Can't Keep

1. Don't promise specific dollar amounts before your underwriting is final
2. Don't promise specific timelines before you've verified the heirship situation
3. Don't promise tax treatment (that's an accountant's domain)
4. Don't promise legal outcomes (that's an attorney's domain)

### Document Every Conversation

1. Notes right after each call
2. Save emails
3. Save text messages
4. Date-stamp everything

### Don't Engage with Threats

If an heir threatens legal action, says they'll report you for fraud, or threatens violence:

1. Don't argue
2. Don't threaten back
3. End the conversation politely
4. Document what was said
5. Consult your attorney before further contact
6. Sometimes these heirs need to go through the Part V leverage tools rather than negotiation

### Be Careful with Vulnerable People

If you detect that an heir has cognitive issues, is elderly and confused, is in active distress, or seems to lack capacity:

1. Slow down significantly
2. Encourage them to involve family or advisors
3. Don't push for signatures
4. Consider whether you should even proceed with this heir
5. Document your observations
6. Consult your attorney

These situations carry elevated legal and ethical risk. When in doubt, don't proceed.

## 16.12 The Call Log and CRM

Your call records are infrastructure. Treat them like it.

### Minimum Data Per Call

1. Heir name and relationship to decedent
2. Date, time, duration
3. Outcome category (engaged / lukewarm / hostile / no-answer / left message)
4. Key topics discussed
5. Specific things they said
6. Follow-up commitments made (by you or them)
7. Next scheduled contact date

### CRM Fields to Build

| Field | Notes |
|---|---|
| Deal ID | Links all heirs to the same property |
| Property address | |
| Decedent | Full name |
| Heir name and relationship | Per heir record |
| Fractional share | Calculated share |
| Current address and phone | |
| Call log | One entry per contact |
| Signed status | Pending / verbal commitment / signed / paid |
| Heir tier | Cooperative / neutral / holdout / hostile |

Described in Chapter 11. Whatever tool you use, build these fields.

### Team Sharing

If you have team members, they all need visibility into the call log. Nothing is worse than two team members calling the same heir with different information.

## 16.13 Calls That Go Really Well — The Fast Conversions

Occasionally, you'll make a first call that converts immediately. The heir is fed up, ready to sign, and looking for a solution.

### Signs You've Got a Fast Conversion

1. They say things like "I've been hoping someone would call"
2. They ask "How do we move forward?" on the first call
3. They immediately want to know timing and amount
4. They mention they've been dealing with other family members on this
5. They want to send you the paperwork they already have

### How to Handle These

1. Don't get excited and rush. Still do your due diligence.
2. Take time to confirm they understand the legal situation.
3. Still send written materials.
4. Still give the family a chance to weigh in.
5. But move as fast as they want to move.

### What to Watch For

Fast conversions are usually legitimate, but sometimes they're signals of problems:

1. Financial desperation (may make heir vulnerable)
2. Capacity issues
3. Hidden agenda (trying to cash out before other heirs know)
4. Pending litigation or adverse claims on the estate

If things feel too easy, do extra verification. Who are the other heirs? What do they think? Is there anything going on you should know about?

## 16.14 Key Takeaways for Operators

Before moving to Chapter 17, internalize these principles:

**The first call is the most important call.** Good first calls convert 40–60%. Bad first calls convert 5–10%.

**Prepare before you dial.** Know the family, the property, the heir's specific situation, and your offer parameters.

**The opening is transparent.** Identify yourself, your company, the property, and your reason for calling. Don't hide anything.

**Rapport before business.** 5–8 minutes of listening and understanding before you talk about the deal.

**Listen more than you talk.** The information heirs share is worth gold. Silence draws out detail.

**Be honest about the math.** Explain the offer range. Explain why it's discounted from theoretical entitlement. Don't pretend you're doing charity.

**Handle objections with truth.** Every common objection has an honest answer. Use it.

**Document everything.** Call logs are infrastructure.

**Parallel calls, not serial calls.** Work multiple heirs simultaneously for momentum and information.

**Respect hard no's.** Don't convert by harassment. Leave doors open.

**Follow up systematically.** Most deals take 3–8 contacts. Have a cadence.

**Protect yourself.** Document. Don't overpromise. Be careful with vulnerable people. Consult attorney when necessary.

**Fast conversions happen.** Don't rush them, but don't slow them down either.
