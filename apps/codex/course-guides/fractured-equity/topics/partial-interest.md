A partial interest acquisition is exactly what it sounds like: you buy one person's fractional ownership share in a property. If four heirs each own 25% of a house worth $200,000, you might pay an heir $8,000 for their 25% share. The other three heirs still own their shares. The house hasn't been sold. But you now own 25% of it — and that 25% comes with a bundle of legal rights that changes everything.

## The Bundle of Rights in Cotenancy

When you acquire a partial interest as a tenant in common, you inherit a specific set of legal rights that every co-owner holds against every other co-owner. These rights are what make a partial interest worth acquiring:

**Right to enter** — As a co-owner, you have the legal right to enter and use the property. You cannot be excluded. If the property is occupied by another co-owner or a tenant, you can assert your right of access.

**Right to pay and seek contribution** — You can pay the property taxes, insurance premiums, and necessary repair costs on the entire property — and then demand that every other co-owner reimburse you for their proportional share. If they refuse, you can sue them for it. This is the contribution right, and it's one of your most powerful tools.

**Right to force partition** — This is the most important right. Any co-owner — regardless of the size of their interest — can file a lawsuit asking a court to partition the property. The court will either divide the property physically (partition in kind) or order it sold and divide the proceeds (partition by sale). You don't need majority ownership. You don't need the other owners' consent. The right to partition is nearly absolute.

**Right to obtain a judgment** — If other co-owners owe you contribution payments and refuse to pay, you can get a monetary judgment against them. If they can't pay the judgment, the court can order a sheriff's sale of their interest in the property to satisfy the debt.

## Acquisition Strategy

The partial interest acquisition process has a predictable sequence. Start by identifying which heirs are cooperative — usually the ones who are paying attention, dealing with the financial burden of the property, or simply ready to be done with it. These are your sellers. You want to acquire as much as possible from cooperative heirs before the hostile heirs understand what's happening.

Once you've acquired enough interest to be a meaningful co-owner, your position shifts. You're no longer just a buyer trying to negotiate — you're a co-owner with legal rights. You can start paying taxes, documenting your expenses, and creating a contribution obligation that the other heirs owe you. You can make formal demands for reimbursement. And you can file a partition action if they won't cooperate.

The partial interest isn't the end state. It's the entry point. The goal is always to acquire the entire property at a discount that reflects the illiquidity and complexity of the fractured ownership. The partial interest gives you the legal tools to get there.
