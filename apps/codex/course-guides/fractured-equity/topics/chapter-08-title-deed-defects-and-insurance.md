## 8.1 Why This Chapter Is Different

Title insurance is not a legal doctrine. It's an industry practice. That distinction matters more than it sounds. Every other chapter in Part II teaches you law — statutes, case law, legal procedures codified and applied through courts. Title insurance is different. It's a private industry, operated by insurance underwriters, who make business decisions about what risks they'll insure based on their own internal standards.

Here's why this is critical: you can do everything right under the law and still have a deal die because a title insurance underwriter won't issue a policy. The converse is also true. Something that seems legally questionable can still close because a title company is willing to insure it. The law is one filter. The title company is a separate, stricter filter. Every deal you do has to pass both.

This chapter covers three related topics that belong together: how title insurance works, what deed defects look like at the foundational level, and how the two interact. The chapter teaches you enough to recognize defects when they appear in chain-of-title work and enough to understand how underwriters respond to them. Chapter 35 at the end of the Codex provides the comprehensive capstone — the full synthesis of every cure available for every type of defect, drawing on tools developed across Parts II through VI. If you want the master treatment of defective title and cures, Chapter 35 is where it lives. This chapter gets you foundationally literate in the topic.

Understanding this material is what separates operators who close deals from operators who identify deals that never close.

## 8.2 What Title Insurance Actually Is

Title insurance is an insurance product that protects against losses arising from defects in title to real property. Unlike other insurance (which protects against future events), title insurance protects against past events — defects, liens, encumbrances, and claims that existed before the policy was issued but that weren't discovered or weren't properly cleared.

Two main types of title insurance policies:

### Owner's Title Insurance Policy

Issued to the buyer (now owner) of the property. Protects the owner against losses if title defects surface after closing. Typical coverage includes:

1. Undisclosed liens or encumbrances
2. Prior deed defects
3. Missing heirs whose interests weren't accounted for
4. Forged or fraudulent documents in the chain
5. Incorrect legal descriptions
6. Boundary disputes with neighbors
7. Improperly recorded documents that later affect title

The owner's policy stays in effect as long as the owner (or their heirs) owns the property. It pays until the insured amount is exhausted if defects surface and cause losses.

### Lender's Title Insurance Policy (Mortgagee Policy)

Issued to the lender who holds a mortgage on the property. Protects the lender's security interest, not the owner's ownership. The lender's policy decreases as the loan is paid down and terminates when the mortgage is paid off.

When a property is sold with a mortgage, the buyer typically pays for both an owner's policy (to protect themselves) and a lender's policy (to protect the lender). The two policies cover different interests and don't overlap significantly.

## 8.3 How Title Insurance Differs from Other Insurance

Understanding the structural difference helps you work with title companies effectively.

**Other insurance works on the probability of future losses.** A car insurance company calculates what percentage of drivers will have accidents, multiplies by the average accident cost, adds profit margin, and charges premiums based on that math. They expect to pay claims and price accordingly.

**Title insurance works on the elimination of past losses.** A title insurance company examines the property's title history before issuing a policy. If they find any defects, they require them to be cured before they'll insure. Their goal is to not pay claims. A title company that pays claims has done its examination badly.

This means:

1. Title insurance underwriting is front-loaded. All the work happens before closing, not after.
2. The examination is the product. What you're really buying isn't just insurance — you're buying the title company's research and their affirmative statement that the title is clear.
3. Underwriters are conservative. They don't want to take risks they didn't evaluate. They'll refuse to insure defects they can't analyze, even if the defects might not cause actual problems.
4. Relationships matter. Because underwriting involves judgment calls, the relationship between the operator and the title officer matters significantly.

## 8.4 How a Title Commitment Works — The Deal's Reality Check

When you're ready to close a deal, the title company issues a title commitment. This is the document that tells you what they will and won't insure.

A title commitment has four critical schedules:

### Schedule A — The Transaction

Identifies the parties, the property being insured, the amount of insurance, and the type of policy. This is descriptive — who's buying what from whom for how much.

### Schedule B — Exceptions from Coverage

Lists all the matters affecting title that the title company will **NOT** insure against. These fall into two categories:

1. **Standard exceptions** (appear on every policy): rights of parties in possession, discrepancies in survey matters, unrecorded easements, taxes not yet due and payable, etc.
2. **Specific exceptions** (unique to the property): specific recorded easements, existing mortgages, specific liens, etc.

Schedule B is what you're accepting. If a matter appears on Schedule B, the title company isn't insuring against it. You're taking the risk.

### Schedule C — Requirements (This Is Where Deals Die)

Lists what must be done before the title company will close and issue the final policy. Common Schedule C requirements:

1. Payoff and release of existing mortgages
2. Satisfaction and release of judgment liens
3. Curative actions on defective deeds
4. Affidavits from specific parties
5. Probate of wills
6. Heirship affidavits or judgments declaring heirship
7. Spousal signatures on homestead conveyances

Every Schedule C requirement is a to-do that must be completed before closing. If you can't satisfy a Schedule C requirement, the deal doesn't close. This is where operators spend most of their title-company time.

### Schedule D — Disclosures

Disclosures required by state law, including the identity of the title insurance company's principals and any financial arrangements. Usually boilerplate and not a source of closing problems.

## 8.5 Schedule C Requirements — The Operator's Primary Adversary

Schedule C is where your deals actually happen — or don't.

### What Schedule C Looks Like in a Fractured Equity Deal

Let's say you're closing a deal where you've acquired from six heirs of a decedent who died intestate in 2004. The title company's Schedule C might require:

1. Affidavit of Heirship for the decedent, signed by two disinterested witnesses, properly recorded
2. Payoff of $47,200 in delinquent property taxes
3. Release of a mechanic's lien recorded in 2009 (which was never released when the work was paid for)
4. Quitclaim deeds from two additional heirs the title examiner identified (beyond your original six) — heirs you need to locate and acquire from
5. Correction deed for a defective conveyance from 1987 in the chain of title
6. Spousal waiver of homestead rights from a surviving spouse (if homestead status applies)
7. Indemnification agreement from you covering certain identified risks

Your job as the operator is to satisfy each of these requirements. Each one has costs, timelines, and sometimes uncertainty about whether it can be satisfied at all.

### How to Respond to Schedule C Items

**Liens and mortgages with payoff amounts:** Pay them at closing from proceeds. Build the payoff into your underwriting.

**Missing heirs or additional heirs identified:** Negotiate and acquire their interests. If they can't be located, see Chapter 32 on missing heirs (indemnification agreements, bonds, etc.).

**Defective deeds in the chain:** Correction deeds (if original parties are available) or quiet title action (if not). Plan for 2–6 months of additional timeline for quiet title. Chapter 35 walks through the full curative toolkit.

**Heirship documentation:** File the appropriate Affidavit of Heirship or Judgment Declaring Heirship (see Chapter 9).

**Spousal waivers:** Locate surviving spouses (even estranged or long-separated) and obtain their signatures. Sometimes requires travel and relationship-building.

**Indemnification requirements:** Review carefully. Understand what risks you're agreeing to indemnify against. Push back on overbroad indemnifications.

## 8.6 The Title-Insurance-As-Shield Principle

Here's the strategic insight that shapes every acquisition in this Codex:

**The moment you have a title insurance policy covering a defect, the defect is functionally cured for purposes of resale.**

Not legally cured. Not academically cured. But cured for all practical purposes — because when you resell the property, the new buyer's title company can accept your existing policy, update it, and issue a new policy to the buyer. The underwriter has already signed off on the risk. You're no longer fighting the underwriting battle.

### Why This Matters

Some title defects are technically legal issues that could theoretically cause problems decades later. A missing heir whose interest couldn't be located. An equitable adoption claim that wasn't formally adjudicated. A defective acknowledgment on a 1962 deed. In a strict legal sense, these might create challenges to title in the future. In an insurance sense, once the underwriter issued a policy despite them, they're handled.

When you acquire a property with title problems and get a title insurance policy covering those problems, you've converted a legally messy property into a marketable property. The next buyer doesn't need to analyze the 1962 deed defect — they can rely on your policy.

### How This Principle Affects Your Strategy

**1. Work toward insurable title, not perfect title.** Not every defect needs to be legally perfect. It needs to be acceptable to an underwriter.

**2. Develop relationships with underwriters who accept complexity.** Some title companies specialize in cleaning up messy titles. They'll accept what standard underwriters won't. These are your partners.

**3. Use indemnification strategically.** When a minor defect can't be perfectly cured but you're willing to indemnify against it, underwriters may accept your indemnification and still issue a policy.

**4. Recognize the asymmetry.** A defect that nobody noticed for 50 years is unlikely to cause problems in the future. A defect that's fresh and prominent is a bigger risk. Underwriters often apply this practical thinking.

## 8.7 Underwriter Rules vs. Legal Rules — Where They Diverge

Underwriters don't always follow legal rules. They follow their own internal guidelines, which are often more conservative than the law. Understanding where these diverge is critical.

### Example 1: The AOH Maturity Rule

**Under Texas law,** an Affidavit of Heirship becomes prima facie evidence of heirship after it has been on record for the statutory period set by Texas Estates Code § 203.001. Before the statutory period runs, the AOH is still useful evidence but doesn't carry the strong presumption of accuracy.

**Underwriter rule:** Most title underwriters require that an AOH have been on record for at least four years before they'll rely on it to establish heirship, even though the law technically allows earlier reliance in certain circumstances.

**What this means for operators:** If the decedent died recently and you need to close quickly, a brand-new AOH may not be sufficient for title purposes even if it's legally adequate. You may need to pursue a Judgment Declaring Heirship (which has no four-year wait) or structure the deal to wait for the AOH to mature.

### Example 2: Outside-Children Research Standards

**Legal rule:** Due diligence in identifying heirs requires reasonable effort based on the specific facts. "Reasonable" is a fact-specific determination.

**Underwriter rule:** Title companies typically require specific documentary evidence that due diligence was performed — marriage record searches, birth record searches, obituary research, sometimes formal genealogical reports. They want paper trails, not just oral representations.

**What this means for operators:** Document your research. Save the Ancestry.com search screenshots. Save the obituary PDFs. Save the genealogist's report. Your research file is what satisfies the title underwriter, not your memory of what you did.

### Example 3: Defective Deeds in the Chain

**Legal rule:** A defective deed may or may not have conveyed title depending on the nature of the defect and subsequent events. Defects can sometimes be cured by adverse possession, by ratification, by equitable principles, or by quiet title.

**Underwriter rule:** Most underwriters require affirmative curative action on identified defects. They don't want to rely on adverse possession that hasn't been adjudicated, or on ratification that hasn't been formally documented.

**What this means for operators:** When a defect is identified, prepare to actively cure it. Don't assume the underwriter will accept your legal theory about why the defect is harmless. Chapter 35 walks through every cure available for every type of defect.

### Example 4: Probate Shortcuts

**Legal rule:** Several heirship-establishment mechanisms exist, including formal probate, affidavits of heirship, muniment of title (proving a will's execution without formal administration), and small estate affidavits.

**Underwriter rule:** Underwriters have strong preferences for some of these over others. In complex cases, some underwriters will only accept a Judgment Declaring Heirship — they won't rely on AOHs or muniment filings. Others accept a wider range.

**What this means for operators:** Know your underwriter. Different underwriters have different standards. One company may accept an AOH where another requires a judgment. This is part of relationship management — knowing which underwriter handles which kinds of cases.

## 8.8 Working with Title Companies — The Relationships That Matter

Your relationships with title companies are among the most valuable assets in your business. Serious operators develop deep relationships with 1–3 title companies who understand their work and can move deals through efficiently.

### What Makes a Good Title Company Relationship

**Responsiveness.** The title officer returns your calls within hours, not days. When a Schedule C requirement needs discussion, you can get on the phone with the underwriter quickly.

**Willingness to think creatively.** When a complex fact pattern arises, the title officer thinks about how to make it work rather than immediately declining.

**Knowledge of your business.** The title officer understands fractured equity deals. They've seen heirship issues before. They know what AOHs look like. You don't have to educate them on the fundamentals.

**Connection to accommodating underwriters.** Some title companies have access to underwriters who are known for handling messy title. Others only work with conservative underwriters. This matters significantly.

**Reliable closing execution.** When you schedule a closing, it happens on time. Documents are prepared correctly. Funds clear as expected.

### How to Build These Relationships

**Give them regular business.** Title companies make money on transaction fees. If you're sending them 10+ deals a year, you become a priority client. If you're sending them one deal a year, you're just another customer.

**Pay promptly.** Always pay title company invoices on time. Even small cash flow issues on your side look like red flags to theirs.

**Don't be a pain.** Show up prepared to closings. Have your documents in order. Don't argue about small fees. The title officer's job is hard; don't make it harder than necessary.

**Bring them interesting problems.** Title officers get bored with routine work. When you bring them a complex heirship situation with good documentation, they appreciate the intellectual challenge. They remember clients who bring interesting deals.

**Pay for the harder deals.** If a deal requires extra research time or negotiation with the underwriter, pay a little more than the base fee. Title officers remember clients who value their work.

## 8.9 Identifying Operator-Friendly Title Companies

Not every title company is a good fit for fractured equity operators. Some are built for routine residential transactions and struggle with anything complex. Others specialize in the kinds of messy titles you'll be bringing.

### Signs of an Operator-Friendly Title Company

1. They've handled AOH-based closings recently
2. They have established underwriter relationships for complex matters
3. The title officer knows what a "Judgment Declaring Heirship" is without having to look it up
4. They're willing to discuss Schedule C requirements before issuing the commitment
5. They understand partial interest acquisitions and don't treat them as unusual
6. They have experience with quiet title actions, defective deeds, missing heirs
7. They're located in or familiar with the counties where you work

### How to Find These Companies

Ask other operators in your market. Local operator networks often share recommendations.

Ask real estate attorneys who handle estate matters. They know which title companies can handle complex work.

Ask auctioneers and trustees who sell estate properties. They've seen title companies in action.

Attend local real estate investor meetings and ask about title company experiences.

### Testing a New Relationship

Start with a simple deal. See how they handle communication, document preparation, closing execution. Then bring them something more complex. Evaluate:

1. Did they ask intelligent questions?
2. Did they anticipate issues?
3. Did they come back with creative solutions?
4. Did they execute closing cleanly?

If they pass these tests on 2–3 deals, they're a good candidate for long-term partnership.

### Red Flags in a Title Company

1. They can't explain Schedule C requirements clearly
2. They resist when you ask to speak with the underwriter
3. They default to "we can't insure that" without exploring alternatives
4. Their fees are significantly higher than competitors without clear justification
5. Their communication is slow and requires constant follow-up
6. They've had multiple closing failures or delays on your deals

## 8.10 Specific Coverage Concerns in Fractured Equity Deals

Certain coverage issues come up repeatedly in this business. Know what to watch for:

### Heirship Coverage

Title companies may exclude coverage for "undisclosed heirs" as a standard exception. When heirship is a central feature of the transaction (as it is in most fractured equity deals), you want this exception removed or modified.

Options:

1. Negotiate removal based on comprehensive AOH and research
2. Accept the exception and use indemnification
3. Pay additional premium for enhanced coverage

### Matters of Survey

Survey exceptions are standard. If you've obtained a current survey, you can often have this exception removed or narrowed. Whether the expense is worth it depends on the deal.

### Creditor Claims

If you acquired the property through a sheriff's sale, tax sale, or similar procedure, title companies may exclude creditor claims. Some of this is unavoidable; some can be negotiated.

### Environmental Matters

Not typically covered by title insurance anyway. Environmental liability is a separate issue addressed by other policies and diligence.

### Easements and Rights of Way

Standard exceptions for matters that would be disclosed by a physical inspection. Not usually a problem unless the exception is broader than it needs to be.

### Federal Tax Liens

Federal tax liens are particular because they can persist even after state-law limitations would normally extinguish claims. Title companies are very careful about federal tax liens. If one exists on your property, plan for careful analysis and possibly IRS-specific curative work.

## 8.11 Title Insurance Costs

Understanding what title insurance actually costs helps you budget accurately.

Texas title insurance premiums are regulated — all title companies charge the same base premium for a given policy amount in a given county. Premium rates are set by the Texas Department of Insurance and published in a fee schedule.

### Typical Premiums by Policy Amount

| Policy Amount | Approximate Owner's Premium |
|---|---|
| $100,000 | ~$832 |
| $200,000 | ~$1,328 |
| $300,000 | ~$1,707 |
| $500,000 | ~$2,450 |
| $1,000,000 | ~$4,200 |

The Texas system includes a "simultaneous issue discount" — if you're buying both an owner's policy and a lender's policy on the same transaction, the lender's policy premium is reduced significantly.

### Beyond the Base Premium

| Item | Typical Cost |
|---|---|
| Title search fees | $150–$450, depending on complexity of chain |
| Examination fees | $100–$300 for the examiner's review |
| Document preparation | $50–$300 for deed and related documents |
| Escrow fees | $150–$500 for closing administration |
| Recording fees | $35–$75 per document |
| Endorsement fees | $50–$150 per endorsement |
| Curative work | Variable — can run $1,000–$5,000+ |

### Typical Total Closing Costs on a $200,000 Resale (Seller Side)

| Item | Cost |
|---|---|
| Title insurance premium (owner's policy, paid by seller in most Texas markets) | $1,328 |
| Search and examination | $300 |
| Document preparation | $150 |
| Escrow | $300 |
| Recording | $100 |
| **Total** | **~$2,200** |

### Who Pays What

Varies by local custom and contract terms. In most of Texas, the seller pays for the owner's title policy; the buyer pays for the lender's policy if a loan is involved. Closing costs are negotiable — everything is ultimately about who agrees to pay.

## 8.12 Special Endorsements That Matter

Beyond the base policy, endorsements can add or modify coverage for specific concerns. Common endorsements in fractured equity deals:

**T-19 Restrictions (Owner's Policy).** Provides enhanced coverage for violations of covenants, conditions, and restrictions. Useful when acquiring properties with old deed restrictions or HOA covenants.

**T-20 Manufactured Housing Unit.** Required when a manufactured home is part of the property. Addresses the specific title issues arising from mobile and manufactured home ownership.

**T-25 Survey.** Provides coverage for matters that would be disclosed by an accurate survey. Allows removal of survey exceptions from Schedule B.

**T-30 Tax Deletion.** Addresses property tax coverage specifics. Relevant when tax delinquency was cleared at closing.

**T-39 Deletion of Arbitration.** Removes the mandatory arbitration clause from the policy, preserving the insured's right to sue in court. Standard recommendation from most real estate attorneys.

Your title company will recommend relevant endorsements based on the specific deal. Understand what each one does and whether it's worth the additional cost.

## 8.13 What Happens When Title Insurance Fails

Title insurance isn't perfect. Even insured properties sometimes face title problems. Understanding what happens in that case helps you evaluate your exposure.

### When a Claim Is Made

1. The insured (property owner) notifies the title insurance company of the alleged defect
2. The title company investigates
3. The title company decides whether the claim is covered and what action to take

If covered, the title company typically does one of three things:

1. Defends the title in court (most common for disputes)
2. Pays to clear the defect (for liens or encumbrances)
3. Pays the loss up to policy limits (for losses that can't be cured)

### Limitations of Coverage

1. Coverage is limited to the policy amount (typically the purchase price)
2. Coverage excludes matters listed on Schedule B
3. Coverage excludes matters created by the insured
4. Coverage excludes matters the insured knew about but didn't disclose

### Practical Reality

Title insurance claims are relatively rare. Industry data suggests that title insurers pay claims on only 5–10% of policies, compared to 70%+ claim rates on casualty insurance. The real value is in the underwriting — the defects found before closing and cured before the policy issues.

## 8.14 Deed Defects — Why This Matters Now

Everything above addressed title insurance. The next several sections address deed defects. These belong in the same chapter because deed defects are the primary thing title insurance underwriters are looking for when they examine title — and they're the primary reason most Schedule C requirements exist in the first place.

A deed defect is any problem with a document in the chain of title that makes the conveyance legally questionable. Some defects are minor and easily cured. Others are severe and can kill a deal entirely. Every operator needs to be able to recognize defects when they appear in a title commitment or a chain-of-title review, understand the severity, and know whether the defect has a practical cure.

This chapter introduces the universe of deed defects at a foundational level — what they are, how to recognize them, and how severely title insurance underwriters treat each category. The comprehensive treatment of every cure available for every defect type is in Chapter 35. Think of these sections as diagnosis; Chapter 35 is treatment.

## 8.15 The Taxonomy of Deed Defects

Deed defects fall into a small number of recognizable categories. Once you understand the categories, recognition becomes straightforward. Every defect you encounter will fit one of these patterns:

### Signature Defects

1. Missing signatures from parties who should have signed (e.g., a spouse who was required to sign but didn't)
2. Signatures by parties without authority (a signature by someone who wasn't actually the owner)
3. Forged signatures
4. Signatures by parties with impaired capacity or under duress
5. Signatures in representative capacity without proper authorization (e.g., an agent signing without a valid power of attorney, or a trustee signing without authority under the trust instrument)

### Acknowledgment Defects

1. Missing notarization
2. Defective notarial acknowledgment (wrong date, wrong county, missing seal, expired commission)
3. Acknowledgment by a notary who was not actually authorized
4. Acknowledgment that doesn't match the signer's actual appearance before the notary

### Legal Description Defects

1. Wrong property described
2. Ambiguous description (cannot be located on a map)
3. Partial description (only a portion of the intended property)
4. Conflict between metes-and-bounds and lot-and-block descriptions
5. Scrivener's errors in the legal description (typographical or transcription mistakes)

### Capacity Defects

1. Grantor was deceased at the time of the conveyance (impossibility)
2. Grantor was a minor (voidable)
3. Grantor lacked mental capacity (voidable)
4. Grantor was an entity that had lost authority (e.g., defunct LLC — see Chapters 36–38)
5. Grantor was an estate or trust whose representative lacked current authority

### Chain of Title Defects

1. Missing links — a deed that should be in the chain but isn't recorded
2. Break in the chain — property appears to have been conveyed away and back, with gaps in the middle
3. Conflicting conveyances — the same grantor appears to have conveyed to two different parties
4. Out-of-order recordings that create ambiguity about priority
5. **Wild deeds** — recorded conveyances from grantors who never had record title

### Authority Defects

1. Deed executed during entity forfeiture (the entity couldn't legally transact)
2. Deed executed by an attorney-in-fact under a revoked or expired power of attorney
3. Deed executed by a trustee in violation of the trust's terms
4. Deed executed by a personal representative without court authorization where required
5. Deed executed by one co-owner purporting to convey another co-owner's interest

### Fraud-Related Defects

1. **Forged deeds** (always void — not curable)
2. Deeds obtained by fraud or duress (voidable by the defrauded party)
3. Deeds executed to defraud creditors (subject to fraudulent-transfer claims)
4. Deeds recorded without the grantor's knowledge or consent

### Recording Defects

1. Deed never recorded (leaves a gap in the public chain even if conveyance was valid between parties)
2. Deed recorded in wrong county
3. Deed recorded with errors that affect indexing
4. Prior-recorded liens or encumbrances not released when they should have been

## 8.16 How Title Insurance Sees Defects — The Insurability Spectrum

From a title insurance underwriter's perspective, every defect falls somewhere on a spectrum from "insurable as-is" to "uninsurable under any circumstances." Understanding where a particular defect falls on the spectrum tells you whether the defect is a minor inconvenience or a deal-killer.

### Category 1: Insurable As-Is

Minor defects that underwriters will insure around without requiring any action. Examples:

1. Old unreleased liens that are clearly satisfied (the mortgage company went out of business 40 years ago; the loan was obviously paid off)
2. Ancient defects where the statute of limitations has clearly run
3. Scrivener's errors that are so obviously typographical that no reasonable person would be confused
4. Minor variations in names across deeds that are clearly the same person

### Category 2: Insurable With Endorsement

Defects that underwriters will insure around by adding specific endorsements to the policy — typically at additional premium cost. Examples:

1. Survey discrepancies that can be addressed by a survey endorsement
2. Access concerns that can be addressed by an access endorsement
3. Specific covenant violations that can be addressed by restrictions endorsements
4. Mineral rights questions that can be addressed by specific coverage endorsements

### Category 3: Insurable After Cure

Defects that must be actively cured before the underwriter will insure. Most significant defects fall in this category. Examples:

1. Missing or additional heirs — must be located and acquired from, or bound by judicial determination
2. Defective prior deeds — must be corrected, ratified, or quieted
3. Unreleased mortgages or liens — must be released or paid off
4. Missing spousal signatures on homestead — must be obtained or quieted
5. Deeds from parties who had lost authority — must be cured through corrective action
6. Breaks in the chain — must be resolved through quiet title or curative filings

### Category 4: Uninsurable Absent Extraordinary Circumstances

Defects so severe that no reasonable cure exists short of extensive litigation, and many title companies simply won't insure regardless of cure attempts. Examples:

1. Forged deeds in the chain (these are void, not voidable)
2. Active ownership disputes with credible competing claims
3. Federal tax liens that cannot be cleared
4. Pending litigation directly affecting title
5. Fraudulent conveyances not yet adjudicated

When a defect is in Category 4, the deal is usually impossible unless the defect can be moved to Category 3 through specific litigation. Chapter 35 covers the options for moving defects between categories through various cure strategies.

## 8.17 Recognizing Defects in a Title Commitment

Title commitments communicate the existence of defects through their structure. Learning to read a commitment for defects is a core operational skill.

### Where Defects Show Up

**In Schedule B (Exceptions).** If the title company is unwilling to insure around a defect, they list it as an exception. This tells you the defect exists and the insurer won't cover it — you'd have to bear the risk or cure the defect to get it covered.

**In Schedule C (Requirements).** If the title company is willing to insure around a defect after you take specific action, they list the required action here. Every Schedule C item is a defect that needs cure before closing.

**In the chain of title itself.** Sometimes defects aren't called out explicitly in the commitment but are visible when you review the underlying documents. An operator who only reads the commitment without reviewing the underlying title work will miss defects that a careful reviewer would catch.

### The Title Commitment Review Checklist

When you receive a title commitment on a fractured-equity deal, walk through these questions:

1. What defects are listed as Schedule B exceptions? Can I live with them, or do I need them addressed?
2. What Schedule C requirements exist? What will each require in time, money, and effort to satisfy?
3. Are there any defects I can see in the underlying chain of title that the commitment doesn't explicitly address? (Title companies sometimes miss things.)
4. Are there any defects that the commitment treats lightly but that seem significant to me?
5. Is the title company's overall picture of the deal consistent with my understanding? If not, why?

A careful commitment review often identifies issues that the deal structure can address before they become closing problems.

## 8.18 The Cure Toolkit — A Foundational Preview

This section provides a high-level preview of the cures available for common defect types. Chapter 35 walks through each cure in detail, including when to use which one, how to execute them, and how they coordinate with other chapters in this Codex.

### For Signature and Acknowledgment Defects

1. **Correction deed** — when original parties are available and willing
2. **Ratification deed** — after-the-fact confirmation of the original intent
3. **Scrivener's affidavit** — for specific notarial errors
4. **Quiet title action** — when original parties can't be reached (Chapter 33)

### For Legal Description Defects

1. Correction deed for scrivener's errors
2. Judicial reformation for substantive mistakes
3. Survey-based resolution
4. Boundary-line agreement among affected owners

### For Capacity Defects

1. Deeds from deceased grantors — cured through the deceased grantor's estate (Chapter 18) or heirship determination (Chapter 33)
2. Deeds from entities that lost authority — cured through entity reinstatement (Chapters 36–38)
3. Deeds from minors — cured through ratification after majority or judicial reformation

### For Chain of Title Defects

1. Missing link — track down the missing deed and record it, or establish the missing conveyance through heirship determination or quiet title
2. Wild deeds — quiet title to establish the true chain
3. Competing conveyances — priority resolution under the recording act, or quiet title

### For Authority Defects

1. Representative authority — obtain corrective documentation from the actual authority holder, or re-execute with proper authority
2. Forfeited entity deeds — reinstate the entity and re-execute (Chapter 37)
3. Trustee authority — resolve through successor trustee procedures (Chapter 39)

### For Recording Defects

1. Unrecorded deeds — obtain certified copies and record
2. Wrong-county recordings — re-record in the correct county
3. Stale unreleased liens — obtain releases or quiet title

### Statute of Limitations Cures

Some defects cure themselves over time. Texas has several statutes of limitations that can ripen title through the passage of time alone — the 3-year, 5-year, 10-year, and 25-year statutes, each with specific requirements. When a defect has existed long enough, the statute of limitations can provide a cure that requires no affirmative action beyond documenting that the time has passed. Chapter 35 walks through each of these.

### When Walking Away Is the Cure

Some defects can't be economically cured. A title issue that would require five years of litigation to resolve on a $50,000 property isn't worth pursuing. Recognizing the uneconomic-to-cure defect is part of good deal evaluation. Chapter 35 addresses the walk-away calculus.

## 8.20 Key Takeaways for Operators

Before moving to Chapter 9, internalize these principles:

**Title insurance is a private industry, not a legal doctrine.** Underwriters make business decisions based on their own standards, which may be stricter than the law requires.

**Every deal has to pass two filters:** legal validity and title company willingness to insure. You need both.

**The Schedule C is your primary adversary.** Schedule C requirements are what must be done before closing. Understanding what typical Schedule C items look like lets you anticipate and plan.

**Title-Insurance-As-Shield:** The moment you have a policy covering a defect, the defect is functionally cured for resale purposes. The next buyer's title company can accept your policy.

**Underwriter rules diverge from legal rules.** Know where. The four-year AOH rule, outside-children documentation standards, defective deed curative standards — these are all areas where underwriters are stricter than the law.

**Relationships matter.** Develop deep partnerships with 1–3 title companies who understand your business. This is a durable competitive advantage.

**Premium is regulated but total costs vary.** The base premium is set by the Texas Department of Insurance. Additional costs for search, examination, escrow, and endorsements vary significantly.

**Documentation defeats underwriter skepticism.** Save your research. Document your due diligence. Paper trails satisfy title companies in ways that oral explanations don't.

**Deed defects fall into recognizable categories.** Signature, acknowledgment, legal description, capacity, chain of title, authority, fraud-related, and recording defects. Recognition is the first step to cure.

**Defects fall on an insurability spectrum.** Category 1: insurable as-is. Category 2: insurable with endorsement. Category 3: insurable after cure. Category 4: uninsurable absent extraordinary circumstances. Your deal's economics depend on where each identified defect falls.

**Every cure has a chapter.** The cures for specific defects are woven throughout this Codex. Chapter 35 synthesizes them into the master curative toolkit — the Codex's capstone.

**Know when to walk away.** Not every deal will be insurable. If you can't find a title company willing to insure a property at any reasonable cost, the deal can't be resold — and if it can't be resold, it's not a deal.
