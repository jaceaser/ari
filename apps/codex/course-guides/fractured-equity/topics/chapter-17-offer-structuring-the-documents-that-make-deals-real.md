## The Gap Between Verbal Yes and Signed Deed

Here's a hard truth about this business: heirs say yes all the time. They say yes on the first call, yes in follow-up conversations, yes when they've had a few days to think about it. And then, when paperwork arrives, something happens. They freeze. They delay. They consult relatives. They change their mind. A significant percentage of verbal yeses never convert to signed deeds.

The offer structuring you use between the verbal yes and the signed deed determines your conversion rate. Bad structure loses 30–50% of verbal commitments. Good structure loses 10–20%. That's the difference between closing 60% of engaged heirs and closing 85% of them.

This chapter teaches you the specific documents, contract language, and deal structures that convert verbal yeses into signed deeds. You'll learn what the Release of Interest actually says, how earnest money works in fractured equity deals, when to use option periods, and how to structure payments in ways that protect you while giving heirs confidence.

Some of this is legal technical material — the specific language that has to appear in enforceable contracts. Some of it is psychological — the structure that makes heirs comfortable moving forward. Both matter.

---

## The Core Documents — An Overview

Before we go deep on any single document, here's the full set of documents that typically appear in a fractured equity acquisition.

**Before Signing**

1. Written Offer Letter
2. Disclosure of Identified Properties (for package deals)
3. Heir Information Sheet

**At Signing**

1. Purchase and Sale Agreement (PSA)
2. Earnest Money Receipt
3. Release of Interest (Deed Without Warranty, Special Warranty Deed, or Quitclaim)
4. Affidavit of Heirship (sometimes — when heir serves as attestant)
5. Memorandum of Contract (optional protective filing)

**At Closing**

1. Deed of Conveyance (recorded)
2. Settlement Statement
3. Heir's Receipt and Release
4. Title Insurance Policy

**Ongoing**

1. Indemnification Agreement (sometimes)
2. Cooperation Agreement

Each document serves a specific purpose. The sections below cover the key ones in detail.

## The Written Offer Letter

Sent within 2–3 days of the first successful call. This is your formal, written statement of what you're proposing. It's not legally binding on either party, but it establishes the foundation for negotiation.

### Core Content of the Offer Letter

**Opening paragraph:** Date, heir's name, reference to the phone call, identification of the property and the decedent.

**Statement of the situation:** "I understand that [decedent] passed away in [year] and owned [property]. Based on my research and conversations with you and other family members, I believe there are approximately [N] heirs with an inherited interest in the property."

**Your Offer**

1. Specific amount: "I am offering you the sum of $[X] for your inherited interest in the property"
2. Payment structure: breakdown of earnest money, signing payment, closing payment
3. Timeline: "The closing would take place within [timeframe] after signing our agreement"
4. Contingencies: title clearance, heir agreement from others, etc.

**What You Will Do**

1. Responsible for all legal fees, title insurance, recording fees, back taxes, and other transaction costs
2. Handle all paperwork and coordinate with title company

**What the Heir Will Do**

1. Sign the attached documents
2. Cooperate with reasonable requests for signatures or information
3. Not negotiate with any other buyer during pendency

**Next Steps**

1. "If this offer is acceptable, please sign and return the attached agreement by [specific date]"
2. "If you have questions or want to discuss changes, please call me at [number]"
3. "I will follow up with you in [timeframe] if I haven't heard back"

**Closing:** Your signature, contact information. Enclose the actual draft PSA if appropriate.

**Tone:** Professional but warm. Clear and specific about numbers. Not pressuring. Leaves room for questions.

**Format:** 1–2 pages maximum. Clean formatting. Your business letterhead. Actual signature, not just a typed name.

## The Purchase and Sale Agreement (PSA)

The PSA is the main contract. Everything else flows from this. In fractured equity deals, the PSA has specific elements that standard real estate contracts don't have.

### Key Provisions Every PSA Must Contain

**1. Identification of the parties.** The heir (with full legal name, marital status, and residence) and you or your entity (with matching specificity).

**2. Identification of the property and the interest being sold.** Full legal description. Specific identification of the interest: "all of Seller's right, title, and interest in and to the above-described real property, including all interest Seller has acquired or will acquire through inheritance from [Decedent]."

**3. Purchase price.** Total amount. Payment structure (earnest money, deed payment, closing payment). Timing of each payment.

**4. Seller's Representations**

- Seller is an heir of the decedent
- Seller has not previously conveyed their interest
- Seller is not subject to any judgments or encumbrances on their interest
- Seller has full capacity to sign

**5. Buyer's Obligations**

- Pay per agreed schedule
- Handle title insurance and closing costs
- Coordinate closing

**6. Closing Conditions**

- Title commitment acceptable
- All required heirship documents obtained
- Sufficient other heirs signed
- No intervening liens
- Deadline for closing (typically 90–180 days from signing)

**7. Remedies.** What happens if either party fails to perform. Typically: earnest money returned if buyer defaults; earnest money forfeited and additional damages possible if seller defaults.

**8. Cooperation Clauses**

- Seller agrees to sign additional documents as needed
- Seller agrees to cooperate with title company and attorneys
- Seller agrees to provide family information as needed

**9. Confidentiality (Optional).** Agreement not to disclose terms to other heirs or outside parties until you authorize.

**10. Entire Agreement and Amendments.** Standard language that this is the complete agreement. Amendments must be in writing.

### Length and Complexity

A well-drafted fractured equity PSA runs 8–15 pages. Your attorney should draft and customize this for each deal. Don't use generic online contracts.

### Attorney Review

Even if you have a template, your attorney should review each PSA for the specific deal's facts. Cost: $300–$800 per PSA. Worth every penny.

## Earnest Money in Fractured Equity Deals

Earnest money — a good-faith deposit paid at signing — is a standard real estate concept. In fractured equity, it works differently than in typical residential transactions.

### Amount

| Transaction Type | Typical Earnest Money |
|---|---|
| Typical residential transaction | 1–3% of purchase price ($2,000–$10,000) |
| Typical fractured equity — individual heir | $500–$2,000 |

The smaller amount reflects:

- Heirs' limited incentive to breach
- Your need to conserve capital across many heir payouts
- The protective purpose (earnest money just needs to be meaningful, not crushing)

### Structure

Earnest money in fractured equity is typically:

- Paid at the moment of PSA signing
- Held by your title company or attorney in escrow (not by you directly)
- Applied toward the purchase price at closing
- Forfeited to you if the heir breaches

### Purpose

**Commitment indicator:** An heir who's unwilling to accept $500 in earnest money isn't really committed.

**Liquidated damages proxy:** Provides you some recovery if heir walks away.

**Skin in the game:** Gives heir reason to follow through.

### Where It Goes

- **NEVER** pay earnest money directly to the heir and expect to get it back if they breach
- **ALWAYS** use an escrow agent (title company, attorney, or licensed escrow company)
- The escrow agreement specifies conditions for release

### Heir's Perspective

Heirs receiving earnest money feel the deal is real. They have something tangible in hand. They're more likely to show up at closing.

### Your Protection

If the heir breaches (refuses to close, sells to someone else, dies before closing), the earnest money:

- Can be retained by you (if the PSA so specifies)
- Provides base damages without requiring complex litigation
- Demonstrates the heir's commitment in any future dispute

## The Release of Interest — The Core Document

This is the document that actually transfers the heir's interest. In Texas, this can take several forms:

1. **Deed Without Warranty** — Transfers whatever interest the heir has, with no warranties
2. **Special Warranty Deed** — Transfers with warranties against the heir's own acts
3. **Quitclaim Deed** — Similar to deed without warranty, common in some transactions
4. **Assignment of Heirship Interest** — A specific form used when the estate hasn't been formally probated

### What the Release of Interest Must Contain

**1. Identification of the grantor (heir).** Full legal name, marital status, current address, "as an heir of [Decedent, full name], who died on [date]."

**2. Identification of the grantee (you or your entity).** Full legal name or entity name, state of formation (if entity), address.

**3. Consideration.** The amount being paid. Often stated as "$10 and other good and valuable consideration" with the actual amount documented separately.

**4. Specific description of the interest.** "All of Grantor's right, title, and interest in and to the real property described below, including all interest inherited or inheritable from [Decedent]." Note that this transfers the heir's entire interest, not just a specific fractional share — the heir may not know their exact fractional share, and you want all of it regardless.

**5. Legal description of the property.** Full legal description, not just an address. Include the county and recording reference if relevant.

**6. Warranty language (or lack thereof).** In Texas, specific language determines whether this is a general warranty deed, special warranty deed, deed without warranty, or quitclaim deed. Choose the type based on what the heir is able and willing to warrant.

**7. Spousal signature (if applicable).** If the heir is married, Texas homestead law may require the spouse's signature even if the spouse has no inheritance interest. When in doubt, get spousal signature.

**8. Notary acknowledgment.** Heir (and spouse if applicable) must sign before a notary. Notary acknowledgment block meets Texas requirements. Heir must have valid government-issued photo ID.

**9. Recording readiness.** Document formatted to meet county recording requirements. Margins, paper size, legibility all compliant.

## Timing — When Does What Get Signed?

The sequence of signatures matters. Here's the typical structure:

### Phase 1: PSA Signing (Day 0)

The heir signs:

- The Purchase and Sale Agreement
- An Earnest Money Receipt

You pay:

- Small earnest money deposit ($500–$2,000) into escrow

What you do **NOT** do at this stage:

- Pay the main purchase price
- Record any deed
- Commit to the full transaction

This is an agreement to proceed, not a closed transaction.

### Phase 2: Deed Signing (Day 30–60, Typically)

Once other heirs are signed or committed, and title cleanup is progressing, you schedule the deed signing.

The heir signs:

- The Release of Interest (Deed)

You pay:

- The main payment (typically 70–80% of total purchase price)

What happens:

- The deed is placed in escrow with your title company
- The deed is **NOT** immediately recorded (depending on strategy)
- Title company holds the deed pending completion of Schedule C requirements

### Phase 3: Closing (Day 90–180, Typically)

The property interest fully transfers, and final payments are made.

At closing:

- Deed is recorded in the county records
- Final payment is made to the heir
- Title insurance policy is issued
- Heir signs any remaining documents

### Variations

For simple deals with cooperative heirs, Phase 2 and Phase 3 can be combined — the heir signs the deed and gets paid in full at the same time, with the deed recorded immediately. This works when title is clean and heir research is complete.

For complex deals with multiple heirs, title issues, or ongoing heirship proceedings, the three-phase structure gives everyone time to address issues without risk.

## The Option Period Structure (Alternative Approach)

Some operators prefer to use an option period structure instead of the straight PSA structure.

### How Option Periods Work

Instead of a PSA that commits both parties subject to contingencies, you and the heir sign:

1. An **Option Agreement** that gives you the right (not obligation) to purchase the interest
2. You pay an **Option Fee** (typically $500–$2,000) for this right
3. The option has a specific exercise period (typically 90–180 days)
4. If you exercise, the option fee applies to the purchase price
5. If you don't exercise, the option fee is retained by the heir

### Advantages

- Gives you more flexibility — you can walk away if the deal doesn't work
- Simpler legal structure than a PSA with contingencies
- Heir gets something (option fee) even if deal doesn't close

### Disadvantages

- Heir may prefer a firmer commitment
- May look less serious than a PSA
- Less protection for you if heir tries to sell to another buyer

### When to Use Option Agreements

- When heirs are nervous about commitment
- When you're uncertain about title or other heirs
- When the deal has significant contingencies

### When to Use PSAs

- When you're committed to moving forward
- When heirs are ready to sign
- When most contingencies are already resolved

Most operators use PSAs as their default. Option agreements are a tool for specific situations.

## The Memorandum of Contract — A Protective Filing

In Texas, you can record a Memorandum of Contract at the county recorder's office. This is a short document (1–2 pages) that puts the world on notice that you have a contract on the property.

### What It Contains

- Your name and the heir's name
- Property legal description
- Date of contract
- General statement that a contract exists

### What It Does NOT Contain

- Price terms
- Payment details
- Full contract language

### Why Record a Memorandum

- Prevents the heir from selling to another buyer (the other buyer would find the Memorandum in a title search)
- Creates notice to competing investors in the county
- Deters tax foreclosure proceedings in some cases
- Reinforces the legitimacy of your contract

### Why NOT to Record a Memorandum

- Creates a cloud on title that you must remove if the deal doesn't close
- Heir may feel the Memorandum is too aggressive
- Can create adverse impressions if title company sees it

### When Operators Typically Use Memoranda

- For deals with long closing timelines
- For deals in competitive markets where other investors are circling
- For deals with higher-value properties

### How to Remove a Memorandum If the Deal Falls Apart

- File a Release of Memorandum with the county
- Sometimes requires quit-claim from the heir to fully clear
- Can delay the heir's next attempted sale if not properly released

Use Memoranda thoughtfully. They're a tool for specific situations, not a default.

## Payment Structures — The Art of Staging

How you stage payments affects both your risk and the heir's engagement.

### Structure 1: 50/50 Split

- 50% at PSA signing + deed execution
- 50% at title clearance / closing

**Advantages:** Strong heir commitment, balanced cash flow

**Disadvantages:** Large upfront payment before title is clear

### Structure 2: Small Earnest, Large Mid, Small Final

- $500–$2,000 at PSA signing (earnest money)
- 70–80% at deed signing (typically 30–60 days after PSA)
- 15–25% at final closing

**Advantages:** Best risk management, keeps heir engaged throughout

**Disadvantages:** Three separate payment events to coordinate

### Structure 3: Full Payment at Deed Signing

- Small earnest money at PSA signing
- Remainder at deed signing

**Advantages:** Simpler, heir gets paid in full

**Disadvantages:** Less leverage for cooperation after deed signing

### Structure 4: Closing-Contingent

- Small earnest money at PSA signing
- Full remainder at final closing (after title cleanup complete)

**Advantages:** You pay nothing substantial until title is clear

**Disadvantages:** Heirs often won't accept this structure

### Which to Use When

| Situation | Recommended Structure |
|---|---|
| Simple, straightforward deals | Structure 3 (full payment at deed) |
| Complex deals with long timelines | Structure 2 (three-phase) |
| Deals with significant title risk | Structure 4 (if heir will accept) |
| Standard cooperative heir deals | Structure 2 or 3 |

## Contingency Clauses — What Can Kill the Deal

Every PSA has contingencies that let you walk away under specific circumstances. The key contingencies in fractured equity:

### Title Contingency

- You can terminate if title cannot be cleared to an insurable standard
- Typically expires 60–90 days after PSA signing
- Most common reason for contract termination

### Other Heirs Contingency

- You can terminate if you can't acquire sufficient other heirs' interests
- Typically requires 75% or higher acquisition to close
- Protects you from partial acquisitions that can't produce a clean sale

### Financing Contingency (for Leverage-Dependent Deals)

- You can terminate if you can't obtain financing on acceptable terms
- Typically not used when paying cash

### Inspection Contingency

- You can terminate if property condition is significantly worse than disclosed
- Typically a short window (30–45 days)
- Not always included in fractured equity PSAs

### Attorney Review Contingency

- You can terminate if your attorney identifies legal problems during initial review
- Typically 15–30 days
- Standard in commercial transactions

### Heir-Side Contingencies (That You Want to Limit)

- Heirs typically don't have contingencies of their own
- Some might try to insert: seller's attorney approval, financial contingencies, appraisal contingencies
- Your attorney should review any heir-added contingencies carefully

## Non-Negotiables — Terms You Never Compromise On

Some contract terms you never compromise on, regardless of how much the heir pushes:

**1. Documented signature before a notary.** Every Release of Interest / Deed must be signed in front of a notary. No exceptions. Mobile notaries can travel to the heir if needed.

**2. Real identification.** The signing heir must present valid government-issued photo ID. Expired IDs, photocopies, and unofficial IDs are not acceptable.

**3. Spousal signature when homestead is involved.** If the property is a Texas homestead, the heir's spouse must sign regardless of their personal interest. This is a legal requirement, not a preference.

**4. Clear chain of title.** You will not close on a property where title cannot be cleared to an insurable standard.

**5. Written documentation of all family representations.** Heirs' statements about family structure must be documented in writing (affidavits or testimonial recitations in the deed).

**6. Recording of the deed.** Eventually, the deed will be recorded. If an heir asks you to never record the deed, something is wrong.

**7. No side agreements.** All terms between you and the heir are in the written agreement. No "we'll handle that separately" or "let's keep that between us" items.

## Common Mistakes in Offer Structuring

New operators make predictable mistakes in offer structuring. Watch for these:

### Mistake 1: Using Generic Real Estate Contracts

Standard residential real estate contracts don't account for fractured equity specifics. You need contracts drafted for this business.

**Mitigation:** Have your attorney draft custom PSA templates. Use them consistently.

### Mistake 2: Paying Heirs Outside of Escrow

Paying directly — especially large amounts — creates risk. Use escrow for all substantial payments.

**Mitigation:** All earnest money and purchase payments flow through a title company or attorney escrow.

### Mistake 3: Relying on Verbal Promises for Anything Important

"The heir said she'd sign for $15K" doesn't mean anything when it's time to close.

**Mitigation:** All material terms in writing. Always.

### Mistake 4: Skipping the Spousal Signature

For homestead properties, skipping the spouse's signature creates title problems that may not surface until you try to resell.

**Mitigation:** When in doubt, get spousal signature. Cost: one extra notary appointment. Benefit: clean title.

### Mistake 5: Using Option Agreements When You Should Use PSAs

Option agreements give heirs the impression you might walk away. For deals you're committed to, use PSAs.

**Mitigation:** Default to PSA structure. Use option agreements only when truly uncertain.

### Mistake 6: Recording the Deed Too Early

Recording the deed before you have all the pieces you need (other heirs, title cleanup, financing) commits you publicly without closing the deal.

**Mitigation:** Hold deeds in escrow with your title company. Record only when all pieces are in place.

### Mistake 7: Failing to Include Cooperation Clauses

After heirs have been paid, they're not motivated to help with later paperwork. Build cooperation into the original agreement.

**Mitigation:** Always include cooperation clauses requiring continued heir cooperation through final title clearance.

## Documents You Provide to the Heir

When an heir signs, they need copies of everything for their own records.

### Provide at Signing

1. Signed copy of the PSA
2. Copy of the earnest money receipt
3. Copy of the Release of Interest / Deed (after notarization)
4. Your business card and contact information
5. Reference list (other families you've worked with, if provided)
6. Attorney's contact information
7. Title company's contact information

### Provide at Closing

1. Final settlement statement
2. Evidence of final payment
3. Confirmation that the deed has been recorded
4. Final copy of the recorded deed

### Why

- Heirs deserve complete documentation of the transaction
- Protects you from later disputes about what was agreed
- Builds trust and reputation for future referrals
- Standard business practice

## Working With Attorneys on Structure

You are not a lawyer. Your attorney should be involved in offer structure decisions for material deals.

### Attorney's Role

- Draft PSA templates you use consistently
- Review each specific deal's structure
- Advise on state-specific issues (particularly Texas homestead)
- Handle document signings when needed
- Provide strategic advice on complex situations

### When to Involve Attorney

- Every new deal type or fact pattern
- Deals above $200K equity spread
- Deals with any complications (missing heirs, contested estates, title defects)
- Any time an heir has their own attorney
- Any time an heir threatens litigation

### Cost

| Item | Cost |
|---|---|
| Template creation | $2,000–$5,000 one-time |
| Per-deal review | $300–$800 |
| Full legal assistance for complex deals | $3,000–$10,000+ |

### Expected Legal Costs per Deal

| Deal Type | Expected Legal Cost |
|---|---|
| Simple acquisitions | $800–$2,000 |
| Complex acquisitions | $3,000–$8,000+ |

Budget accordingly in your underwriting.

## The Follow-Through After Signing

After the heir signs, the real work begins. Here's what happens in the days after signing:

### Day 1 (Signing Day)

- Escrow the earnest money
- Send copy of signed documents to heir
- Update your CRM with signing status
- Begin coordination with title company

### Days 2–7

- Open title order with title company
- Begin heirship document process (AOH filing, JDH petition, etc.)
- Contact other heirs with news (if permitted)
- Update attorney on deal status

### Weeks 2–4

- Monitor title company progress
- Address any Schedule C issues that arise
- Continue other-heir acquisitions
- Check in with signed heir to maintain relationship

### Weeks 4–8

- Schedule deed signing when title is ready
- Coordinate with heir on timing
- Prepare all closing documents
- Confirm final payment logistics

### Weeks 8–12

- Close the deal
- Record the deed
- Provide final documentation to heir
- Update title insurance policy
- Move on to the next heir

The sign-then-disappear pattern kills deals. Stay in touch with signed heirs throughout the process. A signed agreement is the start, not the end.

## Key Takeaways for Operators

**The gap between verbal yes and signed deed is where deals die.** Offer structure determines conversion.

**Written offer letters come first.** Send within 2–3 days of the first call.

**The PSA is the main contract.** Custom-drafted for fractured equity, not generic residential.

**Earnest money is smaller than typical real estate deals.** $500–$2,000 is typical.

**The Release of Interest transfers the actual interest.** Deed without warranty, special warranty deed, or quitclaim depending on situation.

**Stage payments strategically.** Earnest at signing, most at deed signing, balance at closing. This keeps heirs engaged and protects your capital.

**Option agreements are a tool, not a default.** Use for specific situations where commitment is uncertain.

**Memoranda of Contract protect against competing buyers.** Use thoughtfully, not always.

**Attorney involvement is required.** Not optional. Budget for it.

**Non-negotiables are non-negotiables.** Notary, ID, spousal signature when applicable, clear title, written agreement, recording the deed.

**Don't disappear after signing.** Stay in touch through closing.

**Document everything.** Signed copies to heirs. Copies in your files. Digital backups.

When an estate has a legally-appointed administrator or executor, or when one can be appointed, the entire acquisition changes.
