## 7.1 Why This Chapter Exists

You've learned who owns what under Chapters 5 and 6. Now you need to learn how ownership is actually documented, conveyed, and protected. The distinction matters — understanding who the legal owners are doesn't help you if you can't also read a deed, follow a chain of title, recognize a life estate, or tell a warranty deed from a quitclaim deed.

This chapter covers the mechanical layer of real estate: the documents, the recording system, the forms of ownership, the protections that shield property from creditors, and the specific rules that govern how any of it can be transferred. By the end, you should be able to pick up a property's title history and read it like a story — each deed a chapter, each defect a plot twist, each gap a question mark you know how to resolve.

This is foundational material for operators. Every later chapter in this Codex assumes you understand deeds, recording, and the basics of how title moves from one party to another.

## 7.2 Types of Deeds and What Each Actually Does

A deed is the document that transfers ownership of real estate from one person or entity (the **grantor**) to another (the **grantee**). Not all deeds are equal. The type of deed determines what the grantor is promising, what warranties the grantee receives, and what protections exist if problems surface later.

### General Warranty Deed

The strongest form of deed. The grantor warrants that:

1. They have good title
2. The title is free from encumbrances except those specifically mentioned
3. The grantor will defend the title against all claims, including claims arising before the grantor acquired the property

General warranty deeds are used in most arm's-length retail transactions. When someone sells a house to a buyer with bank financing, a general warranty deed is almost always the form used. The buyer gets the maximum possible protection from the seller.

### Special Warranty Deed

A mid-level deed. The grantor warrants against defects that arose during their period of ownership, but not against defects that predated their ownership. If a problem surfaces from a deed signed in 1962, the grantor of a 2018 special warranty deed is not liable for it.

Special warranty deeds are commonly used in commercial transactions, foreclosure sales, estate sales, and some investor-to-investor transactions. Title insurance becomes more important because the grantor's warranties are limited.

### Quitclaim Deed

The weakest form of deed. The grantor conveys whatever interest they have, if any, without warranting that they have any interest at all. If it turns out the grantor had nothing to convey, the grantee has no recourse against the grantor under the deed.

Quitclaim deeds are used when the grantor's interest is uncertain or limited, or when the conveyance is a cleanup (correcting a defect, releasing a possible interest, clarifying ownership). Quitclaim deeds are common in divorce property divisions, heir-to-heir transfers, and title curative work.

### When Operators Use Each Type

When you're acquiring from heirs, the type of deed you accept depends on what's available and what serves your title strategy. A cooperative surviving spouse executing a full conveyance will usually provide a general warranty deed. An heir signing over a fractional interest as part of a larger consolidation may sign a special warranty deed or a quitclaim. When you're resolving a defective conveyance from decades ago, you'll often take quitclaim deeds from the people who might have signed the original — their quitclaim releases whatever interest they might have without warranting the property itself.

When you're selling to an end buyer, you'll typically provide a general warranty deed because that's what retail buyers and their lenders expect. Your title insurance underwriter is the one who actually stands behind the warranties — you're transferring title, but the insurance company is taking the risk.

### Deed Without Warranty

A variant sometimes used in Texas. The grantor conveys with the language "grant, sell, and convey" but without warranty language. It's a conveyance without warranties but stronger than a pure quitclaim. In practice, operators rarely use this form; general warranty, special warranty, and quitclaim cover almost every situation.

### Administrator's Deed and Executor's Deed

These are deeds executed by personal representatives of estates. An **administrator's deed** is signed by an administrator (appointed to an intestate estate); an **executor's deed** is signed by an executor (named in a will). These deeds convey the estate's interest in the property — the warranties are limited to the administrator's or executor's authority to act, not to the underlying title.

Chapter 9 covers administrator and executor authority in detail. For now, understand that when you see an administrator's deed or executor's deed in a chain of title, you're looking at a conveyance from an estate, not from an individual. The title effect depends on whether the administration was properly authorized.

## 7.3 The Elements of a Valid Deed

For a deed to be legally valid, it must contain specific elements. Missing any of these can make the deed defective or void.

### 1. Identification of the Grantor

The person or entity conveying the property. Must be identified with enough specificity to know who is actually signing. "John Smith" alone may be insufficient in a jurisdiction with multiple John Smiths — typically the grantor's full legal name, marital status, and sometimes residence should appear.

### 2. Identification of the Grantee

The person or entity receiving the property. Same specificity requirements. Common errors include misspelled names, wrong entities (LLC name that doesn't match the state's records), or grantees who don't legally exist (an LLC that was never properly formed).

### 3. Words of Conveyance

Specific language indicating the grantor's intent to transfer. Phrases like "does grant, sell, and convey" or "does hereby grant, bargain, sell, and convey" are standard. Without words of conveyance, the document isn't a deed — it's just a recitation of facts.

### 4. Legal Description of the Property

A precise description of the property being conveyed. Can be by metes and bounds (technical surveyor's description), lot and block (subdivision reference with a filed plat), or abstract number (for unsurveyed tracts). A street address alone is **NOT** a sufficient legal description — it's for mail delivery, not property identification. Every deed must have a legal description that distinguishes the property from every other property in the world.

### 5. Consideration

Some statement of consideration (payment or value given for the transfer). "For $10 and other valuable consideration" is standard boilerplate — the actual purchase price doesn't need to appear. For gift deeds, "for love and affection" is acceptable. A deed without any consideration recited may be challenged as invalid.

### 6. Signature of the Grantor

The grantor's actual signature. Not a stamp, not a printed name, not a scanned image in most jurisdictions — a handwritten signature. In Texas, electronic signatures are valid under the Uniform Electronic Transactions Act for most purposes, but recorded deeds still often use wet-ink signatures.

### 7. Acknowledgment Before a Notary

In Texas (and most states), deeds must be acknowledged before a notary public to be recorded. The notary verifies the grantor's identity and witnesses the signature. A deed without proper acknowledgment can still be valid between the grantor and grantee but cannot be recorded, which means it doesn't provide constructive notice to third parties.

### 8. Delivery to the Grantee

The deed must be delivered to the grantee (or their agent) during the grantor's lifetime. Delivery requires both physical transfer and intent to pass title. A deed signed but kept in the grantor's drawer and found after death typically hasn't been delivered and may not convey title.

### 9. Recording (for Priority, Not Validity)

Recording isn't technically required for the deed to be valid between the parties, but it's required to protect against subsequent purchasers. Section 7.5 covers recording.

### What Happens When Elements Are Missing

| Missing Element | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Wrong legal description | Deed may not convey the intended property |
| Missing signature | No conveyance occurred |
| Improper acknowledgment | Can't record, but may still be valid between parties |
| No delivery | No conveyance — title never actually passed |
| Missing consideration recitation | Vulnerable to challenge, particularly by creditors |

When you find historical deeds in a chain of title, check each of these elements. Defects in any of them can create the kinds of title problems that turn clean-looking properties into complicated ones — and complicated properties are where your business lives.

## 7.4 Legal Descriptions — How Properties Are Identified

The legal description is the part of the deed that tells you exactly what real estate is being conveyed. Operators who can't read legal descriptions get the wrong properties, miss overlapping claims, and sign contracts on parcels they didn't realize they were committing to.

Three main types of legal descriptions:

### Metes and Bounds

A description that uses bearings (compass directions) and distances to trace the boundary of the property from a starting point back to the starting point. Used primarily for rural properties, unsubdivided tracts, and older urban parcels.

A metes and bounds description reads like a walking tour: *"Beginning at a point at the intersection of County Road 411 and the east boundary line of the Wilson Survey; thence North 45 degrees 32 minutes East along said boundary line 847.2 feet to an iron rod; thence South 88 degrees 14 minutes East 540.9 feet to an iron rod at a fence corner; thence..."* and so on, until the description returns to the starting point.

Reading metes and bounds descriptions requires practice. The critical elements to check:

1. Does the description close (end where it started)?
2. Are the bearings and distances internally consistent?
3. Does it reference survey monuments or physical markers?
4. Does the acreage stated at the end match what the bearings and distances would produce?

Errors in metes and bounds descriptions are common in older deeds. Sometimes courses don't close. Sometimes a bearing is written backward. Sometimes distances conflict with adjacent property descriptions. Any of these can create boundary disputes or title defects that require curative work.

### Lot and Block

A description that references a recorded plat of subdivision. The plat (a map filed in the county records) divides a larger tract into numbered lots and blocks. A lot-and-block description reads: *"Lot 7, Block 3, Crestwood Addition, an addition to the City of San Antonio, according to the plat thereof recorded in Volume 1248, Page 376, of the Plat Records of Bexar County, Texas."*

This is the most common description type for residential properties in urban and suburban areas. Much simpler to read than metes and bounds. To understand the actual property boundaries, you pull the referenced plat from the county records.

Problems that arise with lot-and-block descriptions:

1. Wrong lot number (typo in the deed)
2. Plat was amended after the deed was recorded
3. Lot was replatted (divided or combined) and the old description no longer matches
4. Boundary disputes between adjacent lots due to fence lines not matching plat

### Abstract Number

A description used for unsurveyed tracts, typically from original Republic of Texas land grants. Reads like: *"Being 42.37 acres, more or less, in the James Morris Survey, Abstract No. 462, Kerr County, Texas."* Often combined with metes and bounds for precise boundary description.

These descriptions exist primarily for rural and agricultural properties in Texas. They reference original land grant surveys that were never resurveyed into standard subdivisions.

### Supplemental References

Good legal descriptions often include multiple reference systems for clarity:

1. Primary description (metes and bounds OR lot and block)
2. Address (for convenience, not for legal identification)
3. Tax ID or parcel number (for cross-reference with tax rolls)
4. Acreage or square footage
5. Previous deed reference (chain of title continuity)

When you're underwriting a deal, make sure the property's current tax rolls, the legal description in the most recent deed, and any surveys you obtain all describe the same parcel. Mismatches are red flags.

## 7.5 Recording Statutes — Why Everything Gets Filed

Texas, like every other state, has a recording system for real estate documents. Every county has a county clerk's office that maintains the official real property records. Deeds, mortgages, liens, easements, plats, and other real estate documents are recorded there.

### Why Recording Matters

Recording provides **constructive notice** to the world of the document's existence. Once a deed is properly recorded, every subsequent party interested in the property is deemed to know about it, whether they actually looked or not.

This is critical for three reasons:

**1. Priority.** When multiple claims exist on the same property, the one recorded first (assuming it was a bona fide transaction) generally takes priority. The first-to-record deed wins over a later deed from the same grantor.

**2. Chain of title.** Title examiners and title insurers rely on recorded documents to trace ownership. An unrecorded deed breaks the chain; subsequent purchasers can treat the property as if the unrecorded transfer never happened.

**3. Notice to creditors and other interested parties.** Recorded documents become part of the public record and are discoverable by lenders, judgment creditors, tax authorities, and anyone else with potential interest in the property.

### Texas Is a "Notice" Jurisdiction

Texas uses a notice-based recording system. An unrecorded deed is still valid between the parties, but it's not effective against a subsequent **bona fide purchaser (BFP)** who had no actual notice of the prior unrecorded deed. The BFP's recorded deed beats the earlier unrecorded one.

### Practical Implications for Operators

**Always record your deeds.** The moment you acquire an interest, record it. Unrecorded interests can be lost if someone else records a competing interest.

**Pull full chains.** When you research a property, pull every recorded instrument affecting it — deeds, liens, easements, judgments. Each document may affect your analysis.

**Watch for gaps.** A chain that jumps from Smith in 1975 directly to Jones in 1990 with nothing in between is a red flag. Either a deed is missing (defective chain) or something wasn't recorded.

**Check recent recordings.** Before closing any deal, check for recordings in the past 30–60 days. A competing investor may have filed a memorandum of contract or a deed that changes the landscape.

### Recording Mechanics in Texas

1. Documents must meet formatting requirements (1-inch margins, specific paper size, legible text)
2. Recording fee is per page, typically $10–$30 for a standard deed
3. The county clerk assigns a recording number, date, and time
4. Recorded documents become permanent public records accessible by anyone
5. Electronic recording is available in most Texas counties through third-party services

## 7.6 Forms of Ownership — How Property Can Be Held

Property can be held in multiple forms, and the form affects how it's transferred, inherited, and divided. The major forms:

### Fee Simple Absolute

The highest form of ownership. A fee simple owner has the full bundle of property rights with no time limit and no conditions. They can sell it, mortgage it, give it away, lease it, use it, or ignore it. At their death, it passes under their will or by intestate succession. Fee simple is what most residential real estate owners hold.

### Fee Simple Determinable

A fee simple ownership that ends automatically if a specified event occurs. For example: *"To my daughter so long as the property is used as a residence."* If the daughter ever uses the property for a business, the ownership automatically terminates and the property reverts to the grantor or the grantor's heirs. Rare in modern deeds but occasionally found in old grants.

### Fee Simple Subject to Condition Subsequent

Similar to determinable but requires action by the grantor to terminate. *"To my son, but if alcohol is ever served on the property, I may reenter and retake possession."* If alcohol is served, the grantor has the right (but not the automatic entitlement) to take back the property. Even rarer in modern deeds.

### Life Estate

An ownership interest measured by someone's lifetime. The **life tenant** has the right to use the property during their life; when they die, the property passes to the **remainderman** (or reverts to the grantor if no remainderman was specified).

Life estates arise from:

1. Express creation in a deed or will ("to my wife for life, then to my children")
2. Operation of law (surviving spouse's 1/3 life estate in separate property under Rule C from Chapter 5)
3. Homestead rights of surviving spouse
4. Enhanced life estate deeds (Lady Bird deeds, discussed below)

Life estates create complicated ownership structures. The life tenant has possessory rights but cannot sell the underlying fee interest. The remainderman has a future interest but cannot take possession until the life tenant's death. Together, they hold all rights to the property, but neither can act alone.

### Remainder Interest

A future interest that takes possession after a life estate terminates. Can be:

**Vested remainder:** Held by identifiable living persons who will definitely take possession when the life estate ends.

**Contingent remainder:** Subject to conditions that must be met before the remainder vests (e.g., "to my grandchildren who reach age 25").

Remainder interests are themselves property interests that can be sold, mortgaged, or inherited. When the life tenant is alive, the remainder interest is worth some fraction of the full fee value depending on the life tenant's life expectancy.

### Reversionary Interest

The interest retained by a grantor who conveys less than fee simple. If a grantor deeds "to my son for life," the grantor retains a **reversionary interest** — the right to take back the property when the son dies, unless the son has descendants who take under some default rule.

### Tenancy in Common

The default form of co-ownership when property is held by multiple owners. Each tenant in common holds an undivided fractional interest (which may be unequal), has the right to possess the whole property, and can sell, mortgage, or devise their fractional interest independently. When a tenant in common dies, their fractional interest passes under their will or by intestate succession — it does **NOT** automatically pass to the other co-owners.

Nearly every fractured property you'll work with is held in tenancy in common, usually because intestate succession created multiple owners over time.

### Joint Tenancy with Right of Survivorship

A form of co-ownership where the surviving owner automatically takes the deceased owner's share. Common in married couples' residential property and sometimes in parent-child ownership. Texas requires specific language in the deed ("joint tenants with right of survivorship") to create a joint tenancy; the default presumption is tenancy in common.

When a joint tenant dies, their interest disappears and the surviving joint tenants' interests expand. No probate is required for the transfer — survivorship happens by operation of law.

### Tenancy by the Entirety

A form of joint tenancy historically reserved for married couples, with added creditor protections. **Not recognized in Texas.** Common in some other states.

### Community Property with Right of Survivorship

A Texas-specific form created by statute. Married couples can hold community property with a survivorship provision that transfers the decedent's interest to the surviving spouse automatically at death, bypassing probate for the property. Requires specific language in the conveyance.

## 7.7 Life Estates in Detail — Including Lady Bird Deeds

Life estates deserve special attention because they're common in fractured equity deals and because they have specific legal consequences.

### Classic Life Estate

Created when a grantor conveys property with a reservation for life, or grants property to one person for life with a remainder to another. The life tenant has use and possession rights. They must pay taxes, maintain the property, and cannot waste it (cause unnecessary damage or reduction in value). They cannot sell the fee interest — only their life estate, which extinguishes at their death.

### Enhanced Life Estate Deed (Lady Bird Deed)

A special form of life estate deed used primarily in Texas and Florida. The grantor reserves a life estate but retains expanded powers during life:

1. Sell the property (terminating the remainder)
2. Mortgage the property
3. Cancel the deed entirely
4. Lease the property
5. Use the property without restriction

At the grantor's death, if the grantor has not exercised any of the reserved powers, the property passes automatically to the named remaindermen without probate.

Lady Bird deeds are used in Medicaid planning (the property doesn't count as transferred during life for look-back purposes but passes at death outside probate) and in simple estate planning where the owner wants to avoid probate but retain full lifetime control.

### How Life Estates Affect Deal Analysis

When you encounter a property with a life estate:

**1. Identify the life tenant.** Who holds the life estate? What's their age and health status? Their life expectancy affects the value of the remainder.

**2. Identify the remaindermen.** Who holds the remainder? Are they identified in a recorded instrument? Have any of them died, creating sub-interests in their own heirs?

**3. Evaluate the payment obligation.** Is the life tenant paying taxes and maintaining the property? If not, the remaindermen may have a cause of action for waste.

**4. Consider pathway options:**
- Acquire from both sides. Purchase the life estate from the life tenant AND the remainder from the remaindermen. Combined, you own the full fee.
- Wait for life estate to terminate.
- Use waste doctrine.
- Quiet title in complex cases.

### Valuing Life Estates and Remainders

The IRS and most courts use actuarial tables to value life estates and remainders based on the life tenant's age. A life tenant aged 85 holds a much smaller fractional value than a life tenant aged 55. The remainder held by remaindermen increases as the life tenant ages.

When negotiating with life tenants and remaindermen separately, these valuations give you a framework for fair offers. A life tenant of advanced age may accept a modest payment for a quick release because their life estate's actuarial value is low. A remainderman whose interest is years away from maturing may accept a discount for immediate cash.

## 7.8 Homestead Protections in Texas

Texas has the strongest homestead protections in the country. Understanding them is essential for any operator working Texas deals.

### What Homestead Is

Homestead is a legal status that attaches to certain property and provides the property with special protections. In Texas, the key protections are:

**Protection from creditors.** Most general unsecured creditors cannot force the sale of homestead to satisfy judgments. Exceptions include:
- Purchase money (the original mortgage)
- Mechanic's and materialmen's liens (if properly executed with spousal consent for homestead work)
- Taxes (ad valorem property tax and federal tax liens)
- Home equity loans (under strict Article XVI § 50 rules)
- Refinancing of homestead-allowed debts

**Spousal signature requirement for conveyance.** Both spouses must sign any deed, lien, mortgage, or release of homestead. A conveyance signed by only one spouse is void as to the homestead.

**Automatic on family use.** Homestead status attaches automatically when property is used as a family home — no special declaration required (though Texas does have a homestead exemption declaration for tax purposes).

**Survivor rights.** Surviving spouse has homestead occupancy rights for life (or until voluntary departure), regardless of who owns the underlying property. Surviving minor children have similar protections.

### Size Limits

| Type | Limit |
|---|---|
| Urban homestead | Up to 10 acres (can be multiple non-adjacent parcels) used as primary residence |
| Rural homestead — families | Up to 200 acres |
| Rural homestead — single adults | Up to 100 acres |

### When Homestead Status Matters in Your Deals

**Conveyance validity.** If you're acquiring from a married person, both spouses must sign the homestead conveyance. Missing spousal signature = defective deed = title problem.

**Surviving spouse occupancy.** If a surviving spouse is living in what was the family homestead, you cannot evict them to clean up the property, even if you own 100% of the underlying fee. They have homestead rights separate from ownership.

**Tax foreclosure exposure.** Homestead doesn't protect against tax foreclosure. A homestead property can be sold at tax auction if taxes are delinquent, just like any other property. This is a common fracture scenario.

**Lien priority.** Most liens are ineffective against homestead. When you're evaluating liens on a property, check whether the property is homestead — that changes which liens you can enforce.

### Non-Homestead Property

Not all property held by individuals is homestead. Investment properties, vacation homes, rental properties, and vacant land are not homestead. These have standard creditor exposure and don't require dual spousal signatures.

## 7.9 Chain of Title — How Properties Are Tracked Through Time

The **chain of title** is the sequence of recorded instruments that connect current ownership back through all prior owners to the original grant from the sovereign (the state, or in Texas, back to original Republic of Texas land grants).

### What a Clean Chain Looks Like

A property has a clean chain when you can trace every conveyance from the current owner back through each prior owner, with each deed properly signed, acknowledged, and recorded. Each link connects to the next — Smith conveyed to Jones, Jones conveyed to Williams, Williams conveyed to Thompson, and Thompson holds today.

### What a Broken Chain Looks Like

A chain breaks when a link is missing, defective, or inconsistent with surrounding links. Common breaks:

**Missing deed.** Smith owned in 1975, Jones owns in 1990, but no deed from Smith to Jones is recorded. Something happened, but the record doesn't show it.

**Deed from someone without apparent title.** Williams conveyed to Thompson in 1995, but no deed from the prior owner to Williams exists. Where did Williams get title to convey?

**Deed from a deceased person.** The recorded deed shows a signature dated 1997, but the grantor died in 1993. The deed is void.

**Deed without proper acknowledgment.** A deed was recorded but the notary block was improperly executed. The recording may be voidable.

**Deed from a dissolved entity.** An LLC that dissolved in 1995 appears to have conveyed in 2003. The entity couldn't validly convey.

**Conflicting deeds.** Smith conveyed to Jones in 1980; Smith also conveyed the same property to Wilson in 1985. Both recorded. Which controls?

### How to Research a Chain

1. Start at the current tax roll. Find the current owner's name as reflected on the county appraisal district.
2. Pull the deed that conveyed to the current owner. The grantor's name on that deed is the previous owner.
3. Work backward. Pull the deed to each previous owner, then the deed to that person, and so on. Aim to trace 40–60 years or to the original source grant, whichever is shorter.
4. Note every instrument in chain. Include mortgages, releases, easements, liens, judgments, and any other recorded documents affecting the property.
5. Identify gaps. Any place where the chain doesn't connect cleanly is a potential title defect.
6. Identify defects. Deeds that don't meet the requirements in Section 7.3 are defects even if they appear to connect.

### Resources for Chain Research

**County clerk's office.** Primary source for recorded documents. Can be searched in person or online in most Texas counties.

**Title plant databases.** Commercial title companies maintain computerized databases that index recorded instruments. Access typically requires a title company relationship.

**Online title tools.** Services like TitlePro, DataTree, PropertyRadar, and others provide title research at various price points.

**Courthouse research services.** For rural counties with poor online access, local researchers will pull documents for a fee.

For professional research, work with your title company. Once you have relationships, they'll run title commitments on properties you're evaluating for a modest fee, and the commitment shows everything affecting the property.

## 7.10 Homestead Declaration vs. Homestead Status

A common source of confusion for operators: the difference between filing a homestead declaration and actually having homestead status.

**Homestead status** attaches automatically when property is used as a family home. No filing required. It's a factual status based on use.

**Homestead declaration** (technically called a Designation of Homestead in Texas) is an optional document that can be recorded to put the world on notice of homestead status. Not required but useful in some situations, particularly for multi-parcel homesteads where the specific boundaries of the homestead need to be clarified.

**Homestead exemption** (for property tax purposes) is a separate concept — a tax exemption that reduces the taxable value of a homestead property. Requires filing with the county appraisal district.

These three things are related but not identical. Property can have homestead status without either a recorded declaration or a claimed tax exemption.

For operators: when analyzing a property's homestead status, look at actual use rather than filings. Is the property used as a family residence? If yes, homestead protections apply regardless of whether anything is filed.

## 7.11 Mineral, Water, and Air Rights

Real property in Texas includes mineral rights, water rights, and (theoretically) air rights, which can be severed and owned separately from surface rights.

### Mineral Rights

In Texas, mineral rights (oil, gas, coal, other underground minerals) can be severed from surface ownership and conveyed separately. When you acquire real estate in Texas, you may or may not acquire the mineral rights. Old deeds often include language like "subject to reservation of all minerals" that means the mineral rights are held by someone else.

This matters in:

1. Rural properties where oil and gas activity is common
2. Older properties where historical severances may exist
3. Properties in active mineral-producing regions (West Texas, Eagle Ford, Permian Basin)

For most residential deals, mineral rights are immaterial — there's no active production and no retail market for the rights. For rural acquisitions, especially in oil-producing counties, mineral rights can add significant value or be entirely absent from the transaction.

### Water Rights

Texas has complex water law, distinguishing between:

1. Surface water (owned by the state, subject to permits)
2. Groundwater (generally owned by the surface owner under the rule of capture, subject to conservation district regulations)
3. Riparian rights (rights of landowners adjacent to watercourses)

For most residential and small-tract investor deals, water rights are part of the standard conveyance. For rural and agricultural deals, water rights may be separately analyzed.

### Air Rights

Rarely severed in practice outside of major commercial urban development. Not relevant for most operators.

## 7.12 Adverse Possession — Brief Overview

Adverse possession allows a person who has used a property for a specified period to claim legal title through a quiet title action. Chapter 31 covers this doctrine in detail; this is a brief overview because it's part of real property law basics.

### Texas Has Multiple Adverse Possession Variants

| Statute | Period | Requirements |
|---|---|---|
| Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code § 16.024 | 3 years | Peaceable and adverse possession with **color of title** |
| § 16.025 | 5 years | Peaceable and adverse possession with recorded deed, payment of taxes, and enclosure or cultivation |
| § 16.026 | 10 years | Peaceable and adverse possession, no color of title required, 160-acre cap |
| § 16.027 | 25 years | Peaceable and adverse possession — the strongest variant |

Common requirements across all variants:

1. Actual possession (physical use)
2. Open and notorious (visible to anyone who looked)
3. Peaceable (not obtained by violence or force)
4. Adverse (without permission of true owner)
5. Continuous for the full statutory period
6. Exclusive (not shared with true owner)

### Why This Matters for Real Property Basics

Adverse possession is part of how title can be cleaned or clouded in ways that aren't reflected in the recorded chain. A long-term occupant with a potential adverse possession claim may be able to cure title defects through a quiet title action — or may be able to challenge the recorded owner's title.

When you encounter a property with a long-term occupant who doesn't hold recorded title, analyze whether adverse possession applies. The occupant may have rights you can acquire, or may be the party with actual title despite the recorded chain.

## 7.13 Key Takeaways for Operators

Before moving to Chapter 8, internalize these principles:

**Deed types matter.** General warranty, special warranty, and quitclaim each carry different warranties. Know what you're accepting and what you're providing in any transaction.

**Deed elements are specific.** Grantor, grantee, words of conveyance, legal description, consideration, signature, acknowledgment, and delivery all must be present for a valid, recordable deed.

**Legal descriptions vary.** Metes and bounds, lot and block, and abstract number descriptions have different mechanics. A street address alone is not a legal description.

**Recording is critical.** Texas is a notice state. Record your deeds immediately. Pull full chains when researching properties. Check for recent filings before closing.

**Forms of ownership have consequences.** Fee simple, life estate, tenancy in common, joint tenancy with right of survivorship — each has different rules for transfer, inheritance, and division.

**Life estates are common in your deals.** They arise from deeds, wills, and operation of law (especially under Rule C from Chapter 5). Lady Bird deeds are a particular Texas variant.

**Homestead protections are powerful.** Both spousal signatures required for conveyance. Surviving spouse has occupancy rights. Most creditors can't force sale. But tax authorities can — and do.

**Chain of title is a story.** Learn to read it. Gaps and defects are either problems to avoid or opportunities to resolve, depending on your strategy.

**Mineral rights sometimes matter.** For rural deals, verify mineral ownership. For urban residential, it's usually not a factor.
