## 5.1 Why This Chapter Is the Foundation of Everything

Intestate succession is the single most important body of law in this business.

More than half the deals you'll work in your career will involve at least one intestate death somewhere in the chain. Many will involve three or four. Every time you open a new file, before you make a single phone call, you're doing intestate succession analysis in your head — Who died? Who were they married to? Did they have children? Who got what share?

The operators who master this material execute at a level most investors never reach. They look at a property with five generations of undocumented deaths, pull the appropriate statutes in their head, and calculate current ownership in twenty minutes. The operators who don't master this material spend weeks confused about who actually owns what, make bad offers to the wrong heirs, and miss critical heirs who later surface and unwind deals.

This chapter is going to be long. Read it all the way through. Read it twice if you need to. The material is dense but it's not difficult — it's just specific. When you've internalized it, every later chapter in this Codex gets easier. When you haven't, every later chapter in this Codex is a guessing game.

The primary framework here is Texas law. Texas is where most of the deals this Codex addresses are located. Where other states differ significantly, it's called out. A state-by-state appendix for the eight or nine other states that matter most is in the back of the Codex.

## 5.2 Intestate vs. Testate — The Base Distinction

When a person dies, they die either testate (with a valid will) or intestate (without one).

**Testate** means the decedent left a valid, legally-executed will that directs how their property should be distributed. The will is admitted to probate. The court follows the will's instructions. Property passes to whoever the will names, in the shares the will specifies.

**Intestate** means the decedent died without a valid will, or with a will that was never admitted to probate, or with a will that a court rejected. In any of those cases, the property passes under the intestate succession statutes of the state where the property is located.

A critical point that catches new operators every time: a will that was never admitted to probate has no legal effect. Not "limited effect." Not "effect after four years." **Zero legal effect on real property.** If Grandma died in 2003 with a will in her drawer that left the house to her oldest son, and nobody ever filed the will for probate, then for purposes of real estate law, Grandma died intestate. Her property passes under intestate succession as if the will never existed.

Under **Texas Estates Code § 256.003**, a will typically must be admitted to probate within four years of the decedent's death. After four years, admission becomes significantly harder and in many cases impossible. The applicant must show they were "not in default" in failing to probate earlier, which is a high bar.

This four-year window is a recurring feature of deals you'll encounter. Every time you see a will from ten or fifteen years ago that was "the family's understanding" but never got filed, you're looking at a de facto intestate situation. The informal family agreement did nothing. The legal owners are whoever intestate succession says they are.

### Why Intestate Matters More Than Testate for This Business

You'll occasionally work testate deals — probated wills with clear instructions. Those deals are usually easier because a single heir or a small number of heirs hold clear title. Cooperation or non-cooperation is the main variable.

Intestate deals are harder. More heirs. More scattered. More confused about their own ownership. But they're also where the margins are. The complexity that makes intestate deals hard is exactly what keeps competition out and profits high. Master intestate succession and you master the core of this business.

## 5.3 The Texas Intestate Hierarchy — The Complete Order

Texas Estates Code § 201.001 and §§ 201.002–201.003 establish a hierarchy of heirs. The law works through the hierarchy in order. Each level inherits only if no one at a higher level exists.

Here's the complete order, simplified for operators:

**1. Surviving Spouse and Descendants (Children, Grandchildren, etc.)**

If the decedent left a surviving spouse, descendants, or both, the property is divided between them according to specific rules that depend on whether property is community or separate, and whether there are outside children. This is where most deals live. The specific rules are in Section 5.4.

**2. Parents (If No Spouse or Descendants)**

If the decedent left no surviving spouse and no descendants, but one or both parents are alive, the parents inherit.

**3. Siblings and Descendants of Predeceased Siblings (If No Parents)**

If the decedent left no spouse, no descendants, and no surviving parents, the siblings inherit. If a sibling predeceased the decedent, that sibling's share passes to their children (the decedent's nieces and nephews) by representation.

**4. Maternal and Paternal Kindred (If No Siblings)**

If there are no surviving spouse, descendants, parents, siblings, or descendants of siblings, the estate splits 50/50 between the maternal and paternal lines. Half goes to the maternal grandparents or their descendants (uncles, aunts, cousins on the mother's side), and half goes to the paternal grandparents or their descendants on the father's side. This is the "lost family" scenario — often 15–25 distant cousins as heirs.

**5. Escheat to the State (If No Kindred)**

If the decedent has no identifiable heirs at any level, the property escheats to the state. This is rare but it happens. For your purposes, if you ever think a property might be heading to escheat, there's almost always a distant cousin somewhere you haven't found yet. Escheat is the last resort, not a first option.

The hierarchy is strict. You don't skip levels. If a decedent had children, the parents don't inherit, even if the children are estranged or absent — the children still inherit. The law operates mechanically through the hierarchy without regard to whether the named heir deserves, wants, or needs the inheritance.

## 5.4 The Spouse-and-Descendants Rules — Where Half of All Deals Live

The specific rules for how property passes when a decedent leaves a surviving spouse, descendants, or both are the most frequently applied rules in this business. The distinctions matter.

The rules depend on three variables:

1. Whether the property is community or separate property
2. Whether the property is real property or personal property
3. Whether the decedent's surviving descendants are all also the surviving spouse's descendants, or whether any of them are outside children

Community property is treated in detail in Chapter 6. For this chapter, the rules are stated at a practical level.

**Rule A: No Outside Children — Community Property — Real Property**

If the decedent left a surviving spouse and all surviving descendants are also the surviving spouse's descendants, the surviving spouse takes 100% of the decedent's community share of real property.

In plain terms: if everyone's children together, the surviving spouse gets everything on the community side.

**Rule B: Outside Children Present — Community Property — Real Property**

If the decedent left a surviving spouse and any surviving descendant is NOT also the surviving spouse's descendant, the decedent's community share of real property passes to all the decedent's children equally — NOT to the surviving spouse.

In plain terms: if even one outside child exists, the rule flips. The spouse keeps only their own community half. The decedent's community half goes to all the decedent's children — inside and outside — in equal shares.

**Rule C: Separate Real Property with Surviving Spouse and Descendants**

If the decedent left separate real property and a surviving spouse plus descendants, the surviving spouse takes a **1/3 life estate** in the separate real property, and the descendants take the underlying fee interest.

Read that carefully. The spouse doesn't get 1/3 ownership. She gets a **1/3 life estate** — the right to use one-third of the property during her lifetime. When she dies, her life estate extinguishes and the descendants' full fee interest matures.

This is where a lot of life estates you'll encounter in practice come from. The law creates them automatically when a spouse dies with separate property and leaves descendants. The family often doesn't realize the life estate exists — they assume either the spouse got everything or the kids got everything. In fact, the kids got the underlying ownership while the spouse got the use.

**Rule D: Separate Real Property with Surviving Spouse and No Descendants**

If the decedent left separate real property and a surviving spouse, but no descendants (and no surviving parents, siblings, or descendants of siblings), the surviving spouse takes 100% of the separate real property.

**Rule E: Separate Real Property with Surviving Spouse and Parents/Siblings but No Descendants**

If the decedent left separate real property, a surviving spouse, and either surviving parents, siblings, or descendants of siblings, the surviving spouse takes 1/2 of the separate real property and the decedent's parents/siblings/nieces-and-nephews take the other 1/2.

This rule surprises most people. A childless decedent's separate real property does **NOT** pass entirely to their spouse. Half goes up and out to the decedent's original family. Important implication: when you encounter a childless decedent who owned separate real property, always identify the decedent's parents, siblings, and descendants of siblings — they're likely legal heirs.

**Rule F: No Surviving Spouse — All to Descendants**

If the decedent had no surviving spouse but had descendants, the descendants take all the property equally at the first generation. If a child predeceased the decedent, that child's share passes to their own descendants by representation (see Section 5.5).

## 5.5 Per Capita vs. Per Stirpes — How Shares Pass When Heirs Predecease

When an heir predeceases the decedent, their share doesn't disappear — it passes to their descendants. But the math of how it passes matters, and there are two systems that produce different results: **per capita** and **per stirpes** (also called "by representation").

Texas uses **per capita with representation**, which is a hybrid system codified in Texas Estates Code § 201.101.

### Scenario: Decedent Has Three Children, Two Alive, One Predeceased with Four Grandchildren

**Under pure per stirpes:** Each branch takes an equal share at the first generation. Living Child A: 1/3. Living Child B: 1/3. Deceased Child C's four children split Child C's 1/3 equally: each grandchild gets 1/12.

**Under per capita at each generation:** All living heirs at the first generation where there are any living heirs take equal shares. The shares of deceased members at that generation are combined and distributed equally among the next generation. Living Child A: 1/3. Living Child B: 1/3. Deceased Child C's 1/3 is divided equally among her four children. Each grandchild of Child C: 1/12.

In this simple example, both systems produce the same result. The difference shows up in more complex scenarios.

### Scenario: All Three Children Predeceased with Varying Numbers of Grandchildren

Child A left one child. Child B left three children. Child C left two children.

**Under pure per stirpes:** Each deceased child's share stays in their branch. Child A's one child takes 1/3. Child B's three children split 1/3 equally — each gets 1/9. Child C's two children split 1/3 equally — each gets 1/6.

**Under Texas per capita with representation:** Because no living heirs exist at the first generation (children), shares are redistributed at the next generation (grandchildren) where there are living heirs. All six grandchildren take equal shares: each gets 1/6.

This is a critical distinction. In the Texas system, shares are equalized among the living heirs at the first generation where any living heirs exist — even if that means collapsing shares across branches.

### The Practical Implication for Operators

When you're calculating shares in a multi-generational fact pattern, always identify the generation where living heirs first appear. If that's the first generation (living children of the decedent), you use pure representation — each branch keeps its share. If it's the second generation or later (no living children, only grandchildren and beyond), you equalize across all heirs at that generation.

This matters because it affects how much you're willing to pay for any particular heir's interest, and because it determines which heirs hold meaningful interests worth pursuing.

Double-check your math on every deal. Keep your attorney in the loop when the fact pattern is complex. A share miscalculation that puts an extra $10,000 in an heir's hand that wasn't supposed to be there will show up in your closing numbers and you won't catch it until you're already out the money.

## 5.6 Outside Children — The Rule That Flips Everything

This is treated separately because it's the single most commonly missed fact-pattern detail in this business.

### The Texas Rule (Estates Code § 201.003)

When the decedent dies intestate, and the decedent left a surviving spouse and any surviving descendant who is NOT also the surviving spouse's descendant, the decedent's community share of real property passes entirely to all the decedent's children — not to the surviving spouse.

### What "Outside Child" Means

An **outside child** is any child of the decedent who is not also a biological or adopted child of the surviving spouse. The most common scenarios:

- **A child from a prior marriage.** Decedent was married before and had children in that marriage.
- **A child from a prior relationship.** Decedent had a child before marriage with someone they never married.
- **A child from an affair or outside relationship during the marriage.** Decedent had a child with someone other than their spouse during the marriage.
- **A child adopted by one spouse only.** Rare, but possible.

Any of these flips the Rule A default.

### How the Rule Applies in Practice

Under normal Rule A (no outside children), the surviving spouse takes 100% of the community share. With outside children, the spouse keeps her own community half (the law never takes that from her), but the decedent's community half — which under Rule A would have passed to the spouse — now passes to all the decedent's children equally. Including the outside children. On equal footing with the inside children.

### Worked Example

Decedent dies intestate. Married for 22 years to current spouse. Three children with current spouse. One child from a relationship 30 years ago that nobody in the current family knows about. The property was community property, $300,000 value.

**Without outside children (Rule A):** Surviving spouse: $300,000 (100%). Three inside children: $0 each.

**With outside children (Rule B):** Surviving spouse keeps her own community half: $150,000. Decedent's community half goes to all four children equally: $37,500 each. Total each inside child: $37,500 (12.5%). Outside child: $37,500 (12.5%). Surviving spouse ends up with $150,000 (50%).

One outside child reduces the spouse's share from 100% to 50% and gives the outside child 12.5% of the property — equal to what each inside child receives.

### Why This Rule Matters So Much

Families with outside children often don't know they have outside children. The decedent kept the prior relationship secret. The outside child may be an adult now, living elsewhere, with no connection to the current family. When the current family tries to sell the property, they assume the surviving spouse owns everything or that only the inside children need to sign. They're wrong. The outside child holds a legal interest that nobody has accounted for.

### Your Job as an Operator

On every deal where the decedent left a surviving spouse AND children, always ask whether the decedent had any children from a prior relationship. If the answer is yes or "I'm not sure," research is required:

1. Pull the decedent's marriage records going back decades
2. Search birth records in states where the decedent lived
3. Check obituaries of the decedent and close relatives for references to children
4. Run the decedent's name through genealogical databases
5. Ask extended family members (aunts, uncles, cousins) who might remember a prior relationship

When an outside child is identified, your deal structure changes significantly. You may need to acquire the outside child's interest separately. You may need to inform the current family of the outside child's existence (with appropriate care). You may need to adjust your AOH to reflect the correct heir structure.

Missing outside children is one of the most common ways deals unwind after closing. An outside heir surfaces a year or two later with a legitimate claim to a share of the proceeds. The title insurance may or may not cover it depending on the specifics. Your reputation as an operator who delivers clean title takes a hit. The profit on that deal gets eaten by litigation costs and settlement payments.

Research carefully. Ask the questions. Don't assume.

## 5.7 Adopted Children — Full Equivalence to Biological Children

Under **Texas Estates Code § 201.054** and similar laws in every other state, legally adopted children inherit as biological children for all purposes of intestate succession. There is no difference in the amount they inherit, the order they inherit, or the rules that apply to them.

### What "Legally Adopted" Means

Formal adoption completed through a court proceeding that resulted in a final adoption decree. The adoption is recorded in the vital records system. An amended birth certificate lists the adoptive parent(s) as the legal parent(s).

### Three Scenarios Worth Knowing

**Scenario 1: Child adopted by two adoptive parents.** The child inherits from both adoptive parents as if biologically born to them. The biological parents are no longer legal parents for inheritance purposes. The adoption severed the legal relationship with the biological parents under the "adoption-out" rule.

**Scenario 2: Child adopted by stepparent while biological parent remains.** If a child is adopted by a stepparent with the consent of one biological parent, the child inherits from both the biological parent and the stepparent. The relationship with the biological parent whose rights were not terminated is preserved.

**Scenario 3: Child who was adopted OUT by biological family.** A child who was given up for adoption no longer inherits from their biological parents by default — the adoption severed that relationship. However, some state statutes preserve the biological inheritance relationship in specific circumstances (adoption by stepparent, for instance). Check the specific state's rules on adoption-out.

### What Does NOT Qualify as Adoption for Intestate Purposes

- **Informal adoption.** Raising a child as your own without legal adoption. The child does not inherit from you under normal intestate rules unless equitable adoption applies (see Section 5.9).
- **Step-parent relationship without adoption.** A stepchild is not an heir of the stepparent unless the stepparent formally adopted them.
- **Guardianship.** A legal guardian is not a parent for inheritance purposes.
- **"Raising as your own."** Decedent told everyone the child was her own. No adoption papers. Not legally an heir.

### Why This Rule Matters for Operators

When you're building a family tree, you need to distinguish between legal children, step-children, informally-raised children, and adopted children. Legal children and legally adopted children are heirs. Step-children and informally-raised children are not — unless adopted.

This leads to one of the most dangerous patterns in this business: the claim that someone who was "raised as one of the family" should be treated as an heir. Informal family relationships, no matter how genuine, don't create inheritance rights. Only legal adoption does.

## 5.8 Half-Siblings and the Half-Blood Rule

When a decedent dies without descendants, spouse, or parents, and the estate passes to siblings, the question arises: do half-siblings inherit equally with full siblings?

### The Texas Rule (Estates Code § 201.057)

Half-siblings inherit, but their share is adjusted. A half-sibling takes half as much as a full sibling. Put differently: each full sibling counts as one share; each half-sibling counts as half a share. The estate is divided according to the total share count.

### Worked Example

Decedent dies intestate with no spouse, no descendants, no parents. Surviving siblings: two full brothers, one half-sister (same mother, different father).

**Share calculation:** Two full brothers: 2 shares (1 each). One half-sister: 0.5 shares. Total shares: 2.5. Each full brother: 1/2.5 = **40% of estate**. Half-sister: 0.5/2.5 = **20% of estate**.

### Exception — When All Siblings Are Half-Blood

If all the decedent's siblings are half-siblings, the half-blood rule doesn't apply — they all share equally. The rule only reduces half-sibling shares when there's a mix of full-blood and half-blood siblings.

### Practical Implications

Half-sibling situations are common in blended families. When the decedent had siblings from multiple marriages of their parents, you'll need to track the genealogy carefully and apply the half-blood calculation.

Not every state uses the Texas half-blood rule. Some states treat half-siblings identically to full siblings. Some states have no half-blood reduction at all. Check the specific state's statute when working out-of-state deals.

## 5.9 Equitable Adoption — The Narrow Doctrine

### What Equitable Adoption Is

In limited circumstances, a court can recognize an "equitable adoption" — treating a child as if legally adopted for inheritance purposes even though no formal adoption ever occurred. Texas recognizes the doctrine narrowly, primarily under the case **Heien v. Crabtree**.

### The Elements Typically Required

1. An agreement to adopt between the biological parent(s) and the would-be adoptive parent(s)
2. The child was placed with the adoptive parents in reliance on that agreement
3. The child performed the filial duties of a child (lived with them, called them parents, etc.)
4. The adoption was never legally completed for some reason

### Why This Matters for Operators

Equitable adoption is a real legal doctrine, but it's narrow, factually rigorous, and requires a court to rule on it. A family's informal belief that someone was "raised as their child" is not equitable adoption. Equitable adoption requires specific fact patterns, specific evidence, and typically a declaratory judgment from a probate court establishing the relationship.

### The Dangerous Pattern

Some investors, when they encounter an heir who claims to have been "raised by" a decedent without formal adoption, treat that claim as if it established heirship. They put the claim in an AOH, have witnesses sign, and proceed with acquisition as if the person was a legal heir. This is not equitable adoption done correctly. It's an unresolved factual claim being treated as established fact.

When the claim is genuine — a child who actually was raised from infancy, called the decedent "mom" or "dad" their whole life, was treated identically to biological children by the family, was named in the decedent's life records — a declaratory judgment proceeding in probate court can establish equitable adoption. Once the court has ruled, the relationship is legally recognized for inheritance purposes.

When the claim is convenient — someone who showed up after the decedent's death asserting an informal relationship that nobody else in the family particularly remembers — trying to use it as a basis for AOH representation is legally fraught and potentially fraudulent.

Equitable adoption exists, it's narrow, it requires a court proceeding to establish properly, and informal AOH representation of equitable adoption without court proceedings is a dangerous practice.

## 5.10 Posthumous Children — Born After the Decedent's Death

### The Rule (Estates Code § 201.056)

A child of the decedent who is born alive after the decedent's death inherits as if born during the decedent's lifetime, provided the child was conceived before the decedent's death and is born within a specified period (typically 10 months in Texas).

### Practical Application

Rare but important. A wife who is pregnant when her husband dies — the child she later bears is an heir of the husband, identical in inheritance rights to any older children. Assisted reproduction technology has created more complex variants (frozen embryos, posthumous conception), and the laws in this area are still developing.

For most deals you'll encounter, posthumous children are obvious — the child was born shortly after the decedent's death, the family knows about the pregnancy, no surprise. If you encounter a more complex scenario involving assisted reproduction, consult your attorney.

## 5.11 Non-Marital Children — Inheritance from Father

### The Historical Issue

Traditionally, non-marital children (children born outside marriage) had limited inheritance rights from their biological fathers under intestate succession. This reflected older legal assumptions that favored marital legitimacy.

### The Modern Texas Rule (Estates Code § 201.051)

A non-marital child inherits from the biological father under specific circumstances:

1. If paternity is established legally during the father's lifetime — through an acknowledgment of paternity, a court order, or similar formal establishment, the child is a legal heir.
2. If paternity can be proven by clear and convincing evidence after the father's death — through DNA testing, acknowledged behavior during the father's lifetime (held out as his child, supported financially, etc.), or other compelling evidence.
3. The child inherits from the mother without additional proof — maternity is established by birth records.

### The Operator's Diagnostic

When you encounter a potential heir who claims to be the non-marital child of a decedent, ask:

1. Was paternity formally acknowledged during the father's lifetime?
2. Is there evidence the father treated the child as his own (school records, financial support, introduction to family)?
3. Is there DNA evidence or living relatives willing to provide DNA for comparison?
4. Did the child have a relationship with the father?

If the evidence is strong, the claim may be valid and the child is a legal heir. If the evidence is weak or nonexistent, the claim may not hold up. Work with your attorney to evaluate the specific facts.

### Disinheritance by Operation of Law — Parental Rights Terminated

**Texas Estates Code § 201.052** addresses a related situation. If a parent's parental rights were terminated (through a court proceeding terminating parental rights before the parent's death), the child does not inherit from that parent. Termination of parental rights severs the legal relationship for inheritance purposes.

This matters occasionally in deals involving families with CPS involvement or adoption-by-stepparent situations. Check whether any parental rights terminations exist in the family's legal history.

## 5.12 Case Study — The Childless Intestate Decedent with Surviving Spouse and Siblings

This specific scenario — a childless decedent with a surviving spouse and siblings — catches operators repeatedly.

### The Fact Pattern

Robert and Linda were married for 27 years. They had no children together. Robert died intestate in 2018. He had no children from any prior relationship. Robert's parents both died before Robert did. Robert had three siblings: Carol (alive), David (died in 2012, left two children — Robert's niece and nephew), and Ellen (alive).

Robert owned a house. The house was separate property — he inherited it from his mother in 1998, before marrying Linda. Value at Robert's death: $240,000.

### Applying the Rules

Robert died with:

1. A surviving spouse (Linda)
2. No descendants (no children)
3. No surviving parents
4. Surviving siblings (Carol, Ellen) and descendants of a predeceased sibling (David's two children)

Because this is separate real property, **Rule E** applies: spouse takes 1/2, parents/siblings/descendants-of-siblings take 1/2.

But wait — Robert's parents both died before him. So within the 1/2 to the decedent's "parents/siblings/descendants of siblings," there's an internal rule about how it's allocated.

### The Second-Level Rule

When both parents are deceased and siblings or descendants of siblings remain, the half that would have gone to the parents' line passes to the siblings and their descendants.

Since both of Robert's parents are deceased, the entire 1/2 collateral share goes to his siblings and his predeceased sibling's descendants.

### The Collateral Allocation

Robert's three siblings were Carol (alive), David (deceased, two children), Ellen (alive):

- Carol takes 1/3 of the collateral 1/2 = **1/6 of the whole = $40,000**
- Ellen takes 1/3 of the collateral 1/2 = **1/6 of the whole = $40,000**
- David's 1/3 of the collateral 1/2 passes to his two children by representation: each gets **1/12 of the whole = $20,000**

### Final Ownership of the $240,000 Property

| Heir | Relationship | Share | Dollar Value |
|------|-------------|-------|-------------|
| Linda | Surviving spouse | 1/2 | $120,000 |
| Carol | Sister | 1/6 | $40,000 |
| Ellen | Sister | 1/6 | $40,000 |
| David's first child | Niece | 1/12 | $20,000 |
| David's second child | Nephew | 1/12 | $20,000 |

### What Most Families Believe vs. the Legal Reality

Most families in this situation believe Linda inherited everything from Robert. Why wouldn't she? She was his wife for 27 years. The house is "their house."

The legal reality: Linda owns half. The other half belongs to Robert's original family — specifically his two sisters and the two children of his deceased brother. Robert's original family may or may not even know about the house. Carol and Ellen may not have spoken to Linda in years. David's children may have been small when he died and have no relationship with Linda at all.

### The Operator's Opportunity

When Linda tries to sell the house, she'll discover she can't sell 100% — she only owns 50%. She can sell her 50%, but no title company will insure the sale to a buyer without the other 50% also conveying. Linda has two choices: find all the collateral heirs and convince them to sell with her, or find a buyer willing to take only her 50% interest.

You're that buyer. You acquire Linda's 50% (or a portion of it). You then acquire the remaining 50% through the collateral heirs — maybe by buying each of Carol, Ellen, and David's children separately, or by applying leverage (contribution, partition, demand letters) to reluctant heirs.

This fact pattern — separate property, childless decedent, surviving spouse plus collateral heirs — is more common than operators expect. Always check whether a decedent's property was separate or community. Always identify the decedent's parents, siblings, and descendants of siblings. The 1/2 that goes to the collateral line is a common source of "hidden" heirs nobody in the current family is tracking.

## 5.13 Non-Texas Intestacy — The High-Level Overview

While this Codex is Texas-primary, here's a high-level overview of how other states differ, so you can recognize when you're in unfamiliar territory.

**Community property states (9 total):** Texas, Louisiana, Arizona, California, Idaho, Nevada, New Mexico, Washington, Wisconsin. All use community/separate property distinctions similar to Texas. The specific intestate rules vary, but the general framework is similar. Louisiana has unique rules from French civil law tradition (including forced heirship for some descendants).

**Common-law states (41 total):** Everywhere else. These states don't have community property. They use different rules for how marital property is classified and distributed. Most common-law states have versions of "elective share" statutes that guarantee a surviving spouse a minimum share of the decedent's estate (typically 1/3 to 1/2), regardless of what the decedent's will says. For intestate purposes, surviving spouses typically take larger shares in common-law states than in community property states.

**Primogeniture? Almost gone.** A few historical states retained vestiges of primogeniture (eldest child inherits) through the 20th century, but no state follows it today. Intestate succession is equal among children in all states.

**Spousal election statutes:** Many states allow a surviving spouse to "elect" against the decedent's will, taking a statutory share (often 1/3) instead of whatever the will specified. This doesn't apply in pure intestate situations (no will), but it's relevant in testate situations.

**Uniform Probate Code states:** About 15 states have adopted versions of the Uniform Probate Code, which standardizes intestate succession rules. The UPC's approach is broadly similar to Texas but with specific differences in share calculations.

**Key takeaway:** If you're working a deal where the decedent died in a non-Texas state or owned non-Texas real estate, consult an attorney licensed in that state. The specific rules matter. Don't assume Texas law applies.
