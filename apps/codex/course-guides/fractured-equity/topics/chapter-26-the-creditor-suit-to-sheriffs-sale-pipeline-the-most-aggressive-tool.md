Before I teach you this tool, I'm going to say something I haven't said in any other chapter:

Not every operator should use this.

The creditor-suit-to-sheriff's-sale pipeline is the most aggressive leverage tool in fractured equity. It works by finding heirs who have outstanding debts against them, coordinating with creditors or pursuing judgments yourself, and acquiring property interests at sheriff's auction — typically below fair market value.

The technique is legal. The procedures are established. The economics can be excellent. But the potential for harm is real, and operators who use this tool without judgment damage real families and damage their own reputations.

I'm going to teach you the tool comprehensively because operators who understand it have the complete toolkit. Some operators will use it occasionally in appropriate situations. Some will use it regularly as part of their business. Some will read this chapter, understand the tool, and decide never to use it. All three approaches can build good businesses.

Chapter 28 covers the ethical framework for all leverage tools. For this chapter specifically, I'll flag ethical considerations as they arise and trust you to apply the framework to your own decisions.

## 26.2 The Core Mechanism

Understand how the pipeline works before deciding whether to use it.

### The Basic Sequence

1. An heir owns a fractional interest in real property
2. That heir has outstanding debts — judgments, unpaid obligations, defaulted loans
3. Creditors have rights to collect against the debtor's assets, including real property
4. Through legal process, the debtor's property interest can be seized and sold at auction to satisfy the debt
5. The sheriff's sale produces a deed conveying the debtor's interest to the buyer
6. Strategic operators can acquire that interest at the sale, typically at a discount

### Three Entry Points

**Entry point 1: Existing creditor with existing judgment.** Someone has already sued the heir and won. Judgment exists. You coordinate with the creditor to execute on the judgment against the heir's property interest.

**Entry point 2: You create the judgment.** You have a legitimate claim against the heir (like a contribution judgment from Chapter 24). You execute on your own judgment against their property interest.

**Entry point 3: You become the creditor.** You purchase a creditor's existing judgment, becoming the judgment creditor. You then execute.

### The Key Legal Framework

Texas Property Code Chapter 52 — "Liens" — governs how judgments become liens and attach to real property. Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code Chapter 34 — "Execution on Judgments" — governs the procedures for executing on property to satisfy judgments.

### What This Produces

1. Sheriff's auction of the heir's fractional interest
2. Deed to the winning bidder
3. Heir's interest transferred to you (if you win)
4. Redemption period in some cases

### What This Does Not Produce

1. Forced sale of the whole property (that's partition)
2. Direct transfer without auction (that's voluntary sale)
3. Automatic acquisition (someone could outbid you)
4. Clean title without additional steps (always more title work needed)

## 26.3 When This Tool Is Appropriate

Ethical operators use this tool selectively. Here's when it's appropriate:

**Appropriate Situation 1: The Heir with Legitimate Substantial Debts.** An heir has real, substantial debts that aren't going away. They're already subject to creditor action. Their property interest is realistically threatened regardless of what you do. In these situations, the heir's interest is going to be sold one way or another. Your involvement might actually be beneficial — you pay fair value at auction, the heir gets something (excess proceeds if any), the creditor gets paid, and the property transfers cleanly.

**Appropriate Situation 2: The Uncooperative Heir with Your Own Judgment.** You have a legitimate judgment (from contribution suit, partition accounting, or other legitimate basis). The heir has refused to satisfy the judgment through any means. Executing on their property interest is the standard legal remedy. This isn't aggressive — it's the mechanism the legal system provides for collecting legitimate judgments. Refusing to use it means absorbing losses others caused you.

**Appropriate Situation 3: The Estate-Trapped Property.** Property has been trapped in fractured equity for years. Multiple heirs with various debts. No coordinated action possible voluntarily. The property is deteriorating, taxes are accumulating, value is being lost. Strategic use of creditor-suit mechanisms can unlock the deadlock, benefiting all parties — including creditors, heirs, and the community that doesn't want abandoned property.

**Appropriate Situation 4: The Coordinated Resolution.** Multiple tools coordinate to resolve a complex situation. Contribution creates judgment. Partition creates pressure. Creditor-suit mechanism provides backup for holdouts. The combined approach achieves fair resolution faster than any single tool.

### When It's NOT Appropriate

**Vulnerable individuals facing financial hardship.** Elderly on fixed incomes, disabled individuals, families in genuine crisis — these are not candidates for aggressive creditor-suit techniques even if legally permissible.

**Manufactured debts.** Don't create debts to create judgments to create leverage. Contribution must be genuine. Claims must be legitimate. Manufacturing leverage invites legal consequences and clearly crosses ethical lines.

**Predatory debt acquisition.** Buying up old, small debts solely to enable creditor-suits on property interests is generally predatory and creates significant legal and reputational risk.

**Bankruptcy situations.** If heir has filed bankruptcy, stay applies. Legal process is very different. Consult bankruptcy attorneys.

**When partition would work.** If partition achieves the same outcome more cleanly, partition. Don't escalate to creditor-suit when simpler tool suffices.

## 26.4 Entry Point 1: Coordinating with Existing Creditors

The simplest version of this tool: an existing creditor already has a judgment against an heir whose property interest you want.

### Finding Existing Judgments

Research is critical. Look for:

1. Public judgment records in county courthouse
2. UCC filings indicating secured debts
3. Court records showing completed lawsuits
4. Credit-related litigation (debt collectors, banks, credit card companies)
5. Judgment abstracting records per Texas Property Code § 52.001 et seq.

### Assessing the Judgment

1. Creditor identity
2. Judgment amount
3. Age of judgment (10-year life in Texas, renewable)
4. Whether abstracted and recorded
5. Whether creditor is actively pursuing collection

### Approaching the Creditor

Once you identify a judgment, approach the creditor carefully. Options:

**Option A: Encourage execution.** Creditor already has the right to execute. You can simply point out that the judgment debtor has a valuable property interest. Creditor may proceed to execute on their own.

**Option B: Purchase the judgment.** Buy the judgment from the creditor. You become the judgment creditor with full rights. Your own attorney executes on the property interest.

**Option C: Coordinate on execution.** Creditor retains judgment, you handle the practical work of executing. Profits divided per agreement.

### Typical Judgment Purchase Economics

1. Creditors often sell old judgments at 10–30% of face value
2. A $25,000 judgment might cost you $5,000–$7,500
3. Older judgments, creditor fatigue, weak collection prospects all reduce price
4. Active, collectible judgments cost more

### Legal Requirements for Judgment Purchase

1. Written assignment of judgment
2. Notice to debtor (may be required)
3. Recording of assignment
4. Your attorney's involvement in process

## 26.5 Entry Point 2: Creating Your Own Judgment

When you have a legitimate basis for a claim, you can pursue a judgment yourself.

### Common Bases for Judgments

**Contribution claims (Chapter 24):** Expenses you've paid for shared property.

**Accounting claims:** Rents collected without sharing, improvements, etc.

**Loan repayments:** If heir borrowed money from you and didn't repay.

**Tort claims:** If heir damaged your property interest.

**Breach of contract:** If heir agreed to sell and didn't follow through.

### The Sequence

1. File suit on legitimate claim
2. Obtain judgment through litigation or default
3. Abstract judgment per § 52.001 creating lien against debtor's real property
4. Execute on lien against specific property interest
5. Sheriff's sale produces deed

### Critical Ethical Line

The underlying claim must be genuine. A fabricated or inflated claim filed to create leverage is legally actionable against you and ethically unacceptable. Your attorney should not be willing to file claims they know to be weak. If a claim can't stand on its own merits as a money claim, it shouldn't be filed just for leverage.

## 26.6 The Lien Attachment Process

Once you have a judgment, it must be properly abstracted and recorded to become a lien against real property.

### Texas Property Code § 52.001

Establishes that an abstract of judgment properly recorded creates a lien on real property owned by the debtor (or after-acquired) in the county where recorded. The lien continues for 10 years from recording (with possibility of renewal).

### The Abstracting Process

1. Obtain certified copy of judgment
2. Prepare Abstract of Judgment form (specific format required)
3. File Abstract with county clerk in county where debtor owns property
4. Pay recording fee
5. Lien attaches upon recording

### What the Lien Accomplishes

1. Creates legal claim against debtor's real property
2. Priority over later-created liens
3. Enables execution and forced sale
4. Clouds title in ways that may force debtor action
5. Survives debtor's refinancing attempts

### Multi-County Considerations

If debtor owns property in multiple counties, abstract judgment must be recorded in each county separately. Each recording creates lien only in that county.

## 26.7 Execution and the Writ Process

With judgment abstracted and lien established, execution is the next step.

### Writ of Execution

Formal document issued by court directing sheriff to seize and sell debtor's property to satisfy judgment. Your attorney applies for writ. Court issues. Sheriff serves.

### Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code § 34.0035

Governs writ of execution procedures including specific requirements for service, notice, and conduct of sale.

### The Sheriff's Role

1. Receives writ of execution
2. Levies on specific property (seizes legal rights)
3. Provides required notices
4. Conducts public auction
5. Issues deed to successful bidder
6. Returns proceeds to court

### Notice Requirements

1. Notice to debtor (specific statutory requirements)
2. Public notice of sale (newspaper publication)
3. Posting at courthouse
4. Specific timing requirements (usually 21–30 days before sale)

### What's Being Sold

The sheriff's sale conveys only the debtor's interest in the property — not the whole property. If debtor owns 1/4 interest, sale conveys only that 1/4 interest. This is an important distinction. The buyer becomes a cotenant with the other heirs, subject to all the dynamics of fractured equity.

## 26.8 Homestead Protections Matter

Texas has some of the strongest homestead protections in the nation. These protections affect what can and cannot be forced to sale.

### Texas Constitution Article XVI § 50

Establishes homestead protection against forced sale except for:

1. Purchase money liens
2. Tax liens
3. Home equity liens (strict requirements)
4. Specific other exceptions

Ordinary judgment creditors cannot force sale of homestead property.

### What This Means for Creditor-Suits

1. If property is debtor's homestead, general creditor judgments can't force sale of their interest
2. Homestead-specific debts (purchase money, home equity) can
3. Homestead status depends on actual use, not just designation

### Homestead Abandonment

Homestead protection can be lost through:

1. Abandonment (moving out permanently)
2. Rental (in some circumstances)
3. Owner's death (passes to heirs)

### For Fractured Equity Properties

Many fractured equity properties are no longer homesteads:

1. Decedent is dead (homestead passes to specific beneficiaries)
2. No one currently lives in property
3. Property is rental or vacant

Homestead issues still require analysis but are often less protective than for owner-occupied property.

### Critical Analysis Before Proceeding

1. Is the property currently anyone's homestead?
2. Is debtor protected by someone else's homestead rights (surviving spouse, minor child)?
3. What's the homestead status of the specific property interest?

Attorney analysis essential before any creditor-suit action against debtor's property interest.

## 26.9 The Sheriff's Sale Itself

The auction is the culmination of the process.

### When and Where

1. First Tuesday of each month for most Texas counties
2. County courthouse or designated location
3. Public auction format
4. Typically between 10 AM and 4 PM

### The Auction Procedure

1. Sheriff announces property being sold
2. Reads description and details
3. Opens bidding
4. Accepts cash or cashier's check bids
5. Awards to highest bidder
6. Issues sheriff's deed

### Bidder Requirements

1. Must be present at auction (or represented)
2. Must have immediate funds for bid amount
3. Must meet any specific county requirements

### Typical Bidding Dynamics

1. Opening bid often set at judgment amount
2. Bidding typically starts low
3. Other bidders may appear (professional auction buyers)
4. Property often sells at fraction of market value

### The Judgment Creditor's Position

If you are the judgment creditor, you have an important advantage: you can bid using the judgment as credit rather than cash. You don't need to bring the full cash equivalent of your bid if your judgment covers it.

Example: You have $25,000 judgment. You bid $25,000. No cash required — your bid is credit against the judgment.

If others bid higher, you receive the excess in cash (up to judgment amount).

### After the Sale

1. Sheriff's deed issued to winning bidder
2. Deed recorded
3. Proceeds distributed: first to judgment (with costs), excess to debtor
4. Winning bidder becomes new cotenant

## 26.10 Post-Sale Strategy

Winning the sale is just the beginning.

### Your New Position

1. Cotenant with remaining heirs/owners
2. Subject to all rights and obligations of cotenancy
3. Can now use partition (Chapter 25) or other tools to force full resolution
4. Combined with any prior interest you had, may have majority interest

### Immediate Actions

1. Record sheriff's deed
2. Notify other cotenants of new ownership
3. Update property tax records
4. Assess property condition
5. Plan next strategic moves

### Redemption Considerations

In some Texas sheriff's sale situations, there's a redemption period during which the debtor can reclaim the property by paying sale price plus costs. Specific redemption rules depend on the type of sale and property involved.

For sales under general execution (most creditor-suit situations), no statutory redemption period typically exists. For tax sales (Chapter 27), redemption periods do apply. Verify with your attorney for specific situations.

### Title Considerations

1. Sheriff's deed conveys only debtor's interest
2. Title companies may require additional work before insuring
3. Sheriff's deed may carry specific warranties/disclaimers
4. Future resale requires careful title work

## 26.11 The Ethical Framework Revisited

Before showing examples, let me be explicit about the ethical framework for this tool.

### Legitimate Uses

1. Collecting on genuine debts owed
2. Enforcing legitimate judgments
3. Resolving stuck fractional interest situations
4. Facilitating outcomes that were going to happen anyway

### Illegitimate Uses

1. Fabricating claims to create leverage
2. Targeting vulnerable individuals for financial exploitation
3. Aggressive debt acquisition specifically to enable property seizure
4. Misleading debtors about their rights
5. Coordinating with others to freeze out heirs from information or advice

### The "Would You Tell" Test

Would you be comfortable explaining your actions to:

1. The heir's family members
2. Local journalists
3. Your own family
4. Other operators in your market
5. The judge presiding over your case

If not, reconsider.

### Fair Value Principle

Even in creditor-suit acquisitions, you have discretion in how aggressively you bid and what you pay relative to market. Operators who win at sheriff's sales and then voluntarily pay additional amounts to the former debtor when property value exceeded the sale price demonstrate ethical practice. Operators who extract maximum value regardless of fairness — don't.

### Reputation Effects

Word travels in real estate communities. Title companies, attorneys, judges, and other operators all know who uses these tools and how. Aggressive operators get shut out of deals, refused by title companies, and treated with suspicion by courts. Fair operators build sustainable businesses.

## 26.12 Practical Example — A Complete Pipeline Case

Let me walk through a complete creditor-suit-to-sheriff's-sale case.

### The Situation

**Property:** 3-bedroom home in South San Antonio. ARV $195,000.

**Decedent:** Died 2017 intestate. 5 heirs at 1/5 each.

**Your position:** Acquired 4 of 5 heir interests through voluntary purchase over 18 months. Total cost: $62,000.

**Remaining heir:** Brother James. Has refused to engage despite numerous attempts. Living out of state. Known to have outstanding debts.

### Research Phase

Due diligence on James reveals:

1. Existing judgment from a credit card company: $18,500 (6 years old)
2. Medical debt in collections: unknown amount
3. Several small debts in various stages
4. James owns no other real property (researched in 3 counties where he's lived)
5. Not in bankruptcy
6. Not claiming homestead in the subject property (lives elsewhere)

### Decision Framework

Your options:

1. Partition (Chapter 25) to force sale of whole property
2. Contribution suit (Chapter 24) based on taxes you've paid
3. Creditor-suit pipeline through existing judgment
4. Continued voluntary attempts

**Decision:** Contribution suit (you've paid $4,800 in property taxes over 2 years) combined with potential creditor-suit leverage. Pursue both paths in parallel.

### Contribution Suit Filing (Month 1)

1. File contribution suit for $3,840 (80% of taxes paid)
2. Serve James
3. James defaults (doesn't respond)
4. Default judgment entered Month 3 for $3,840 plus costs
5. Total judgment: $4,200 including costs and interest

### Judgment Abstracting (Month 4)

1. Abstract judgment per § 52.001
2. Record in Bexar County (where property is)
3. Lien attaches to James's 1/5 interest in the property

### Assessment of Execution Options

Your $4,200 judgment alone isn't large enough to make sheriff's sale particularly meaningful — the auction proceeds would just pay you back. But combining with other creditors' rights might create more leverage. You investigate purchasing the credit card company's judgment.

### Credit Card Judgment Purchase (Month 5)

1. Contact credit card company's collection department
2. Offer $4,000 for the $18,500 judgment
3. Negotiation over 30 days
4. Settle at $5,500 for judgment assignment
5. Execute written assignment of judgment
6. Record assignment
7. You are now also holder of the $18,500 credit card judgment

### Combined Position

1. Your contribution judgment: $4,200
2. Credit card judgment (assigned to you): $18,500
3. Total judgments: $22,700
4. Both liens against James's 1/5 interest

### Attempted Final Voluntary Resolution (Month 6)

Before proceeding to sheriff's sale, make final offer to James:

"James, you now have two judgments totaling $22,700 against your interest in the property. At the upcoming sheriff's sale, your interest will be sold to satisfy these judgments. Before that happens, I'm offering to purchase your interest directly for $25,000 cash. This resolves the judgments, gives you funds in your pocket, and avoids the auction process."

James's response: Still no engagement.

### Execution Filing (Month 7)

1. Attorney prepares writ of execution
2. Court issues writ
3. Sheriff receives writ
4. Levy on James's interest
5. Sale scheduled for first Tuesday of following month
6. Public notice given (newspaper, courthouse posting)
7. Notice to James at last known address

### Sheriff's Sale (Month 8)

1. Auction held at Bexar County Courthouse
2. Property interest (1/5 share) announced
3. Two other bidders present (professional auction buyers)
4. Bidding opens at $5,000
5. Other bidders bid up to $15,000
6. You bid $22,000 using judgment credit (no cash out)
7. Other bidders drop out (can't match without larger cash)
8. You win

### Post-Sale Processing

1. Sheriff's deed issued to you
2. Deed recorded
3. Proceeds distribution: judgment satisfied via credit
4. Excess over judgments would go to James (none in this case)

### Final Outcome

1. Acquired 1/5 interest for: $4,200 contribution suit costs + $5,500 judgment purchase + $1,200 sheriff's sale costs = **$10,900 total**
2. Combined with your existing 4/5: Now own 100%
3. Total acquisition cost (all 5 interests): $62,000 + $10,900 = **$72,900**
4. Property ARV: $195,000
5. After rehab of $18,000 and sale costs of $12,000: **Net profit $92,000**

### Timeline Summary

| Month | Event |
|-------|-------|
| Month 1 | File contribution suit |
| Month 3 | Default judgment entered |
| Month 4 | Judgment abstracted and recorded |
| Month 5 | Credit card judgment purchased |
| Month 6 | Final offer to James |
| Month 7 | Writ of execution filed |
| Month 8 | Sheriff's sale — you acquire |

Eight months from filing to sale. Cost of legal process: approximately $7,500. Net economic result excellent.

### Ethical Assessment

Is this case ethically acceptable?

1. James had genuine debts (not manufactured)
2. Multiple voluntary resolution attempts were made
3. Final offer exceeded his realistic recovery at auction
4. James was given full legal notice at multiple stages
5. Sale proceeds satisfied legitimate debts
6. James could have engaged at any point and kept his interest

This represents appropriate use of the tool. James had rights he chose not to exercise. The tool achieved a fair outcome through proper legal procedures.

## 26.13 When Things Go Wrong

This tool has specific failure modes worth understanding.

**Failure Mode 1: Bankruptcy Filing.** Debtor files bankruptcy during the process. Automatic stay halts all collection activity. Response: Consult bankruptcy attorney. Wait for stay relief or bankruptcy completion. Sometimes you can continue through bankruptcy procedure. Sometimes you lose judgment in discharge.

**Failure Mode 2: Other Bidders Outbid You.** Professional auction buyers or other interested parties outbid you at sheriff's sale. Response: Your judgment still gets satisfied from proceeds. You didn't win the interest but you recovered on your judgment. Depending on your goals, this may be acceptable or may require further action.

**Failure Mode 3: Homestead Claims.** Debtor or family member asserts homestead protection that stops execution. Response: Thorough pre-execution research prevents most of these. If asserted after filing, attorney addresses the specific claim.

**Failure Mode 4: Judgment Validity Challenged.** Debtor challenges the underlying judgment through motion to vacate or appeal. Response: Well-obtained judgments survive challenges. Poorly-obtained judgments sometimes don't. Another reason to have solid legal basis for any claim.

**Failure Mode 5: Title Company Won't Insure.** After acquisition, title companies may not want to insure sheriff's sale properties. Response: Work with specific title companies who handle these properties. Understand title may have exceptions.

**Failure Mode 6: Community Reaction.** Use of this tool becomes known. Community members, other heirs, local professionals react negatively. Response: Only use this tool in appropriate situations. When used appropriately, can be defended. When used inappropriately, consequences are severe.

## 26.14 Coordinating with Other Tools

The creditor-suit pipeline rarely stands alone. It coordinates with other tools.

**With Contribution Suits (Chapter 24).** Contribution suits create judgments. Judgments enable creditor-suit execution. Natural progression from one to the other when circumstances warrant.

**With Partition Actions (Chapter 25).** Partition requires ownership interest. Creditor-suit acquisition provides that interest. Creditor-suit then partition to complete acquisition. Alternatively: Partition action filed, creditor-suit pressure applied to holdouts, settlement often follows.

**With Tax Sale Intervention (Chapter 27).** If property going to tax sale, interacting with creditor-suit timing matters. Coordination with attorney essential to avoid conflicts.

**With Voluntary Acquisition.** Often the threat of creditor-suit pipeline creates voluntary settlement. The tool's value is often in not needing to use it.

## 26.15 Key Takeaways for Operators

**This is the most aggressive leverage tool.** Not every operator should use this. Use selectively and with judgment.

**The core mechanism** involves debt, judgment, lien, execution, sheriff's sale. Sequence produces deed conveying debtor's property interest.

**Three entry points exist:** existing creditor coordination, creating your own judgment, purchasing existing judgments.

**Appropriate situations include** legitimate debts, uncooperative heirs with legitimate judgments against them, stuck properties, coordinated resolutions.

**Inappropriate uses include** targeting vulnerable individuals, manufactured debts, predatory debt acquisition, when simpler tools suffice.

**Legal framework** is Texas Property Code Chapter 52 and Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code Chapter 34.

**Abstracting creates the lien.** Judgment must be properly abstracted in county where property is located.

**Execution requires writ.** Sheriff conducts sale after proper notice.

**Homestead protections matter greatly.** Attorney analysis essential before proceeding.

**Judgment creditor advantage at auction:** bid using judgment as credit rather than cash.

**Post-sale you become cotenant.** Still need other tools (partition) for full property acquisition often.

**Ethical framework is essential.** The "would you tell" test. Fair value principle. Reputation effects.

**Most often the threat creates voluntary settlement.** The tool's greatest value is often not needing to fully execute it.

**Failure modes include** bankruptcy, outbidding, homestead claims, challenges to judgment, title issues, community reaction.

**Coordinates with other tools** — contribution, partition, tax sale, voluntary acquisition. Not a standalone tool.

## 26.16 A Final Word Before Chapter 27

Now that you understand this tool comprehensively, you have a decision to make: will you ever use it?

Some reasonable positions:

**Position A: Never use it.** The ethical concerns outweigh the benefits. You'll build a business on the other tools and accept that some deals won't close.

**Position B: Use it rarely, only in specific situations.** When all conditions are right — legitimate debts, genuine attempts at voluntary resolution, fair value paid — the tool can be appropriate.

**Position C: Make it part of regular practice.** If you do this, commit to the ethical framework. Use the "would you tell" test on every case. Build a reputation for fair dealing even while using aggressive tools.

All three positions can produce sustainable businesses. The position you can't sustain is using this tool without ethical consideration. That path leads to reputation damage, legal problems, and community backlash that eventually destroys the business.

Whatever position you take, make it deliberately.
