## 3.1 Where We Are

Bonus Chapter 36 addressed finding defunct LLCs and the people behind them. Bonus Chapter 37 addressed the legal framework — why entities become inactive, how Texas law allows them to be reinstated, and the three pathways for acquiring the property they hold. This chapter addresses the mechanics of actually doing the deal. The conversations. The negotiation. The coordination with the Comptroller and the Secretary of State. The paperwork. The funds flow. The control of the entity once you have it. The closing of the real property transaction.

This is the operational heart of the work. Everything before this chapter is orientation. This chapter is where theory becomes practice. The sequence is not rigid — every deal varies — but the elements presented here appear in almost every defunct-LLC acquisition, and the operator who knows them can direct the deal rather than be directed by it.

As throughout the Bonus Section, Texas is the illustrative jurisdiction. The Comptroller of Public Accounts and the Texas Secretary of State are Texas-specific offices with Texas-specific procedures. Every state has equivalents with different names and different mechanics. If you are working elsewhere, engage local counsel and a CPA who handles that state's reinstatement work.

## 3.2 First Contact With the Members

You have identified the members, managers, or heirs of deceased members who hold authority over the defunct entity. Now you reach out. This first contact is the highest-leverage conversation in the entire deal. It can move the rest of the process forward quickly or produce a closed door that takes months to reopen. Handle it with care.

### The Objective of the First Contact

The first conversation has four purposes, in order:

1. Establish who you are and why you are calling — clearly and without hiding anything
2. Communicate that you understand their situation — their entity is in forfeiture, their property is stuck, they have a legal problem they may not have realized
3. Offer a path forward — an acquisition structure where you handle the cleanup and they receive payment for the property
4. Invite them to verify everything with their own attorney before agreeing to anything

### The Opening Approach

By phone or in writing — phone is preferable when you can get through. A usable opener:

*Hello, this is [your name] with [your company]. I work with property owners whose LLCs have gone into forfeiture. I see that [LLC name] is currently in forfeiture at the Texas Comptroller and that it owns a property at [address]. I wanted to reach out because this situation means the LLC cannot currently sell that property without going through a cleanup process. I'm not with the state, I'm not collections, and you're not in trouble. I buy properties in this situation and I handle the cleanup as part of the transaction. I wanted to see whether you'd be open to a brief conversation about what that might look like. Whatever we discuss, you would want to verify with your own attorney before committing to anything.*

Notice what this opener does. It identifies you, explains the specific factual situation (which the member may not have realized), assures the member this is not an enforcement contact, explains the opportunity at a high level, and pre-invites independent legal review. That combination lowers defensiveness and opens the door to the actual conversation.

### What the Member Typically Does Not Know

In most first contacts, you are delivering new information. The member may know the LLC exists but not that it is in forfeiture. May know it is in forfeiture but not understand the legal consequences. May know there is property involved but not understand that the property cannot currently be conveyed. May assume nothing can be done or that cleanup would cost more than the property is worth. Educating the member about the actual situation — without being condescending — is a significant part of the first conversation.

### What the Member Typically Wants to Know

1. Who you are and how you got their information
2. How much money they would receive and when
3. Whether they personally owe the franchise tax
4. Whether they could lose the property through some other action if they do nothing
5. What happens to the LLC itself after the transaction
6. What their attorney would say

Have honest answers ready for each. The member who feels informed and respected is a cooperative counterparty. The member who feels pressured or talked-around is a counterparty who slows the deal or walks away.

### Multiple Members

When there are several members, you typically start with one — the most reachable, usually the one whose name appeared most prominently in the entity records. That first member becomes an ally who introduces you to the other members, vouches for you (or does not), and helps coordinate the group. Reaching out to all members cold at the same time often creates confusion; reaching the first one well usually produces the second and third introductions naturally.

### Heirs of Deceased Members

When a member has died, you are talking to the heirs who inherited the membership interest. The conversation is slightly different — they may have even less context about the LLC than a living member would, because the deceased member may have told them little about it. The opening includes acknowledging the family's loss, identifying the estate and heirship situation you need to work through (which may require probate or determination of heirship under the Part VI tools of the main Codex), and proposing a path that works around the estate complication.

## 3.3 Preliminary Diligence Before Contracting

Between the first conversation and the purchase contract, you complete the diligence picture. The goal is to understand the full entity situation, the full property situation, and the full member situation well enough to structure a deal that will actually close.

### Entity Diligence

1. Full history at the Secretary of State — certificate of formation, all amendments, annual or periodic reports filed, current registered agent and registered office, status changes and dates
2. Full history at the Comptroller — franchise tax status, years of non-filing, any franchise tax returns that were filed, current franchise tax account status, tentative payoff balance
3. Operating agreement if obtainable — from the members, the original organizing attorney, tax preparers, or the entity's files
4. Identification and current status of all members — not just the ones you have been talking to; members you have not contacted yet must be identified and located
5. Litigation searches at state and federal court level for the entity and for each member individually
6. Bankruptcy searches for the entity and for each member individually
7. UCC searches at the Secretary of State

### Property Diligence

1. Full chain of title — how the LLC acquired the property, any conveyances within or out of the entity
2. Current property tax status, including any delinquencies and any pending tax foreclosure calendar
3. Recorded liens, mortgages, mechanics liens, judgment liens, HOA liens
4. Current appraised value from the county appraisal district
5. Estimated market value from comparable sales
6. Physical condition — drive by, walk the perimeter, or inspect if access permits
7. Occupancy status — vacant, owner-occupied, tenant-occupied
8. Zoning, use restrictions, known environmental concerns

### The Precise Franchise Tax Payoff

Your CPA should obtain a precise franchise tax payoff amount from the Comptroller before you commit to a deal structure. The preliminary estimate you worked with during qualification may differ from the actual payoff. The difference can be small or large — large enough to change whether a deal makes sense. Do not commit the final purchase price until the real number is in hand.

Getting the precise payoff involves the CPA submitting a request on behalf of the entity (with the cooperating members' authorization) or the members directly. The Comptroller provides a tax clearance inquiry response that identifies:

1. Each year of past-due franchise tax reports
2. The tax owed for each year — often zero under the no-tax-due threshold, sometimes substantial
3. Accumulated penalties per year
4. Accumulated interest
5. The total payoff to bring the entity into good standing

This number then becomes a known input to the deal structure rather than an estimated one.

## 3.4 Deal Structuring

With diligence complete, you structure the deal. This means selecting the pathway, setting the price, allocating the costs, and drafting the purchase agreement.

### Pathway Selection in Practice

Your attorney and CPA recommend a pathway based on the specific facts. Common selection patterns:

**Pathway 1 — Reinstate and direct sale.** Most common. Works when members are cooperative, the franchise tax payoff is manageable, the operating agreement permits the sale, and the title company will insure after reinstatement. Cleanest and usually fastest.

**Pathway 2 — Membership interest acquisition.** Works when the entity wrapper has value, when the franchise tax obligation is large enough to benefit from restructuring across a transfer, or when the operating agreement or member configuration makes interest transfer cleaner than property transfer.

**Pathway 3 — Winding up with distribution.** Works when the entity has no value beyond the property, when franchise tax is too large to support straight reinstatement, or when multiple members are more readily worked with individually after receiving distributed property in their own names.

### Price

The purchase price in a defunct-LLC deal has to absorb not just the property transaction but also the cleanup costs. Work backward from your exit:

1. Estimated value of the property at your planned exit
2. Less your required profit margin
3. Less rehab or improvement costs if applicable
4. Less holding costs during the cleanup and exit process
5. Less franchise tax cleanup cost
6. Less attorney, CPA, and title fees
7. Less closing costs
8. **Equals the maximum price available to pay to the members for the property or the membership interests**

The members are usually willing to sell at prices substantially below straight market value because without your intervention they cannot sell at all. But do not assume — run the math for the specific deal.

### Handling the Franchise Tax Payoff in the Price

Mechanically, the franchise tax cleanup can be handled several ways:

**Acquirer pays the franchise tax directly, deducted from the purchase price.** Most common. The purchase agreement states a total price; at closing, the franchise tax payoff goes directly from escrow to the Comptroller, and the remaining balance goes to the members. This keeps the accounting clean and ensures the franchise tax is actually paid.

**Members pay the franchise tax themselves, funded by a payment from the acquirer.** Less common. The acquirer wires funds to the members, who in turn pay the Comptroller. Works only with trusted counterparties because the acquirer loses control of whether the payment actually occurs.

**Acquirer funds the cleanup before contracting.** Rare. Some deals are structured with the acquirer paying franchise tax cleanup upfront as part of the negotiation, essentially earning sweat equity and cash investment before the purchase price is fully set. Creates risk because cleanup costs are incurred before a binding purchase agreement exists.

For most transactions, the first approach is preferable — clean, verifiable, and handled through the title company or escrow agent at closing.

### The Purchase Agreement

The purchase agreement captures the deal in writing. Whether you are buying the property from the entity (Pathway 1), the membership interests from the members (Pathway 2), or the distributed property from the members (Pathway 3), certain elements recur:

1. Identification of the parties — acquirer and the seller(s)
2. Description of what is being sold — property legal description for Pathway 1 and Pathway 3, or specific membership interests for Pathway 2
3. Total purchase price and payment structure
4. Allocation of franchise tax payoff, attorney fees, CPA fees, title insurance, and closing costs
5. Conditions precedent to closing — reinstatement completion, clear title, required consents, any required court approvals
6. Representations and warranties from the sellers — ownership of what they are selling, authority to sell, no undisclosed liens, and so on
7. Seller indemnifications — protection against undisclosed claims, undisclosed liabilities, or breach of representations
8. Escrow and holdback provisions if the deal structure includes any amount being held after closing
9. Timing milestones — target reinstatement date, target closing date, outside closing date beyond which either party can terminate
10. Default provisions — what happens if either party fails to perform

### Contingencies

A defunct-LLC purchase agreement typically includes contingencies for:

1. Satisfactory completion of the reinstatement process
2. Issuance of a title commitment without exceptions the acquirer finds unacceptable
3. Receipt of any required consents from non-selling members
4. Confirmation that the franchise tax payoff is within the estimated range
5. Clearance of any litigation, bankruptcy, or other adverse matters disclosed during diligence
6. Satisfactory physical inspection of the property, if not previously completed

Contingencies protect the acquirer against the deal breaking in ways that were foreseeable but not yet confirmed at contracting. Overuse of contingencies signals uncertainty and can make members nervous. Underuse leaves the acquirer exposed. Your attorney strikes the right balance for each deal.

## 3.5 Taking Control of the LLC — Pathway 2 Mechanics

For Pathway 2 deals — where the acquirer buys the membership interests rather than the property — a specific set of mechanics applies. This section walks through how you actually step into control of the entity.

### Assignment of Membership Interests

Each selling member executes an assignment of their membership interest to the acquirer. The assignment is a written document that typically includes:

1. Identification of the assignor (the selling member) and the assignee (the acquirer)
2. Identification of the LLC and the specific percentage or units of interest being assigned
3. Consideration — the amount being paid for the interest
4. Representations by the assignor that they own the interest free of encumbrances and have authority to transfer
5. Acknowledgment that the transfer may be subject to operating agreement restrictions, any required consents, and applicable securities laws
6. Effective date of the transfer

Assignments are prepared by your attorney and reviewed by counsel for the selling members when available. They are signed at closing (or in escrow ahead of closing) and delivered to the acquirer upon funding.

### Required Consents

Review of the operating agreement is essential. Many operating agreements require consent from non-selling members for transfers to outside parties. Options when consent is required:

1. Obtain the required consents from the non-selling members, often as part of the same closing
2. Acquire all of the membership interests simultaneously so that the consent question is moot — there are no non-selling members left to object
3. Amend the operating agreement to remove or modify the consent requirement, if the amendment provisions permit
4. Restructure the deal around the consent issue — for example, acquiring only economic rights (which many operating agreements permit without consent) rather than full membership rights

### The Economic Rights / Governance Rights Distinction

Texas law, and many operating agreements, distinguish between the **economic rights** associated with a membership interest (the right to distributions, the right to allocations of profit and loss) and the **governance rights** (the right to vote, the right to participate in management). A transfer by assignment often automatically conveys economic rights to the transferee, but governance rights may require additional consent from the existing members before the transferee becomes a full member with voting authority.

For defunct-LLC acquisitions, governance rights matter — the acquirer typically wants to control the entity, not just receive distributions from it. The purchase agreement and the assignment documents should specifically address conveyance of governance rights, and any required consents from existing members to admit the acquirer as a full member with full rights should be obtained contemporaneously with the assignment.

### Updating the Entity's Records

After the assignments close and you become the controlling member, you update the entity's records:

1. **Internal membership records** — the ledger of members and their interests is updated to reflect the new ownership
2. **Operating agreement** — if the old operating agreement is being replaced, a new one is adopted; if it is being amended, the amendments are executed
3. **Banking** — existing LLC bank accounts, if any, are either closed, transferred to new signature authority, or terminated in favor of new accounts
4. **Tax records** — the CPA coordinates with the Comptroller and with the IRS (for federal tax purposes) to reflect the new ownership

### Acting as the Controlling Member

Once the assignments are complete and consents obtained, you are the controlling member. You can now authorize the entity's actions — including initiating reinstatement, hiring professionals, selling property, borrowing money, and eventually either operating the entity or winding it up. The authority that was diffused across the prior members is now concentrated in you (or in you plus any co-acquirers if the purchase was shared).

## 3.6 Reinstatement — The Practical Process

Reinstatement is the administrative work that restores a forfeited entity to active status. This section walks through what actually happens, step by step.

### Authorization

Reinstatement requires authorization from a person with authority to act on behalf of the entity. In Pathway 1 deals, this is the cooperating members or managers who agree to authorize the cleanup before the property sale closes. In Pathway 2 deals, this is you as the new controlling member after the membership interest assignments have closed. Either way, the authorization is typically documented in a written resolution or consent of the governing persons — your attorney drafts this as part of the standard deal paperwork.

### Past-Due Report Preparation

Your CPA prepares all past-due franchise tax reports. For each missing year, the CPA:

1. Determines whether the entity was above or below the no-tax-due threshold for that year
2. Calculates any actual franchise tax owed for years above the threshold
3. Prepares the formal franchise tax report for that year on the Comptroller's required form
4. Includes any supporting schedules or attachments required for the specific year

For entities that had no-tax-due years in sequence, the preparation is often straightforward — the no-tax-due report is a simpler filing than a full tax return. For entities that had years above the threshold, the work is more involved, and the CPA may need additional information about the entity's financial activity in those years.

### Submitting the Past-Due Reports

The past-due reports are submitted to the Comptroller along with payment of the accumulated tax, penalty, and interest balance. Submission can be electronic (through the Comptroller's online systems) or by mail, depending on the entity's specific situation and the CPA's preferred process. Processing time at the Comptroller varies — typically two to four weeks for the submission to be fully processed, sometimes longer if the Comptroller has questions.

### Obtaining the Tax Clearance Letter

Once all past-due reports have been filed and all amounts paid, the Comptroller issues a **tax clearance letter** (formally, a certificate of account status used for the purpose of reinstatement). This letter confirms that the entity is in good standing for franchise tax purposes. The letter is required for the reinstatement application at the Secretary of State.

Your CPA requests the tax clearance letter through the Comptroller's online system or by written request. The Comptroller typically issues the letter within one to two weeks once all filings have cleared. The letter has a limited validity period after issuance, so the timing of the request is coordinated with the anticipated filing of the reinstatement application.

### The Reinstatement Application at the Secretary of State

With the tax clearance letter in hand, the reinstatement application is filed with the Texas Secretary of State. The application (on the Secretary of State's prescribed form) typically includes:

1. The entity's name and file number
2. A statement that the entity seeks reinstatement
3. Confirmation that the entity has cured the deficiency that led to forfeiture
4. Current registered agent and registered office information
5. Signature of an authorized governing person
6. The filing fee (currently $75 for LLC reinstatement, subject to change; confirm with your attorney before filing)
7. The tax clearance letter from the Comptroller attached as required documentation

### Registered Agent and Registered Office

A reinstated entity must have a current, valid registered agent and registered office in Texas. If the prior registered agent relationship has lapsed (the commercial registered agent service was never paid, the individual registered agent has died or moved), the reinstatement cannot be completed until a new registered agent is appointed. Options for the new registered agent:

1. A commercial registered agent service (CT Corporation, Incorp Services, Cogency Global, LegalZoom, and others)
2. An individual Texas resident willing to serve — often the acquirer or the acquirer's attorney
3. A Texas-based entity that provides registered agent services as part of its business

Commercial registered agent services typically cost $100 to $300 per year. Using a commercial service is usually the cleanest path because it eliminates the risk of future failures when individuals move or pass away.

### Secretary of State Processing

The Secretary of State processes reinstatement applications in the order received. Standard processing time is typically two to six weeks, depending on volume. Expedited processing is available for additional fees. Once the application is processed and approved, the Secretary of State updates the entity's status to "in existence" or "active," the public records reflect the reinstatement, and the entity is legally able to transact again.

### Confirmation of Reinstatement

The Secretary of State issues a confirmation of reinstatement that your attorney retains for the file. The confirmation is evidence of the reinstatement date and status, and is typically reviewed by the title company as part of closing the real property transaction. Verify that the entity's status at the Comptroller has also been updated to reflect current good standing — occasionally the Comptroller's records lag, and confirmation from both offices may be needed.

## 3.7 Title Work — What Insurers Want to See

Title insurance is usually the tightest gate in a defunct-LLC acquisition. Title insurers underwrite these transactions carefully because property conveyed during a period when the grantor entity lacked capacity can create later claims. Understanding what title insurers want to see — and working with insurers who have experience with these deals — is essential.

### The Title Commitment

The title company prepares a title commitment based on a search of the property's chain of title. The commitment identifies:

1. **Schedule A** — the parties, the property description, the coverage amount
2. **Schedule B** — exceptions from coverage (restrictions, easements, liens, and any specific issues related to the entity's forfeiture history)
3. **Requirements** that must be satisfied before the policy will issue

For defunct-LLC transactions, the title commitment often contains specific exceptions or requirements related to the entity's status — for example, a requirement that reinstatement be completed before closing, or an exception for any defect arising from the entity's prior forfeiture. Your attorney reviews the commitment carefully and negotiates with the title company to remove, modify, or insure around problematic exceptions.

### Common Title Company Requirements

1. Proof of reinstatement — the Secretary of State confirmation and the Comptroller tax clearance letter
2. Current registered agent and registered office confirmation
3. Resolution or written consent of the governing persons authorizing the transaction
4. Evidence of membership identity and authority if Pathway 2 mechanics are involved
5. Sometimes an indemnification from the selling entity or its principals
6. Sometimes an endorsement specifically addressing the forfeiture period
7. Occasionally a title company will require a quiet title action if the forfeiture has been long enough or the chain of title raises concerns

### Working With the Right Title Company

Not every title company in Texas is equipped or willing to insure defunct-entity transactions. Developing relationships with specific title companies and specific underwriters who understand these deals is a significant part of building the business for this work. Experienced title companies:

1. Know what documentation they need and ask for it early
2. Have underwriters who will commit to insurability before you invest heavily in cleanup
3. Price their premiums and endorsements predictably for this type of transaction
4. Coordinate smoothly with your attorney and with the Comptroller/SoS reinstatement process
5. Do not withdraw late in the process

When a title company withdraws or refuses to insure, the deal either shifts to another title company (if time allows) or dies. The operator with two or three strong title company relationships has backup options; the operator with only one does not.

## 3.8 Closing the Real Property Transaction

With reinstatement complete, the title commitment acceptable, and all contingencies resolved, the transaction closes.

### Closing Mechanics — Pathway 1

For a Pathway 1 closing (reinstated entity sells the property directly to the acquirer):

1. The reinstated entity executes the deed conveying the property to the acquirer; the deed is signed by an authorized governing person (typically a manager or member acting under a resolution)
2. The acquirer's purchase funds flow to the title company or escrow agent
3. The title company disburses per the closing statement — franchise tax payoff to the Comptroller if not already paid pre-closing, attorney and CPA fees, title insurance premium, recording fees, any liens or encumbrances being cleared, and the net proceeds to the selling members per the allocation in the purchase agreement
4. The deed records in the county real property records
5. The title insurance policy issues to the acquirer

### Closing Mechanics — Pathway 2

For a Pathway 2 closing (acquirer previously acquired the membership interests and now, as the controlling member, causes the reinstated entity to sell to itself or to a related entity):

1. The membership interest assignments have already closed before the property transaction
2. The acquirer, acting as controlling member, authorizes the reinstated LLC to convey the property — either directly to the acquirer individually, or to a separate holding entity the acquirer controls, or to hold the property within the LLC if that is the plan
3. If conveying out of the LLC, the deed is executed by the acquirer in his controlling-member capacity
4. If holding the property in the LLC, no property conveyance happens — the entity continues to own the property, but now under the acquirer's control
5. Funds flow and recording depend on whether a property transfer is happening and how the acquirer structures the post-acquisition ownership

### Closing Mechanics — Pathway 3

For a Pathway 3 closing (entity is wound up, property distributed to members, members then sell individually):

1. The winding-up paperwork and any required court approvals have been completed
2. The entity executes a distribution deed conveying the property from the entity to the members individually in their respective percentages
3. The distribution deed records
4. The members, now holding the property individually, execute individual deeds to the acquirer
5. The acquirer's funds flow through escrow to the members according to their respective percentages
6. The entity is formally terminated through a certificate of termination filed with the Secretary of State
7. Title insurance issues on the individual-to-acquirer deeds

### The Closing Statement

The closing statement (HUD-1 or ALTA settlement statement, depending on transaction type) documents the full flow of funds. For defunct-LLC transactions, the closing statement typically shows:

| Line Item | Direction |
|---|---|
| Gross purchase price | To the transaction |
| Franchise tax payoff to the Comptroller | Out to state |
| Attorney fees for the acquirer's counsel | Out |
| CPA fees for the reinstatement work | Out |
| Title insurance premium and endorsement costs | Out |
| Recording fees | Out |
| Prorated property taxes | Adjusted |
| Other liens or encumbrances being paid at closing | Out |
| Net proceeds to the selling members | Out to sellers |
| Acquirer's total cash-to-close | In from acquirer |

Every number on the closing statement should be verifiable against source documents — franchise tax payoff per the Comptroller, property tax per the county, and so on. A sloppy closing statement creates disputes; a clean one closes the transaction definitively.

## 3.9 Post-Closing Cleanup

After the closing, a few items remain.

### If the Entity Remains in Existence

1. For Pathway 2 closings where the entity is retained, the acquirer operates the entity going forward — maintaining registered agent, filing annual franchise tax reports, and keeping the entity in good standing
2. Operating agreement is updated or replaced to reflect new ownership structure
3. Bank accounts are opened or transferred to new signature authority
4. The entity's tax year and accounting methods are confirmed with the CPA

### If the Entity Is Being Terminated

1. For Pathway 1 and Pathway 3 closings where the entity is no longer needed, the entity is wound up and terminated
2. Winding up involves final franchise tax report, notice to any remaining creditors, distribution of any residual assets, and payment of any final obligations
3. Certificate of termination is filed with the Secretary of State
4. Final tax return is filed with the IRS
5. The entity ceases to exist

### File Retention

Retain the complete deal file indefinitely. Defunct-LLC transactions can produce questions years later — a previously unknown heir of a deceased member, a previously undisclosed creditor of the entity, a title issue that emerges during a later resale. The file is the operator's defense when those questions come. At minimum the file should include:

1. All entity research and diligence documents
2. All correspondence with members and managers
3. Purchase agreement and all amendments
4. All reinstatement documents, including the past-due reports, tax clearance letter, and Secretary of State confirmation
5. Title commitment, title policy, and any endorsements
6. Closing documents including the deed, closing statement, and funding records
7. Post-closing entity documents, if applicable

### Property Transition

Parallel to the entity cleanup, the property itself transitions to the acquirer's operation:

1. Insurance on the property is bound in the acquirer's name
2. Utilities are transferred to the acquirer
3. Property tax authority records are updated to reflect the new owner
4. Any existing leases are addressed — assumed, renegotiated, or terminated per their terms
5. Rehab or management work begins per the acquirer's plan

---

## 3.10 Realistic Timeline and Cost

Straightforward defunct-LLC deals with cooperative members and manageable franchise tax obligations typically run **four to six months** from first contact to closing. Professional fees, franchise tax cleanup, and closing costs beyond the purchase price typically total **$8,000 to $25,000**. Complex deals — multiple members, deceased members requiring heirship work, litigation or bankruptcy exposure, large franchise tax balances, or out-of-state members — can run **nine to eighteen months** and **$25,000 to $75,000 or more**.

The dominant time components are the Comptroller work (typically two to six weeks for past-due report processing and tax clearance issuance) and the Secretary of State reinstatement (typically two to six weeks of processing after submission). Both offices have queues that cannot be accelerated for most transactions. Plan accordingly and communicate the timeline honestly to members so they are not surprised when the deal takes as long as it actually takes.

## 3.11 Exits at a Glance

Once the property is acquired, the standard real estate exit options apply. Match the exit to the property characteristics, the market, and your capital position.

1. **Wholesaling** — assign the contract to an end buyer before closing; the end buyer must be willing to close on a property that came out of entity cleanup
2. **Wholetailing** — close, light cleanup, quick retail resale
3. **Fix and flip** — rehabilitate and resell at a higher price
4. **Rehab and hold** — rehabilitate and keep as a rental for long-term cash flow
5. **Buy and hold as-is** — rent the property in current condition without significant rehab
6. **Owner financing the resale** — sell to an end buyer with seller financing to capture interest income and expand the buyer pool
7. **Partition exit** — when the defunct LLC owned a fractional interest in a co-owned property, use partition to convert the fractional interest to cash
8. **Land-banking** — hold raw land long-term for eventual development or assemblage
9. **Refinance out** — after the title seasons, refinance to recover acquisition capital while keeping the property

The exit strategy should influence the acquisition price you are willing to pay. Underwrite to a specific exit and walk from deals that do not pencil at that exit's economics. An exit picked after closing, when the acquisition price has already been committed, is an exit that too often does not produce the returns the deal needed.

## 3.12 Key Takeaways

**First contact is the highest-leverage conversation.** Clear, honest, informative; explain the situation to the member; invite independent legal review; earn the right to continue the discussion.

**Preliminary diligence before contracting.** Full entity history, full property history, precise franchise tax payoff from the Comptroller, complete identification of all members, litigation and bankruptcy searches, operating agreement review.

**Pathway selection drives everything.** Reinstate and sell, acquire membership interests, or wind up and distribute. Pathway is an attorney-and-CPA decision based on the facts.

**Price backs out from the exit.** Estimated exit value, less required margin, less rehab costs, less holding costs, less franchise tax cleanup, less fees, equals what you can pay the members.

**Franchise tax payoff usually flows through closing.** Acquirer pays the Comptroller directly through the title company or escrow agent at closing, out of the purchase price.

**Pathway 2 takeover involves assignments, consents, and governance updates.** Membership interest assignments from sellers to acquirer, required consents from non-selling members, operating agreement review and potential amendment, entity record updates, and explicit conveyance of both economic and governance rights.

**Reinstatement is real administrative work.** Authorization from governing persons, past-due franchise tax report preparation by the CPA, submission and processing at the Comptroller, tax clearance letter, reinstatement application at the Secretary of State with fees and registered agent confirmation, and processing by the Secretary of State.

**Title insurance is the tightest gate.** Not all title companies will insure defunct-entity transactions. Work with experienced title companies and their underwriters. Provide documentation proactively. Address exceptions and requirements before closing.

**Closing mechanics differ by pathway.** Pathway 1 — reinstated entity deeds directly to acquirer. Pathway 2 — membership interest assignments close first, then acquirer controls subsequent property decisions. Pathway 3 — entity distributes to members, members deed to acquirer.

**Post-closing cleanup finishes the work.** Entity operation or termination, file retention, property operational transition.

**Timeline runs four to six months minimum.** Complex cases run nine to eighteen months or more. Communicate realistically.

**Standard real estate exits apply.** Wholesaling, wholetailing, fix and flip, rehab and hold, buy and hold, owner financing resale, partition, land-banking, refinance-out. Underwrite to a specific exit.

**State variation reminder.** Texas is illustrative. Every state has its own equivalents with different names and mechanics. Engage local professionals.
