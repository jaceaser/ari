## A Note Before We Begin

Bonus Chapters 39 and 40 cover the trust side of this advanced-acquisition work. As throughout the Bonus Section, Texas is the illustrative jurisdiction. Every statutory reference and procedural description is drawn from Texas law and Texas practice. But the underlying opportunity — acquiring real property held by trusts that have become dormant, abandoned, or stranded — is not a Texas-only play. It works in every state with its own version of trust law and its own real property records. The statute names change. The court procedures change. The title insurance treatment changes. The principles do not.

Before implementing any strategy from these chapters, consult a real estate attorney and a trust/probate attorney licensed in the state where the property sits, a CPA familiar with trust taxation, and the title professionals who will insure the transaction. Verify every specific before you act. These chapters are a map of Texas terrain; they are not a substitute for qualified local guidance in your state.

## 4.1 The Trust Opportunity

Defunct LLCs have an acquisition cousin: dormant trusts. A trust is a legal arrangement in which a trustee holds legal title to property for the benefit of one or more beneficiaries under terms stated in a trust instrument. Like LLCs, trusts can hold real property. Like LLCs, trusts can become stranded — unable to transact, with nobody clearly authorized to act, with beneficiaries who have lost track of what they own. And like defunct LLCs, stranded trusts create acquisition opportunities for operators who understand the legal framework and the practical procedures for unsticking them.

The underlying logic is the same logic you have now seen twice in this Codex. Title is stranded in a form that cannot currently be transacted against. The people with authority are diffused, missing, deceased, or uninformed. A specific procedural toolkit exists to resolve the situation. The operator who understands the framework can step into the space that other investors, title companies, and real estate professionals walk away from.

What changes between defunct-LLC work and dormant-trust work is the statute codex, the procedural mechanisms, and the specific people you deal with. Instead of the Texas Business Organizations Code and Tax Code, you work from the **Texas Trust Code** (Texas Property Code, Subtitle B, Title 9, beginning at § 111.001) and related provisions. Instead of members and managers, you work with trustees and beneficiaries. Instead of reinstatement filings, you work with successor trustee appointments, court orders, and beneficiary consents. Some of the tools from defunct-LLC work port directly; others have trust-specific analogues; and some aspects of trust work have no LLC counterpart at all.

## 4.2 Why a Trust Becomes Dormant

Unlike LLCs, trusts do not have an annual filing obligation with the Secretary of State or a franchise tax obligation that triggers formal forfeiture. A trust does not get forfeited. But trusts still become dormant and stranded through several characteristic pathways, each of which produces a recognizable signature in the records.

### Trustee Dies Without a Successor

The trust instrument names a trustee but does not name a successor, or names a successor who has also died. When the trustee dies, no one is immediately authorized to act for the trust. Property held in the trust's name sits stranded until a court appoints a successor trustee or until the beneficiaries take action to resolve the gap. This is one of the most common causes of dormant-trust situations — particularly with older trusts whose drafting did not anticipate long time horizons.

### Trustee Disappears or Becomes Unreachable

The named trustee is still alive but has moved, retired, lost capacity, or simply stopped responding. Sometimes the trustee was a family member who had a falling out. Sometimes the trustee was a professional who has retired and closed their practice without transferring the trust. The trust instrument may provide for replacement mechanisms, but those mechanisms often require action by beneficiaries or by a court, and if nobody takes action, the trust stays stranded.

### Beneficiaries Die and Their Heirs Lose Track

A beneficiary dies, and the beneficiary's share typically passes either under the trust instrument's terms to a named successor beneficiary, or under the beneficiary's own estate plan, or to the beneficiary's heirs under intestate succession. When the beneficiary's family does not know the trust exists — because the beneficiary never told them, because the paperwork was lost, because a generation passed without anyone checking — the inherited beneficial interest becomes invisible. The trust may continue to hold property with beneficiaries who exist legally but who do not know they are beneficiaries.

### Trust Terms Have Been Fulfilled But the Trust Was Never Terminated

A trust created for a specific purpose — to hold property until a child reached a certain age, to hold property during the grantor's lifetime, to provide for a spouse until remarriage — may have satisfied its terminating condition years or decades ago. Under the trust instrument, the trust should have terminated and the property should have been distributed. But the formal termination and distribution never happened. Property continues to sit in the trust's name even though the trust, by its own terms, has ended.

### Grantor Died and the Trust Was Never Administered

A living trust was created, funded with real property, and then never properly administered after the grantor's death. The successor trustee — often a family member — did not understand the administration requirements, did not distribute the property, did not file final tax returns, and simply moved on. The trust sits with property in its name, the grantor dead, and the administration incomplete.

### The Current Generation Has Lost Track

Trusts can have very long lifespans. A trust created by a grandparent in 1965 for the benefit of grandchildren born in the 1970s may still be in existence today, with original trustees deceased, original beneficiaries now elderly or also deceased, and the current generation of heirs only vaguely aware that the trust exists. The paperwork is scattered. Nobody can find a current copy of the trust instrument. The property sits in the trust's name on the deed records.

### The Trustee Is an Entity That Has Gone Defunct

Sometimes the trustee is itself a legal entity — an LLC, a corporation, a bank trust department that was reorganized. When the trustee entity goes out of existence, the trust loses its trustee. Occasionally you will encounter a situation where both the defunct-LLC framework (Bonus Chapters 36 through 38) and the dormant-trust framework apply to the same property — the trustee LLC has been forfeited, and the trust it served has been stranded as a consequence. These layered cases are solvable, but they require both toolkits working simultaneously.

### The Grantor's Intent Has Become Impossible

A trust drafted to accomplish a purpose that has become impossible, illegal, or impractical. A charitable trust whose designated charity no longer exists. A trust with terms that conflict with current tax law or property law. Administration has stalled because no one is sure how to proceed, and the property sits indefinitely.

## 4.3 Why This Opportunity Has Not Been Mined Out

Dormant trusts, like defunct LLCs, stay an open acquisition channel for specific reasons:

1. Most real estate investors do not understand trust law well enough to recognize a dormant trust situation on the deed records
2. Title companies will not insure transactions where trustee authority is unclear or where the trust appears inactive, which discourages casual buyers
3. The cleanup work often requires court intervention — appointing a successor trustee, construing ambiguous trust terms, modifying or terminating the trust — which adds complexity most operators do not want to learn
4. Trust documents are private; unlike LLC filings, the trust instrument itself is not usually in public records, which makes preliminary diagnosis harder
5. The beneficiaries often do not realize they have an interest until someone finds them and explains
6. When beneficiaries have died, the heirship work from Part VI of the main Codex may be required on top of the trust work
7. Traditional sellers and buyers cannot move these properties, so they do not compete for them

The combination of trust law knowledge, willingness to work with probate and trust courts, relationships with title companies that will insure post-cleanup trust transactions, and the ability to coordinate with beneficiaries and potential successor trustees creates a moat that keeps most operators out of this channel. The operator who invests in the skills can build a consistent pipeline from a source of deals that few competitors will ever touch.

## 4.4 State Variation

Texas trust law is primarily codified in the Texas Trust Code, which is found in the Texas Property Code beginning at **§ 111.001**. Other states have trust law that shares the broad outlines — most states have adopted some version of the Uniform Trust Code with variations — but specific procedures and statutory citations differ significantly state to state. A few areas where variation matters:

1. The procedures for appointing a successor trustee when none is named in the trust instrument
2. The grounds for modifying or terminating a trust through court action
3. The standards for trustee accountability and the beneficiaries' rights to information
4. The treatment of trust property for tax purposes
5. The title insurance industry's standards for trust-held property transactions
6. The availability of nonjudicial settlement agreements as alternatives to court proceedings

If your target property is in a state other than Texas, engage a trust and estate attorney licensed there. Have that attorney translate the framework in these chapters into your state's specifics before you commit capital. The Texas statute numbers will not save you in Colorado, and assuming they will is an expensive mistake.

## 4.5 Who We Are Looking For

Before working through finding techniques, understand who the relevant people in a trust acquisition actually are. Unlike LLCs — where the universe is members, managers, and their heirs — trust work involves a wider set of parties with distinct legal roles. Mapping those roles correctly is not a preliminary administrative task; it is the analytical foundation for everything that follows.

### The Trustee

The **trustee** holds legal title to trust property and has authority to manage it on behalf of the beneficiaries. The trustee named in the trust instrument is typically named in deeds conveying property into the trust, signed documents, and sometimes in recorded trust summaries. The trustee is the natural starting point for contact — if the trustee is alive, responsive, and still serving, the trustee may be able to sell the property in accordance with the trust instrument, and the rest of the work is simpler.

### The Successor Trustee

Trust instruments typically name one or more **successor trustees** to take over if the original trustee dies, resigns, or becomes unable to serve. When the original trustee is gone and a successor is named in the instrument, the successor steps in with authority. When no successor is named — or when the named successors have also been lost — the trust needs a court-appointed successor before it can transact. This is a critical distinction in the qualification phase: finding the instrument tells you whether a court proceeding is inevitable or avoidable.

### The Beneficiaries

**Beneficiaries** are the people (or entities, or charitable purposes) for whose benefit the trust holds property. Beneficiaries have equitable ownership — they have the right to the benefits the trust produces — but they typically do not have direct authority to transact over trust property. What beneficiaries do have:

1. Rights to receive distributions according to the trust's terms
2. Rights to receive information from the trustee about the trust's operations
3. Rights to initiate court proceedings when the trustee is failing to act
4. In some circumstances, rights to consent to trust modifications or terminations
5. The ability to acquire the trust property if the trust terminates and distributes assets to them

Beneficiaries are not owners in the ordinary sense, but they are far from irrelevant. In most dormant-trust acquisitions, beneficiary cooperation is essential — and in many cases, beneficiaries who do not know they are beneficiaries are the most cooperative people in the entire deal once they are found and informed.

### The Grantor or Settlor

The **grantor** (also called the settlor or trustor depending on the document) is the person who created the trust and funded it with property. In revocable living trusts, the grantor often also serves as the initial trustee and is often also a beneficiary. When the grantor dies, the trust typically becomes irrevocable and a successor trustee takes over. For dormant-trust acquisition work, the grantor is often long deceased — but the grantor's identity matters because the trust instrument is sometimes located only through the grantor's estate records or the grantor's attorney's files.

### Heirs of Deceased Trustees

When the trustee has died and no successor is immediately available, the deceased trustee's estate may hold the trust's records and may, through the personal representative, have a role in coordinating the transition. Finding the deceased trustee's estate and working with the executor or administrator is often part of finding the trust document itself, not just finding the trust's parties.

### Heirs of Deceased Beneficiaries

When a beneficiary has died, the beneficial interest typically passes either to a successor beneficiary named in the trust or to the beneficiary's own heirs. This is where trust work crosses directly into the heirship work from Part VI of the main Codex. A determination of heirship for the deceased beneficiary establishes who inherited the beneficial interest — and until that question is resolved, the beneficiary's share of any trust distribution or consent is in legal limbo.

### Other Interested Parties

- **Remainder beneficiaries** — parties entitled to receive property when the trust terminates
- **Contingent beneficiaries** — parties whose interests depend on events that have not yet occurred
- **Creditors** of the trust or of its beneficiaries
- **Taxing authorities** with claims for unpaid trust taxes
- The **Attorney General of Texas**, in cases involving charitable trusts

### The Complete Picture

Most dormant-trust acquisitions involve multiple categories of the above parties simultaneously. A typical case might include one deceased trustee, one successor trustee who has lost track of the trust, two living beneficiaries, one deceased beneficiary whose heirs must be identified through heirship work, and a charitable remainder beneficiary. The operator maps all of these categories as part of diligence, because acquisition strategy depends on knowing exactly who has what authority and whose cooperation is needed.

## 4.6 Finding Dormant Trusts Holding Real Property

The finding technique for dormant trusts is different from the finding technique for defunct LLCs, because trusts have less public-records footprint. A trust does not file with the Secretary of State. There is no central trust registry. What trusts do have is their appearance in the real property records — because when a trust holds real property, the deeds conveying property into the trust, and sometimes summaries of the trust, are recorded.

### Deed Records — The Primary Source

The systematic finding technique starts with the county real property records. Pull deeds in a county of interest and filter for grantees that are trusts rather than individuals or business entities. Trust-held property typically shows in the deed records in formats like:

1. "The John Smith Family Trust dated January 15, 1985"
2. "Mary Johnson, Trustee of the Johnson Revocable Living Trust"
3. "ABC Land Trust"
4. "The 2001 Thompson Children's Trust"
5. "[Name], Trustee" without further trust identification

The trust name and the trustee name appearing on a deed are your starting points. From there you investigate further to determine whether the trust appears active or dormant.

### Recorded Trust Memoranda

Texas allows (but does not require) the recording of a memorandum of trust in the real property records. Under **Tex. Prop. Code § 114.086**, a memorandum of trust may be recorded to provide public notice of the trust's existence and of the trustee's authority. When a memorandum is recorded, it typically identifies:

1. The trust name and date
2. The grantor and the current trustee
3. Confirmation that the trustee has authority to act for the trust with respect to the property
4. Sometimes other specific authority provisions

When a memorandum exists, it gives you a data starting point — the grantor's name, the trustee's name, and often the date of the trust. When no memorandum exists and the deed is the only trust reference, you have less information and more investigative work ahead. The absence of a memorandum is not itself a dormancy signal, but it does increase the diagnostic burden.

### Property Tax Records

County appraisal district records often show trust-held property with the trust as owner and sometimes with a mailing address for the trustee. When property taxes are being paid from a specific address, that address is a lead — the person paying the taxes is often still connected to the trust even when other records have gone stale. When property taxes have not been paid, the delinquency is itself a signal that the trust may be dormant. A trust with an active, engaged trustee almost never runs into property tax delinquency.

### Probate and Estate Records

When the grantor of a trust has died, the grantor's probate file — if one exists — often contains references to the trust. Inventory documents, will provisions, and administrator's reports may identify the trust, the trustee, and sometimes the beneficiaries. Reviewing probate filings in target counties periodically produces leads to trusts whose grantors have recently died but whose administration may not have been completed. This is one of the most productive recurring-research sources in the dormant-trust pipeline.

### Court Filings

Lawsuits involving trusts — disputes among beneficiaries, actions for trustee accounting, actions to construe ambiguous trust terms — appear in court records. Case captions and pleadings provide information about the trust, the parties, and current status. Texas district courts handle most trust matters. A court case involving a trust is not always a dormancy signal, but it is always an information source.

### Attorney and Trust Department Files

Many trusts were drafted by attorneys or administered by bank trust departments. When an attorney retires or a trust department is reorganized, trust files sometimes get transferred, archived, or lost. Identifying the drafting attorney (sometimes named on the recorded deed or in a memorandum of trust) and reaching out to the attorney's current practice, the attorney's successor firm, or the State Bar of Texas's records preservation program can surface trust documents that would otherwise be lost. This is particularly valuable when the trust instrument itself cannot be located through family members.

### Family Interviews

Once you have identified a target trust and some of the people connected to it, family interviews often produce information that records cannot. A grandchild may remember that her grandmother "had some kind of trust" that held farmland. A family attorney's name gets recalled. A box of old papers turns up in an attic. Trust work leans on family memory more than LLC work does because trust documents are private by design — they were never meant to be in a public registry, and finding them often requires someone to open a drawer and look.

### Commercial Data Providers

Some commercial real estate data providers flag trust-held properties and can help build lists more efficiently than manual deed-record pulls. Subscription-based tools like those mentioned in Bonus Chapter 36 — Property Radar, Reonomy, ATTOM Data Solutions — may provide trust-owner filtering as part of their ownership data. These tools are not a substitute for deed-record analysis, but they can accelerate the initial list-building phase and help you prioritize which properties to investigate further.

## 4.7 Initial Qualification

Not every trust-held property is a defunct-trust opportunity. Many trusts are active and well-administered. The qualification step filters your list down to the subset likely to be stranded. Time spent on qualification is never wasted — it saves you from chasing deals that will not pay off.

### Signals of a Dormant Trust

1. Trust named on the deed, but no recent recorded activity — no recent refinancing, no recent sale, no recent memorandum
2. Property tax records showing mailing address undeliverable or tax bills going to a deceased person's old address
3. Property showing signs of neglect — unmowed lawn, deferred maintenance, vacancy
4. Property tax delinquency
5. Trustee named in the deed is known to be deceased (from separate research)
6. Trust appears to have been created decades ago and no recent activity is visible
7. Grantor identified in the trust name is deceased and no trust administration appears in probate records

Any two or three of these signals together constitutes a qualified lead worth deeper investigation. One signal alone is a note; multiple signals are a deal worth pursuing.

### Property-Level Qualification

1. Property type suitable for your acquisition criteria
2. Approximate market value supporting the work involved
3. Property condition acceptable or rehab budget justified
4. Tax status and lien picture
5. Physical access for investigation

Trust work costs more to clean up than standard fractured-equity work, and those costs come before acquisition. The equity spread must be wide enough to absorb them. A $35,000 equity spread on a trust deal that requires a court-appointed successor trustee proceeding is not a deal — it's a loss waiting to happen.

### Trust-Level Qualification

1. Apparent age of the trust — older trusts are often more dormant
2. Ability to locate the trust instrument — if the instrument is genuinely lost, the work is harder and longer
3. Number of apparent beneficiaries — single-beneficiary trusts are usually simpler than multi-beneficiary
4. Apparent posture of any living trustees — if the trustee is alive and responsive, you may be looking at a cooperative-trustee situation rather than a dormant-trust situation
5. Evidence of prior court proceedings involving the trust

### Economic Qualification

| Cost Category | Description |
|---|---|
| Trust cleanup costs | Successor trustee appointment, court approvals, trust construction or termination proceedings |
| Heirship work | For any deceased beneficiaries, Part VI determination of heirship |
| Acquisition costs | Purchasing beneficiary interests or facilitating trustee-led sale |
| Holding costs | Property taxes, insurance, maintenance during cleanup timeline |
| Exit costs | Retail sale commissions, closing costs, transfer taxes |

Net margin after all costs — not just the spread between purchase price and ARV — is the qualification filter. Trust deals that look attractive on the surface often look less attractive once court costs and timeline holding costs are modeled. Run the economics before committing time to the people-finding work.

## 4.8 Finding the People Behind the Trust

Once a trust target is qualified, you find the actual people — trustees living and dead, beneficiaries living and dead, and the heirs of deceased parties. The methodology is the **Chapter 30 breadcrumbing framework** from the main Codex, adapted to the trust context. The breadcrumbing skills transfer directly; what changes is the starting points and the specific trails.

### What You Already Have

1. Trust name and sometimes date from the deed or memorandum
2. Trustee name and sometimes address from the deed or memorandum
3. Grantor's name if the trust is named after the grantor
4. Property tax mailing address, which may point to a current contact
5. Any names appearing in recorded documents connected to the trust

### The First Move — Locate the Trust Instrument

The trust instrument itself is the single most important document in this work. It identifies the trustees, the beneficiaries, the grantor's intent, and the provisions governing how the trust operates. Finding it is the first investigative task. Everything else — strategy, structure, cost estimate — depends on what the instrument says.

1. Contact the trustee named in the deed
2. Contact the trustee's estate if the trustee has died
3. Contact the grantor's estate — if the grantor's estate was probated, the trust instrument may be in the probate file
4. Contact the drafting attorney if identifiable — from deed references, from memoranda, or from questions to the grantor's family
5. Contact the bank trust department if the trustee was institutional
6. Check the grantor's and trustee's personal records if family members can access them

### Working Without the Trust Instrument

Sometimes the trust instrument cannot be found. Your attorney will advise on the workarounds, which may include:

1. Constructing the trust's essential terms from deed recitals, memoranda of trust, and secondary evidence
2. Court proceedings to construe the trust based on available evidence
3. Seeking a judicial declaration of the trust's existence and essential terms
4. In some circumstances, treating the property as held on a resulting trust or a constructive trust under equitable principles

Missing trust instruments are a solvable problem but a time-consuming one. Budget accordingly if this is your situation. A lost instrument adds months to the timeline and several thousand dollars in attorney fees to the cost side of the ledger.

### Finding Living Trustees

When a living trustee has simply lost touch or become unresponsive, standard Chapter 30 breadcrumbing finds them. Start with the address on the deed or memorandum, skip-trace for current contact information, search obituaries to confirm the trustee is still alive, and work social media and community networks as needed. The Chapter 30 Social Breadcrumbing Method and the Advanced Breadcrumbing Skip Tracing Method both apply without modification.

### Deceased Trustees and Their Successors

When the trustee has died:

1. Obtain the death certificate to confirm the date and place of death
2. Search probate records to determine whether the trustee's estate was administered
3. If the estate was administered, the personal representative may have coordinated or may have the trust file
4. Review the trust instrument (once located) for named successor trustees
5. Track down any named successor trustees using the same breadcrumbing techniques
6. If no successor trustee is available — all named successors have died, resigned, or are unreachable — the case will likely require a court-appointed successor trustee

**Walk the full successor chain.** Trust instruments often name two or three successor trustees in priority order. The first may have died, but the second or third may still be living and willing to serve. Do not assume court involvement is necessary until you have confirmed that every named successor in the instrument is unavailable. Finding an available named successor saves months of court time.

### Finding Beneficiaries

Once the trust instrument is located and identifies the beneficiaries, you find them using Chapter 30 techniques. Living beneficiaries who are simply not aware of the trust are often the easiest to locate and the most cooperative once contacted — they are happy to learn of property they did not know they had a claim to. Beneficiaries who learn they inherited an interest tend to engage quickly and helpfully, because from their perspective, an operator showing up with information about an asset they never knew they had is a gift.

### Heirs of Deceased Beneficiaries

When a beneficiary has died, the beneficial interest passes either to named successor beneficiaries under the trust or to the deceased beneficiary's heirs. The path depends on the trust's terms:

1. If the trust names successor beneficiaries and those are still living or identifiable, the interest follows the trust's own terms
2. If the trust is silent on death of beneficiary and general trust law controls, the interest typically passes through the beneficiary's estate
3. When the beneficiary's estate has not been administered, a determination of heirship may be required
4. The Part VI tools of the main Codex apply directly to this heirship work

This is where trust work and heirship work become a single intertwined process. The operator who has built both skill sets handles this naturally. The operator who is new to heirship work discovers at this point that the Chapter 30 and Part VI investment was not optional — it was load-bearing infrastructure for the trust channel.

## 4.9 Scenario Patterns

Dormant-trust situations cluster into recognizable patterns. Knowing the patterns helps you qualify faster, plan the approach, and avoid surprises that should have been anticipated.

### The Post-Grantor Incomplete Administration

A living trust set up by a grantor who has since died. The successor trustee is a family member who did not understand the administration requirements. Property is titled in the trust, the grantor is dead, and nothing has been done to complete the transition. Often the successor trustee is cooperative once the situation is explained — they are relieved to have help rather than defensive. These are often the easiest dormant-trust cases to resolve because the named successor trustee is still alive, still technically has authority, and simply needed someone to explain what needed to happen.

### The Lost-Trustee Trust

The named trustee has died or vanished. No successor is readily available. The trust needs a court-appointed successor before the property can be sold. Beneficiaries may or may not know the trust exists; sometimes finding the beneficiaries is the work that then produces the motion for successor trustee. In these cases, the beneficiaries themselves often become the petitioners for successor trustee appointment once they understand their rights and interests.

### The Multi-Generation Family Trust

A trust created decades ago by a grandparent, with beneficiaries spanning two or three generations. Original beneficiaries are elderly or deceased. Property has been sitting in the trust's name while family generations have changed. Resolution involves identifying all living beneficiaries, handling the heirship of any deceased beneficiaries, and coordinating the trust-level action with the heir-level work. These cases are the most complex and the most time-consuming in the dormant-trust category — but they also tend to have the most motivated parties, because everyone involved has been sitting on an unresolved asset for years or decades.

### The Charitable Trust with a Failed Purpose

A trust created for a charitable purpose that has become impossible or impractical — a named charity no longer exists, the specified use can no longer be accomplished. The trust continues to hold property. Resolution typically involves court action under cy pres or related doctrines to modify the trust's purpose. These are complex cases and typically involve participation by the **Texas Attorney General**. Budget more time and more attorney cost than any other category. Assess carefully before committing to a charitable trust case.

### The Lost-Instrument Trust

The trust instrument itself cannot be found. Deed records show property titled in the trust, but nobody has a copy of the governing document. Resolution involves constructing the trust's essential terms from secondary evidence and obtaining court approval to proceed. Difficult but workable with qualified counsel. The key early investigative task is assembling every available secondary source — deed recitals, memoranda of trust, correspondence, family statements — so that your attorney can build the strongest possible evidentiary record.

### The Revocable Trust with Elderly Grantor in Care

A revocable trust whose grantor is still living but in a care facility or with significant cognitive decline. The grantor may or may not have capacity to revoke the trust or direct a sale. The successor trustee may or may not have authority to act during the grantor's incapacity, depending on the trust's terms. These cases can involve the capacity-and-representative-signing framework from **Chapter 32** of the main Codex alongside the trust framework. Two analytical tracks running simultaneously — trust law and capacity law — require an attorney who is comfortable with both.

### The Never-Funded Trust

Occasionally a trust instrument exists but the property you are investigating was never actually conveyed to the trust — the deed references to the trust may be aspirational or may reflect a partial conveyance that was never completed. These cases often turn out not to be dormant-trust acquisitions at all; the property may actually be held in the individual name of the intended grantor, and the acquisition strategy shifts to ordinary fractured-equity or heirship work. Discovering this early saves time — verifying that the deed actually conveys to the trust (not just mentions a trust) is one of the first qualification checks.

## 4.10 Building a Systematic Target List

The same systematic approach that builds a defunct-LLC pipeline builds a dormant-trust pipeline, adapted for the different source records. The pipeline thinking is identical; the inputs are different.

### Define the Geographic Footprint

1. Counties with online deed records searchable for trust grantees
2. Counties where you have relationships with title companies willing to handle trust transactions
3. Counties with sufficient trust-held property volume to produce regular leads

Start with one or two counties where you already have relationships and records access. Expand as the pipeline matures.

### Define the Property Profile

1. Types and price ranges you are equipped to acquire
2. Condition tolerance matching your operational capability
3. Minimum equity spread threshold that supports the expected cleanup cost

### Establish Recurring Research Cadence

| Research Activity | Frequency |
|---|---|
| Deed record pulls — trust-grantee conveyances | Monthly |
| Probate filing review — recently deceased grantors and trustees | Monthly |
| Tax delinquency list monitoring — trust-held properties | Quarterly |
| Court filing review — trust proceedings | Monthly |
| Commercial data provider trust-owner list refresh | Quarterly |

### Maintain the Pipeline

1. Each lead enters a staged process — initial qualification, deeper research, people-finding, outreach, negotiation, contract, cleanup, closing
2. Trust deals move slowly — often slower than defunct-LLC deals because of the greater likelihood of court involvement
3. Dead leads return over time as family circumstances change — a trustee who was unreachable in 2020 may have died in 2023, triggering new activity
4. Document every research step — the work product becomes evidence in later proceedings

### Relationship Infrastructure

The relationships that make this channel work:

1. **Trust and estate attorneys** who can handle successor trustee appointments and trust administration issues
2. **Title companies** comfortable with trust transactions and willing to insure post-cleanup trust conveyances
3. **CPAs** familiar with trust taxation, particularly for trusts that have not filed required tax returns
4. **Probate attorneys** for the crossover cases involving deceased grantors or beneficiaries
5. **Court contacts and reporters** for the counties where trust proceedings are filed

Build these relationships before you need them on a live deal. Walking into a court proceeding without established counsel who knows the local trust docket is the wrong way to execute.

## 4.11 Common Pitfalls in Finding and Qualifying

**Confusing an active trust for a dormant one.** Many trust-held properties are well-administered by trustees who are simply not active sellers. Verify the dormancy signals before investing significant time. A trustee who responds to your first letter with "yes, the trust is active and we are not interested in selling" is not a dormant-trust deal — it's a dead lead. Move on.

**Assuming the trust instrument will be easy to find.** Trust documents are private. Finding a copy can be weeks of work. Plan for this time and build it into your diligence timeline before committing to a deal.

**Missing the successor trustee chain.** Trust instruments often name multiple successor trustees. The first named successor may have died, but the second named successor may still be living and willing to serve. Walk the full chain before concluding that court intervention is required.

**Overlooking beneficiaries who do not know they are beneficiaries.** Some of the most cooperative beneficiaries are those who never knew they had an interest. Find them — they will usually engage eagerly, because from their perspective the operator is bringing them news about an asset they had no idea existed.

**Not anticipating the need for court intervention.** Unlike defunct-LLC reinstatement (which is administrative), many dormant-trust cases require court orders — successor trustee appointment, trust construction, trust termination. Budget for court involvement from the start of your economic model, not as an afterthought when you're already committed.

**Underestimating the heirship work.** Deceased beneficiaries drag the Part VI toolkit into the matter. The trust work and the heirship work have to be coordinated. An attorney who understands trust law but not heirship work, or vice versa, is inadequate for these cases.

**Ignoring the grantor's intent.** Texas trust law protects the grantor's intent. Courts are often reluctant to modify or terminate trusts in ways that defeat the grantor's evident purpose. Structure acquisitions around the grantor's intent, not against it. A deal structure that requires a court to override the grantor's purpose is a deal with significant judicial risk.

**Skipping the charitable trust complexity.** When the trust has a charitable element, the Texas Attorney General has a role. These cases are more complex. Know whether your target involves charitable beneficiaries before you commit. One Attorney General involvement transforms a six-month deal into a multi-year proceeding.

**Treating the trustee as the only relevant party.** In many trust situations, the trustee cannot act without beneficiary consent or court approval. Identify beneficiaries in parallel with identifying trustees. A deal built entirely around trustee cooperation that ignores beneficiary consent requirements falls apart at the attorney's desk.

**Applying defunct-LLC procedures to trust work.** Trust law is a different framework. Reinstatement, membership interests, operating agreements — these have no direct trust equivalents. Work from trust-specific concepts and resist the temptation to assume that the LLC playbook translates.

**Assuming Texas procedures apply elsewhere.** Trust law varies more state to state than entity law in some respects. Local counsel every time outside Texas, and engage that counsel before you start the people-finding work — not after.

## 4.12 Key Takeaways

**Dormant trusts parallel defunct LLCs as an acquisition channel.** Stranded title, diffused authority, specialized procedural tools — but a different legal framework that requires a different skill set and different professional relationships.

**Texas is illustrative.** Every state has trust law with its own statutory citations and procedures. Engage local counsel where your target property sits before committing capital.

**Consult qualified professionals.** Real estate attorney, trust and estate attorney, probate attorney, CPA familiar with trust taxation, and experienced title professionals. All five categories may be needed on a single dormant-trust deal.

**Trusts become dormant for recognizable reasons.** Trustee death without successor, trustee disappearance, beneficiary death with lost heirs, fulfilled but unterminated trust purposes, unadministered post-grantor trusts, generational loss, trustee-entity failure, impossible grantor intent. Each of these produces a recognizable signature in the records.

**Finding dormant trusts starts with deed records.** Trust-held properties appear in the deed records with trust names and trustee names. Cross-reference with signals of dormancy — no recent activity, tax delinquency, deceased trustees, incomplete administration.

**The people involved are broader than in LLC work.** Trustees, successor trustees, beneficiaries, grantors, heirs of any deceased parties, remainder and contingent beneficiaries, and sometimes creditors or the Attorney General for charitable trusts. Map all categories before building the acquisition strategy.

**Finding the trust instrument is the first investigative task.** The trust document is private but essential. Look in the trustee's records, the grantor's estate, the drafting attorney's files, or institutional trust departments. Working without the trust instrument is possible but harder — and every workaround costs time and money.

**Finding the people uses Chapter 30 methodology.** The Social Breadcrumbing Method first, then the Advanced Breadcrumbing Skip Tracing Method. Specialized trails triggered by the specific facts of each case — deceased trustee's estate, probate files, family interviews.

**Deceased beneficiaries bridge to heirship work.** The Part VI tools of the main Codex apply to determining who inherited beneficial interests. The trust work and the heirship work must be coordinated, not treated as sequential.

**Systematic pipeline built from deed records, probate filings, and court filings.** Trust deals move slower than LLC deals, with more likelihood of court involvement. Plan timelines and capital accordingly. Budget twelve to eighteen months for complex court cases from identification to closing.

**Document the finding work.** Research and outreach records become evidence later, the same way diligent search affidavits work in heirship cases. Keep notes on every contact attempt, every source searched, every lead followed.
