## The Different Aggressive Tool

Tax sale intervention is different from the creditor-suit pipeline. Both tools are aggressive. Both involve acquiring property interests through forced legal processes. But the ethical posture is fundamentally different.

In creditor-suit situations, you're often accelerating an outcome that might have happened anyway but on different timing. The pressure you create affects the heir's choices.

In tax sale situations, the outcome is already happening. The property is already going to be lost. The only question is who will own it after the government takes it — the taxing authority's chosen buyer, a random investor, or you. Your intervention is often the thing that prevents the worst outcome for the original owners.

This is why experienced operators often view tax sale work as the cleanest aggressive tool in the toolkit. When you find a property facing tax sale and intervene to prevent the loss — whether by paying taxes in exchange for interest, by coordinating with heirs, or by bidding at the sale itself — you're usually creating a better outcome than what would have happened without you.

That said, tax sale work has its own dynamics, legal requirements, ethical considerations, and risks.

## The Texas Tax Sale Landscape

Understand the landscape before engaging.

### The Property Tax System

Texas property taxes are assessed annually by local taxing authorities (county, city, school district, special districts). Property owners must pay by January 31 each year or face penalties and interest.

Unpaid property taxes accrue:

1. Penalty starts February 1 (6% in first month)
2. Interest accrues monthly
3. Attorney fees add when accounts go to collection (typically 15-20%)
4. Total can exceed 50% of original tax bill within 2 years

### The Statutory Framework

**Texas Tax Code Chapter 33 — "Delinquency"** covers the collection process:

- § 33.01 — Penalties and interest
- § 33.07 — Additional collection fees
- § 33.41 — Suits to collect delinquent taxes
- Other provisions governing notices, procedures, sales

**Texas Tax Code Chapter 34 — "Tax Sales and Redemption"** covers:

- § 34.01 — Tax sales of real property
- § 34.21 — Right of redemption
- § 34.05 — Resale by taxing authority
- Other provisions on sale procedures and redemption rights

### The Sale Process

1. Taxing authority sues for delinquent taxes
2. Judgment obtained
3. Order of sale issued
4. Property advertised for auction
5. Public sale conducted
6. Deed issued to winning bidder
7. Redemption period begins (if applicable)

### Properties at Risk

In any Texas market, properties at risk of tax sale include:

1. Inherited properties where heirs don't coordinate taxes
2. Absentee owner properties forgotten over time
3. Properties where owner cannot act for themselves or is deceased
4. Properties with disputed ownership
5. Properties where owner is financially distressed
6. Fractured equity properties — the core focus of this Codex

## Why This Intersects with Fractured Equity

Fractured equity properties are disproportionately at risk of tax sale.

### Why Fractured Properties Don't Pay Taxes

**Coordination failure:** Multiple heirs each think the others will pay. Nobody pays.

**Financial inability:** Heirs collectively can't afford the tax burden they individually owe small shares of.

**Lack of awareness:** Heirs don't realize they own the property or the tax obligation.

**Dispute paralysis:** Heirs who are in conflict can't agree on anything, including taxes.

**Distance:** Heirs scattered geographically don't manage local obligations.

**Emotional disengagement:** Properties tied to painful family history get neglected.

### The Strategic Opportunity

For operators, this creates opportunity:

1. Property already identified as distressed
2. Owners often ready to let go if approached correctly
3. Intervention prevents total loss
4. Economics can be excellent
5. Ethical framing favorable

### The Ethical Framing

When you intervene on a property heading to tax sale:

- **Without your intervention:** Property lost to tax sale, heirs get nothing
- **With your intervention:** Heirs get something, property transfers cleanly, community preserves housing

This is fundamentally different from aggressive creditor actions. The bad outcome was already coming; you're offering a better alternative.

## Three Approaches to Tax Sale Intervention

Three distinct approaches exist, each appropriate for different situations.

### Approach 1: Pre-Sale Intervention

Identify properties heading to tax sale. Contact owners before sale. Acquire or coordinate to prevent sale.

**Mechanism:** You pay the taxes, or negotiate acquisition that includes tax resolution. Property transfers before taxing authority takes action.

**Timing:** Best approach. Owners still have options. Sale hasn't occurred. Clean acquisition possible.

**Outcome:** Property transfers to you. Heirs receive whatever you negotiate. No sale happens.

### Approach 2: Bidding at Tax Sale

When pre-sale approach hasn't worked, bid at the sale itself.

**Mechanism:** You attend tax sale auction. Bid for property. Win if bid is highest.

**Timing:** At the sale date. Property owners have exhausted pre-sale options.

**Outcome:** You acquire at auction price. Original owners may have redemption rights.

### Approach 3: Post-Sale Redemption

In some Texas tax sales, original owners have a redemption period. You can work with owners to redeem and coordinate ownership transfer.

**Mechanism:** After sale but within redemption period, you fund redemption in exchange for ownership interest or full transfer.

**Timing:** Between sale and expiration of redemption rights.

**Outcome:** Sale is undone. Property transfers per your agreement with owners.

## Approach 1 — Pre-Sale Intervention in Detail

Most common and often most productive approach.

### Finding Properties Heading to Tax Sale

Research sources:

1. County tax collector's delinquent tax lists (often public)
2. Advertised tax sale lists (published before sales)
3. Lawsuits filed for delinquent tax collection
4. Paid data services (TaxNetUSA, DataTree, others)
5. Public records showing multi-year delinquency

### Evaluating the Opportunity

For each property, assess:

1. Property ARV
2. Total tax liability (all years, plus penalties/interest/fees)
3. Ownership structure (fractured equity? single owner?)
4. Occupancy status
5. Distance to sale date
6. Other liens and encumbrances

### Contacting Owners

Standard cold outreach but with specific context:

> "Hi [name], my name is [your name] from [company]. I'm calling because I understand your family owns [property address], and the taxes are behind. A tax sale has been scheduled for [date]. I wanted to reach out to see if we could help find a solution that works for your family before that happens."

This framing:

1. Acknowledges the situation directly
2. Offers help rather than exploitation
3. Creates urgency without manipulation
4. Opens conversation rather than closing it

### Negotiating the Deal

Pre-sale deals typically structure as:

**Full acquisition:** You purchase the property (or fractional interests from heirs), pay off tax delinquency at closing, heirs receive agreed consideration.

**Partial acquisition with tax assumption:** You acquire fractional interest, agree to handle taxes going forward. Other owners remain but relieved of tax burden.

**Tax payment in exchange for interest:** You pay the tax delinquency. In exchange, you receive an ownership interest (typically proportional to what you paid).

Each structure has different economics and legal implications. Your attorney helps structure appropriately.

### Timing Considerations

1. Tax sales scheduled weeks in advance (public notice)
2. Sale can sometimes be postponed but not eliminated without payment
3. Faster you act, more flexibility in structuring
4. Eleventh-hour interventions are risky

### Documentation

1. Purchase agreement (or deed as applicable)
2. Tax payment proof
3. Recording promptly
4. Notice to tax collector of ownership change

---

## Approach 2 — Bidding at Tax Sale

When pre-sale doesn't work, the auction itself is the next intervention point.

### Preparation Before Sale

Research each property you might bid on:

1. Current ARV
2. Condition
3. Occupancy
4. Liens beyond taxes
5. Title complexity
6. Your bidding ceiling

### Due Diligence Limitations

Tax sales don't allow traditional inspections. You buy what you see from outside.

1. No interior inspection typically available
2. Limited access to property records
3. Unknown condition risks
4. Surprise occupants possible

### The Sale Procedure

1. Properties typically sold at county courthouse
2. First Tuesday of month in most Texas counties
3. Registration often required
4. Cash or cashier's check required
5. Properties announced one at a time
6. Minimum bid equals tax amount owed

### Bidding Strategy

1. Know your ceiling before the sale
2. Don't get emotional bidding
3. Walk away when bids exceed your ceiling
4. Have cash ready for immediate payment
5. Multiple properties possible — budget across them

### What You Receive

1. Sheriff's deed or tax deed
2. All rights of former owners (subject to redemption)
3. Title that needs curative work before retail sale
4. Property with unknown condition

### Post-Sale Actions

1. Record the deed immediately
2. Visit and secure property
3. Assess condition
4. Handle any occupants per eviction procedures
5. Wait through redemption period (if applicable)
6. Begin title clearing work

## Approach 3 — Post-Sale Redemption

After a tax sale, original owners sometimes have redemption rights. Understanding this creates opportunities.

### Texas Redemption Periods

Under Texas Tax Code § 34.21, redemption periods apply:

1. **Residential homestead, agricultural land:** 2-year redemption
2. **Other property:** 6-month (180 day) redemption
3. Starting from date of recorded sheriff's deed

### Redemption Amount

To redeem, original owners must pay:

1. Price paid at sale
2. Amount of taxes with interest and costs
3. Penalty (25% of sale price for first year, 50% for second year on residential)
4. Any expenses by purchaser (taxes paid, necessary maintenance, etc.)

### The Strategic Opportunity

When property sells at tax sale but original owners have redemption rights:

1. Original owners may want to redeem but can't afford to
2. Operators can partner with owners
3. Operator funds redemption in exchange for ownership
4. Sale is effectively undone
5. Cleaner title than tax sale deed would produce

### Structuring Redemption Deals

**Option A: Direct redemption, ownership transfers.** Owner redeems through you. You become owner. Owner receives agreed consideration.

**Option B: Fractional redemption interest.** Operator redeems in exchange for percentage interest. Original owners retain remaining interest.

**Option C: Redemption loan.** Operator lends redemption amount, secured by property. Owner keeps property if loan repaid.

Each structure has different legal implications. Attorney structuring essential.

### Timing Requirements

1. Must complete before redemption period expires
2. Earlier in period, more options
3. Last days of redemption can be rushed and risky

## The Economics of Tax Sale Work

Different from voluntary deals. Understanding the economics is critical.

### Pre-Sale Intervention Economics

**Example:** Property with $180,000 ARV. Tax delinquency of $22,000.

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Acquire interests from 4 heirs at $8,000 each | $32,000 |
| Pay delinquent taxes | $22,000 |
| Total acquisition | $54,000 |
| Rehab + carrying + selling | $25,000 |
| Sale at $180,000 | **Net $70,000+ profit** |

Pre-sale deals generally produce higher returns than tax sale bidding because you're not competing at auction and pay fair market to heirs who want the alternative to loss.

### Tax Sale Bidding Economics

**Example:** Same property sold at auction.

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Winning bid | $45,000 |
| Heir payments | $0 (owners lost everything at sale) |
| Title clearing costs | $3,000-$5,000 |
| Rehab + carrying + selling | $25,000-$30,000 |
| Sale at $180,000 | **Net $95,000-$100,000 profit** |

Higher potential profit but higher risk (condition, title, competition).

### Redemption Deal Economics

**Example:** Same property, you redeem after someone else wins auction at $45,000.

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Year 1 redemption | $45,000 + 25% = $56,250 |
| Your cost to redeem | $56,250 |
| Plus consideration to original owners | varies |
| Total cost | $60,000-$75,000 |
| Net profit on resale | $60,000-$80,000 |

### Which Approach Wins

Depends on:

1. How early you find the property
2. Your access to owners
3. Competition at auctions in your market
4. Your capital availability
5. Your risk tolerance

## Building a Tax Sale Pipeline

Operators who do this regularly build pipelines.

### Research Systems

1. Regular review of delinquent lists
2. Target criteria (value, location, ownership structure)
3. Filtering for fractured equity likelihood
4. Tracking properties through tax status changes

### Outreach Systems

1. Letter campaigns to delinquent property owners
2. Phone outreach to reachable owners
3. Field work (visiting properties, talking to neighbors)
4. Attorney referrals from probate and estate practices

### Sale Attendance

1. Regular attendance at county tax sales
2. Relationships with auction staff
3. Network of other regular bidders
4. Systematic bidding protocols

### Post-Sale Tracking

1. Properties that sold at recent auctions
2. Redemption period monitoring
3. Outreach to original owners
4. Building redemption deal opportunities

### Capital Requirements

Tax sale work requires liquid capital:

1. Cashier's checks for auctions
2. Ability to close fast on pre-sale deals
3. Funds for redemption deals within statutory windows
4. Pipeline requires standing capacity, not just deal-specific capital

## Legal Complications and How to Handle Them

Tax sale work has specific legal complications.

**Complication 1: Clouded Title Post-Sale**

Tax sale deeds don't convey clean title. Prior liens, encumbrances, and claims can persist.

**Response:** Quiet title action typically required before retail sale. Add 6-12 months and $3,000-$8,000 to timeline.

---

**Complication 2: IRS and Other Federal Liens**

Federal liens generally survive tax sales. IRS liens, student loan liens, federal judgment liens — these don't disappear with tax sale.

**Response:** Research federal liens before acquiring. Factor into acquisition cost. Sometimes federal liens make deals uneconomic.

---

**Complication 3: Redemption Surprises**

Original owners sometimes redeem unexpectedly, often through family help at the last moment.

**Response:** Budget for possible redemption return. Don't invest heavily in rehab during redemption period.

---

**Complication 4: Occupants on Property**

Tax sales don't remove occupants. Former owners may still be living in property.

**Response:** Eviction procedures apply. Budget for 30-60 day eviction process.

---

**Complication 5: Title Insurance Challenges**

Tax sale properties may be difficult to insure for retail sale.

**Response:** Specific title companies handle these. Build relationships. Expect higher costs.

---

**Complication 6: Prior Owner Legal Action**

Former owners sometimes sue to unwind tax sales, claiming procedural defects.

**Response:** Proper procedures at sale typically survive challenges. Attorney review important.

## The Ethical Dimensions

Tax sale work has specific ethical considerations distinct from other leverage tools.

### The Ethical Strength

Tax sale intervention often represents the ethically cleanest aggressive approach:

1. Bad outcome coming regardless (tax sale happening anyway)
2. Your intervention usually improves outcome for original owners
3. Community benefits from properties being maintained
4. Tax authority gets paid
5. You earn fair return for work and capital

### The Ethical Challenges

But concerns exist:

**Information asymmetry:** Owners often don't fully understand their tax situation. You know more about options than they do. How much do you educate them about alternatives?

**Time pressure:** Tax sales create urgency that can drive bad decisions. Are you creating urgency or responding to it?

**Vulnerable populations:** Property tax delinquency disproportionately affects elderly, financially distressed, grieving families. Are your methods appropriate for these populations?

**Pricing fairness:** What price do you pay? Minimum that gets deal done? Fair market? Something in between?

### Ethical Practice Principles

1. Fully inform owners about their tax situation and options
2. Pay prices that leave owners meaningful recovery
3. Don't create false urgency beyond the real deadlines
4. Respect owners' right to seek other alternatives
5. Document fair dealing in every transaction

### The "Better Alternative" Test

For each tax sale deal, ask: Is this outcome better for the owners than what would have happened without me?

Usually the answer is yes — they get something rather than nothing. But if you're paying so little that they barely recover more than the tax amount, the ethical case weakens.

Fair tax sale work creates outcomes where owners preserve meaningful value while you earn fair return. Extractive tax sale work takes most of the value while leaving owners near nothing. The first builds sustainable business. The second doesn't.

## Practical Example — A Complete Tax Sale Deal

### Finding the Opportunity

Research of Bexar County delinquent tax list identifies 3-bedroom home in Northwest San Antonio.

- ARV: $215,000
- 4 years delinquent taxes
- Total tax amount with penalties and fees: $28,500
- Scheduled for tax sale in 45 days

### Ownership Research

Property records show:

- Owner of record: Maria Alvarez (died 2019)
- 5 children as heirs at 1/5 each
- No probate opened
- Property has been unoccupied since death
- 3 children local (San Antonio), 2 living out of state

### Initial Contact

Oldest daughter, Sofia, agrees to meet. In that meeting, Sofia explains:

1. Family has been overwhelmed since mother's death
2. Nobody knew how to handle the property
3. Couldn't agree on what to do
4. Nobody could afford the taxes alone
5. They've received foreclosure notices but felt paralyzed

### Family Meeting

You arrange a call with all 5 siblings and explain the situation:

1. Tax sale scheduled in 45 days
2. Without intervention, family loses property entirely
3. Tax sale proceeds first pay taxes, then small amount might return to heirs
4. Alternative: You acquire property, pay off taxes, give each heir agreed amount

### Negotiating Terms

Initial offer:

- $12,000 to each heir ($60,000 total)
- You pay all taxes and fees ($28,500)
- Total cost to you: $88,500

Family counters at $15,000 each. You settle at $13,500 each ($67,500 total + $28,500 taxes = $96,000 total acquisition cost).

### Deal Structuring

With 45-day timeline, need fast execution:

1. AOH approach (no probate time)
2. All 5 heirs sign Release of Interest
3. Tax payment at closing to resolve delinquency
4. Title company confirms clear chain

### Execution Timeline

| Days | Action |
|---|---|
| Day 1-10 | Sign Release agreements with all 5 heirs |
| Day 10-20 | Title work (expedited) |
| Day 20-30 | AOH filed and recorded |
| Day 30-35 | Legal documents prepared |
| Day 35-40 | Closing with all heirs |
| Day 40 | Tax payment to taxing authority, sale cancelled |

### Closing Details

- All 5 heirs received $13,500 at closing
- Tax authority received $28,500 (full delinquency + sale costs)
- You received title to property
- Sale cancelled by taxing authority
- Total acquisition: $96,000

### Property Assessment and Rehab

- Property in decent condition but needs work
- Rehab budget: $22,000 (paint, flooring, kitchen refresh, minor repairs)
- Timeline: 3 months

### Resale

- Listed at $219,900 after rehab
- Multiple offers within 10 days
- Accepted offer: $215,000

### Final Numbers

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Revenue | $215,000 |
| Acquisition | $96,000 |
| Rehab | $22,000 |
| Holding costs (4 months) | $2,800 |
| Selling costs (6.5%) | $13,975 |
| **Total costs** | **$134,775** |
| **Net profit** | **$80,225** |

**Timeline:** Found Week 0. Acquired Week 6. Rehab complete Month 4. Sold Month 7. Total cycle: 7 months. Annualized return on capital excellent.

### Ethical Assessment

How does this transaction stand ethically?

1. Each heir received $13,500 ($67,500 total to family)
2. Without intervention: Tax sale would have netted them perhaps $0-$5,000 total after taxes
3. Family avoided losing property entirely
4. Each heir's alternative was nothing; their outcome was $13,500 each
5. Time pressure existed but was real (tax sale scheduled)
6. Full information provided to family

This represents appropriate tax sale intervention. The family improved their position significantly from the alternative. You earned fair return for capital, risk, and work. Everyone benefited from the transaction occurring.

## When Things Go Wrong

Tax sale work has its own failure modes.

**Failure Mode 1: Owner Finds Last-Minute Funds**

Family member, loan, or resource appears at last minute. Owner pays taxes and deal disappears.

**Response:** Accept it. Move to next deal. This is normal in tax sale work.

---

**Failure Mode 2: Property Condition Worse Than Expected**

You can't inspect before tax sale. Post-acquisition, property worse than appeared.

**Response:** Renegotiate pre-sale deals; absorb losses on tax sale acquisitions. Build condition contingencies into underwriting.

---

**Failure Mode 3: Surprise Liens**

Federal tax liens, IRS liens, or other priorities discovered after acquisition.

**Response:** Research thoroughly before bidding. On pre-sale deals, make liens part of due diligence.

---

**Failure Mode 4: Former Owner Litigation**

Former owners sue to unwind sale or acquisition.

**Response:** Proper procedures usually survive. Document fair dealing. Attorney handles litigation.

---

**Failure Mode 5: Auction Competition**

Tax sales attract professional bidders. Prices get bid up beyond your economics.

**Response:** Set ceiling in advance. Walk away. Pre-sale work offers better economics.

---

**Failure Mode 6: Redemption Takes Back Property**

You win at tax sale. During redemption period, original owner redeems.

**Response:** You receive redemption payment plus penalty. Net positive on your investment but didn't achieve property acquisition.

## Key Takeaways for Operators

Before moving to Chapter 28 (Ethical Boundaries), internalize these principles:

**Tax sale work is ethically distinct** from creditor-suit work. Usually prevents bad outcome rather than forcing one.

**Texas framework is Tax Code Chapters 33-34.** Specific procedures, timing, and rights govern.

**Fractured equity properties** are disproportionately at tax sale risk.

**Three approaches exist:** pre-sale intervention, auction bidding, post-sale redemption.

**Pre-sale intervention is usually best.** Earlier action = more flexibility = better economics = better outcomes for all parties.

**Tax sale bidding requires preparation.** Know property, know ceiling, have cash ready.

**Redemption periods create opportunities.** 2-year for residential/agricultural, 180 days for other.

**Legal complications include** clouded title, federal liens, redemption, occupants, title insurance, prior owner litigation.

**Economics vary by approach.** Pre-sale: fair return with lower risk. Auction: higher potential with higher risk.

**Building a pipeline** requires research systems, outreach systems, sale attendance, post-sale tracking, and standing capital.

**Ethical practice principles:** inform owners fully, pay fair prices, don't create false urgency, respect alternatives, document fair dealing.

**The "better alternative" test:** is this outcome better for owners than what would have happened without you?

**Failure modes include** last-minute payments, condition surprises, liens, litigation, auction competition, redemption.

**Tax sale work is a skill that rewards practice.** Regular operators outperform occasional ones.
