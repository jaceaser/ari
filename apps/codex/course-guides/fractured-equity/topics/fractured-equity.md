Fractured Equity is the legal acquisition of real estate through the pathways that exist when ownership is fractured, contested, unclear, or abandoned.

Every technique in this system flows from that sentence. When the ownership of a property is clean — one living owner, clear title, current on taxes, ready to sell — you don't need these tools. A wholesaler, a flipper, and a traditional buyer can all compete for that deal.

But when the ownership is fractured across twelve heirs scattered across three states, two of whom are dead and left children of their own, one of whom is in prison, one of whom is in Indonesia, and two of whom haven't spoken to each other in twenty years — that property sits. It sits for years. Sometimes decades. Nobody can figure out how to close it, so nobody tries.

These properties aren't hidden. They're on every tax delinquency list in every county in America. They're in every probate court. They're in the title chains of every small-town courthouse. The reason nobody is working them isn't that they're hard to find. It's that most operators don't know how to resolve the fractures.

## The Four Types of Fracture

A property can be fractured in four distinct ways:

**Ownership fracture** — The property is held by multiple people who each own a piece of it. A decedent dies without a will and leaves a house to four children. Each child owns 25% as a tenant in common. None of them agreed to be co-owners with each other, and none of them knows how to buy the others out.

**Title fracture** — The chain of recorded deeds has a break, a gap, a defect, or an ambiguity that prevents the current possessor from proving clean ownership. A deed from 1963 was never recorded. A grantor's name is misspelled across three instruments. An heir signed a deed without proper notarization.

**Possession fracture** — The person using the property isn't the same as the person who owns it on paper, or multiple people have partial rights to use the property simultaneously. A widow has a life estate. A tenant has a lease. Adverse possession claims cloud the picture.

**Payment obligation fracture** — The duty to maintain the property — pay taxes, pay the mortgage, pay for repairs — is spread across multiple parties, each of whom assumes the other is handling it. Nobody pays. The taxes go delinquent. The property deteriorates.

The deals that pay the biggest spreads have all four of these fractures in play.
