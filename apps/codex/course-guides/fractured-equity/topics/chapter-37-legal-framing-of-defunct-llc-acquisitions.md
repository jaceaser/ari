# Chapter 37: Legal Framing of Defunct LLC Acquisitions

*How Entities Go Defunct and How to Bring Them Back*

## 2.1 Why This Matters

Chapter 36 addressed how to find defunct LLCs that hold real property and how to find the people whose cooperation you need. This chapter addresses what "defunct" actually means in legal terms, the laws that put LLCs into that state, and the laws that allow you to bring them back out. Without this framework, you cannot evaluate what you are looking at, cannot speak sensibly to members and attorneys, and cannot structure a deal.

Texas is the illustrative jurisdiction throughout. Every other state has equivalent concepts under different names, different statute numbers, and different procedural details. The structure is the same in every state — states want their business entities to file reports and pay taxes; entities that do not get penalized through loss of rights; states provide a cleanup path back to good standing. But you cannot assume Texas procedures apply anywhere else. If your target is in another state, engage a real estate attorney and CPA licensed there and verify every specific before you act.

## 2.2 Why an LLC Goes Inactive

An LLC in Texas does not disappear because the members stopped caring about it. It stays on the Secretary of State's records. It keeps holding whatever property is titled in its name. What changes is the entity's legal status — from active and able to transact, to one of several inactive states that limit or suspend what the entity can legally do. Two mechanisms account for almost all inactive Texas LLCs.

### Franchise Tax Forfeiture

Texas imposes an annual franchise tax administered by the Texas Comptroller of Public Accounts. Every taxable entity must file an annual franchise tax report — even when no tax is actually owed, which is common for small LLCs whose revenue falls under the no-tax-due threshold. The filing obligation exists regardless of whether tax is owed. When an LLC fails to file for long enough, the Comptroller forfeits the entity's corporate privileges under **Tex. Tax Code § 171.251**. The forfeiture is certified to the Secretary of State and becomes public record.

Franchise tax forfeiture is the most common cause of defunct-LLC situations in Texas. Most of the opportunities you will find in this work are in this state.

### Administrative Termination by the Secretary of State

Separately, the Secretary of State can involuntarily terminate an entity for failure to meet non-tax filing obligations — primarily failure to maintain a registered agent and registered office, or failure to file certain required reports. This is less common than franchise tax forfeiture but does happen, particularly with out-of-state owners who let registered agent services lapse.

### Voluntary Termination Without Winding Up

Occasionally members file a certificate of termination on their own but never complete the proper winding-up steps — distributing assets, notifying creditors, transferring property out of the entity's name. The entity is technically terminated on paper while real property still sits in its name on the deed records. This produces a stranded-property situation similar to the forfeiture cases but with different legal nuances.

### Other Causes

A few other paths lead to inactive status — court-ordered dissolution, certain automatic dissolution events under an operating agreement, expiration of a stated period of duration in the certificate of formation. These are uncommon in real-world defunct-LLC acquisition work. The two main doors are franchise tax forfeiture and Secretary of State administrative termination.

## 2.3 What Happens Legally When an LLC Is Forfeited

Under **Tex. Tax Code § 171.252**, a Texas LLC in franchise tax forfeiture loses specific rights:

1. It cannot sue or defend in Texas courts
2. It loses its right to transact business in Texas
3. Officers, directors, managers, and members may become personally liable for debts the entity incurs while in forfeiture (**§ 171.255**)

In plain language: the LLC exists on paper but its legal hands are tied. It cannot sign a deed that produces clean title. It cannot defend an eviction. It cannot foreclose a lien. The property it owns is stuck because the entity that owns it cannot act on its behalf.

The personal liability exposure matters for the members — they have a real incentive to resolve the forfeiture, because every day the entity stays forfeited extends the window in which they could be personally on the hook for entity-related debts. This is often part of why members will cooperate with you when you approach them.

Administrative termination by the Secretary of State has similar consequences with its own statutory basis, and voluntary termination ends the entity's existence altogether subject to a post-termination survival period for claims. The practical result in every case is the same: the entity cannot execute valid transactions, and the property stays where it is until something changes.

## 2.4 Reinstatement — Bringing the Entity Back

Texas provides a mechanism to restore a forfeited LLC to good standing. This is called **reinstatement**. Reinstatement is the single most important statutory tool in most defunct-LLC acquisition work — because once the entity is reinstated, it can sign deeds, transfer property, and transact normally. The forfeiture problem is solved.

### Franchise Tax Reinstatement — Tex. Tax Code § 171.313

The principal reinstatement pathway. Requirements:

1. File all past-due franchise tax reports
2. Pay all past-due franchise taxes, penalties, and interest
3. Obtain a tax clearance letter from the Comptroller confirming the entity is in good standing
4. File an application for reinstatement with the Secretary of State along with the tax clearance letter
5. Pay the filing fees associated with reinstatement
6. Confirm or update the entity's registered agent and registered office

Once the Secretary of State processes the reinstatement, the entity is restored to active status and can transact again. Reinstatement under § 171.313 has no absolute time limit in most cases — entities can generally be reinstated years or even decades after forfeiture, provided the member or acquirer is willing to pay the accumulated back taxes, penalties, and interest.

### Secretary of State Reinstatement — BOC § 11.253

When the Secretary of State has administratively terminated an entity (rather than the Comptroller forfeiting it), a separate reinstatement pathway under **Texas Business Organizations Code § 11.253** applies. The requirements are analogous — cure the deficiency, pay fees, update filings — but the procedural track is different. Some entities have problems on both sides and must reinstate through both. Your attorney will identify which track or tracks apply in your specific case.

### Cost of Reinstatement

Franchise tax cleanup is usually the largest cost component. Ranges vary widely:

1. Small defunct LLCs with no-tax-due filings in most years: a few hundred to a few thousand dollars in back taxes, penalties, and fees
2. Mid-range cases: $3,000 to $15,000
3. Long-dormant entities with substantial actual tax owed: $15,000 to $75,000 or more
4. Attorney and CPA fees to manage the process: typically $3,000 to $10,000

Every case must be specifically diagnosed — have your CPA obtain a precise payoff amount from the Comptroller before committing to the acquisition structure. Reinstatement that looked affordable on an estimate sometimes proves uneconomic once the real number comes in.

### Timeline

A cooperative reinstatement typically takes 30 to 90 days from application to restored active status — longer if multiple reports must be prepared and filed, longer still if the Comptroller has questions about specific filings. Budget time accordingly. Nothing about this process is fast.

## 2.5 Other States Follow the Same Structure With Different Details

Every state has a version of this framework. The general pattern repeats:

1. States require their business entities to file annual or periodic reports and, in many states, to pay some form of entity-level tax
2. Entities that fail to comply lose rights through a forfeiture, suspension, or administrative dissolution mechanism
3. A reinstatement or revival pathway exists to restore the entity to good standing
4. Real property held by inactive entities cannot be conveyed cleanly until the entity is restored or is wound up in accordance with the state's procedures

The differences state to state are in the specifics. California calls it **suspension** and administers it through the Franchise Tax Board. Florida uses **administrative dissolution** by the Division of Corporations. Delaware uses a franchise tax and **certificate-of-revival** mechanism. New York has separate tracks for state tax issues and filing issues. The names change. The fees change. The exact procedural steps change. The underlying opportunity — a stranded entity with stranded property and a legal pathway back — exists in every state.

If you are working outside Texas, engage a real estate attorney, a CPA, and title professionals licensed in the state where the property sits. Have them walk you through the state's equivalent of what this chapter describes. Do not port Texas procedures into another state — the concepts travel, the statutes do not.

## 2.6 The Three Acquisition Pathways

Once you understand why an LLC is defunct and how reinstatement works, you can evaluate how to actually acquire the property. There are three primary pathways. Most deals use one of the three, sometimes blended. Your attorney will select and structure the specific path for your specific case.

### Pathway 1 — Reinstatement and Direct Sale

The entity is reinstated. Once active, it executes a deed conveying the property to you. Funds flow through a normal closing with the franchise tax payoff handled at the closing table, typically out of the purchase price. The entity may then be wound up and terminated if no other purpose remains. This is the cleanest pathway when members are cooperative, the franchise tax balance is manageable, and the operating agreement permits the sale with available member consent.

### Pathway 2 — Acquiring Membership Interests

Instead of buying the property from the entity, you buy the entity itself — acquiring all or controlling membership interests from the existing members. Once you control the entity, you direct the reinstatement and the subsequent operations. This pathway fits when the entity has value beyond the property (other assets, tax attributes, contracts), when the franchise tax burden makes straight reinstatement uneconomic but the entity wrapper is worth preserving, or when the operating agreement and member situation make interest transfer cleaner than property transfer.

### Pathway 3 — Winding Up and Distribution to Members

The entity is formally wound up under the Texas Business Organizations Code. Winding up distributes the real property from the entity to the members, who then hold it individually (as co-owners if multiple, sole owner if one). Once members hold the property in their individual names, they sell it to you in their individual capacities. The entity is then terminated. This pathway fits when the entity has no value beyond the property, when the franchise tax balance is too large for reinstatement to make economic sense, or when the operating agreement and member dynamics make working directly with members after distribution simpler than working through the entity.

### Selection Among Pathways

Which pathway fits is an attorney-and-CPA decision based on the specific facts — the franchise tax balance, the number and posture of members, the operating agreement terms, any other entity assets or liabilities, the title company's requirements, and your exit strategy. The operator's job is not to pick the pathway alone. The operator's job is to understand the three pathways well enough to have an informed conversation with counsel and structure the deal around the pathway counsel recommends.

## 2.7 What Can Stop a Deal

Several specific situations can derail an otherwise workable defunct-LLC acquisition. Operators should know these exist so they can spot them early.

### Bankruptcy

If the LLC, or any of its members in their individual capacities, has filed for federal bankruptcy protection (Chapter 7, Chapter 11, or Chapter 13), the **bankruptcy automatic stay** under **11 U.S.C. § 362** takes effect. Property in bankruptcy cannot be transferred without bankruptcy court permission. The entire acquisition pivots into a bankruptcy matter with its own rules, its own professionals, and its own timing. If bankruptcy is present, stop and consult bankruptcy counsel before doing anything else. A deal structured as if the bankruptcy did not exist will be unwound by the bankruptcy court and can expose the operator to serious consequences.

### Pending Litigation

A defunct LLC involved in active lawsuits — whether as plaintiff or defendant — is more complicated than a truly dormant one. The forfeiture status prevents the entity from prosecuting or defending most claims until reinstatement. The other parties to the litigation may have rights that affect the property. A full litigation search at the county and federal court level during your qualification process catches this; do not skip it.

### Operating Agreement Transfer Restrictions

Most operating agreements restrict the members' ability to transfer membership interests to outside parties — rights of first refusal, required consents from other members, buy-sell triggers. Before Pathway 2 can proceed, the operating agreement must be reviewed. When the operating agreement blocks the transfer outright and the blocking members will not consent, either the restrictions must be negotiated around, the operating agreement must be amended, or the deal pivots to Pathway 1 or Pathway 3.

### Unreachable or Uncooperative Members

Sometimes some of the members cannot be found, even after diligent Chapter 36 breadcrumbing. Sometimes members who can be found refuse to cooperate. These situations either pivot to judicial winding up (a lawsuit asking the court to direct the entity's resolution) or require deal restructuring to work around the blocking parties. Judicial options exist but add time and cost.

### Deceased Members

When a member has died, the member's interest passes through his estate to his heirs. This bridges the work into the Part VI tools of the main Codex — determination of heirship, working with representative capacities, binding unknown heirs. The defunct-LLC work and the heirship work must be coordinated. The operator with both skill sets can resolve situations that defeat operators with only one.

### Title Insurance Issues

Some title companies will not issue policies on properties acquired through defunct-entity cleanup, even after reinstatement. The underwriting concerns vary. Working with title companies that have experience with these transactions, and engaging them early in the deal process, is essential. If the title company will not ultimately issue a policy, the exit value of the property is substantially reduced — no clean resale is possible without insurable title.

## 2.8 Working With Counsel

Defunct-LLC acquisition is not solo work. Even experienced operators engage specialized counsel on every deal. The professionals you need:

1. A real estate attorney comfortable with entity cleanup transactions
2. A business entity attorney if the entity issues are complex (unclear operating agreements, multiple members in dispute, judicial winding up possibilities)
3. A CPA familiar with Texas franchise tax (or the equivalent in another state)
4. Title professionals who have handled defunct-entity transactions before
5. A bankruptcy attorney if any bankruptcy has been filed or is threatened
6. For deceased-member cases, a probate attorney to coordinate the estate work

The role of this chapter is to give you the orientation — the framework, the vocabulary, the concepts — that allows you to work effectively with these professionals. It does not give you the detailed legal implementation. That implementation is specific to each deal and must be executed by licensed counsel who know the facts. Your job as operator is to structure the deal, coordinate the professionals, fund the transaction, and close it. Your professionals' job is the specific legal execution.

## 2.9 Key Takeaways

**Defunct LLCs become defunct for specific statutory reasons.** Franchise tax forfeiture at the Comptroller (Tex. Tax Code § 171.251) is the most common. Administrative termination by the Secretary of State is the second. Voluntary termination without proper winding-up is a third. Other causes are uncommon.

**Forfeiture suspends the entity's ability to transact.** It cannot sue, defend, or execute binding deeds. Members and managers can face personal liability for debts incurred during forfeiture. The entity still exists, but its legal capacity is frozen.

**Reinstatement restores the entity.** Under Tex. Tax Code § 171.313 for tax-forfeited entities, or under Tex. Bus. Orgs. Code § 11.253 for administratively terminated entities. Past-due reports and tax balance must be paid, tax clearance obtained, and the Secretary of State filing completed.

**Every other state has the same structure with different details.** Engage local counsel in the state where your target property sits. Do not port Texas specifics.

**Three primary acquisition pathways.** Reinstate and sell. Acquire membership interests. Wind up and distribute to members, who then sell individually. Selection is an attorney-and-CPA decision.

**Bankruptcy stops everything.** If any bankruptcy has been filed involving the entity or its members, stop and consult bankruptcy counsel before proceeding.

**Operating agreement transfer restrictions matter.** Review the operating agreement before committing to a deal structure.

**Deceased members bridge to heirship work.** The Part VI tools of the main Codex apply to acquiring the interests of deceased members.

**Title insurance is the other critical gate.** Engage title professionals early. Without insurable title, your exit value is limited.

**Work with qualified counsel every time.** Real estate attorney, entity attorney, CPA, title professionals, and specialized counsel as needed. Defunct-LLC acquisition is a team exercise.
