## Using Structured Holdbacks to Close Deals Others Can't

Most operators think of escrow as something the title company handles automatically — funds come in, funds go out, done. That's closing escrow, and it happens on every deal.

But escrow as a strategy is something different entirely. It's the tool you use when:

1. A title issue can't be fully resolved before closing but you're confident it will be
2. An heir needs protection against other heirs backing out
3. You need time to verify something after closing without delaying the close
4. A creditor or taxing authority needs payment from proceeds after a waiting period
5. A contingent event (removal of a judgment, discharge of bankruptcy, release from a pending lawsuit) must happen before final disbursement

Strategic escrow lets you close deals that would otherwise fall apart. The property transfers. Funds flow. But specific amounts are held back under written agreement until specific events occur.

This chapter teaches you how to structure escrow deliberately. What types of escrow exist. What escrow agreements need to contain. How to negotiate escrow terms with title companies, heirs, and attorneys. When escrow is the right answer and when it's the wrong answer. The common escrow traps and how to avoid them.

An operator who can't use escrow strategically closes only the clean deals. An operator who can use escrow strategically closes deals nobody else can.

---

## 20.2 The Three Categories of Escrow in Real Estate Deals

Not all escrow is the same. Three distinct categories exist:

### Category 1: Closing Escrow (Routine)

The title company or attorney holds funds during the closing process itself. Funds come in from buyer before closing, get disbursed at closing per the closing statement.

This is automatic on every deal. Not strategic.

### Category 2: Earnest Money Escrow (Contract Protection)

Buyer's good-faith deposit held by a neutral party during the contract period. Refundable if deal fails due to specific conditions; forfeitable if buyer defaults.

Standard practice. Sometimes strategic in fractured equity deals — larger deposits show commitment to skeptical heirs.

### Category 3: Post-Closing Escrow / Holdback Escrow (Strategic)

Funds retained after closing, held in escrow, released upon specific conditions being met.

This is the strategic escrow that this chapter focuses on. It's the tool that makes difficult deals possible.

**The Mechanics of Post-Closing Escrow**

1. Deal closes
2. Property transfers to buyer
3. A portion of purchase price (the "holdback") is retained in escrow account
4. Written escrow agreement defines conditions for release
5. When conditions are met, escrow agent releases funds
6. If conditions aren't met, funds return per the agreement

This is fundamentally different from closing escrow because the closing has already happened but full payment is still pending.

## 20.3 When Escrow Is the Right Tool

Specific situations where strategic escrow solves problems:

### Situation 1: The Unreleased Lien That Will Be Released

A lien appears on title commitment. The lienholder has agreed to release it — payoff negotiated, lawsuit settled, judgment satisfied. The release hasn't been recorded yet.

Options:
1. Wait for the release (delays closing, risks losing deal momentum)
2. Close without addressing (title company won't insure)
3. Close with holdback for the lien amount + 20% buffer, release upon recording of lien release

The holdback approach lets you close on schedule while protecting against the risk that the release doesn't actually happen.

### Situation 2: The Pending Judgment

A lawsuit against an heir is pending. Judgment could become a lien on their interest. You want to close before the judgment attaches.

Options:
1. Wait for judgment resolution (could take years)
2. Close without addressing (title exception for the pending litigation)
3. Close with holdback for potential judgment amount, release upon case dismissal or final disposition

### Situation 3: The Tax Lien That's Being Negotiated

IRS or state tax lien against an heir. Heir's attorney is negotiating payoff. Final amount not yet confirmed.

Options:
1. Close after negotiation complete (unknown timeline)
2. Close with estimate of payoff (risk of coming up short)
3. Close with holdback exceeding highest probable payoff, release balance after actual payoff recorded

### Situation 4: The Creditor Claim Period for Estate Sales

Texas Estates Code establishes creditor claim periods for estates. Closing before the period expires carries risk that creditors will later assert claims affecting the property.

Options:
1. Wait until period expires (delays 4-6 months)
2. Close and hope no creditors emerge (title underwriter often won't accept)
3. Close with holdback for potential creditor claims, release upon expiration of claim period

### Situation 5: The Missing Heir Who May or May Not Have Interest

A potential heir has been identified but can't be located. Their interest is uncertain. You're closing with the heirs who signed.

Options:
1. Judicial proceeding to adjudicate missing heir's interest (JDH, cure quiet title)
2. Close with title exception for missing heir's interest (undesirable)
3. Close with holdback for missing heir's potential interest, release after curative work or statute of limitations

### Situation 6: The Condition of Title Affecting Value

A title defect exists that affects property value but can be cured over time. Buyer wants to close but wants assurance that cure happens.

Options:
1. Require seller to cure before closing (delays deal)
2. Close and accept the defect (buyer takes on curative work)
3. Close with holdback for curative costs + buffer, release upon completion of cure

### Situation 7: The Repair or Condition Escrow

Property needs repair that will be completed post-closing by seller. Buyer wants protection that repairs actually happen.

Options:
1. Require repairs before closing (delays, seller may not cooperate)
2. Close with credit for repairs (buyer takes full responsibility)
3. Close with holdback for repair amount, release upon verified completion

In each situation, escrow lets the deal close now while protecting against the specific risk.

## 20.4 When Escrow Is the Wrong Tool

Escrow isn't always the answer. Sometimes it's a crutch that masks a bigger problem.

**The fundamental title failure:** The title isn't going to be curable. An escrow holdback doesn't make a bad title good. It just delays the loss.

**The uncooperative counterparty:** If the heir or administrator won't engage with straightforward issues, an escrow structure that requires future cooperation is high-risk. They won't cooperate post-closing either.

**The undefined condition:** If the condition for release can't be clearly defined, the escrow is a dispute waiting to happen. "Released when situation is resolved" is useless — you need specific, verifiable triggers.

**The inadequate buffer:** If the holdback amount doesn't actually cover the potential loss, you're still exposed. Under-escrowing is often worse than not escrowing at all because it creates false confidence.

**The hostile attorney:** Opposing counsel fighting every escrow term will create a document so convoluted that disputes become inevitable. If you can't get to reasonable agreement, the deal isn't ready.

**The short-term fix for a long-term problem:** Some problems need actual resolution, not delay. If the real answer is "litigate the judgment now" or "probate the estate properly," escrow is just kicking the can.

## 20.5 The Escrow Agreement — Essential Components

Every strategic escrow requires a written escrow agreement. Not a handshake. Not an understanding. A written agreement signed by all parties, held by the escrow agent.

### Component 1: Parties Identified

1. **Depositor** — the party whose funds are held
2. **Beneficiary** — the party who could ultimately receive funds
3. **Escrow agent** — the neutral third party holding funds

All three must be clearly identified with full legal names and addresses.

### Component 2: Funds Identified

1. Specific amount held in escrow
2. Specific source (usually closing proceeds)
3. Account where funds are held
4. Interest treatment (who earns any interest on held funds)

### Component 3: Conditions for Release

This is the most critical section. Must specify:

1. Exact conditions that trigger release to beneficiary
2. Exact conditions that trigger return to depositor
3. Who provides proof of conditions being met
4. What form of proof is acceptable
5. Timeline for each condition

Vague language here causes disputes. Specific language prevents them.

### Component 4: Escrow Agent Duties

1. What the agent must do upon receiving proof
2. Notice requirements before release
3. Dispute resolution procedure
4. Limitations on agent liability

### Component 5: Expiration Date

Every escrow needs an expiration. What happens if conditions are never met?

1. Default release to one party (usually depositor)
2. Return to all parties for disposition
3. Extension mechanism if parties agree

Open-ended escrow creates endless disputes. Always include expiration.

### Component 6: Dispute Resolution

If parties disagree about release conditions:

1. Who decides?
2. What process?
3. Who pays for dispute resolution?
4. Can the agent interplead funds into court if dispute?

### Component 7: Amendment Provisions

How can the agreement be modified? Usually requires written consent of all parties.

### Component 8: Notice Provisions

How do parties notify each other and the agent? Addresses, methods, timing.

### Component 9: Governing Law

Which state's law applies. For Texas deals, typically Texas law.

### Component 10: Agent Compensation

How much the agent charges. Who pays. When paid.

## 20.6 Selecting the Escrow Agent

Who holds the money matters.

### Option 1: Title Company

Most common choice.

**Advantages:**
1. Already involved in the transaction
2. Familiar with real estate escrow
3. Bonded and insured
4. Regulated

**Disadvantages:**
1. May not want complicated escrow arrangements
2. Standard agreements may not fit unusual situations
3. Fees vary by company

### Option 2: Attorney Trust Account

Attorney holds funds in IOLTA or client trust account.

**Advantages:**
1. Flexible for unusual arrangements
2. Attorney can draft tailored escrow agreement
3. Professional judgment on release decisions

**Disadvantages:**
1. Attorney must not represent either party (creates conflict)
2. Fees can be higher
3. Some attorneys won't hold escrow due to liability

### Option 3: Bank Escrow Services

Commercial banks offer escrow services for business transactions.

**Advantages:**
1. Institutional stability
2. Clear separation from parties
3. Interest-bearing accounts available

**Disadvantages:**
1. More expensive
2. Less real estate-specific experience
3. May require significant minimum amounts

### Option 4: Independent Escrow Company

Specialized escrow companies exist in some markets.

**Advantages:**
1. Focused expertise
2. Neutral by design
3. Familiar with complex structures

**Disadvantages:**
1. Less common in some areas
2. Additional vendor to manage
3. Fees vary

### What to Evaluate When Selecting

1. Experience with complex escrow
2. Willingness to take on the specific arrangement
3. Fee structure
4. Insurance and bonding
5. References
6. Responsiveness and communication

For fractured equity deals with typical holdback amounts, title company escrow handles most situations. For unusual arrangements, attorney trust or independent escrow may be better.

## 20.7 Structuring the Holdback Amount

How much to hold back requires calculation, not guessing.

**Formula: Maximum Potential Loss + Safety Buffer.**

### Step 1: Identify Maximum Potential Loss

1. Lien amount including interest and penalties
2. Tax payoff including accrued amounts
3. Judgment amount including costs
4. Creditor claim potential
5. Repair cost with contingency
6. Whatever the specific risk is

### Step 2: Add Safety Buffer

Typically 15-25% over maximum potential loss. Buffer protects against:

1. Interest accruing during escrow period
2. Underestimation of maximum amount
3. Related costs (attorney fees to resolve, etc.)
4. Unknown developments

### Step 3: Consider Tax Implications

In some cases, the escrow amount affects tax treatment. Consult your tax advisor for large escrows.

### Step 4: Document the Calculation

The escrow agreement should reflect why the holdback is the amount it is. If disputed later, your calculation supports the structure.

### Example 1: Lien Holdback

Lien amount: $45,000. Per-diem interest: $15 per day. Expected resolution: 90 days.

| Component | Amount |
|---|---|
| Principal | $45,000 |
| Accrued interest (90 days × $15) | $1,350 |
| Subtotal | $46,350 |
| Buffer (20%) | $9,270 |
| **Holdback amount** | **$55,620** |

### Example 2: Potential Judgment Holdback

Lawsuit against heir seeks $125,000. Heir's attorney believes exposure is $50,000-$75,000. Expected resolution: 12 months.

**Using maximum claim:**

| Component | Amount |
|---|---|
| Maximum claim | $125,000 |
| Buffer (15%) | $18,750 |
| **Holdback amount** | **$143,750** |

**Using probable outcome:**

| Component | Amount |
|---|---|
| Probable exposure | $75,000 |
| Buffer (25%) | $18,750 |
| **Holdback amount** | **$93,750** |

Decide which approach matches your risk tolerance.

### Example 3: Missing Heir Holdback

Missing heir's potential interest: 1/6 of property. Property purchased for $180,000 from cooperating heirs (representing 5/6 interest).

| Component | Amount |
|---|---|
| Missing heir's proportional value (1/6 of $216,000) | $36,000 |
| Buffer (25%) | $9,000 |
| **Holdback amount** | **$45,000** |

## 20.8 The Escrow Release Mechanism — Making It Work

How funds actually get released when conditions are met:

### Trigger Documents

Escrow agreements specify what proof triggers release. Common triggers:

- **Recorded release of lien:** Copy of release showing recording information
- **Final judgment:** Certified copy showing case disposition
- **Satisfaction of judgment:** Recorded satisfaction
- **Expiration of creditor claim period:** Attorney certification
- **Recorded affidavit:** Specific affidavit about missing heir or other issue
- **Completion certificate:** For repairs or condition-based escrows
- **Tax payoff confirmation:** Letter from taxing authority

### The Release Request Process

1. Party entitled to release submits written request to escrow agent
2. Request includes required trigger documents
3. Agent reviews documents for compliance with agreement
4. Agent notifies other party of release request
5. Other party has notice period to object (typically 10-15 days)
6. If no objection, agent releases funds
7. If objection, dispute resolution procedures activate

### What Can Go Wrong

1. Trigger document doesn't quite match agreement requirements
2. Agent requires additional documentation
3. Other party objects on procedural grounds
4. Agent is slow to process
5. Documents get lost

### Mitigation

1. Over-document everything
2. Build in reasonable agent response times
3. Copy all parties on all communications
4. Use trackable delivery (certified mail, electronic with receipt)
5. Follow up in writing if delays occur

## 20.9 Dispute Resolution Within Escrow

Even with clear agreements, disputes happen. The agreement should address how:

### Step 1: Negotiation Between Parties

Agent may suspend release process pending party negotiation. Typical period: 30-60 days.

### Step 2: Mediation

Agreement may require mediation before formal dispute. Neutral mediator helps parties reach agreement.

### Step 3: Arbitration or Interpleader

If negotiation/mediation fails:

**Arbitration:** Neutral arbitrator decides. Agreement specifies rules (typically AAA or similar).

**Interpleader:** Agent deposits funds with court and withdraws. Court decides release. Parties pay court costs.

### Step 4: Litigation

As a last resort, parties can sue. Expensive, slow, but sometimes necessary.

### Agent's Protection During Disputes

Well-drafted escrow agreements protect the agent during disputes:

1. No obligation to resolve factual disputes
2. Right to interplead when parties disagree
3. Indemnification by parties
4. Recovery of legal fees if dragged into litigation

Agents won't take on escrows without these protections. Don't fight them.

## 20.10 The Heir Payout Escrow — Protecting Multi-Heir Deals

A specific escrow structure deserves its own section: heir payout escrow.

### The Problem

You're closing a multi-heir deal. Some heirs have signed Releases; others are in process. The first heir to sign receives payment but the deal isn't complete until all heirs signed. What if later heirs don't sign?

### Standard Approach (Risky)

Pay each heir as they sign. You're spending capital before having complete title. If you can't close with all heirs, you may own partial interests at full prices.

### Escrow Approach (Protected)

Each heir's payout goes into escrow upon signing their Release. Escrow holds the payment until the master closing completes.

### Escrow Agreement Terms

1. Heir's interest transfers to you upon signing Release
2. Payment to heir is held in escrow
3. Upon master closing (all heirs signed, title clear), escrow releases payment to heir
4. If master closing doesn't happen by deadline, heir has right to rescind Release and recover interest in exchange for escrow return

### Benefits

1. **Heir is protected:** They get paid when deal actually closes
2. **You're protected:** You don't commit capital before title is complete
3. **Deal is protected:** If it can't close, it unwinds cleanly
4. **Creates incentive:** All heirs have interest in deal completing

### Heir Acceptance

Some heirs prefer immediate payment. Others prefer escrow if explained properly:

> "Look, if one of your siblings backs out, neither of us wants you holding money you'd have to return. Let's put it in escrow at [title company]. When everyone signs and the deal closes, you get paid. If somehow it falls apart, you get your interest back and get your money back. Nobody loses."

Most heirs agree when they understand the structure. The ones who don't agree are often the ones who were going to cause problems anyway.

## 20.11 The Construction/Repair Escrow

When property needs repair that will be completed post-closing, escrow structures this cleanly.

### Who Does the Repair

1. Seller (less common in fractured equity)
2. Buyer (standard for wholesalers and flippers)
3. Third-party contractor (sometimes used)

### Escrow for Seller-Completed Repairs

1. Holdback for repair cost + contingency
2. Release upon verified completion
3. Conditions for completion verified by independent inspection
4. Partial release for partial completion
5. Default to buyer if not completed by deadline

### Escrow for Buyer-Completed Repairs (with Seller Providing Credit)

Less common structure. Seller provides credit for known repairs. Escrow holds the credit pending buyer's confirmation of use.

### Most Fractured Equity Deals

Don't use construction escrow. Buyer takes property as-is, handles repairs themselves with their own funds. The seller isn't involved in repairs.

But for specific situations — property has code violations that must be cured, HOA requires compliance work — construction escrow may apply.

## 20.12 The Tax/Creditor Escrow

For estate deals, tax and creditor escrows are common.

### The Tax Escrow

Property has significant delinquent taxes. Final payoff amount includes interest and penalties that accrue until payment. Title company estimates payoff at closing but may be off.

**Structure:**

1. Holdback for estimated payoff + buffer (typically 10%)
2. At closing, funds wired to tax authority for actual payoff
3. Any excess held in escrow
4. Within 30 days, taxing authority confirms actual payoff
5. Excess escrow returns to seller or to closing proceeds

This protects against underpayment while not requiring massive buffers.

### The Creditor Escrow (Estate Deals)

Estate is within creditor claim period. Administrator is required to allow creditors opportunity to file claims. Claims could affect available distribution.

**Structure:**

1. Holdback equal to estimated maximum creditor claims + buffer
2. Period runs for statutory claim period (typically 4 months from notice to creditors)
3. Legitimate claims paid from escrow
4. Remaining funds distributed to heirs/beneficiaries

This lets estate sales happen before claim period ends while protecting creditor rights.

## 20.13 Interest on Escrow Funds

When funds sit in escrow, they sometimes earn interest. Who gets it matters.

**Non-interest-bearing:** Common for short-term escrows. Funds in non-interest accounts. Nobody earns anything.

**Interest to depositor:** Funds in interest-bearing account. Depositor entitled to accrued interest. Standard for longer-term escrows.

**Interest to beneficiary:** Funds in interest-bearing account. Beneficiary entitled to accrued interest. Less common, typically negotiated.

**Interest shared:** Specific split in escrow agreement. Can be based on outcome.

**Interest to agent:** Attorney IOLTA accounts. Interest goes to state bar legal services programs. Common for attorney-held funds.

For fractured equity deals with holdbacks of 30 days to 12 months, interest often isn't significant. For larger, longer holdbacks, specify treatment clearly.

**Tax Consequences:** Interest earned in escrow is typically taxable to whoever has rights to it. The escrow agreement and 1099 reporting need to align. Consult your tax advisor for significant amounts.

## 20.14 Escrow Fees and Costs

What escrow costs varies by provider and structure.

| Provider | Typical Fee Range |
|---|---|
| Title company closing escrow | $250-$1,500 |
| Title company post-closing holdback | $300-$1,500 additional |
| Attorney trust account | $500-$2,500 (part of hourly) |
| Bank escrow | $500-$3,000 setup + monthly fees |
| Independent escrow company | $500-$5,000 |

**Factors affecting cost:**
1. Complexity of release conditions
2. Length of escrow period
3. Amount held (some base fees on amount)
4. Number of expected transactions

**Who Pays:** Usually buyer, but negotiable. Sometimes shared with seller. Include in closing statement.

**When Fees Are Paid:** Options are at closing from proceeds, periodically during escrow period, or upon release. Specify in escrow agreement.

## 20.15 Common Escrow Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Over many deals, certain escrow mistakes recur.

### Mistake 1: Vague Release Conditions

"Released upon satisfactory resolution" means nothing. Releases require specific, verifiable triggers.

**Fix:** "Released upon recording of Release of Lien in Bexar County real property records, executed by [Lienholder] and referencing Volume [X], Page [Y]."

### Mistake 2: Inadequate Holdback Amount

Holdback is less than the actual potential loss. Funds insufficient when triggered.

**Fix:** Calculate maximum potential loss plus meaningful buffer. Err on the side of too much rather than too little.

### Mistake 3: No Expiration

Escrow runs indefinitely. Funds locked up forever if conditions never met.

**Fix:** Always include expiration date with default disposition.

### Mistake 4: No Dispute Resolution

When parties disagree, no mechanism to resolve.

**Fix:** Include dispute resolution procedure. Interpleader option for agent.

### Mistake 5: Unrealistic Timeline

Conditions can't be met in timeframe. Default triggers before cure possible.

**Fix:** Research realistic timelines before setting. Include extension mechanism.

### Mistake 6: Agent Won't Accept the Agreement

Draft agreement in isolation, then discover agent won't take it.

**Fix:** Involve agent in drafting or at least in review before signing. Get agent's acceptance in writing.

### Mistake 7: Insufficient Documentation

Trigger documents don't actually match what's required.

**Fix:** Specify exactly what documents trigger release. Provide templates if possible.

### Mistake 8: Party Dies or Becomes Incapacitated

Heir or other party can't participate in release. Escrow stuck.

**Fix:** Include provisions for successor parties (estates, guardians, etc.).

### Mistake 9: Tax Consequences Not Addressed

IRS treats escrow funds in specific ways. Unaddressed issues cause tax problems.

**Fix:** Consult tax advisor for significant escrows. Address 1099 reporting.

### Mistake 10: No Mechanism to Amend

Circumstances change. Agreement can't adapt.

**Fix:** Include amendment provision requiring written consent of all parties.

## 20.16 Practical Example — A Complete Escrow Structure

Let me walk through a complete escrow deal.

### The Deal

Four-heir intestate estate. Three heirs cooperative, fourth heir (brother living out of state) estranged and difficult to locate. AOH approach used. Fourth heir's signature isn't required for AOH but his interest is technically unresolved.

### The Concern

Fourth heir could later surface and assert interest. While Texas AOH procedure and statutory limitations offer significant protection, buyer wants additional comfort.

### The Structure — Purchase Terms

1. Total purchase price from three cooperating heirs: $240,000 (representing ~75% interest, 3 of 4 heirs at equal shares)
2. Heir payouts: $80,000 each to the three cooperating heirs
3. Buyer's risk exposure: fourth heir's potential 25% interest = $80,000

### The Escrow

Additional $40,000 held in escrow (representing partial protection against fourth heir's potential interest).

### Escrow Agreement Terms

**Parties:** Buyer (operator), Cooperating Heirs (3 named), Escrow Agent (title company)

**Funds:** $40,000 deposited at closing from buyer's proceeds

**Release conditions:**

1. **Primary:** Upon four years elapsing from recording date of AOH without challenge by missing heir (statute of limitations approach)
2. **Alternative:** Upon obtaining signed Release from missing heir (if located)
3. **Alternative:** Upon quiet title action or JDH establishing no interest in missing heir

**Release to whom:** Cooperating Heirs, divided equally

**Default disposition:** If challenge occurs and fourth heir's interest is recognized, escrow funds pay fourth heir's interest (subject to additional funds required if escrow insufficient)

**Expiration:** Five years from closing

**Dispute resolution:** Interpleader to Bexar County District Court

**Agent fees:** $600 at setup, $50 per month maintenance, paid by buyer

### What This Achieves

1. Deal closes now
2. Three cooperating heirs get immediate payment ($80,000 each)
3. Buyer has property and can pursue resale or rehab
4. Additional protection against fourth heir's potential claim
5. Clear path to cooperating heirs eventually receiving additional $40,000 (divided three ways = $13,333 each) if no challenge occurs
6. Clear path to handling challenge if it occurs

### Alternative if Challenge Occurs

Fourth heir surfaces at year three. Claims full 25% interest. Your options:

1. Pay escrow funds plus additional amount to acquire his interest
2. Litigate validity of his claim
3. Return escrow to settlement fund and litigate

The escrow provides structure and partial funding for whatever response is needed.

### Five Years Later, if No Challenge

AOH aged beyond statutory challenge period. Four-year limitations typically run. Escrow releases $40,000 to cooperating heirs ($13,333 each additional distribution).

### Real-World Outcome

Most missing-heir situations don't result in challenges. Statute of limitations runs. Escrow releases. Everyone's protected through the structure but nobody's funds are lost.

## 20.17 Key Takeaways for Operators

**Escrow is a strategic tool, not just closing mechanics.** Used properly, it closes deals that would otherwise fall apart.

**Three categories exist:** closing escrow (routine), earnest money (contract protection), and post-closing holdback (strategic). This chapter is about #3.

**Know when escrow is the right tool.** Unreleased liens, pending judgments, creditor claims, missing heirs, curable title defects — these are escrow situations.

**Know when escrow is the wrong tool.** Fundamental title failures, uncooperative counterparties, undefined conditions, inadequate buffers — escrow doesn't fix these.

**The escrow agreement has 10 essential components.** Parties, funds, release conditions, agent duties, expiration, dispute resolution, amendment provisions, notices, governing law, agent compensation.

**Select the agent carefully.** Title company, attorney trust, bank escrow, or independent escrow company. Each has advantages for different situations.

**Calculate the holdback deliberately.** Maximum potential loss plus meaningful buffer. Document the calculation.

**Release mechanisms need specific trigger documents.** Vague triggers cause disputes. Specific triggers enable clean release.

**Build dispute resolution into every agreement.** Interpleader is the agent's escape valve. Arbitration or mediation for parties.

**Heir payout escrow protects multi-heir deals.** Funds sit in escrow until master closing. Heirs and buyer both protected.

**Construction escrow is rare in fractured equity.** Most operators take property as-is without repair escrows.

**Tax/creditor escrows handle estate deal timing.** Let estate sales happen before full resolution of claim periods.

**Address interest treatment, fees, and tax consequences.** Small details that matter.

**Common mistakes are predictable.** Vague conditions, inadequate amounts, no expiration, no dispute mechanism. Avoid them.

**Escrow expands the universe of closeable deals.** Operators who master escrow close deals others can't.
