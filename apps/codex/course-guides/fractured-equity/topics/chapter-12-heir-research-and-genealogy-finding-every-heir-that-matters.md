## 12.1 Why Heir Research Is the Hidden Skill

Most real estate investors don't know how to do genealogical research. It's not a standard skill in the industry. Wholesalers don't need it. Flippers don't need it. Buy-and-hold operators don't need it.

In Fractured Equity, heir research is the skill that separates operators who close deals from operators who identify deals that fall apart.

Here's why: every deal that involves a decedent requires you to know who the legal heirs are. Not just the ones the family tells you about — all of them. Missing a single legal heir means the deal will fail title review, or worse, you close and an unidentified heir surfaces months later demanding a share of the proceeds.

Most families don't know their own full heir structure. They assume the surviving spouse inherited everything. They forget that Grandpa had a child from his first marriage in 1952. They don't realize that their deceased aunt's kids are legal heirs because their aunt held a share she never conveyed. The operator who does proper research catches these details. The operator who doesn't gets blindsided.

This chapter teaches you the full heir research methodology: how to build a family tree, where to find the records, how to identify outside children, how to locate missing heirs, and how to document your research well enough that a title underwriter will rely on it.

---

## 12.2 What You're Trying to Build

For every decedent in your deal, you need to construct a complete heir tree. Not just names — full identification sufficient to contact each person and document their heirship for title purposes.

**For Each Heir, You Need**

1. Full legal name (including any known variations, maiden names, previous married names)
2. Date of birth (approximate is often sufficient)
3. Current address (and historical addresses if helpful)
4. Phone numbers (current and past)
5. Email addresses if available
6. Relationship to the decedent (documented, not just claimed)
7. Relationship to any intermediate deceased heirs (e.g., "grandchild of decedent through predeceased son John")
8. Date of death if the heir themselves is deceased (which creates a new sub-tree)

**For Each Intermediate Relationship, You Need Documentation**

1. Marriage certificates
2. Birth certificates or equivalent proof of parentage
3. Adoption decrees if applicable
4. Divorce decrees (which may affect spousal status at death)
5. Death certificates for deceased heirs
6. Evidence of outside children (acknowledgment of paternity, DNA evidence, court-established parentage)

This is substantially more than what most operators build. Most operators settle for "a list of names and phone numbers." That level of research will fail when the title underwriter asks for documentation, when an attorney ad litem investigates, or when a disputed heir surfaces and challenges the deal.

Build the research to the standard that survives scrutiny, and you build a business that closes deals. Build it to a lower standard, and you build a business that almost-closes deals.

## 12.3 The Core Research Workflow

Heir research follows a repeatable sequence. Here's the workflow applied to any new decedent:

**Step 1: Confirm the Decedent**

1. Full legal name
2. Date of birth
3. Date of death
4. Location of death (county)
5. Place of residence at death
6. Whether they left a will

Sources: Tax rolls, obituary, death certificate, probate filings if any.

**Step 2: Confirm Marital History**

1. All marriages (in order)
2. Dates and locations of marriages
3. Dates and locations of divorces (if any)
4. Whether any marriages ended only by death of the spouse
5. Identity of surviving spouse (if any)

Sources: Marriage records (county clerk), divorce records (district court), obituaries, family interviews.

**Step 3: Identify All Children**

1. Children from the current (or most recent) marriage
2. Children from any prior marriages
3. Children from non-marital relationships (outside children)
4. Adopted children
5. Stepchildren (who are NOT legal heirs unless adopted — but verify adoption status)
6. Deceased children (who create sub-trees of their own descendants)

Sources: Birth records, obituaries, census records, family interviews, genealogical databases.

**Step 4: For Each Deceased Child, Repeat the Process**

1. Identify the deceased child's spouse(s) at death
2. Identify the deceased child's children
3. Track any further deaths that create additional sub-trees

**Step 5: Handle Collateral Heirs If Relevant**

If the decedent died without children and without a surviving spouse (or died with separate property passing under Rule E from Chapter 6), collateral heirs matter:

1. Parents (if still alive)
2. Siblings
3. Children of deceased siblings (nieces and nephews)
4. More distant kindred if no closer relatives survive

**Step 6: Verify Every Relationship with Documentation**

Don't trust oral representations. Pull the records. Document the sources.

**Step 7: Locate Current Contact Information for Each Living Heir**

Skip tracing, address verification, phone research.

**Step 8: Document the Full Tree**

Visual family tree. List of heirs with fractional shares. Source citations.

## 12.4 Source Materials — Where the Records Actually Live

To build a complete heir tree, you'll pull records from many sources. Here's the full catalog.

**Vital Records (Births, Deaths, Marriages, Divorces)**

1. Texas Department of State Health Services (DSHS) for Texas records
2. State vital records offices for records in other states
3. County clerks for marriage records, locally
4. District clerks for divorce decrees (in Texas, divorces are typically in district courts)

Access varies by state and type of record. Some are freely available online. Others require requests and fees. Birth records within the past 75 years and death records within the past 25 years are typically restricted in Texas (privacy protections).

**Probate Records**

1. County probate courts for wills, administrations, and related filings
2. Some counties have online access; others require in-person research

**Property Records**

1. County clerk's office for recorded deeds, liens, plats, and other real estate documents
2. County appraisal district for current property tax information
3. Many counties have online search tools

**Obituaries**

1. Local newspaper websites
2. Legacy.com (aggregator of obituaries from many newspapers)
3. Funeral home websites
4. Ancestry.com's historical newspaper collection

Obituaries are the single richest source for family identification. A good obituary will list the decedent's surviving spouse, children (often including children from prior marriages), grandchildren, parents, siblings, and sometimes predeceased relatives. Start every heir research with an obituary search.

**Genealogy Databases**

**Ancestry.com** — the largest genealogical database. Census records, vital records, public records, family trees built by other users.

**FamilySearch.org** — free service run by the LDS church. Comparable database to Ancestry but free.

**Newspapers.com** — historical newspaper archives, useful for obituaries and marriage announcements.

**Findagrave.com** — cemetery records, often with family information and obituary excerpts.

**MyHeritage** — similar to Ancestry; particularly strong for international records.

**Census Records**

US Census records from 1790 forward are publicly available after 72 years. Records from 1950 and earlier are accessible. Census records show household composition — who lived together in a household at each census — which helps confirm family structure.

**Social Security Records**

**Social Security Death Index (SSDI)** — publicly available through various genealogy services. Confirms deaths and provides dates.

**Social Security Applications and Claims Index** — available through Ancestry. Can include parent names.

**Court Records**

1. Family court for custody cases, divorce proceedings, adoption proceedings
2. District court for civil cases
3. County court for misdemeanor cases (occasionally useful)

**Military Records**

1. National Personnel Records Center for military service records
2. VA records for veteran information

**Immigration and Naturalization Records**

1. USCIS records for naturalized citizens
2. National Archives for historical records

**Voter Registration Records**

1. County elections office for current registered voter information
2. Sometimes useful for current address confirmation

**Professional Licensing**

1. State licensing boards for various professions
2. Sometimes useful for locating living heirs in professional careers

## 12.5 The Obituary Starting Point

Every heir research starts with a thorough obituary search. Here's how to do it well.

**Where to Find Obituaries**

1. Local newspaper websites for the newspaper serving the decedent's residence
2. Legacy.com for many local newspapers' obituaries aggregated
3. Ancestry.com historical newspapers collection
4. Newspapers.com — broader archive including smaller papers
5. Funeral home websites — many funeral homes post obituaries on their sites
6. Findagrave.com — sometimes includes obituary text
7. Social media — families often post obituaries or memorial posts

**What to Extract from Obituaries**

A good obituary contains:

1. Full name and any "also known as" variations
2. Date and location of birth
3. Date and location of death
4. Age at death
5. Name of spouse (and whether the spouse is preceding or surviving)
6. Names of parents (usually "son/daughter of...")
7. Names of children, often with their spouses and locations
8. Names of grandchildren, sometimes with locations
9. Names of siblings, with indication whether living or deceased
10. Nieces, nephews, and other notable relatives
11. Funeral service information
12. Memorial fund information

The obituary is often the first complete snapshot of the family you'll have access to. Read it carefully. Identify every named person.

**Warning Signs in Obituaries**

**Vague references** like "stepchildren and step-grandchildren too numerous to mention." This can indicate a blended family with outside children. Research further.

**"Preceded in death by his son [name]"** without mentioning the son's own children. Those grandchildren may still be heirs through representation.

**Missing decedents.** An obituary that doesn't mention the person's first marriage or early life may be hiding family history.

**Late-in-life remarriages** mentioned briefly. These can affect community property and homestead rights.

**Multiple obituaries.** Sometimes different family members post different obituaries emphasizing different relationships. Read them all.

**Cross-Referencing Obituaries**

Once you have the decedent's obituary, look up obituaries for the named relatives:

1. Obituary for any deceased spouses
2. Obituaries for any deceased children
3. Obituaries for parents and grandparents if you're going up the tree

Each additional obituary adds detail to the family picture.

## 12.6 Identifying Outside Children — The Critical Skill

As covered in Chapters 5 and 6, outside children are the single most commonly missed fact-pattern detail in this business. They flip ownership rules and create the fractures that define deals.

Your research has to specifically investigate whether outside children exist.

**Red Flags in the Decedent's History**

**Multiple marriages.** Each prior marriage is a potential source of outside children.

**Long gaps in the biographical record.** Periods where the decedent's location or activities are unclear.

**Obituary omissions.** Obituaries that don't fully cover the decedent's early life or first marriage.

**Age patterns.** A decedent who married late in life may have had earlier relationships.

**Family conflicts.** Families that don't talk about specific members, or visible estrangements.

**Research for Outside Children**

**Prior marriages.** Pull marriage records for the decedent going back to their teens. Any marriage earlier than the current/most recent one may have produced children.

**Divorce records.** Divorce decrees often list children of the marriage by name. Pull every divorce record you can find.

**Child support records.** Where available, these records indicate children acknowledged for support purposes.

**Census records.** Check census records throughout the decedent's adulthood. Children listed in households are children who need to be accounted for.

**Social Security Applications and Claims Index.** Sometimes lists parents for individuals who applied for SSN, which can identify children by looking for people who listed the decedent as a parent.

**DNA databases.** AncestryDNA, 23andMe, and similar services are increasingly identifying previously-unknown children. If a family has been using these services, outside children sometimes surface there first.

**Family interviews.** Direct questions to surviving family members: "Did the decedent have any children from relationships before [the known spouse]? Are there any other children we should know about?"

**Acknowledgments of paternity.** In some states, these are recorded and searchable. In Texas, they're filed with the Bureau of Vital Statistics.

**Interview Technique**

When asking about outside children, framing matters. Don't be accusatory. Don't imply wrongdoing. The framing that works:

> "I'm doing the research a title company will require. They need to confirm every legal heir, including any children from relationships that might not be in the current family. Are you aware of any children the decedent had from prior relationships or from relationships outside the marriage?"

Most family members, asked directly and professionally, will tell you what they know — or admit they don't know. The ones who stonewall usually know something and don't want to say.

**Documenting the "No Outside Children" Conclusion**

If your research doesn't find outside children, document the research. What sources you checked. What you looked for. What you found (or didn't find). The documentation protects you if an outside child later surfaces — it shows you did diligent research and weren't negligent.

## 12.7 Social Security Death Index and Death Verification

For any person you believe to be deceased (decedent, predeceased heirs, etc.), verify the death independently.

**Sources for Death Verification**

**Social Security Death Index (SSDI).** The Social Security Administration's records of reported deaths. Covers most deaths since 1936, with increasing completeness over time. Available through Ancestry.com, FamilySearch.org, and other services.

**State death certificates.** The primary official document.

**Obituaries.** Usually confirm the death even if the certificate isn't available.

**Findagrave.com.** Burial records.

**Why This Matters**

**Confirming heirship dates.** A predeceased heir's date of death determines the intestate succession rules applied to their share.

**Confirming living status.** For claimed-living heirs, SSDI checks confirm they're actually alive.

**Catching "presumed dead" heirs.** Heirs thought to have died decades ago but whose deaths weren't reported. These create complications.

**Finding Apparent Heirs Who May Actually Be Dead**

Run every claimed-living heir through SSDI. If SSDI shows a death, investigate further before proceeding. The apparent heir may actually be deceased, meaning their own children (if any) are the actual heirs.

## 12.8 Skip Tracing — Finding Living Heirs' Current Contact Information

Once you've identified a living heir, you need current contact information. This is skip tracing.

**Skip Tracing Tools**

**TLOxp** — comprehensive commercial database

**LexisNexis** — includes address and relationship data

**BeenVerified**, **WhitePages**, **Spokeo** — consumer-grade services, less comprehensive

**PropertyRadar**, **BatchLeads** — real estate-focused services with contact info

**Social media** — Facebook, LinkedIn, Instagram — often show current location

**Google searches** — name + city + occupation often produces results

**Voter registration records** — current address for registered voters

**Information You're Looking For**

1. Current residential address
2. Phone numbers (landline and mobile)
3. Email addresses
4. Employer and work address
5. Social media profiles

**Verification**

Don't just take the first result. Cross-reference:

1. Address matches between multiple sources
2. Phone numbers that connect to the right person when called
3. Social media profiles that match age and location
4. Property records in the person's name

**Handling Common Names**

"John Smith" is going to have thousands of matches. Narrow by:

1. Date of birth (even approximate)
2. Middle name or middle initial
3. Location history
4. Known relatives
5. Age

**When Heirs Can't Be Found**

Some heirs genuinely can't be located even with diligent research. When you've exhausted normal channels:

1. Hire a licensed private investigator (cost: $500–$3,000 per difficult heir)
2. Use international genealogy services if the heir may be abroad
3. Consider service by publication if you need to proceed without the heir
4. Document your diligent search extensively

Chapter 32 covers missing heirs in depth. For research purposes, understand that some heirs will resist being found, and you need to handle these situations systematically.

## 12.9 Building the Visual Family Tree

A family tree diagram makes heir research comprehensible. Build one for every deal involving more than 3–4 heirs.

**What a Good Family Tree Shows**

1. The decedent at a central position
2. All spouses connected appropriately
3. All children (including outside children) as nodes
4. Deceased relatives clearly marked (death dates)
5. Surviving relatives as endpoints
6. Fractional shares calculated at each living node

**Tools for Building Trees**

**Ancestry.com tree builder** — built into the Ancestry service, useful if you're already doing research there

**FamilySearch.org tree builder** — free alternative

**Lucidchart**, **Miro**, **draw.io** — general diagramming tools

**Genogram Pro or similar genealogy-specific tools** — for complex trees

**Excel** — manually, for simple trees

**Tree Maintenance**

As research proceeds, the tree evolves. New heirs emerge. Relationships clarify. Keep the tree updated; treat it as the living document of the case.

**Sharing the Tree**

**With your attorney:** They'll use it to draft AOHs, petitions, and other heirship documents.

**With the title company:** They'll use it to satisfy their examination requirements.

**With heirs themselves:** Sometimes useful in conversations to show understanding of the family structure.

**Internally on your team:** Everyone working the deal should understand the ownership picture.

## 12.10 Calculating Fractional Shares

Once you have the heir tree, calculate each heir's fractional share. This applies the rules from Chapters 5 and 6 (intestate succession and community property) to the specific fact pattern.

**The Calculation Process**

1. Determine the applicable rule. Community property with no outside children? Community property with outside children? Separate property with descendants? Each has different allocation rules.
2. Apply the rule to determine spousal share. How much goes to the surviving spouse (if any)?
3. Allocate the remaining share among descendants (or other heirs). By representation, equally, or per stirpes depending on the fact pattern.
4. Trace through any deceased descendants. Their shares pass to their own descendants (if any) by representation.
5. Express each heir's share as a fraction. 1/6, 1/12, 1/24, etc.
6. Verify the fractions sum to 100%. If they don't, your calculation is wrong somewhere.

**Example Calculation**

Decedent died intestate with community property. Surviving spouse. Three children (two with the surviving spouse, one outside child from prior relationship). One of the inside children predeceased the decedent, leaving two grandchildren.

Rule: Community property with outside children (Rule B from Chapter 5). Spouse keeps her own 1/2. Decedent's 1/2 passes to all decedent's children equally.

Decedent's 1/2 divides among three "child branches":

1. Inside child #1 (living): 1/3 of decedent's 1/2 = **1/6 of total**
2. Inside child #2 (predeceased, 2 grandchildren): 1/3 of decedent's 1/2 = 1/6 of total, split between the 2 grandchildren = **1/12 each**
3. Outside child (living): 1/3 of decedent's 1/2 = **1/6 of total**

Final allocation:

| Heir | Fraction | Percentage |
|---|---|---|
| Spouse | 1/2 | 50.00% |
| Inside child #1 | 1/6 | 16.67% |
| Grandchild A (from deceased inside child #2) | 1/12 | 8.33% |
| Grandchild B (from deceased inside child #2) | 1/12 | 8.33% |
| Outside child | 1/6 | 16.67% |
| **Total** | | **100.00%** |

Verification: 50% + 16.67% + 8.33% + 8.33% + 16.67% = 100%. ✓

This is the level of calculation you need for every deal. Get it right before you start acquiring — paying too much to one heir because you miscalculated their share is a common and expensive mistake.

## 12.11 Handling Unusual Heir Situations

Some research situations require special handling.

**Incarcerated Heirs**

1. Use the Federal Bureau of Prisons Inmate Locator (for federal inmates) or state department of corrections (for state inmates)
2. Mail contact is typically through the correctional facility's standard mail procedures
3. Mobile notaries who can visit prisons are a specialized resource; not all will
4. Allow extra time — prison mail is slow, scheduling is complex

**International Heirs**

1. Genealogical research across borders uses different databases
2. Consular assistance may be needed for some signings
3. Foreign documents may need apostilles for use in US
4. Translation requirements apply for non-English documents
5. Timeline extends significantly

**Heirs with Mental Capacity Issues**

1. Guardianship or conservatorship may be in place
2. The guardian/conservator acts on the heir's behalf
3. Court approval may be required for significant transactions
4. Document the capacity situation carefully

**Heirs Who Are Minors**

1. Minors can't sign deeds directly
2. A guardian ad litem or custodial parent signs on their behalf
3. Court approval is often required for real estate transactions involving minors' property
4. Timeline and cost increase significantly

**Heirs in Active Military Service**

1. Servicemembers Civil Relief Act (SCRA) provides special protections
2. Military service can stay civil proceedings against the servicemember
3. Verify active duty status through the DMDC website

**Heirs Who Are Hostile or Estranged**

1. Research continues normally
2. Contact is handled through the leverage mechanisms in Part V
3. Document attempts at outreach for later use if legal proceedings become necessary

## 12.12 Documenting Research for Title Underwriter Review

Title underwriters often want to see the research that supports the heirship conclusions in an AOH or JDH. Build the documentation as you go.

**Research File for Each Deal**

1. The family tree diagram
2. A list of heirs with their fractional shares and verification sources
3. Copies of source documents: obituaries, marriage certificates, birth certificates, death certificates, divorce decrees, adoption decrees, census records, and any other supporting records
4. Research notes: what you searched, what you found, what you didn't find (and why that's significant), any reasonable assumptions you made
5. Skip tracing records for each heir
6. Communication logs

**Why This Documentation Matters**

1. Title underwriters may review before issuing policies
2. Attorney ad litems in JDH proceedings request it
3. If an heir surfaces later, your documentation demonstrates diligent research
4. In litigation, your research file is evidence

**File Organization**

Keep a folder (physical or digital) for each deal containing all research materials. When the deal closes, archive it. You may need it later if issues arise.

## 12.13 Research Time and Cost — Realistic Expectations

Heir research is substantial work. Here's what it actually costs in time and money.

**Simple Case (Single Decedent, 3–6 Clearly-Identified Heirs, Clean Family)**

1. Time: 6–15 hours
2. Out-of-pocket costs: $50–$200 (vital records, database subscriptions)
3. Timeline: 1–3 weeks

**Moderate Case (2 Generations of Death, 8–12 Heirs, Some Research Challenges)**

1. Time: 15–30 hours
2. Out-of-pocket costs: $200–$500
3. Timeline: 3–6 weeks

**Complex Case (3+ Generations, Outside Children, Missing Heirs, Multiple States)**

1. Time: 30–80 hours
2. Out-of-pocket costs: $500–$3,000+ (including potential PI or genealogist fees)
3. Timeline: 2–4 months

**When to Hire a Professional Genealogist**

1. Complex cases with many potential heirs
2. International research
3. Research requiring specialized skills (DNA analysis, paleography, foreign-language records)
4. Cases where documentation needs to meet court or underwriter scrutiny

Professional genealogists typically charge $50–$150 per hour. Full research reports for complex cases run $2,000–$8,000. For deals with substantial equity spreads, this investment is often worth it.

## 12.14 The Research Report

For complex deals, produce a written research report summarizing your findings. Useful for:

1. Providing to attorneys drafting AOHs
2. Providing to title companies
3. Documenting research for your own records
4. Supporting JDH petitions

**Report Contents**

1. Case identification: Property address, legal description, case reference
2. Subject identification: Decedent's full identification
3. Marital history: All marriages with dates and sources
4. Children analysis: All identified children with supporting documentation, including specifically any outside children or children from prior relationships
5. Descendants analysis: For each child, further generations if applicable
6. Collateral heirs (if relevant): Parents, siblings, etc.
7. Heir table: Complete list of current legal heirs with fractional shares
8. Source list: All documents consulted and their citations
9. Diligent search documentation: What you searched for each missing or unlocatable heir
10. Conclusions: Your determination of heirship with supporting rationale

Length varies by complexity — simple cases 2–4 pages, complex cases 15–30 pages.

## 12.15 Key Takeaways for Operators

Before moving to Chapter 13, internalize these principles:

**Heir research is the hidden skill that separates successful operators from the rest.** Most investors can't do this work.

**Every deal requires a complete heir analysis.** Not just "the family's understanding" — actual research verifying all legal heirs.

**Obituaries are the starting point.** Every research begins there.

**Outside children are the most commonly missed detail.** Specifically investigate for them.

**Verify every relationship with documentation.** Oral representations are not enough.

**Skip trace every living heir.** You need current contact information before acquisition begins.

**Social Security Death Index confirms deaths.** Run it on everyone you think is deceased.

**Build visual family trees.** They make complex cases comprehensible.

**Calculate fractional shares carefully.** Miscalculations lead to overpayments that you won't catch until too late.

**Document diligent search.** For any heir you can't find, document what you did to look. This protects you later.

**Research time and cost scale with complexity.** Simple case: 10 hours, $100. Complex case: 60 hours, $2,000+.

**Professional genealogists are worth it for complex cases.** $2,000–$8,000 for research on a $200K+ spread deal is a small investment.

Chapter 13 introduces the Fact-Pattern Matrix — a diagnostic framework that takes your heir research and applies it to deal analysis. You'll learn to look at any fractured property's fact pattern and identify, within minutes:

1. Which category of fracture is at play (or which combination of categories)
2. Which resolution pathway fits best
3. Which legal tools apply
4. What the likely cost and timeline are
5. Whether the deal is worth pursuing

Chapter 14 covers underwriting in practice — taking the economic principles from Chapter 3 and applying them to specific deals with specific numbers. Chapter 15 closes Part III with the Package-Properties research methodology.

You now know how to find deals (Chapter 11) and how to research the heirs (Chapter 12). Chapter 13 teaches you how to put them together and make decisions.
