## 11.1 Why Deal Flow Is the Whole Game

Every business starts with lead flow. You can have the best legal knowledge, the best title company relationships, the best execution systems — and if you don't have deals coming in, none of it matters.

Most real estate investors who fail at fractured equity fail at the lead flow stage. They spend weeks reading Codexs and setting up systems, then can't figure out where to find actual properties to work on. They make a few phone calls, hit dead ends, and give up.

This chapter fixes that. It teaches you the lead sources that actually produce fractured equity deals, the list-building methodology that turns raw data into targeted opportunities, and the pipeline construction that produces a steady flow of deals once your business is running.

The principles in this chapter apply whether you're working in a rural Texas county with 8,000 residents or a major metro area with 2 million. The mechanics adapt to scale; the fundamentals don't change.

## 11.2 Tax Foreclosure Is the Engine

Before we talk about any specific lead source, understand this central truth about the fractured-equity business: **tax foreclosure is the engine that makes this entire methodology work.** Not one lead source among several. The engine. Every other lead source in this chapter is a spoke around this hub.

Here is why.

### Tax Delinquency Is the Symptom That Reveals the Disease

Properties become fractured for the reasons Chapter 2 walked through — deaths without probate, divorces without conveyances, entity failures, trust dormancy, fraud, missing heirs. But fractures are mostly invisible in the public record. A property sitting in a dead grandfather's name for twenty years looks identical to a property that just sold last month — unless you know where to look.

**Tax delinquency is where you look.** When property taxes stop getting paid, something has gone wrong with the ownership. Nobody lives there. Nobody has authority. Nobody cares, or nobody can act. Unpaid taxes are the external symptom of the internal ownership problem. The taxing authority publishes the symptom list publicly, county by county, year after year. That publication is the operator's primary data source.

A living owner with clear title pays property taxes. They have the obligation, the authority, and the motivation. A fractured property often does not have any of those. The heirs don't know they owe taxes on property they never claimed. The missing spouse doesn't even know the property exists. The defunct LLC cannot legally issue a payment. The dormant trust's trustee died years ago. So the taxes go unpaid. And the unpaid-tax list becomes a roster of exactly the properties an operator is looking for.

### The Taxing Authority's Interest Aligns With the Operator's Interest

Every other party to a fractured property may have reason to leave the situation unresolved. Heirs might prefer to ignore complexity. Co-owners might benefit from the status quo. Creditors might prefer to wait. But the taxing authority has exactly one interest: collect the tax. The taxing authority will force resolution eventually, through tax foreclosure, if nobody else does.

That taxing-authority pressure is the operator's ally. It creates the timeline that makes the deal happen. Without tax pressure, many fractured situations would persist indefinitely — the heirs would do nothing forever because nothing was forcing them to act. With tax pressure, the clock is running. Do something, or lose the property. Accept the operator's offer, or let the county take it for unpaid taxes.

Every conversation an operator has with heirs of a tax-delinquent property happens under this timer. The heirs know the property is in trouble. They know something has to happen. They know either they act or someone else will. That reality is why cold calls on tax-delinquent properties produce responses that cold calls on non-delinquent properties do not.

### Tax Foreclosure Creates the Valuation Discount

Fractured-equity operators buy below market. The spread between what the operator pays and what the property is worth after the title is cleaned up is where the profit comes from. What makes that spread available? Tax pressure, primarily. An heir facing the loss of the property to tax foreclosure is motivated to accept less than full value. A family that has been arguing for ten years about what to do with Grandmother's house is motivated to settle once the tax foreclosure notice arrives. The pressure converts theoretical value into actual cash for the heirs, at a price that leaves margin for the operator to fix the title and exit.

Without that pressure, the same families might hold out for higher prices, or simply refuse to act. With it, deals happen at prices that make economic sense for everyone — the heirs receive money they had written off, the operator earns margin for solving the problem, the property returns to productive use, the taxing authority gets paid. The tax-foreclosure framework aligns incentives across every party. That is why it works so well as the engine of this business.

### Tax Foreclosure Is Every Chapter's Unspoken Context

As you read the rest of this Codex, notice how often the tax-foreclosure backdrop quietly informs what is being taught:

1. Chapter 12's heir research begins with a tax-delinquent property — that is why you are researching the heirs in the first place
2. Chapter 13's fact-pattern matrix classifies tax-delinquent properties with multi-year backlogs as high-priority deal candidates
3. Chapter 14's underwriting calculates the tax payoff as part of the acquisition cost, and the tax-sale prevention as part of the upside
4. Chapter 16's first heir call opens with awareness that the heir knows about the tax problem
5. Chapter 17's offer structuring includes tax-delinquency cleanup as a deal term
6. Chapter 18's administrator/executor shortcut is especially potent on tax-delinquent estates — because the administrator's authority to sell often flows directly from the need to pay the decedent's debts
7. Chapter 23's leverage framework identifies tax pressure as the most important external force an operator can lean on when cooperation is limited
8. Chapter 24's contribution suits often flow from one co-owner having paid the taxes that all should have paid — the contribution suit recovers those tax payments from the non-paying co-owners
9. Chapter 25's partition actions are frequently triggered by one co-owner's refusal to contribute to tax obligations
10. Chapter 26's creditor-suit-to-sheriff's-sale pipeline is the amplification of tax-contribution claims into a forced-sale remedy
11. Chapter 27's tax sale intervention is the direct use of tax-foreclosure procedures as an acquisition pathway
12. Chapter 30's breadcrumbing often targets heirs or former owners whose last known contact with the property was before the taxes stopped being paid
13. Chapter 35's capstone synthesis recognizes tax foreclosure as one of the core pillars of the whole curative framework

Hold this in your head as you read: almost every deal in this Codex has a tax-foreclosure story embedded in it. The specifics vary — sometimes the tax foreclosure is imminent and driving the timeline, sometimes it has been pending for years and creating accumulated pressure, sometimes it has already happened and created title issues that need curing — but the taxing authority is almost always somewhere in the picture. Operators who internalize this see the business clearly. Operators who miss it are confused by why certain techniques work.

### The Four Operational Uses of Tax-Foreclosure Pressure

Tax foreclosure shows up in an operator's work in four different operational modes. Each one is a tool in the toolkit.

**1. Discovery.** Tax delinquency lists are the starting point for finding fractured properties. This chapter is about this mode primarily.

**2. Timeline.** The tax-foreclosure clock forces decisions. Heirs cannot put off a decision indefinitely because the clock is running. This mode accelerates deals.

**3. Leverage.** When voluntary cooperation fails, one co-owner paying the taxes creates a contribution claim that can be escalated to a forced sale via Chapter 26's pipeline. This mode produces deals when heirs refuse to cooperate. The pipeline works because tax-paying co-owners have a legal right to reimbursement from non-paying co-owners.

**4. Acquisition.** Tax-foreclosure sales themselves are an acquisition channel. Chapter 27 covers the direct use of tax sales as a pathway to acquire properties. This mode is separate from the others but uses the same underlying framework.

Operators who learn to recognize which mode applies in a given situation work the business with clarity. The first-time heir call on a tax-delinquent property is using mode 1 and mode 2. A contribution suit against uncooperative co-heirs is using mode 3. Bidding at a tax sale is using mode 4. The same underlying pressure — tax foreclosure — is being deployed differently depending on the specific situation.

## 11.3 Where Fractured Properties Actually Come From

With the tax-foreclosure engine established as the hub, here is how fractured properties surface in the public record. Understanding where fractures show up is the map of where to look.

Fractured properties surface in three primary ways:

**1. Tax delinquency lists.** Every county in Texas publishes a list of properties with delinquent property taxes. Many of those properties are fractured — the reason taxes aren't being paid is that nobody has the authority to write the check. Tax delinquency lists are the single richest source of fractured equity opportunities. This is not one source among three. This is the primary source, and the other sources orbit around it.

**2. Probate court filings.** When someone dies and the estate is being probated, the filings are public record. New probate filings represent new inheritance situations, some of which involve fractured properties. Probate courts also reveal old unprobated estates when creditors or heirs finally file long after death. Probate filings often cross-reference with tax delinquency — the deaths driving probate cases are often the same deaths driving the tax-payment breakdown.

**3. Word of mouth and referrals.** Once you've done a few deals, heirs from past deals refer you to other family members with similar problems. Attorneys who handle estate matters refer cases they can't help with directly. Title companies refer situations they can't cleanly insure. This category becomes more important over time as your network develops.

Each of these sources feeds different parts of your pipeline. Tax delinquency lists are volume-oriented — many properties, many of which won't qualify, but the ones that do are usually immediately workable. Probate filings are quality-oriented — fewer matches, but each one is a fresh opportunity the moment you see it. Referrals are relationship-oriented — they come slowly but often represent the best deals.

## 11.4 Tax Delinquency Lists — How to Work the Primary Source

Tax delinquency lists are how most serious operators build their deal flow. Every county tax assessor's office publishes or makes available a list of properties with unpaid property taxes. Your job is to get those lists, filter them to find the fractured properties, and work the filtered list.

### How Tax Delinquency Lists Are Available

In Texas, tax delinquency information is public record and typically available through:

**County tax assessor/collector websites.** Many counties publish the delinquent tax list online, searchable by property or owner. Some publish a downloadable list updated periodically.

**Tax foreclosure attorneys.** Firms like Linebarger, Perdue Brandon, McCreary Veselka Bragg & Allen, and others represent taxing authorities in foreclosure actions. They publish lists of properties scheduled for tax sale.

**Third-party data aggregators.** Services like PropertyRadar, DataTree, BatchLeads, and others aggregate tax delinquency data from multiple counties, often with additional information layered in.

**In-person requests.** For counties without good online access, you can request the delinquent tax list in person at the tax assessor's office, sometimes for a small fee.

### What the List Contains

A typical tax delinquency list shows, for each delinquent property:

1. Property address and/or legal description
2. Owner name as reflected in tax records
3. Total amount of delinquent taxes
4. Years of delinquency
5. Sometimes appraised value
6. Sometimes scheduled foreclosure auction dates

### Reading the Tax Delinquency List for Fracture Signals

Beyond the raw data, the tax delinquency list contains signals about which properties are fractured versus which are simply operated by owners behind on their obligations. Learn to read these signals:

**Length of delinquency.** Properties with 3+ years of delinquency are more likely to be fractured than properties with 1 year. A single year of delinquency is often just a cash flow problem for the owner. Multiple years suggests structural problems — no single person with authority to pay. A property with 8+ years of delinquency almost certainly has structural ownership issues.

**Owner name patterns.** Properties where the owner name is "Estate of John Smith" or where the same name appears on the tax roll for someone who died years ago are strong fractured equity signals. Search obituaries for the owner name — if the owner died 5 or 15 or 30 years ago and the tax bill is still addressed to them, you've found a fracture.

**Multiple owners named.** Tax rolls sometimes show "Smith, John et al." or list multiple family names. Multi-owner properties are often fractured.

**Mailing address mismatches.** When the tax bill mailing address is different from the property address, it often means the owner has moved and may not even know about the property anymore. A mailing address that is also returned as undeliverable is a very strong fracture signal.

**LLC or entity owner names.** Properties owned by LLCs that show as forfeited on the Secretary of State records are fractured by definition. See Chapters 36 through 38 of this Codex.

**Trust owner names.** Properties owned by trusts (e.g., "Smith Family Trust") where the trustee has died and no successor was appointed are fractured. See Chapters 38 and 39.

**Assessed value.** Most operators have an equity spread floor (e.g., $75,000 — Chapter 3). Properties below that threshold in assessed value rarely produce deals meeting the floor. Filter out properties below a minimum value threshold.

**Geographic focus.** Start in counties you know or can efficiently work. Don't try to operate in 50 counties; pick 2–4 and build deep expertise.

From a list of 1,000 tax-delinquent properties, you might filter down to 30–50 fractured equity candidates worth deeper investigation.

### Reading the Timeline on a Tax-Delinquent Property

Tax foreclosure in Texas has a specific timeline that every operator should understand. Taxes become delinquent February 1 following the tax year. Penalties and interest accrue. The taxing authority can file a tax lawsuit after delinquency, typically after a year or more. The lawsuit results in a tax judgment and tax-sale order. The property is sold at public auction. After the sale, a redemption period runs — typically two years for homestead or agricultural property, six months for other property — during which certain parties can redeem.

Understanding this timeline helps the operator read a tax-delinquent property correctly. A property with one year of delinquency is early in the timeline; plenty of time to work the deal cooperatively. A property with scheduled foreclosure in 60 days is late in the timeline; the clock is running and decisions must happen fast. A property already sold at tax sale and sitting in redemption is a different opportunity entirely — potentially acquired through the pre-redemption rights of heirs, or through the post-redemption rights of the tax-sale purchaser.

Chapter 27 walks through tax-sale intervention in detail. For lead-sourcing purposes, the main insight is that the timeline creates different kinds of opportunities at different stages. Early-stage tax-delinquent properties are the volume of the business. Late-stage tax-delinquent properties are the time-pressured deals where fastest operator wins. Properties that have already been through tax sale are specialized acquisitions requiring specific knowledge.

## 11.5 Probate Court as a Lead Source

Probate filings are fresh opportunities. When a probate case is filed, the death just happened or recently occurred. Heirs are actively dealing with the estate. Property ownership is in flux. This is often the best time to identify and engage with heirs — especially if combined with tax-delinquency screening.

### What to Watch for in Probate Filings

1. New probate cases involving real property
2. Dependent administration filings (which often indicate complex or contested estates)
3. Applications to determine heirship
4. Applications for small estate affidavits
5. Old estates being reopened (creditors or heirs filing after long delay)

### How to Access Probate Filings

**County probate court websites.** Most Texas counties post probate filings online. Search by date or party name.

**Court clerk's office.** In-person access to probate dockets and individual case files.

**County-by-county probate alerts.** Some services send daily or weekly alerts on new probate filings in specified counties.

**PACER** for federal cases (rare in fractured equity).

### What You're Looking for in Each Filing

When you review a probate filing, key data points:

1. The decedent's name and date of death
2. Whether a will was filed
3. The real estate listed in the estate inventory (if filed)
4. The applicant (often an heir or personal representative)
5. The attorneys involved

### Cross-Referencing Probate with Tax Delinquency

This is where the magic happens. When you cross-reference probate filings against tax delinquency lists, you find the richest deals.

A property that:

1. Is tax-delinquent AND
2. Has an owner named in a probate filing AND
3. Has multi-year delinquency

Is almost always a fractured equity opportunity worth pursuing. The tax delinquency identifies the symptom. The probate filing provides the context. Together they tell you exactly what went wrong with the title — the decedent died, the estate got messy, the taxes stopped getting paid, and nobody has cleaned it up. That is the canonical fractured-equity fact pattern, and cross-referencing probate and tax data surfaces it reliably.

## 11.6 Other Lead Sources — The Secondary Channels

Beyond tax delinquency and probate, several other sources produce fractured equity leads. Each serves a different purpose. Most of these work best when combined with tax-delinquency screening — the secondary channels gain power when cross-referenced against the primary tax data.

**Code violation lists.** Cities maintain lists of properties with code enforcement violations — abandoned structures, weed complaints, unsafe buildings. Many of these properties are fractured; the reason nobody's addressing the violations is the same reason nobody's paying taxes.

**HOA delinquency lists.** In HOA communities, properties with delinquent dues often have the same ownership problems as properties with delinquent taxes. HOAs sometimes publish or share these lists.

**Vacant property lists.** Some cities maintain registries of vacant properties. A long-vacant property is often a fractured property.

**MLS expired listings.** Properties that were listed on the MLS but didn't sell (and expired) sometimes had title problems that killed the deal. A search of expired listings combined with title research can identify these.

**Auction lists.** Tax foreclosure auctions, sheriff's sales, and similar public auctions sometimes list properties that don't sell due to title problems. Properties that fail at auction multiple times often have fracture issues.

**Real estate attorney referrals.** Attorneys who handle estate work encounter fractured properties constantly — often when heirs come to them wanting to sell but can't get clean title. A good relationship with 2–3 estate attorneys produces a steady stream of referrals.

**Title company referrals.** Title companies sometimes decline to insure deals because of heirship issues or defective deeds. They occasionally refer these to operators who can resolve the issues.

**Door knocking and driving for dollars.** Old-school but still works. A vacant, overgrown, obviously-abandoned property in an otherwise-maintained neighborhood almost certainly has a story. Research the address.

**Obituary searches.** Obituaries are published constantly. Combined with property record cross-referencing (does the decedent own real estate? Is it in probate? Is the property tax-delinquent?), obituaries generate fresh leads immediately after death. Some operators maintain automated obituary monitoring in counties they work.

**Direct mail campaigns.** Targeted direct mail to specific property classes (tax-delinquent, heirship-apparent, etc.) produces response rates of 0.5% to 2%. At scale, this generates leads, though competition is significant in most markets.

## 11.7 Building Your List — The Technical Workflow

Let me walk through the practical workflow for building and maintaining a deal-sourcing list. The workflow starts and ends with tax delinquency as the primary data spine; other sources layer onto it.

### Step 1: Choose Your Counties

Start with 2–4 counties where you can efficiently work. Consider:

1. Counties where you live or have connections
2. Counties with sufficient volume of tax-delinquent properties
3. Counties where courthouse research is practical (online access, reasonable driving distance)
4. Counties where your title company operates

### Step 2: Obtain the Raw Tax-Delinquency Data

Pull the tax delinquency list for each target county. Download, export, or request it. Frequency varies — some counties update weekly, others monthly or quarterly. Note how often each county publishes and build your cadence accordingly. This is the spine of your data pipeline.

### Step 3: Enrich the Data

Take the raw list and add information:

1. Look up each property's assessed value (filter for minimum thresholds)
2. Research the listed owner (obituary search, probate search)
3. Check years of delinquency
4. Note any scheduled foreclosure auctions
5. Flag mailing-address mismatches
6. Identify entity owners (LLC, trust) and check their status

### Step 4: Filter to Qualified Candidates

Apply your criteria:

1. Minimum assessed value (e.g., $100,000+)
2. Multiple years of delinquency (e.g., 3+)
3. Signs of death or ownership complexity
4. Specific ZIP codes or areas if applicable

### Step 5: Prioritize by Timeline

Among qualified candidates, prioritize based on tax-foreclosure timeline:

1. Properties with upcoming foreclosure dates (time pressure creates deal opportunity)
2. Higher-value properties (bigger spreads)
3. Clearer fact patterns (easier to work)
4. Properties where the heirs' timeline needs resolution soon

### Step 6: Research the Top of the List

For your top 20–40 candidates, do deeper research:

1. Pull full title chain
2. Identify all potential heirs
3. Research the decedent's family
4. Assess equity spread and deal complexity

### Step 7: Contact Heirs

From your researched top of list, identify the priority deals and begin heir contact. Chapter 12 covers the research methodology in depth; Chapters 16–18 cover the contact methodology. Your opening conversations will reference the tax-delinquency situation directly — because that is the reason you found them, and the reason they should want to talk to you.

### Step 8: Maintain the Pipeline

1. Refresh your list as new data publishes
2. Track which leads produced deals and which didn't
3. Remove dead leads; add new candidates
4. Build historical data on which filters produce best results

### List Management Tools

Operators use various tools to manage this workflow:

**Excel or Google Sheets** — simple, flexible, works well up to ~1,000 properties

**Custom CRM systems** — Podio, Airtable, REsimpli, etc. — more structure, better for multi-person teams

**Specialized real estate tools** — DealMachine, BatchLeads, InvestorLift, PropStream, etc. — integrated list-building and contact management

Pick tools that match your scale and team size. Don't over-invest in technology before you have deal flow; don't under-invest once deal flow is established.

## 11.8 Cross-Referencing for High-Quality Leads

The single most important technique in lead qualification is cross-referencing multiple data sources. Any one data point has false positives. Combined data points have much higher signal. The primary axis of cross-reference is always tax delinquency — the other data sources enrich the tax-delinquent list into prioritized, workable leads.

### The Canonical Cross-Reference

1. Property is on the tax delinquency list AND
2. Listed owner is named in an obituary indicating death 5+ years ago AND
3. The property has had no recent deed activity AND
4. The property has no open probate filing

A property meeting all four criteria is almost certainly a fractured equity deal. This is the first-priority cross-reference for essentially every operator.

### Other Valuable Cross-References

1. Tax-delinquent + multi-year + owner name matches a past decedent in county records
2. Tax-delinquent + vacant (from code enforcement) + multi-year
3. Open probate + real estate on estate inventory + filing older than 6 months with no closing + property is also tax-delinquent
4. Tax-delinquent + code violation + vacant
5. Tax-delinquent + owned by forfeited LLC or dissolved entity
6. Tax-delinquent + owned by trust with no successor trustee filed
7. Expired MLS listing + title commitment issues (if accessible) + subsequent tax delinquency

### Automating Cross-References

Larger operators build automated cross-reference pipelines:

1. Scripts that pull tax delinquency data (the spine of everything)
2. Scripts that scrape obituary publications
3. Scripts that check probate court filings
4. Scripts that check Secretary of State entity status for corporate owners
5. Scripts that combine these into ranked lists

This is not essential starting out. Manual cross-referencing works fine at the scale of a new operator. As you grow, automation reduces time per qualified lead.

## 11.9 Working Rural vs. Urban Counties

Different counties have different characteristics that affect lead flow strategy. The tax-delinquency pipeline works everywhere, but the details differ.

### Rural Counties

1. Smaller volume of tax-delinquent properties
2. Less competition from other operators
3. Older ownership patterns with more multi-generational fractures
4. More reliance on in-person research (some records aren't online)
5. Relationships with county officials matter more
6. Properties often have lower value but larger spreads on a percentage basis
7. Tax delinquency timelines may move more slowly — foreclosures delayed by administrative constraints

### Urban Counties

1. Much larger volume of tax-delinquent properties
2. More competition from other operators
3. More institutional owners (banks, LLCs) — fewer fractured heirs but more defunct-entity situations
4. Better online research tools
5. Larger property values
6. Often more competition but deeper inventory
7. Tax-foreclosure machinery often more organized and predictable

### Suburban Counties

1. Middle ground on most variables
2. Can be underserved (not as heavily worked as urban, more data available than rural)

Many successful operators work a mix — one or two rural counties for relationship-based deals and one urban or suburban county for volume. The rural county relationships build over time; the urban county feeds the volume machine.

## 11.10 Understanding Your Competition

Most markets have competing operators pursuing similar deals. Understanding the competitive landscape helps you position yourself.

### Types of Competitors

**Serious fractured-equity operators.** Your direct competition. Running similar playbooks. Often concentrated in particular sub-markets or counties.

**Wholesalers fishing at the margins.** Wholesalers who occasionally encounter fractured properties but don't know how to work them. They'll call heirs, offer low prices, and walk away when complexity surfaces.

**Traditional investors.** Flippers, buy-and-hold investors who occasionally encounter fractured properties but view them as too complex.

**Speculative flippers.** Operators who buy at tax sales hoping title problems will resolve themselves. Usually losing money but creating market noise.

**End-buyer investors.** Operators at the sheriff's sales and tax auctions looking for the rare clean opportunity.

### Signs You're in a Competitive Market

1. Tax-delinquent properties you call have already been contacted by other investors
2. Memorandums of contract filed by other investors appear on properties you research
3. Heirs mention receiving multiple offers or direct mail pieces
4. Auction attendance is heavy
5. Prices at tax sales run above what the math should support

### Positioning Against Competition

Competition isn't the end of the world. In most markets, there's more deal flow than any single operator can handle. Your positioning comes from:

**Speed to contact.** Whoever reaches heirs first has a relationship advantage.

**Quality of research.** Heirs respond to operators who've done their homework. Scattered telemarketer-style contacts don't build trust.

**Professional execution.** Operators who close deals cleanly, pay on time, and deliver what they promise build reputations. Heirs call back about other properties.

**Specialization.** If you're better than competitors at one particular sub-type (e.g., properties with international heirs, or defunct entities, or multi-generational estates), you capture those deals consistently.

Lead generation costs vary dramatically depending on approach.

## 11.13 Building a Pipeline That Produces Consistent Deal Flow

Deal flow is the function of a pipeline, not of individual campaigns. Here's how to build one that produces consistently.

### The Weekly Rhythm

**Monday:** Review new data (tax delinquencies, probate filings, obituaries). Add new candidates to your list.

**Tuesday–Wednesday:** Research new candidates. Pull title chains. Build family trees. Identify top priorities.

**Thursday:** Heir contact activities. Phone calls to the highest-priority new candidates. Follow-ups with existing conversations.

**Friday:** Deal management. Move current deals forward — attorney coordination, signing scheduling, title company communication.

This is the rhythm most solo operators settle into. Team operations split these functions across team members.

### The Monthly Cadence

1. Month 1 of a new county: heavy list-building
2. Month 2: working the top of the list
3. Month 3: signing first deals
4. Month 4–6: closing first deals, building relationships, refining approach
5. Month 6+: steady-state deal flow

### The Quarterly Cadence

1. Quarter 1 of each year: Review lead sources, expand to new counties if appropriate
2. Quarter 2: Double down on what's working
3. Quarter 3: Evaluate return per lead source, adjust investment
4. Quarter 4: Plan for next year, invest in infrastructure

### Key Metrics to Track

1. Raw leads per week
2. Qualified leads per week (passing initial filter)
3. Researched leads per week
4. Heir contacts attempted per week
5. Genuine conversations per week
6. Verbal commitments per week
7. Signed deals per week
8. Closed deals per month

Track these by source. Over time, you'll see which sources produce the best conversion, and you'll invest more in those.

## 11.14 Key Takeaways for Operators

Before moving to Chapter 12, internalize these principles:

**Tax foreclosure is the engine.** Every other lead source in this chapter is a spoke around the tax-delinquency hub. Deal flow in this business comes primarily from the tax-delinquency list.

**Tax delinquency reveals fracture.** Unpaid taxes are the external symptom of internal ownership problems. Properties that stop paying taxes are properties where something has gone wrong with title.

**The taxing authority is your ally.** Its interest in collecting taxes creates the timeline that drives deals. Without tax pressure, many fractured situations would persist indefinitely.

**Tax foreclosure shows up in four operational modes.** Discovery (finding deals), Timeline (forcing decisions), Leverage (contribution suits and beyond), Acquisition (tax-sale pathways). Know which mode applies to each situation.

**Deal flow is the whole game.** Every other skill is useless without leads.

**Probate filings are the freshest leads.** Review them weekly. Cross-reference against tax delinquency for the highest-quality deals.

**Cross-reference for quality.** Tax-delinquent + dead owner + multi-year delinquency = highest-quality leads.

**Filter aggressively.** From 1,000 raw leads, you're looking for 30–50 qualified candidates and maybe 10 deal-grade opportunities.

**Start with 2–4 counties.** Don't try to work 50 counties. Build depth before breadth.

**Lead costs scale with operation size.** $500/year works at the beginning; $30,000/year works at scale. Match your investment to your deal volume.

**Track conversion rates.** Raw leads → qualified → researched → contacted → conversing → committed → signed → closed. Know your numbers at each stage.

**Build a weekly rhythm.** Leads → Research → Contact → Deals. Repeat. Every week. The operators who maintain the rhythm beat the operators who work in fits and starts.

**Expect competition but don't fear it.** Most markets have more deal flow than any single operator can handle. Differentiate on speed, research quality, and execution.
