## 6.1 Why This Matters More Than You Think

If you're working Texas deals, you need to understand community property as thoroughly as you understand intestate succession. The two bodies of law are inseparable. You cannot do an intestate succession analysis without first classifying the property as community or separate, because the rules for distributing each are different.

More than half the mistakes I see new operators make — overpaying an heir, missing a co-owner, representing heirship incorrectly in an affidavit of heirship — come from community property confusion. They assume the surviving spouse owns everything. They don't notice that a property inherited during marriage is separate property, not community. They don't understand how community property survives a divorce that was never finalized.

This chapter fixes that. By the time you finish it, you'll classify every property in your deal flow correctly, apply the right rules to each, and catch the community property traps that cost other investors money.

Texas is one of nine community property states, and it has the most fully developed community property law of any of them. The rules here apply in full to Texas. The general framework applies — with variations — to the other community property states (Louisiana, Arizona, California, Idaho, Nevada, New Mexico, Washington, Wisconsin). Common-law states don't use this framework at all, and their rules are covered in Section 6.11.

---

## 6.2 The Core Distinction — Community vs. Separate

At the highest level, Texas classifies all property owned by a married person into two categories: community property and separate property.

**Community property** is everything owned by either spouse that is not separate property. It's the default category. Unless a specific exception applies, property acquired during marriage is community property, owned equally by both spouses regardless of whose name is on the title.

**Separate property** is everything that falls into one of three categories:

1. Property owned by a spouse before the marriage
2. Property acquired during marriage by gift, devise, or descent (inheritance)
3. Property recovered for personal injuries sustained during marriage (except for compensation for loss of earning capacity during the marriage)

Those three categories are exhaustive. If a piece of property doesn't fit one of them, it's community property.

### The Presumption

Texas law presumes that all property possessed by either spouse during marriage is community property. Anyone asserting that specific property is separate property must prove it by "clear and convincing evidence" — a higher standard than the typical "preponderance of the evidence" in civil cases.

This presumption matters. When you're analyzing a deal and you don't have documentation showing a property is separate, you assume it's community. The burden is on whoever claims separate to prove separate.

---

## 6.3 How Property Gets Characterized as Community or Separate

The classification depends on the nature of the acquisition, not the form of title or who's on the deed.

**Purchased during marriage with community funds → Community property.** A husband buys a house during marriage using money earned during the marriage. The deed is in his name only. The property is still community property. His wife has a 50% interest even though she never signed the deed.

**Purchased before marriage → Separate property of the buying spouse.** A husband owned a house before marriage. He continues to own it after marriage. The wife does not acquire any community interest in the pre-marital property simply by marrying him. It remains his separate property.

**Inherited during marriage → Separate property of the inheriting spouse.** A wife inherits her grandmother's farm during marriage. The farm is the wife's separate property, not community, because it came to her by descent (inheritance). Her husband has no community interest in the farm, even though she acquired it during the marriage.

**Gift during marriage → Separate property of the receiving spouse.** A father gives his daughter a rental property as a gift during her marriage. The property is the daughter's separate property. Her husband has no community interest because it was acquired by gift, not purchased with community funds.

**Purchased during marriage with separate funds → Separate property, but proof is needed.** A wife uses money she inherited before marriage to buy a house during marriage. The house is her separate property because the funds used to buy it were separate. But proving this requires traceable documentation — bank statements, inheritance records, purchase documents — showing the separate funds were used.

**Purchased during marriage with commingled funds → Community property unless traced.** Most couples commingle their separate funds with community funds over time. Once funds are commingled, the burden of proving which portion is separate becomes extremely difficult. In practice, commingled accounts are usually treated as community, and property purchased from those accounts is community property.

## 6.4 The Inception-of-Title Rule

When classification is ambiguous, Texas applies the inception-of-title rule: the character of the property is determined at the moment of acquisition.

If title to a piece of property was acquired before marriage, it's separate — even if the spouse made mortgage payments with community funds during marriage. The community has a reimbursement claim for its contributions, but the property itself remains separate.

If title was acquired during marriage using community funds, it's community — even if the spouse later inherits money and uses it to pay off the mortgage. The payoff doesn't retroactively change the property's character.

### Worked Example

A man buys a house in 2005 for $100,000, putting $20,000 down and taking an $80,000 mortgage. He marries in 2008. He makes all mortgage payments from his paycheck during the marriage, paying off the mortgage entirely by 2020. The house is now worth $300,000.

**Classification:** The house is the husband's separate property. Title was acquired in 2005, before the marriage. The community has a reimbursement claim for the mortgage payments made with community funds (the "Gleich" claim or similar equitable reimbursement), which is a separate issue from the property's character. The house itself remains separate property.

### Practical Implication for Operators

When you're evaluating a deal, look at when the decedent acquired the property relative to when they married. Pre-marital acquisition = separate. Acquisition during marriage with community funds = community. Acquisition during marriage by inheritance or gift = separate.

This matters because under the intestate succession rules in Chapter 5, separate real property passes differently than community real property — and the outside children rule flips different things depending on the classification.

## 6.5 Title in One Spouse's Name — Not Determinative

A common mistake: assuming that a property with only one spouse's name on the deed is that spouse's separate property. It's not necessarily.

Title in one spouse's name is not the same as separate property. A deed that reads "John Smith, a married person" is a deed to a piece of community property if the acquisition occurred during marriage with community funds. John's wife has a 50% community interest even though her name is nowhere on the deed.

This is why spousal signatures matter so much in Texas transactions. When community property is conveyed, both spouses must sign the deed — even if only one spouse is named as the grantor on the deed's face. A deed signed by only one spouse on a community property conveyance has limited legal effect and is vulnerable to challenge.

### The Homestead Complication

Texas imposes an additional requirement on conveyances of homestead property: both spouses must sign, regardless of whether the property is community or separate. If the husband owns a house as his separate property (inherited from his mother) but lives in it with his wife as the family homestead, the wife must sign any conveyance or the deed is void as to homestead rights.

This is a trap for investors working deals where one spouse claims to own the property alone. If the property is homestead, the non-owning spouse's signature is still required for a valid conveyance. Failing to get it means the deed is defective and the sale will not clear a title commitment.

### What This Means for Your Deals

Always ask: was the decedent married at acquisition? Was the decedent married at death? Did the decedent and spouse occupy the property as a homestead? If any of those facts apply, you need to analyze spousal interests carefully — whether by direct community ownership or by homestead rights.

## 6.6 Community Property at Death — Where the Money Is

When a spouse dies, the community property estate is divided in a specific way that's critical to understand.

The surviving spouse retains their 1/2 of the community property automatically. This happens by operation of law, not by inheritance. The surviving spouse already owned their 1/2 during the marriage. Death doesn't change that. No probate is needed for the surviving spouse's 1/2 — it's already theirs.

The decedent's 1/2 of the community property passes under testate or intestate succession.

1. If the decedent had a will, the will directs how the decedent's 1/2 passes
2. If the decedent died intestate, the 1/2 passes under the intestate succession rules from Chapter 5

This is where the outside children rule applies. Under Rule A (no outside children), the decedent's 1/2 community share passes to the surviving spouse — effectively reunifying the whole 100% in the surviving spouse. Under Rule B (outside children present), the decedent's 1/2 passes to all the decedent's children equally, leaving the surviving spouse with only her own 1/2.

### Worked Example

Community property real estate worth $400,000. Husband dies intestate. Wife is still alive. No outside children.

1. Wife's 1/2 of community: $200,000 (already hers, retains automatically)
2. Husband's 1/2 of community: $200,000 (passes under intestate succession)
3. Under Rule A (no outside children): Husband's 1/2 passes to Wife
4. **Final: Wife owns 100% of the $400,000 property**

Same facts, but with one outside child of the Husband:

1. Wife's 1/2 of community: $200,000 (already hers)
2. Husband's 1/2 of community: $200,000 (passes under Rule B)
3. Under Rule B: Husband's 1/2 passes to all his children equally (1 outside child + 0 inside children = 1 child takes it all, in this scenario)
4. **Final: Wife owns 50% ($200,000), outside child owns 50% ($200,000)**

The wife's share dropped from 100% to 50% because of one outside child. The outside child — someone the wife likely never met — now owns half the house.

This is exactly the fact pattern where operators make deals. Wife needs to sell. She can only sell her 50%. Outside child owns the other 50% and may not even know. You research, find the outside child, acquire from both sides, and consolidate to 100%.

## 6.7 Quasi-Community Property and Out-of-State Acquisitions

Couples who move to Texas from non-community-property states bring property with them that was acquired under different rules. Texas handles this through the doctrine of quasi-community property.

**Quasi-community property** is property that was acquired by either spouse while domiciled in a non-community-property state, but that would have been community property had they been domiciled in Texas at the time of acquisition.

### The Practical Rule at Death

If a couple moves from Ohio to Texas, and the wife dies in Texas, her Texas-situated real estate is treated as quasi-community for purposes of dividing her estate. The out-of-state property is treated under its home state's law.

### Why This Matters for Operators

Couples who moved to Texas during their marriage have property that might be characterized differently depending on when and where it was acquired. A family home purchased in Ohio in 1985, sold in 1998, with the proceeds used to buy a Texas home in 1999 — that Texas home might be traced to the Ohio home's separate or community character depending on the facts.

If you're working a deal involving an out-of-state couple or a recently-relocated family, check the dates and locations of property acquisitions. The classification may be more complex than it first appears.

## 6.8 Separate Property at Death

Separate real property is distributed differently than community real property. The rules are in Texas Estates Code § 201.002.

**If the Decedent Left a Surviving Spouse and Descendants**

1. Surviving spouse takes a 1/3 life estate
2. Descendants take the underlying fee

**If the Decedent Left a Surviving Spouse and No Descendants**

1. Surviving spouse takes 1/2
2. Parents, siblings, or descendants of siblings take the other 1/2

**If the Decedent Left a Surviving Spouse, No Descendants, No Parents, No Siblings**

1. Surviving spouse takes 100%

**If the Decedent Left No Surviving Spouse**

1. Property passes entirely to descendants, or if no descendants, to parents, siblings, etc. under the normal hierarchy

The separate property rules often surprise families because they produce different results than community property rules. A surviving spouse who assumed she inherited everything often discovers she has a 1/3 life estate and the kids own the house subject to her life estate — or that she shares ownership 50/50 with her deceased husband's siblings.

## 6.9 Partition and Exchange Agreements

Texas law allows spouses to modify their property arrangements through written agreements. Two main types:

**Partition or exchange agreements** (Texas Family Code § 4.102) let spouses convert community property into separate property. A couple can agree that a specific asset — say, a rental property — will be one spouse's separate property even though it would otherwise be community. These agreements must be in writing, signed by both spouses, and can be either before or during marriage.

**Premarital agreements** (prenuptial agreements, Texas Family Code § 4.001 et seq.) address how property will be classified and distributed if the marriage ends. Premarital agreements can include provisions about how specific assets will be characterized.

**Postmarital agreements** (similar to prenuptial but executed during marriage) are valid under Texas law for specific purposes but have limitations.

### Why This Matters for Operators

When you're analyzing a deal, check whether any partition or exchange agreements exist. If a couple signed a partition agreement converting community property to separate property, the rules for distribution at death are different than they would be for normal community property.

These agreements are rare but not unheard of. They're more common among wealthy couples, people with significant pre-marital assets, or couples who had prior marriages. The agreement might be recorded in county records if it affected real estate, or it might only exist in private papers. When a family mentions the existence of such an agreement, get a copy.

## 6.10 Divorce Cases and the Never-Finalized Divorce

One of the fact patterns that catches operators repeatedly: a couple separated but never formally divorced. The separation may have lasted for decades. Then one of them dies.

**The legal reality:** If the divorce was never finalized, the couple is still legally married. The surviving "spouse" has community property rights, surviving-spouse inheritance rights under Chapter 5, and homestead rights — regardless of how long they've been apart.

### Worked Example

Husband and wife separated in 1988. Wife moved to another state. They never divorced. Husband continued living in the marital home. Husband formed a new relationship and had a child with a new woman in 1995. Husband died intestate in 2020 without ever divorcing his wife.

**Legal Analysis**

1. The home was purchased during the 1988 marriage with community funds → community property
2. The wife, though separated for 32 years, is still his legal surviving spouse
3. The new woman is not a legal spouse — they never married
4. The child with the new woman is the husband's outside child for Rule B purposes
5. Under Rule B (outside children present), husband's 1/2 community share passes to all his children (zero with the wife, one with the new woman) → the outside child gets the husband's entire 1/2 community share
6. Legal wife keeps her 1/2 community share
7. **Final: Legal wife owns 50%, outside child owns 50%**

The new woman who lived with husband for 25 years has zero legal claim to the property. The legal wife who hasn't spoken to husband in 32 years owns half of it. The outside child owns the other half.

### Practical Implications

When evaluating any deal, verify marital status. Don't rely on the family's description. Pull marriage records. Check for divorce decrees. If no divorce was ever filed, the legal spouse has rights regardless of the apparent state of the relationship.

This is particularly common in communities where informal separations are more frequent than formal divorces. Lower-income families sometimes avoid divorce due to cost or lack of access to legal services. The result is legal spouses scattered across decades who still hold community property rights nobody in the current household recognizes.

## 6.11 Community Property Across Other States

The nine community property states share the basic framework but differ in specifics.

**Louisiana** uses French-derived civil law. It has forced heirship for certain classes of descendants — a parent cannot fully disinherit a child under specific circumstances, regardless of what the will says. Community property rules are otherwise similar to Texas.

**California** recognizes community property. Separate property rules are similar to Texas. California also recognizes "quasi-community property" for out-of-state acquisitions. Community property with right of survivorship is a common ownership form in California.

**Arizona, Nevada, New Mexico, Idaho, Washington, Wisconsin** — all community property states with similar general frameworks to Texas. Specific rules for characterization, management, and distribution vary. Consult state-specific counsel when working deals in these states.

### Common-Law States (Everywhere Else)

Common-law states don't use community property. They use a system called "separate property" in marriage — each spouse owns what's titled in their name, regardless of whether acquired before or during marriage. At death, the surviving spouse typically gets statutory rights:

**Elective share** — a spouse can elect to take a statutory share (often 1/3) of the decedent's estate instead of whatever the will says.

**Dower/curtesy** — older doctrines (largely abolished) giving a surviving spouse life estate rights in the other spouse's real property.

**Intestate share** — if the decedent died without a will, the surviving spouse typically takes a larger share than they would in a community property state.

The key practical difference: in community property states, the surviving spouse already owns 1/2 of acquired-during-marriage property as a matter of community ownership. Death doesn't transfer anything to them — they already had it. In common-law states, property titled in one spouse belongs to that spouse alone, and the surviving spouse takes only what the law gives them at death.

When you're working deals across state lines, verify which system governs the property. Don't assume Texas community property law applies to out-of-state real estate.

## 6.12 How Community Property Interacts with the Outside Children Rule

The community property rules together with the outside children rule from Chapter 5 is where most operators get confused. Here is the full interaction mapped explicitly.

### The Standard Case (Rule A — No Outside Children)

1. Property is community
2. Husband dies intestate
3. Wife is his only spouse, all children are theirs together
4. Wife's 1/2 community share: already hers (retains automatically)
5. Husband's 1/2 community share: passes to wife under Rule A
6. **Wife owns 100%**

### With Outside Children (Rule B)

1. Property is community
2. Husband dies intestate
3. Wife is his only spouse, but husband has at least one outside child
4. Wife's 1/2 community share: already hers
5. Husband's 1/2 community share: passes to all husband's children equally — NOT to wife
6. **Wife owns 50%, children together own 50% divided equally**

### If Property Is Separate (Not Community)

1. Property is husband's separate property (pre-marital, inherited, or gift)
2. Husband dies intestate
3. Wife and descendants survive
4. Wife gets 1/3 life estate (use of 1/3 during her lifetime)
5. Descendants get underlying fee
6. Outside children rule doesn't change this calculation — all of husband's descendants, inside and outside, share in the fee equally

### If Property Is Separate and No Descendants

1. Property is husband's separate property
2. Husband dies intestate
3. Wife survives, no children (no inside, no outside)
4. Husband's parents dead, but siblings survive
5. Wife takes 1/2
6. Siblings take 1/2 (divided among surviving siblings and representatives of deceased siblings)
7. **Wife owns 50%, husband's siblings/nieces/nephews own 50%**

### The Diagnostic Framework

Every deal analysis should proceed through these questions in order:

1. Was the decedent married at death?
2. Was the property community or separate?
3. Did the decedent have any descendants? (Including outside children)
4. Did the decedent have any parents, siblings, or descendants of siblings?
5. Under the applicable rules, who holds what share now?

Run this framework every time. It will catch almost every community property error.

## 6.13 Case Study — The Long Marriage with a Hidden Child

### Fact Pattern

Michael and Susan married in 1971. They had two children together, now middle-aged adults. They owned a house purchased in 1985 with funds earned during the marriage — clearly community property. The house is now worth $340,000 with no mortgage.

Michael died intestate in 2022. Susan assumed she inherited everything — 53-year marriage, clean family, house in her husband's name for sentimental reasons (he handled the paperwork).

Two months after Michael's death, Susan received a certified letter from an attorney representing "Robert Jameson, son of Michael Smith." Robert, age 51, claimed he was Michael's son from a relationship in the early 1970s — before Susan met Michael. Michael had never told Susan. Robert's mother had raised him alone. Robert had made peace with his missing father decades ago, but when he saw Michael's obituary, he realized his biological father had just died, and contacted an attorney.

Robert had a birth certificate showing Michael as the father (Michael had signed the acknowledgment of paternity in 1971, before ever meeting Susan) and DNA results from testing conducted with Robert's mother's cooperation.

### Legal Analysis

Michael died intestate. The house was community property. Under Rule B (outside child exists), Michael's community 1/2 share passes to all his children equally, not to Susan.

1. Susan's community 1/2: $170,000 (already hers, automatic)
2. Michael's community 1/2: $170,000, passing to three children (two inside + one outside)
3. Each child receives: $56,667 (one-third of Michael's 1/2)

### Final Ownership

| Owner | Share | Value |
|---|---|---|
| Susan | 1/2 (50%) | $170,000 |
| Inside daughter | 1/6 (16.67%) | $56,667 |
| Inside son | 1/6 (16.67%) | $56,667 |
| Robert (outside son) | 1/6 (16.67%) | $56,667 |

Susan's assumed 100% became 50%. Her two children, who never expected to inherit anything during her lifetime, now own 33.34% combined. Robert, the outside son nobody in the family knew existed, owns 16.67%.

### The Deal Opportunity

Susan and her children want to sell. They don't know Robert. They've never met him. He's a stranger demanding his share.

You, the operator, can help everyone. You acquire Susan's 50% and the children's 33.34% (with appropriate negotiation — they'd rather get their share now than wait for a contested sale). You then negotiate separately with Robert for his 16.67%.

Or you might acquire Robert's 16.67% first — he's probably motivated to settle, wants the money, has no emotional connection to the property. Then you use your 16.67% interest as a cotenant to pressure a negotiated resolution with Susan's family.

Either way, you're solving a problem. Susan's family can't sell without Robert. Robert can't sell his fractional interest to anyone except another family member (no retail buyer wants 16.67% of a house they can't possess). The operator stands in the middle, facilitates, acquires, and consolidates.

### What Susan Paid — And What She Should Have Paid

Susan initially consulted an attorney who told her to ignore Robert's claim and "see what happens." That was bad advice. Three months later, Robert's attorney filed a suit for partition. Susan spent $28,000 defending before settling at fair value. If Susan had hired a competent real estate attorney upfront to evaluate Robert's claim (which was clearly valid given the acknowledgment of paternity and DNA evidence), she could have negotiated a quicker settlement at lower cost — or sold the entire property through an operator who could handle the complexity.

The lesson for operators: Susan's family situation is exactly the kind of deal we work. Our value is that we come in with the research done, the claim evaluated, a path to resolution, and cash to close. We don't treat the outside child as an inconvenience; we treat them as a legal cotenant who needs to be acquired alongside the other heirs.

## 6.14 Community Property and Homestead — The Double Protection

A special note on homestead protections in Texas.

Texas grants homestead property additional protections beyond normal community/separate rules. The key homestead rules that affect operators:

**Conveyance of homestead requires both spousal signatures.** Regardless of community or separate characterization, both spouses must sign any conveyance of a homestead. This is protective of family stability — the law doesn't let one spouse sell the family home without the other's consent.

**Surviving spouse has homestead rights regardless of ownership.** A surviving spouse has the right to occupy the homestead for the remainder of her life (or until she voluntarily leaves), even if the homestead was the decedent's separate property and passes to someone else's descendants under the intestate rules.

This creates a practical life estate that may not show up in ownership records but that affects whether the property can be cleanly sold while the surviving spouse lives there.

### Operator Implications

When a surviving spouse is still alive and living in the property, you have two tracks to consider: acquiring ownership interests (from the legal title holders) AND resolving the surviving spouse's homestead rights (from the surviving spouse). Even if you've acquired 100% of the legal ownership, a surviving spouse's homestead right may prevent you from selling with clean title until the spouse releases or vacates.

This is why experienced operators often approach the surviving spouse first when they're still alive and living in the property. A homestead release is a key document. Without it, your acquisition is incomplete.

## 6.15 Key Takeaways for Operators

Before moving to Chapter 7, internalize these principles:

**Presumption is community.** Property possessed during marriage is presumed community unless proven separate by clear and convincing evidence.

**Three categories of separate property:** Pre-marital, inheritance/gift, personal injury recovery. Everything else is community.

**Inception-of-title rule.** Character is determined at acquisition. Later events don't retroactively change it (though they may create reimbursement claims).

**Title in one name doesn't mean separate.** A deed in one spouse's name can still be community property if acquired during marriage with community funds.

**Surviving spouse automatically retains their 1/2 of community.** This isn't inheritance — it's existing ownership. The decedent's 1/2 passes under succession rules.

**Outside children flip the community rule.** With no outside children, the spouse takes the decedent's 1/2 community share too. With any outside children, the decedent's 1/2 passes to all the decedent's children equally.

**Separate property has different rules.** 1/3 life estate to spouse when descendants exist. 1/2 to spouse and 1/2 to collateral heirs when no descendants exist.

**Never-finalized divorces leave legal spouses alive.** Long-separated couples who never divorced are still legally married. The legal spouse has full rights regardless of the relationship's apparent state.

**Homestead adds protection.** Both spouses must sign conveyances. Surviving spouses have homestead occupancy rights separate from ownership.
