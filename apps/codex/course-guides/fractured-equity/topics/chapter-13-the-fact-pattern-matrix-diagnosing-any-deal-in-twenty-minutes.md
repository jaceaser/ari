## 13.1 Why the Fact-Pattern Matrix Exists

You've learned the law (Part II). You've learned how to find deals (Chapter 11). You've learned how to research heirs (Chapter 12). Now you need a system for putting it all together.

The Fact-Pattern Matrix is that system.

When you encounter a new property, you face the same core question every time: is this a deal, and if so, which kind? Experienced operators answer that question in 15–30 minutes by running through a standard diagnostic. New operators can take weeks stumbling through the same analysis, often getting it wrong.

This chapter teaches you the diagnostic framework. We'll walk through the matrix step by step, show you how to apply it to real fact patterns, and give you the decision rules that tell you whether to pursue, pass, or escalate to advanced techniques.

The matrix isn't a theoretical framework. It's a working tool. After you read this chapter and work through a few deals, you'll run the matrix automatically in your head without consciously thinking about it. That's when you know you've internalized the business.

---

## 13.2 The Matrix at a High Level

The Fact-Pattern Matrix has four dimensions:

**Dimension 1: Fracture Type.** What kind of fracture does this property have? Ownership, title, possession, payment, or some combination?

**Dimension 2: Heir Landscape.** How many heirs? How complex? Any missing or hostile ones? Any outside children complicating ownership?

**Dimension 3: Title Condition.** Clean chain or defective? What curative work is required?

**Dimension 4: Deal Economics.** What's the equity spread? What are the acquisition costs likely to be? Is the net worth the effort?

Each dimension produces a classification. Combined, the four classifications identify the resolution pathway — the specific playbook that applies to this deal type.

---

## 13.3 Dimension 1 — Fracture Type Classification

The four fracture types were introduced in Chapter 1 and expanded in Chapter 2. Here's the practical diagnostic:

### Ownership Fracture (Type O)

**Signs:**
1. Multiple owners on the tax roll
2. Decedent's name still on tax records after death
3. Estate open in probate but not closed
4. "John Smith et al." style ownership

**Classification:**
1. **O1:** Single decedent, 2–5 heirs, clean relationships
2. **O2:** Single decedent, 6+ heirs, or complex family structure
3. **O3:** Multi-generational, deaths across 2+ generations
4. **O4:** Multi-generational with outside children flipping ownership rules

### Title Fracture (Type T)

**Signs:**
1. Chain of title has gaps, defects, or irregularities
2. Deed in chain has missing spousal signature
3. Tax sale or foreclosure with procedural questions
4. Corporate entity dissolved before conveying

**Classification:**
1. **T1:** Simple defect that can be cured with corrective deed
2. **T2:** Complex defect requiring quiet title action
3. **T3:** Multiple stacked defects across chain
4. **T4:** Dissolved entity conveyance requiring wind-up

### Possession Fracture (Type P)

**Signs:**
1. Life estate separate from remainder
2. Long-term occupant without recorded title
3. Adverse possession claim implicit or explicit
4. Homestead rights of surviving spouse

**Classification:**
1. **P1:** Living life tenant, cooperative family
2. **P2:** Living life tenant, hostile or uncooperative
3. **P3:** Adverse possession situation with documented occupancy
4. **P4:** Multiple possessory claims overlapping

### Payment Obligation Fracture (Type Y)

**Signs:**
1. Multi-year tax delinquency
2. HOA delinquency
3. Utility disconnections
4. Code enforcement violations

**Classification:**
1. **Y1:** Under 3 years delinquent
2. **Y2:** 3–6 years delinquent with no scheduled foreclosure
3. **Y3:** 3–6 years delinquent with scheduled foreclosure (time pressure)
4. **Y4:** Beyond normal delinquency, into emergency territory

### Stacked Fractures

Most real deals have more than one fracture type. Label them with compound codes:

1. **O2+Y3:** 6 heirs and scheduled foreclosure = urgent deal requiring rapid execution
2. **O3+T2+Y2:** Multi-generational, defective deed, moderate delinquency = complex long deal
3. **O4+T3+P2+Y4:** Every fracture stacked = the richest deal category, 6+ month timeline, large profits

The code shorthand lets you communicate with team members about deal types quickly.

## 13.4 Dimension 2 — Heir Landscape Classification

Once you've identified that a property has an ownership or possession fracture, you need to classify the heir landscape. This builds on your research from Chapter 12.

### Heir Count Category

1. **H1:** 1–3 heirs
2. **H2:** 4–8 heirs
3. **H3:** 9–15 heirs
4. **H4:** 16+ heirs

### Heir Cooperation Category

1. **C1:** All heirs cooperative (willing to engage, cooperate with paperwork)
2. **C2:** Mostly cooperative with 1–2 holdouts
3. **C3:** Mixed cooperation with significant resistance
4. **C4:** Hostile or largely non-responsive

### Heir Locatability Category

1. **L1:** All heirs located and contactable
2. **L2:** Most heirs located; 1–2 requiring additional research
3. **L3:** Several heirs missing; PI work likely required
4. **L4:** Multiple heirs untraceable; service by publication will be needed

### Outside Children Flag

1. **OC:No** — No outside children identified or suspected
2. **OC:Maybe** — Research inconclusive; further investigation needed
3. **OC:Yes** — Outside children confirmed; affects calculations

### Special Situation Flags

1. **MIN** — Minor heirs requiring guardian ad litem
2. **INC** — Incarcerated heir(s)
3. **INT** — International heir(s)
4. **CAP** — Heirs with capacity issues
5. **MIL** — Heirs in active military service

**Combined heir landscape code example:** H2 C2 L2 OC:No INC

Translation: 4–8 heirs, mostly cooperative with 1–2 holdouts, most located with some research remaining, no outside children suspected, one heir is incarcerated.

## 13.5 Dimension 3 — Title Condition Classification

From your title research — running a chain, pulling a title commitment, reviewing recorded instruments — classify the title condition:

### Chain Condition

1. **CH1:** Clean chain, no defects
2. **CH2:** Minor defect that can be cured with corrective deed
3. **CH3:** Significant defect requiring quiet title action
4. **CH4:** Multiple defects or dissolved entity issues

### Lien and Encumbrance Condition

1. **EN1:** Only current taxes and clean easements
2. **EN2:** Old mortgage lien requiring payoff; clean otherwise
3. **EN3:** Multiple liens requiring resolution
4. **EN4:** Complex encumbrance situation (federal tax liens, contested liens, etc.)

### Title Insurance Condition

1. **TI1:** Will be insurable with standard AOH
2. **TI2:** Will require JDH for insurability
3. **TI3:** Requires quiet title action before insurable
4. **TI4:** Multiple curative steps required for insurability

The title condition is critical because it determines both cost and timeline. A CH1 EN1 TI1 deal can close in 75 days. A CH4 EN3 TI3 deal is a 9-month project.

## 13.6 Dimension 4 — Deal Economics Classification

Apply Chapter 3's economic framework:

### Equity Spread

1. **$1:** Under $75,000 (below floor — pass unless exception applies)
2. **$2:** $75,000–$100,000 (minimum acceptable)
3. **$3:** $100,000–$200,000 (target range)
4. **$4:** $200,000–$500,000 (high-value deal)
5. **$5:** $500,000+ (rare, major deal)

### Estimated Total Costs

Apply Chapter 3's cost decomposition to estimate total expenses:

1. Heir payouts
2. Attorney fees
3. Notary/travel
4. Junk removal
5. Schedule C surprise budget
6. Realtor commissions
7. Title and closing
8. Financing costs

Sum to estimated total costs.

### Estimated Net Profit

Equity spread minus estimated total costs equals estimated net.

### Economic Classification

1. **E1:** Estimated net under $30,000 (marginal)
2. **E2:** Estimated net $30,000–$60,000 (acceptable)
3. **E3:** Estimated net $60,000–$100,000 (strong)
4. **E4:** Estimated net $100,000+ (excellent)

## 13.7 The Complete Matrix Code

Putting the four dimensions together produces a complete deal classification.

**Example 1:** O2 T1 Y3 / H2 C1 L1 OC:No / CH2 EN2 TI1 / $3 E3

Translation: Multi-heir ownership fracture with a minor title defect and scheduled foreclosure. 4–8 cooperative, locatable heirs with no outside children. Minor curative work, single mortgage to pay off, insurable with AOH. Target-range equity spread with strong projected net.

This is a great deal — clean execution, strong profit, moderate timeline.

**Example 2:** O4 T3 P2 Y4 / H3 C3 L2 OC:Yes INC / CH4 EN3 TI3 / $4 E4

Translation: Multi-generational ownership fracture with outside children, multiple title defects, hostile life tenant, emergency tax situation. 9–15 heirs with mixed cooperation, some location issues, confirmed outside children, one incarcerated. Complex title condition requiring quiet title plus lien resolution. Insurability requires multiple curative steps. High-value spread with excellent projected net.

This is a career deal — extremely complex, 8–12 month timeline, significant capital outlay, but $100K+ net at completion and likely multiple additional properties in the package.

**Example 3:** O1 T1 Y2 / H1 C1 L1 OC:No / CH1 EN1 TI1 / $2 E1

Translation: Single clear heir with a cooperative executor, small delinquency, clean title. One heir, all good. No curative issues. Acceptable minimum spread with marginal projected net.

This is a skip deal — net under $30K isn't worth the effort unless it's a package or strategic.

## 13.8 The 20-Minute Diagnostic — Applied

Here's how the matrix actually gets applied in practice. You've identified a tax-delinquent property. You're deciding whether to invest research time. Here's the 20-minute diagnostic that answers the question.

### Minute 1–3: Basic Property Data

1. Pull the tax record. Note current owner name, assessed value, years of delinquency, scheduled foreclosure date if any.
2. Note basic property info: address, legal description, size, property type.

### Minute 3–5: Quick Obituary Search

1. Search the owner name in obituaries.
2. Confirm whether the owner is deceased.
3. Note date of death.
4. Read the obituary for family structure clues.

### Minute 5–10: Rough Heir Count

1. From the obituary, count surviving family members.
2. Identify any predeceased relatives mentioned (potential sub-trees).
3. Look for signs of multiple marriages or blended family.
4. Estimate total current heir count.

### Minute 10–13: Quick Title Check

1. Pull the most recent recorded deed to the decedent.
2. Note any obvious chain issues (gaps, old dates, dissolved entity grantors).
3. Check for recorded liens visible in the county records.
4. Assess whether chain appears clean or problematic.

### Minute 13–16: Quick Economics

1. Estimate property as-is value (from appraisal district or quick MLS comparison).
2. Subtract delinquent taxes.
3. Subtract any obvious liens.
4. Estimate equity spread.
5. Quick mental check on whether spread exceeds floor.

### Minute 16–20: Matrix Classification

1. Apply Dimension 1: Which fracture types?
2. Apply Dimension 2: Approximate heir landscape code.
3. Apply Dimension 3: Approximate title condition.
4. Apply Dimension 4: Approximate economics.
5. Generate compound matrix code.
6. Make pursue/pass decision.

### Decision Output

**PURSUE — standard:** O1–O2, H1–H2, clean title, $3–E3 or better. Use standard acquisition playbook (Chapter 16 onward).

**PURSUE — complex:** O3–O4 or stacked fractures, any heir landscape, $3–E3 or better. Use complex acquisition playbook with appropriate budget and timeline.

**PURSUE — emergency:** Y3 or Y4 (time pressure). Fast-track research and contact if economics support.

**ESCALATE:** Need more research before deciding. Flag for secondary review.

**PASS — below floor:** Economics don't work. Log and move on.

**PASS — infeasible:** Title or heir situation too complex for the deal size.

After 20 minutes, you've made a decision about whether this property is worth another 10 hours of deeper research. That's the power of the matrix — it lets you triage.

## 13.9 Applying the Matrix to the Priority List

Chapter 11 taught you to filter raw tax delinquency lists down to 30–50 qualified candidates. Now apply the matrix to prioritize those candidates.

**Tier A (Work First)**
1. Complete matrix codes available
2. E3 or better economics
3. Simple to moderate fracture type
4. Time pressure if any (Y3 or Y4)

**Tier B (Work Second)**
1. Matrix codes available
2. E2 or better economics
3. Moderate complexity
4. No immediate time pressure

**Tier C (Deeper Research Needed)**
1. Classification uncertain
2. Economics promising but not verified
3. Complexity unclear

**Tier D (Pass or Defer)**
1. Economics clearly below floor
2. Complexity exceeds likely return
3. No viable resolution pathway identified

From your 30–50 qualified candidates, you'll typically end up with 5–10 Tier A deals (the ones you actively pursue), 10–15 Tier B deals (second priority), 5–10 Tier C deals (needing more research), and the rest in Tier D.

Work Tier A first. As those close out, promote Tier B and C candidates based on updated matrix analysis.

## 13.10 Common Fact-Pattern Codes and Their Playbooks

Certain matrix codes appear frequently enough that you should have standard playbooks for them. Here are the most common:

### "The Simple Intestate Death"

**Code:** O1 / H1–H2 C1 L1 OC:No / CH1 EN1 TI1 / $2–$3 E2–E3

**Characteristics:**
1. Single decedent
2. 2–5 cooperative heirs
3. Clean title
4. Modest delinquency

**Playbook:**
1. AOH approach (if death is 4+ years old)
2. Single-round negotiation with heirs
3. Title company handles closing
4. 60–90 day timeline
5. Expect $40K–$70K net

---

### "The Multi-Heir Coordination Deal"

**Code:** O2 / H2–H3 C1–C2 L1–L2 OC:No / CH1–CH2 EN1–EN2 TI1–TI2 / $3 E2–E3

**Characteristics:**
1. Single decedent, multiple heirs
2. Some research required
3. Clean or mildly defective title
4. Standard economics

**Playbook:**
1. Full heir research (Chapter 12)
2. JDH or AOH depending on maturity and underwriter
3. Parallel negotiation with all heirs
4. 90–150 day timeline
5. Expect $50K–$80K net

---

### "The Contested Estate"

**Code:** O2–O3 / H2–H3 C2–C3 L1–L2 OC:Maybe / CH1–CH2 EN1–EN2 TI2 / $3 E2–E3

**Characteristics:**
1. Some hostile heirs
2. Contested family dynamics
3. Underwriter may require JDH
4. Standard economics but longer timeline

**Playbook:**
1. Standard research plus extra care on outside children
2. JDH likely required
3. Leverage tools for holdouts (Part V)
4. 5–8 month timeline
5. Expect $50K–$80K net

---

### "The Multi-Generational Spread"

**Code:** O3–O4 / H3–H4 C2–C3 L2–L3 OC:Yes / CH2–CH3 EN1–EN2 TI2–TI3 / $3–$4 E3–E4

**Characteristics:**
1. 2+ generations of intestate deaths
2. Large heir count
3. Outside children present
4. Some title curative work
5. Strong economics

**Playbook:**
1. Comprehensive genealogy including outside children
2. JDH highly recommended
3. Package check for additional properties
4. Extensive heir acquisition
5. 6–12 month timeline
6. Expect $100K+ net, possibly much more

---

### "The Every-Fracture Deal"

**Code:** O4 T3 P2 Y4 / H3–H4 C3 L3 OC:Yes INC/INT / CH4 EN3–EN4 TI3–TI4 / $4–$5 E4

**Characteristics:**
1. All four fracture types stacked
2. Hostile and missing heirs
3. Multiple title defects
4. Complex liens and encumbrances
5. Large spread

**Playbook:**
1. Multiple legal tracks running parallel
2. Creditor-suit-to-sheriff's-sale pipeline for holdouts
3. Quiet title action for title defects
4. Waste doctrine for hostile life tenant
5. International genealogy if applicable
6. 9–18 month timeline
7. Expect $150K+ net, often much more
8. These are the career-defining deals

---

### "The Tax-Sale Pull-Back"

**Code:** Any O, T, or P / any H / any CH–EN / Y4 / $3–$4 E3–E4

**Characteristics:**
1. Scheduled tax sale within 30–90 days
2. Economics strong
3. Foreclosing entity may have service defects

**Playbook:**
1. Immediate title analysis
2. Verify service of process on all heirs
3. TRO or settlement negotiation with taxing authority's law firm if service defective
4. Rapid heir acquisition in parallel
5. Close before scheduled sale
6. 30–75 day timeline
7. Expect $50K–$150K+ net given the urgency premium

---

### "The Defunct Entity Deal"

**Code:** O or T / H1–H2 for entity owners / CH3–CH4 / $2–$4 E2–E4

**Characteristics:**
1. Property held by dissolved LLC or corporation
2. Members/officers may be deceased
3. Requires entity wind-up or reinstatement
4. Often hidden from general market

**Playbook:**
1. Entity research (Secretary of State records, old governance documents)
2. Reinstatement if possible, or court-ordered wind-up
3. Member/officer acquisition
4. 4–8 month timeline
5. Covered specifically in Chapters 36–38
6. Expect $50K–$100K+ net

## 13.11 When the Matrix Says "Walk Away"

The matrix also helps you say no. Certain codes should trigger automatic passes:

**Pass: E1 economics** (net under $30K). Unless it's in a package, a referral situation, or otherwise strategic, sub-$30K deals aren't worth the work.

**Pass: Infeasible title condition.** Some title defects can't be cured economically. A deed from 1963 with a forged signature involving parties who all died decades ago may not be curable at any cost that makes the deal work. Pass.

**Pass: Hostile heir structure with weak economics.** If the economics only work when all heirs cooperate, and the heir landscape shows significant hostility, the deal will collapse. Pass unless economics are strong enough to absorb legal process.

**Pass: Untenable time pressure with incomplete research.** A scheduled tax sale in 14 days, with unresolved heirship questions and no clear pathway — the odds of closing are too low. Pass or defer.

**Pass: Geographic scope beyond reach.** A deal in a county where you don't have attorney relationships, title company access, or local knowledge. Unless it's an exceptional opportunity justifying the investment, pass.

**Pass: Capital requirements exceed available capacity.** A deal that requires $80K in heir payouts when you have $50K in working capital. Pass or partner unless the deal is compelling enough to seek outside capital.

The discipline of passing on bad deals preserves capital and time for better deals. Operators who pursue everything they find end up with their pipeline clogged with marginal deals that never close, while better opportunities pass them by.

## 13.12 The Matrix in Team Operations

Once your business has multiple team members, the matrix becomes a shared language that accelerates communication.

### Standard Team Workflow

**Lead Qualification (done by research staff):** Applies Dimensions 1–2 based on public records. Assigns preliminary matrix code.

**Deep Research (done by research or analyst staff):** Applies Dimensions 3–4 based on title research and economic analysis. Refines matrix code.

**Deal Review (done by operator/principal):** Evaluates full matrix code. Assigns tier (A/B/C/D). Approves pursuit or rejection.

**Execution (done by acquisition staff):** Works assigned playbook based on matrix code.

**Status Updates:** Team members report status using matrix language — "O3 T2 deal in Travis — H3 is now H4, one grandchild emerged from outside relationship, regrading to O4."

The shorthand saves time. Instead of explaining every detail, team members communicate in standardized codes that everyone understands.

### Matrix-Based Reporting

1. How many deals in pipeline by matrix type?
2. Which matrix types are converting best?
3. Which matrix types are taking longest?
4. Which matrix types have highest average net?
5. Are we correctly classifying deals up front?

Tracking these metrics over time improves your judgment. You learn which fact patterns are actually worth your time.

## 13.13 Tracking and Learning from the Matrix

Every deal you work teaches you something. Capture the lessons in a structured way.

### Post-Deal Review on Every Closed Deal

1. What was the matrix code at qualification?
2. What was the matrix code at closing (did it change during the deal)?
3. What surprises emerged?
4. How long did it actually take versus estimate?
5. How did actual net compare to estimated net?
6. What would you do differently?

### Post-Deal Review on Every Passed or Failed Deal

1. What was the matrix code?
2. What triggered the pass (or the failure if you pursued and failed)?
3. Would you pass the same deal today?
4. What did you learn about this fact pattern type?

Over 20–40 deals, you'll build a personal dataset that sharpens your matrix judgment. You'll know which O3 deals actually convert (and which don't). You'll know which H3 deals can be worked efficiently (and which are money pits). You'll know which title defects are curable within budget (and which aren't).

This is how operators develop judgment. Not through reading books but through running the matrix on real deals, capturing outcomes, and updating their internal calibration.

## 13.14 Common Diagnostic Errors

New operators make predictable mistakes when applying the matrix. Watch for these:

**Error 1: Underestimating Heir Count**

Operators look at the obituary, count the listed survivors, and conclude "5 heirs." They haven't accounted for:

1. Children of predeceased siblings
2. Outside children
3. Grandchildren of deceased heirs
4. Spouses of deceased heirs (who may hold community property interests)

Actual heir counts often exceed obituary-based estimates by 30–50%.

**Error 2: Assuming Cooperation Based on Limited Contact**

An heir who sounded interested in one phone call may not be cooperative when paperwork arrives. Don't code C1 based on one conversation. Use C1 only after multiple touchpoints confirm cooperation.

**Error 3: Missing Outside Children**

The single biggest error. Specifically search for outside children in every deal with a married decedent. OC:No requires affirmative research, not just absence of mentions.

**Error 4: Underestimating Title Defect Costs**

A defective deed from 1974 might seem like "just needs a corrective deed." In practice, getting all the original parties (or their heirs) to sign a corrective deed is often impossible, and you end up needing a quiet title action. Estimate conservatively.

**Error 5: Over-Optimistic Economics**

Heir payouts always cost more than expected. Attorney fees run long. Schedule C surprises happen. Build buffers into estimates. If the E3 classification only holds with perfect execution, it's actually E2.

**Error 6: Ignoring Capital Constraints**

A great deal is still a bad deal if you don't have the capital to work it. Match deals to available capital, not just to attractive spreads.

**Error 7: Emotional Attachment to Specific Deals**

You'll find deals where the fact pattern is fascinating, the family dynamics are intriguing, and the puzzle is compelling. Your emotional interest in the puzzle is not the same as the economic viability of the deal. Let the matrix be your discipline.

## 13.15 Key Takeaways for Operators

Before moving to Chapter 14, internalize these principles:

**The matrix is a diagnostic tool, not a theoretical framework.** Use it on every deal.

**Four dimensions: Fracture Type, Heir Landscape, Title Condition, Deal Economics.** Every deal has a classification in each.

**The 20-minute diagnostic filters your pipeline.** Apply it to every candidate before committing deeper research time.

**Common fact patterns have standard playbooks.** Learn the top 6–8 patterns and their associated execution approaches.

**Pass decisions are as valuable as pursue decisions.** Bad deals clog pipelines.

**Matrix shorthand accelerates team communication.** Build the language into your operations.

**Track and learn from every deal.** Your matrix judgment sharpens with data.

**Common diagnostic errors are avoidable with discipline.** Watch for them specifically.

**The matrix doesn't replace judgment; it supports judgment.** Complex deals require experience that the matrix alone can't provide. Experienced operators use the matrix as a quick filter, then apply deeper analysis to candidates that pass the filter.
