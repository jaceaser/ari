## 6.1 What a MERP Deal Really Is

A MERP deal is not simply a house with a Medicaid problem. It is an estate property where a state recovery claim may affect negotiations, timeline, net proceeds, title clearance, or the willingness of heirs and professionals to move. The inexperienced operator fixates on the claim itself. The experienced operator sees the full acquisition stack.

The subject of this chapter is not Medicaid policy for its own sake. The subject is execution. In the field, a MERP deal is an estate deal where a Medicaid Estate Recovery Program claim or notice is part of the problem stack. It is rarely the only problem. These files usually arrive carrying death, heirship, probate uncertainty, tax delinquency, foreclosure pressure, title defects, occupants, access issues, and a family that is overwhelmed or frozen.

The operator's job is not to be impressed by the mess. The operator's job is to diagnose the stack, identify where authority can be created, control the timeline, price the risk correctly, and close the deal using lawful but creative acquisition methods. That is the focus of this chapter.

Texas is the illustrative jurisdiction throughout because that is where the language and examples in this Codex are grounded. Every other state has its own probate procedure, estate-recovery framework, and title practice. If the target property sits elsewhere, use qualified local counsel to translate the process.

### The Six-Problem Stack

When a MERP deal lands on your desk, you are typically looking at some combination of these six problems running simultaneously:

**1. Death and authority problem.** Nobody has current authority to sign or sell. There is no executor, no administrator, no active probate, and possibly no heirship document that a title company will accept. You cannot close without signing authority. Everything else is secondary to this.

**2. Heirship problem.** The heirs may be unknown, scattered, fighting, or missing. One heir may be easy to find and very motivated. Another may be unreachable. A third may be deceased with their own heirship problem layered underneath. The file cannot move to closing until every heir who holds an ownership interest has either signed or has been legally accounted for.

**3. Probate problem.** No administration is open, or the wrong administration is open, or the file is stale. A probate that was opened and abandoned years ago creates its own problems. A probate that was never opened is a different problem. An heirship determination that was done incorrectly is yet another. The probate landscape has to be mapped before you can decide how to build authority.

**4. Claim problem.** MERP, taxes, judgment creditors, liens, or combinations of them are reducing or threatening the estate. This is where most operators get stuck — they see the MERP claim and walk, without ever calculating whether the claim is negotiable, whether it is time-barred, whether the property has enough equity to absorb it, or whether there is a creative structure that gets the deal closed despite the claim.

**5. Property-control problem.** Occupants, no access, no utilities, neglect, theft, or active nuisance conditions. The physical asset may be deteriorating while you work the paper. Someone may be living in the house who has no legal right to be there but has been there for two years. The property may have been stripped or vandalized. You may not even be able to get inside to take photos.

**6. Timeline problem.** Tax foreclosure, code enforcement, family stress, or deterioration are eroding the opportunity. Every clock that is running against you costs money. The tax foreclosure sale is the most acute because once it happens, the equity that justified the deal may be gone.

This is why these deals feel impossible to ordinary investors. Each problem on its own is manageable. The difficulty is that they tend to arrive stacked together. That stack is also the reason spreads can be large. Most buyers, agents, wholesalers, and even some attorneys walk the moment the file requires coordinated problem solving.

## 6.2 First Contact With Heirs and Decision-Makers

The first conversation in a MERP deal is not about impressing anyone with knowledge. It is about reducing confusion and creating enough trust to keep the file moving. Families in these deals are often tired, embarrassed, suspicious, or grieving. They may have already been told by somebody that nothing can be done.

The opening should be calm, specific, and reality-based. You are identifying the property, acknowledging the estate issues, explaining that multiple items have to be solved before a closing can happen, and offering a path where you coordinate that cleanup as part of the transaction. Do not oversell certainty. Difficult estate deals reward controlled confidence, not bravado.

The operator also needs to identify who the real decision-makers are. Sometimes that is the independent administrator. Sometimes it is one strong heir carrying the entire file. Sometimes it is a sibling pair. Sometimes the legal signer and the emotional decision-maker are different people. A file can die if you only speak to the person with paperwork and ignore the person who actually controls the family consensus.

**Five things to establish in the first conversation:**

1. Identify the legal authority already in place, if any.
2. Identify who is emotionally driving the family decision.
3. Identify who is resisting, absent, or creating drag.
4. Identify what the family believes the house is worth and what they believe can be fixed.
5. Identify the time pressure: taxes, foreclosure, occupants, code, or maintenance collapse.

The first conversation is successful when the family understands that the deal is not simple, that there is a route forward, and that you are prepared to work through the route instead of disappearing when the first obstacle appears. That last point matters more than almost anything else. These families have seen people disappear. Staying is a differentiator.

## 6.3 Preliminary Diligence Before Contracting

Before you commit to a structure, complete the diligence picture. In these files, shallow diligence destroys deals because the visible problem is rarely the only problem. Underwrite from documents, county records, direct observation, and counsel input — not from what somebody told you over the phone.

**Six diligence tracks to complete before contracting:**

**1. Authority diligence.** Confirm whether probate is open, closed, pending, or absent. Identify whether there is an executor, administrator, heirship affidavit, small-estate route, court order, or no present authority at all. Do not guess — pull the county probate index.

**2. Heirship diligence.** Build the family tree. Confirm deaths, marriages, children, predeceased heirs, and any minors or incapacitated heirs whose interests create representative-signing issues. One missed heir can blow up a closing that took four months to build.

**3. Claim diligence.** Obtain the MERP notice or correspondence if it exists. Confirm tax delinquencies, tax-foreclosure posture, utility balances, code liens, HOA balances, mortgages, judgments, and municipal issues. The MERP claim amount matters — so does whether it is negotiable, whether a time-bar applies, and what the state's current recovery posture is for this type of property.

**4. Property diligence.** Confirm occupancy, access, utilities, theft, damage, nuisance conditions, and whether the property is still protectable or already collapsing. You need eyes on the property before you commit to anything.

**5. Title diligence.** Run the chain, identify recorded defects, check for deed gaps, unreleased liens, probate gaps, and insurability concerns. Get the title commitment early enough that its Schedule C exceptions inform your structure rather than surprise you at closing.

**6. Timeline diligence.** Identify every calendar that can beat you: foreclosure sale date, probate hearing date, code deadline, eviction timeline, insurance cancellation, vandalism pattern, and weather exposure. A deal that needed sixty days of work and has forty-five days before a tax sale has a different underwrite than a deal with six months of runway.

Do not underwrite from rumor. Underwrite from documents, county records, direct observation, and counsel input. The profit in these deals comes from solving facts, not inventing them.

## 6.4 Deal Structuring

MERP deals reward creative acquisition structure because the legal cleanup and the economic cleanup rarely happen on the same day. The best operators separate control from final closing, and final closing from final exit. Several pathways are common.

**Pathway 1 — Contract Early, Close Late.** This is the classic structure when the file needs time to clear authority, resolve claim posture, or let title get comfortable. You lock in the opportunity before the family loses momentum, then work the file forward while the contract holds the economics together. The family has certainty. You have control. The cleanup happens inside that structure.

**Pathway 2 — Assignment Structure.** Where the contract and title posture permit it, the operator secures the file, moves the cleanup forward, and assigns the position to a better-capitalized or better-situated end buyer. This is useful when the real value is in deal creation, family coordination, and solving the pre-closing chaos rather than in long-term ownership.

**Pathway 3 — Double Close.** Where assignment is undesirable or impossible, the operator closes into the problem, then closes out almost immediately to the end buyer or retail buyer. This is often cleaner for title presentation and cleaner for optics with the parties.

**Pathway 4 — Close, Stabilize, and Wholetail.** This is the structure many operators miss. The property does not need a full rehab to create a large spread. If the house can be closed in as-is condition, controlled legally, cleaned, secured, and cosmetically stabilized, a rapid retail resale can produce a second layer of profit. The estate chaos was the barrier to entry. Once you solve the chaos, a retail buyer sees a clean title and a clean house — not the six-problem stack you started with.

**Pathway 5 — Cleanup First, Contract After.** Less common, but sometimes necessary where authority is too weak or the family is too disorganized to sign a meaningful agreement until the probate or heirship picture is partially repaired. You do some of the work before you have a signed contract, which means you carry more risk. Use this pathway only when the other pathways are genuinely unavailable.

Hybrid structures are common. A file may start as a contract-early deal, convert into a double close, and end as a wholetail after possession issues are solved. Creative structuring is not a gimmick — it is sequencing. You solve the right problem in the right order.

## 6.5 Price and Terms

These deals are won in the underwriting, not in the storytelling. Price has to back out from the actual exit while fully respecting how expensive chaos can be.

**The underwriting waterfall:**

1. Estimated realistic exit value — not fantasy retail value.
2. Less required profit margin for the level of uncertainty.
3. Less cleanup and stabilization costs.
4. Less eviction, trash-out, lock-change, lawn, boarding, and security costs.
5. Less legal and probate costs.
6. Less title-curative costs and closing costs.
7. Less taxes, code, utilities, and any other balances that must be cured.
8. Equals the maximum all-in acquisition number.

Every one of those deductions has to be real. If you do not know what the eviction will cost in this county with this occupant type, find out before you offer. If you do not know what the probate attorney charges for the specific proceeding the file needs, get a quote before you offer. Guessing on any line item in this waterfall is how you give back the spread before you ever reach closing.

The agreement also needs time language that reflects the file. Conventional short closing periods can suffocate complex estate deals. You need enough runway to solve authority, title, and possession without giving the family an unlimited option to drift. Extensions, cleanup contingencies, and outside-closing dates should be set deliberately. A sixty-day extension at the operator's option with a notice requirement is a very different instrument than an open-ended "we'll close when we're ready" handshake.

## 6.6 Title, Probate, and Claim Coordination

In these files, title companies and attorneys are not scenery. They are part of the operating system. The wrong title company kills momentum. The wrong lawyer turns every answer into a lecture about why something is difficult. The right professionals help map a path and keep the file moving.

The operator's role is to coordinate, not to freelance legal advice. You are gathering the death records, family map, probate documents, notices, delinquency figures, and title facts so the attorney and title company can give focused guidance instead of starting blind every time a new issue surfaces. Showing up organized is not a minor thing — it is often the difference between a three-week title review and a three-month one.

**The five-step sequence for title and probate coordination:**

1. Solve authority first: who can actually sign.
2. Solve claim posture next: what must be paid, negotiated, or worked around for clear closing.
3. Solve title support next: what documents or orders the title company needs to insure.
4. Solve possession and property-control next: whether you can safely take the property and protect it.
5. Solve exit last: wholetail, wholesale, flip, hold, or hybrid.

A common mistake is trying to skip straight to the exit before authority and title have a believable path. Difficult estate deals close because the file is sequenced, not because somebody talks louder. The family does not need to be impressed. The title company does not need enthusiasm. They both need a file that is organized and moving in the right direction.

## 6.7 Occupancy, Access, and Property-Control Problems

MERP deals often come with physical and human problems that are more immediate than the paper problems. Occupants may not be paying. The property may be unsecured. Utilities may be off. Access may be blocked. Family members may be afraid to enter the property. The clock on deterioration is always running.

The operator has to distinguish between what can be solved before closing and what must be solved after closing. Trying to solve every physical problem before authority exists can waste time and increase liability. But ignoring a dangerous or rapidly collapsing property can destroy value before you ever reach the table.

**Five rules for physical property management in MERP deals:**

1. Secure legal access as early as possible.
2. Document condition before anybody touches the property.
3. Use the legal system for occupants — do not freelance removal.
4. Stabilize first: secure, clean, prevent further damage, stop easy theft.
5. Improve only to the level the chosen exit requires.

This is why wholetail frequently fits these deals. A house that looks impossible in estate posture may need only modest cleanup, primer, debris removal, and basic stabilization after closing to unlock a much stronger retail outcome. You are not building a show house. You are removing the visible chaos so a retail buyer can see a house instead of a problem.

## 6.8 Closing Mechanics

A clean closing on a dirty file is built long before the signing date. By the time the file reaches the table, every major question should already have an answer: who signs, what gets paid, what title will insure, what documents record, and what happens the moment funding occurs.

**Five things to confirm before the closing date is set:**

1. Confirm the exact signing authority and signature capacity. Who signs, in what legal capacity, and is that person legally able to do so?
2. Confirm every payoff and curing item that must be funded through closing. Surprises at the closing table on a complex estate deal are not surprises — they are preparation failures.
3. Confirm the title company's documentary requirements and recorded-instrument order.
4. Confirm possession plan: vacant at closing, post-closing occupancy agreement, or immediate legal action after closing.
5. Confirm the first seventy-two-hour property-control plan: locks, cleanout, lawn, photos, insurance, utilities, and contractor access.

In very strong files, the operator closes once and exits later. In thinner or more volatile files, same-day or near-term back-end disposition may be the safer route. The correct closing mechanics are the ones that protect margin while respecting actual risk.

## 6.9 Post-Closing Cleanup and Exit

Closing is not the end of the work. In many of these files, closing merely changes the kind of control you have. The immediate post-closing period is where operators either protect the spread or give it back.

**The post-closing sequence:**

1. Secure the property immediately.
2. Start formal eviction or possession process if occupants remain.
3. Remove debris and obvious nuisance conditions quickly.
4. Complete only the level of work needed for the chosen exit.
5. Reprice based on actual condition, not pre-closing assumptions.

Common exits include wholesale, wholetail, full retail rehab, or hold. The key is not to romanticize a bigger exit. If the spread is excellent on a wholetail after minimal stabilization, do not force a six-month rehab thesis onto a property whose main value was getting it closed in the first place. The deal was won in the acquisition. The exit just has to convert it.

## 6.10 Realistic Timeline and Cost

These are not weekend deals. Authority repair, family coordination, claim review, title underwriting, and possession problems all take time. The exact timeline depends on the file, but the operator should assume that difficult estate acquisitions require patience and structured follow-up rather than quick emotional bursts of effort.

**Three timeline tiers based on file complexity:**

**Fast file with existing authority and manageable claim stack:** 30 to 90 days. You may already have an executor or administrator, the MERP claim is documented and negotiable, title has a clear path, and the occupancy situation is manageable. This is the exception, not the rule, in MERP deals.

**Moderate file with heirship, title, and occupancy drag:** 90 to 180 days. You need to create authority through a probate proceeding or heirship document, the claim takes time to resolve, title has a few defects that need curing, and there is at least one occupant who will not leave voluntarily. This is the most common category.

**Heavy file with probate work, claim uncertainty, foreclosure pressure, and resistant occupants:** 6 months or longer. Everything is hard at once. You may need a full probate proceeding, significant claim negotiation, a contested title cure, and a formal eviction running simultaneously. These deals can produce exceptional results, but they require the full toolkit and real patience.

The cost profile also rises quickly if the operator guesses instead of sequences. Attorney time, title work, cleanup, taxes, eviction, insurance, lawn, boarding, and carrying costs can all eat margin. Underwrite pessimistically and let the actual result surprise you on the upside.

## 6.11 Key Takeaways

**MERP deals are not about Medicaid trivia.** They are about closing complicated estate properties when a MERP claim is one problem among many. The operator who wins these deals understands that the claim is just one item in the stack — not the defining feature.

**The operator's edge is sequencing.** Solve authority. Solve claim posture. Solve title. Solve possession. Then solve exit. Attempting to skip any step in that sequence either wastes money or kills deals.

**Creative methods do not mean reckless methods.** Contract-early structures, assignments, double closes, wholetails, delayed closings, and hybrid approaches are all tools. The power is in knowing which tool fits which file — and in having the patience to use the right one instead of the fastest one.

**Physical problems and legal problems must be handled on separate clocks.** Do not ignore the property, and do not outrun the paperwork. The property is deteriorating while you solve the paper. The paper creates the authority you need to protect the property. Both tracks have to be managed simultaneously and deliberately.

**These deals can produce excellent spreads precisely because most people walk away when the file becomes multi-layered.** The spread belongs to the operator who can stay calm, stay organized, and keep the file moving when every other buyer has already left the room. That is the real competitive advantage — not knowledge, not relationships, not capital. It is the willingness and the skill to stay in the file when everybody else walked.
