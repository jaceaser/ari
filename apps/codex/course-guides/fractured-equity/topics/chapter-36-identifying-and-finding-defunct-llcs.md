# Chapter 36: Identifying and Finding Defunct LLCs

*The Stranded-Entity Opportunity*

## A Note Before We Begin

This Bonus Section uses Texas as its illustrative jurisdiction. Every statutory citation, every procedural description, every example is drawn from Texas law and Texas practice. But the underlying strategy — acquiring real property through defunct, dissolved, or abandoned legal entities and through dormant trusts — is not a Texas-only play. It works in every state. The names of the statutes change. The filing procedures change. The reinstatement thresholds change. The title insurance treatment changes. But the core opportunity exists wherever there are real property records, business entities that can become inactive, and trusts that can become dormant.

Before you implement any strategy in this Bonus Section, consult a real estate attorney licensed in the state where the property sits, a CPA or financial professional familiar with the entity and tax considerations of that state, and the title professionals who will ultimately insure your transaction. Verify the specific statutes, the specific procedures, the specific fees, and the specific pitfalls that apply in your state. Do not assume that what I describe under Texas law applies identically anywhere else. The concepts port. The procedures do not.

With that said, what follows is the Texas implementation of a strategy you can adapt anywhere. Read it for the methodology, then translate it to your state with qualified professional help.

---

## 1.1 The Opportunity

Somewhere in every county in Texas there are pieces of real property sitting on the deed records owned by LLCs that no longer legally exist. The LLC forfeited its charter five years ago for failure to pay franchise taxes. The members scattered or died. The registered agent address is outdated. Nobody has authority to sign a deed. Nobody is paying property taxes, or somebody is paying them without having any clear path to the property. The title is locked inside a dead entity, and the property is, for most practical purposes, stranded.

These stranded properties are an acquisition channel most operators do not understand. Title companies will not insure transactions on property held by a forfeited entity without cleanup. Real estate agents will not list because there is no legally competent seller. Traditional buyers will not touch the situation. The owners of record — the members, the managers, the heirs of deceased members — often do not realize they have a problem until someone explains it to them, and often cannot afford the legal work to fix it on their own. The operator who understands the diagnostic tools, the procedural pathways, and the practical implementation can step into that vacuum.

This is a fractured-equity-adjacent strategy. The underlying logic is the same as what you have learned throughout this Codex: title exists in a form that cannot be transacted against, the people with legal authority are diffused or missing, and a specific procedural toolkit resolves the situation. What changes is the statute Codex you work from. Instead of the Texas Estates Code, you work from the Texas Business Organizations Code. Instead of finding heirs, you find members. Instead of heirship determinations, you run reinstatements, membership interest acquisitions, or statutory winding-up proceedings. The operator who has mastered the heirship work already has most of the skill set — the breadcrumbing, the procedural patience, the comfort working in the slow-moving records of the state and county — needed to do this work too.

## 1.2 Why LLCs Become Defunct

Understanding why LLCs end up defunct is the first step toward finding them systematically. The common causes, each of which produces a recognizable signature in the records:

### Franchise Tax Forfeiture

By far the most common cause in Texas. Every Texas LLC must file annual franchise tax reports with the Texas Comptroller of Public Accounts. When an LLC misses filings for long enough — typically more than a year — the Comptroller certifies the forfeiture to the Secretary of State, and the entity's right to transact business is forfeited under **Tex. Tax Code § 171.251**. The entity still exists as a matter of record, but it has lost its legal capacity to sue, defend, or transact. Property titled in the entity's name stays titled in the entity's name, but the entity can no longer legally sign a deed conveying that property.

### Administrative Dissolution by the Secretary of State

The Secretary of State has separate authority under **Tex. Bus. Orgs. Code Chapter 11** to involuntarily terminate entities that fail to meet filing requirements — maintaining a registered agent and registered office, filing required periodic reports, responding to notices from the Secretary of State. Administrative termination is less common than tax forfeiture but happens, particularly with out-of-state owners who let the registered-agent service lapse.

### Voluntary Termination Without Winding Up

Sometimes the members decide they are done with the LLC and file termination paperwork, but they never complete the winding-up process. The statutory winding-up procedure under **Tex. Bus. Orgs. Code §§ 11.051 through 11.055** requires distributing assets, notifying creditors, and other specific steps. When those steps are skipped, the entity may be terminated on paper while property still sits in its name on the deed records. This creates a peculiar situation — the entity technically no longer exists, but the title has never moved out of its name.

### Abandonment After Member Death

A sole member dies. The surviving family does not know what to do with the LLC, does not understand that it owns real property, or decides to do nothing. Franchise tax filings lapse. Forfeiture follows. The property sits in the LLC's name, the LLC sits in forfeiture, and the member's estate may or may not have been probated. Often the person who signed for the LLC on property records died ten or fifteen years ago and nobody in the current family even remembers the entity existed.

### Member Disputes

Two or more members fell out. One wants to sell the property; the other does not. One refuses to sign franchise tax reports; the other cannot compel him to. The LLC falls into forfeiture through paralysis rather than through neglect. These situations can be particularly productive for operators who understand how to negotiate with estranged co-members — because at least one of the members is usually motivated to get the property unstuck.

### Out-of-State Owners Who Did Not Understand Texas Obligations

A California investor bought a Texas rental property through a Texas LLC in 2008. He did not fully understand that the Texas entity required ongoing franchise tax filings separate from his California compliance. Filings lapsed. Forfeiture followed. The investor may still own other properties, may still be reachable, and may be grateful to learn that his stranded Texas asset can be unstuck.

### Single-Purpose LLCs for Deals That Went Sideways

A developer formed a single-purpose LLC to hold one parcel for an intended development. The development never happened — financing fell through, market conditions changed, a partner backed out. The parcel sat. The LLC's annual filings stopped. Over time, the LLC went into forfeiture, the parcel sat on the deed records, and the developer moved on. These are often clean cases because one person made all the relevant decisions and that person is findable.

### Generational Loss

An LLC formed by an older generation for a rental or investment property. The original members died. The heirs inherited the membership interests but never understood the entity mechanics or the filing obligations. The LLC fell into forfeiture, and the heirs may not even know that formalities were neglected. When found, these heirs are often happy to have someone help them resolve the mess.

## 1.3 Why This Opportunity Has Not Been Mined Out

Real estate operators are generally good at finding opportunities, and opportunities that are obvious to many get bid up or mined out quickly. Defunct LLC acquisitions have stayed open as a channel for specific reasons:

1. Most investors do not understand Texas entity law well enough to recognize a defunct LLC on the deed records, let alone know what to do about it
2. Title companies will not insure transactions on property held by a forfeited entity without cleanup, which discourages casual buyers
3. Traditional sellers — real estate agents, foreclosure buyers, wholesalers — cannot easily move these properties, so they do not compete for them
4. The cleanup work requires coordination among entity law, property law, and sometimes probate or heirship law — few operators have all three skill sets
5. The members of the defunct LLC often do not realize they own a problem until an operator explains it to them
6. Even when members realize, they often lack the capital and the knowledge to reinstate, clear, and sell the property themselves
7. The properties do not show up in ordinary marketing channels — they are not listed, not at auction, not in the usual wholesaler pipelines

The combination of specialized knowledge, specialized relationships with title companies, and specialized acquisition procedures creates a moat that keeps most operators out of this channel. For the operator who invests in learning the work, the channel can be consistently productive.

## 1.4 State Variation

Before going further, the same reminder I opened with. The Texas specifics in this chapter — franchise tax, forfeiture certification, Secretary of State administrative termination, reinstatement under **Tex. Tax Code § 171.313**, winding up under **Tex. Bus. Orgs. Code Chapter 11** — are Texas-specific. Other states have analogous concepts with different names, different thresholds, and different procedures:

1. California uses Franchise Tax Board suspension and different reinstatement procedures
2. Florida uses administrative dissolution by the Division of Corporations with its own reinstatement framework
3. Delaware uses a different fee-based forfeiture mechanism with its own revival procedures
4. New York has separate state-tax-related and filing-related dissolution tracks
5. Each state's real estate title treatment of property held by a dissolved entity has its own nuances, its own typical title insurance positions, and its own local attorney expertise

If you are working in a state other than Texas — or if your Texas target happens to be an LLC organized under another state's laws that registered to do business in Texas — you need qualified local counsel to translate what follows into your jurisdiction. This Codex is not a substitute for that translation.

## 1.5 Who We Are Looking For

Before we work through the finding techniques, be clear about who the relevant people actually are. When you identify a defunct LLC holding property, the people whose cooperation or whose interest you need to acquire fall into several categories — and your acquisition strategy will shift depending on who is in each category.

### The Members

**Members** are the owners of an LLC. In a Texas LLC, membership interests are the economic and governance rights in the entity. Depending on the LLC's structure, there may be one member or many. Members are typically not listed in the public filings — the Secretary of State filings name the organizer, the registered agent, and sometimes the managers, but not the members. Members are identified through the operating agreement, through tax filings, or through other documentation that may or may not be easily accessible.

### The Managers

**Managers** run the LLC on behalf of the members. In a manager-managed LLC, managers have authority to bind the entity in its operations. In a member-managed LLC, the members themselves are the managers. Managers are often listed in public filings, particularly in the entity's certificate of formation or in periodic reports. Identifying the managers gets you one leg of the authority question — but you will usually also need to identify the members, because major decisions like selling real property typically require member approval even in manager-managed LLCs.

### The Organizer

The **organizer** is the person who filed the certificate of formation that created the LLC. The organizer is named in the certificate of formation but is often not a member — often the organizer is a lawyer, a filing service, or a paralegal who filed the paperwork on behalf of the actual owners. The organizer is useful as a potential lead to the actual members, but the organizer is not usually someone whose approval you need for the transaction.

### The Registered Agent

The **registered agent** is the person or entity designated to receive legal process on behalf of the LLC. Registered agents are always listed in public filings. They can be individuals (often a member or the member's lawyer) or commercial registered agent services (CT Corporation, Incorp Services, Cogency Global, and so on). The registered agent may or may not have current contact information for the members — commercial registered agents typically have at least a billing contact on file, which can be the first lead back to a real person.

### Heirs of Deceased Members

When a member has died, the membership interest typically passes through the member's estate to the member's heirs. This is where this work crosses back into the Part VI territory of the Codex. If a member died and his estate was properly probated, the personal representative of the estate or the specific heir who received the membership interest has authority. If the estate was not probated, you are now doing heirship work — running a determination of heirship for the decedent to establish who inherited what, including the membership interest in the defunct LLC.

### Secondary and Successor Parties

- Creditors of the LLC with claims against the entity's property
- Tax authorities — the Texas Comptroller for franchise tax debts, county tax assessor-collectors for property tax debts
- Buyers who may have had prior contracts with the LLC for the property and never closed
- Tenants of the property who may have occupancy rights
- Judgment creditors with liens recorded against the LLC or against specific members

The complete picture of "who we're looking for" usually includes people from multiple categories above. Your acquisition plan will be shaped by who you find, who you cannot find, who is cooperative, and who is not.

## 1.6 Finding Defunct LLCs — The Core Sources

Now the practical work of finding defunct LLCs that hold real property. The sources, in rough order of productivity:

### Secretary of State Entity Search

The Texas Secretary of State maintains a searchable online database of all entities filed in Texas. The search lets you:

1. Look up any entity by name or entity file number
2. See the entity's current status — active, franchise tax forfeiture, voluntarily terminated, administratively terminated
3. See the date of each status change
4. See the registered agent and registered office address
5. See the date of formation and the entity file number
6. See the full history of filings on the entity

The Secretary of State search is the primary diagnostic tool for any defunct-LLC lead. When you encounter an LLC in the deed records, your first move is to look up its current status. If the status is anything other than "In existence" or "Active," you have a potential defunct-LLC opportunity.

### The Texas Comptroller's Franchise Tax Account Status

Separate from the Secretary of State records, the Texas Comptroller maintains franchise tax account status for each entity. Tools like the Comptroller's Taxable Entity Search show:

1. Whether the entity is in good standing for franchise tax purposes
2. Whether the entity is in forfeiture for failure to file reports or pay taxes
3. Whether the entity has been reinstated
4. Sometimes additional details on the nature of the forfeiture

The Comptroller and Secretary of State records sometimes differ because they track different things. An entity may appear active at the Secretary of State but be in franchise tax forfeiture — which for practical purposes is still a defunct-entity situation.

### County Real Property Records Cross-Referenced With Entity Status

This is the systematic finding technique. The process:

1. Pull deed records for a county or area of interest
2. Filter for grantees that are LLCs, corporations, partnerships, or trusts rather than individuals
3. For each entity-held property, look up the entity's current status at the Secretary of State and Comptroller
4. Flag any property where the entity is in forfeiture, administratively terminated, or voluntarily terminated without apparent completion of winding up
5. Further filter by property characteristics — type, value, apparent vacancy, tax status

In most counties, the deed records are searchable online or through the county clerk's office. Automated services can speed this up substantially, but the manual version is workable for operators starting out in a specific county.

### Franchise Tax Forfeiture Lists

The Texas Secretary of State periodically certifies batches of entities that have been forfeited for franchise tax reasons. While the specific bulk-data availability and format can change, the operational point is that large numbers of entities move into forfeiture on a predictable cycle, and identifying which of those entities hold real property is the finding exercise. Some commercial data providers aggregate this information and sell it. Some operators build their own lists by combining Comptroller data with deed record research.

### Tax Delinquency Lists

County tax assessor-collectors maintain lists of properties with delinquent property taxes. These lists are public and are frequently published by law firms that handle delinquent tax collection for the county (Linebarger Goggan Blair & Sampson; McCreary, Veselka, Bragg & Allen; Perdue Brandon Fielder Collins & Mott; and others depending on the county).

Cross-reference delinquent property tax lists with entity ownership. Where the delinquent owner is a forfeited LLC, you have a double-stranded situation — an entity in forfeiture holding a property in tax delinquency. These properties are particularly motivated because the tax foreclosure clock is running, creating urgency for the members to resolve the situation.

### Probate and Heirship Filings

Probate court filings sometimes reveal defunct LLCs as estate assets. When a member of a now-forfeited LLC has died and his estate is being probated, the inventory or administrator's documents may identify the LLC and its property. Reviewing probate filings regularly in target counties produces leads that have both an entity problem and an heirship problem — and the operator who understands both can resolve the situation when nobody else can.

### Assumed Name Certificates

Texas entities that do business under an assumed name file assumed name certificates at the county level. These filings sometimes reveal defunct LLCs operating under alternate names, and sometimes reveal the human identities behind entities that otherwise show only on paper.

### UCC Filings

Uniform Commercial Code filings at the Texas Secretary of State show secured transactions. When a defunct LLC was a debtor under a UCC filing, the filing names the debtor's address at the time — sometimes providing a lead to the members' historical contact information.

### Litigation Searches

Texas court records, both state and federal, sometimes reveal defunct LLCs through litigation in which the entity was a party. Case captions, pleadings, and judgments provide information about the people behind the entity. Occasionally a prior lawsuit will have identified the members by name and current address.

### Property Management Red Flags

Properties exhibiting signs of absentee or neglected ownership — unmowed lawns, deferred maintenance, vacancy, vandalism, squatters — are worth checking against the entity ownership records. When the owner is an entity and the entity is defunct, the property's visible condition often matches the entity's administrative condition.

### Commercial Data Providers

Services like PropertyRadar, Reonomy, ATTOM Data Solutions, and specialized entity-status providers aggregate property data with entity status data. Subscription costs vary from modest to substantial. The value of paid data is that it can surface defunct-entity-held properties systematically rather than one at a time.

## 1.7 Initial Qualification

Once you have a list of defunct LLCs holding real property, not every lead is worth pursuing. Initial qualification filters the list down to the subset worth the time investment.

### Property-Level Qualification

| Factor | What to Assess |
|--------|---------------|
| Property type | Single-family, multi-family, commercial, raw land, agricultural — does it match your acquisition strategy? |
| Property condition | Habitable, needs work, dilapidated, vacant lot |
| Approximate current market value | At minimum a rough estimate from county appraisal data and comparable sales |
| Tax status | Current, delinquent, in foreclosure process |
| Liens and encumbrances | Mortgages, mechanic's liens, tax liens, judgment liens, HOA liens |
| Physical access | Can you view the property, or is it gated, locked, or inaccessible? |

### Entity-Level Qualification

| Factor | What to Assess |
|--------|---------------|
| Date of forfeiture or dissolution | How long has the entity been defunct? |
| Franchise tax balance owed | Sometimes a number that makes reinstatement uneconomic |
| Nature of the forfeiture | Tax forfeiture is usually reinstate-able; certain other terminations are more complex |
| Prior litigation history | Any pending cases or unresolved judgments? |
| Number of members, if ascertainable | Sole-member LLCs are often cleaner than multi-member situations |
| Registered agent currency | Is the registered agent still in business and able to forward communications? |

### Economic Qualification

1. Estimated cost of reinstatement and cleanup — franchise tax back-payments, attorney fees, title work
2. Estimated cost of acquisition from the members
3. Estimated property value after cleanup
4. Estimated exit value if you execute your planned exit
5. Net margin after all costs
6. Timeline to close and the holding costs during that timeline

### The Filter

After qualification, you will typically reduce an initial list of dozens or hundreds of defunct-LLC-held properties to a short list of a handful worth active pursuit. The short list is where the real work begins — deeper entity history, deeper property diligence, and outreach to find the members.

## 1.8 Finding the People Behind the Entity

Once a target is qualified, you need to find the actual people whose cooperation you need — the members, the managers, and if any are deceased, their heirs. The methodology is exactly the methodology from Chapter 30 of the main Codex: **The Social Breadcrumbing Method** runs first, producing intel, followed by **The Advanced Breadcrumbing Skip Tracing Method**, layered with specialized trails triggered by what the social work produces. The difference in this context is only the starting points — what you have to begin with, and what records you pull first.

### What You Already Have

When you qualify a defunct-LLC target, you already have:

1. The entity's full Secretary of State history
2. Names of any organizers, registered agents, managers named in public filings
3. Any addresses named in those filings
4. The certificate of formation — sometimes with identifying information about the initial governing persons
5. The deed records showing who signed the deed on behalf of the LLC when the property was acquired
6. Any UCC filings naming the entity and its addresses
7. Any litigation captions naming the entity

### The First Move — Deed Signatory

Often the single most valuable piece of information is already in the deed records. When the LLC acquired the property, someone signed the deed as an "authorized person" or "manager" on behalf of the entity. That signature identifies a specific person — by name, and often with an address in the acknowledgment block. That person is almost always a member or a manager of the LLC. Start there.

### The Second Move — Registered Agent

The registered agent is public record. If the registered agent is an individual, that individual is a lead to the members. If the registered agent is a commercial service, contacting the commercial service typically produces a forwarding address or at least confirms whether the registered agent relationship is still active. A commercial registered agent that has lost contact with its client entity is a signal that the entity is truly dormant — but the commercial agent may have a last-known billing address for the client.

### The Third Move — Certificate of Formation

The certificate of formation filed at formation names the organizer and sometimes the initial governing persons (members or managers). Even if the organizer was just a lawyer or filing service, the certificate often contains contact information that points back to the actual owners — a post office box, a physical address, sometimes a phone number.

### The Fourth Move — Method 1 Starting Point

With the deed signatory name and address, the registered agent information, and the certificate of formation contacts, you now have multiple names and addresses to run through the Social Breadcrumbing Method. Apply Chapter 30 directly:

1. Skip-trace each known person for current contact information
2. Search obituaries for each name — a signatory or manager may have died
3. Look for each person on social media
4. Contact neighbors at the last-known addresses
5. Contact associates — if the signatory was a lawyer, you can often contact the law firm; if a filing service, you can contact the service
6. Follow connected entities — sometimes the same person organized multiple LLCs, all sharing a network of related contacts

### Deceased Members — Bridging to Heirship Work

When a member of the defunct LLC has died, the Part VI tools from the main Codex come into play. A deceased member's membership interest has passed through his estate to his heirs. To identify and acquire that membership interest, you may need to:

1. Confirm the death and obtain the death certificate
2. Identify whether the estate was probated and if so, who received the membership interest
3. If the estate was not probated, pursue a determination of heirship for the decedent that identifies the inheritors of the membership interest
4. Coordinate with the estate's personal representative or the identified heirs

This is the characteristic bridge between defunct-entity work and heirship work — many defunct LLCs have at least one deceased member, and resolving the entity therefore requires working through the estate of that member. The operator who has mastered both skill sets can resolve situations that stymie operators who only know one side.

### The Member Identification Problem

Unlike the deed signatory, the organizer, and the registered agent — who are typically named in public records — the members of the LLC are frequently not in public records. Texas generally does not require the Secretary of State filings to list members. Member identity is typically contained in:

1. The LLC's operating agreement — a private document often not in public files
2. The LLC's tax returns — private and not publicly accessible
3. Sometimes, the registered agent's internal records
4. Sometimes, bank records if the LLC maintained accounts
5. Sometimes, litigation filings that identified the members
6. Sometimes, prior property acquisitions or refinances that required lender identification of members

Tracking down the **operating agreement** is often the central investigative task. Sources include the original organizer (if a lawyer, his file may still exist), the registered agent, the accountant who handled the LLC's tax filings (if identifiable from the Comptroller records or from prior documents), or the members themselves once one or more have been identified through other channels.

### Working Without the Operating Agreement

Sometimes the operating agreement is genuinely lost. Nobody has a copy. The lawyer who drafted it has died or retired without preserving the file. In those situations, your attorney will advise on a default-rules approach — Texas default rules for LLC governance under the Texas Business Organizations Code apply in the absence of a written operating agreement. Member identification then depends on whatever documentary evidence exists in the record — tax filings, banking records, statements of members that can be located, and sometimes sworn affidavits from people who knew the LLC's operations.

## 1.9 Scenario Patterns

Defunct-LLC situations tend to cluster into recognizable patterns. Knowing the patterns helps you qualify faster and plan the approach more efficiently.

### The Sole-Member Abandonment

A single-member LLC where the member moved out of state, got busy with other things, or simply walked away. Filings lapsed. Forfeiture followed. The property sits. When you find the sole member, often a single conversation resolves the direction of the deal — either he wants to reinstate and sell, or he wants to abandon further, or he had forgotten he owned it altogether. Single-member situations are usually the cleanest and fastest to resolve.

### The Deceased-Member Stranding

A member died, often years ago. The family knows the LLC existed but does not know what to do with it. No probate was filed, or the probate did not explicitly address the membership interest. The property has been sitting, taxes perhaps paid from family funds or perhaps not, and nobody has current authority. Resolution requires heirship work on the deceased member first, then entity reinstatement or winding up.

### The Out-of-State Investor

The LLC was formed in Texas by an investor from California, New York, Florida, or elsewhere. The investor never fully understood Texas franchise tax. Filings lapsed. The investor may still own many other properties and may not have realized the Texas entity was in forfeiture until you contacted him. These situations are often cooperative — the investor wants to resolve the problem but needs a partner with Texas expertise to do so.

### The Estranged Co-Members

Two or more members who had a falling out. One has been blocking franchise tax filings, or both have refused to cooperate with each other for years. Reaching out to the member who wants to sell, and then negotiating an acquisition structure that either buys out the blocking member or routes around him, is the technique. Sometimes partition or winding-up proceedings force the blocking member to the table.

### The Developer Who Moved On

A single-purpose LLC formed for a development that never happened. The developer is alive, reachable, and usually relieved to resolve the stranded asset. These are typically cooperative deals with a well-defined property and a well-defined history.

### The Lost-to-Time Entity

An LLC from fifteen, twenty, or more years ago. Original members have died. Their children and grandchildren have only fragmentary knowledge that the LLC ever existed. The property has been forgotten. In these cases, you are doing extensive breadcrumbing just to identify the people whose interest you need to acquire. The work is longer and more expensive, but the property is often undervalued because so few operators can navigate the complexity.

### The Tax-Foreclosure-Pending Stranding

A defunct LLC where property taxes have not been paid for years. Tax foreclosure is either pending or imminent. The members may be findable but are either unable to pay or unaware of the urgency. This is a high-time-pressure version of the opportunity — resolution requires coordinating entity reinstatement, member acquisition, and tax-lien resolution, often simultaneously.

## 1.10 Building a Systematic Target List

Operators who succeed at this work typically do not wait for defunct-LLC opportunities to find them. They build a systematic process that identifies target properties continuously. A working approach:

### Define the Geographic Footprint

1. Specific counties where you have title company relationships, attorney relationships, and market knowledge
2. Counties with deed records that are searchable online — reduces research cost significantly
3. Counties with enough volume of entity-held property to produce regular leads

### Define the Property Profile

1. Property types you are equipped to buy — single-family, multi-family, land, commercial
2. Value ranges that match your capital position and deal criteria
3. Condition tolerance — are you equipped to handle dilapidated or needs-work properties

### Establish Recurring Research Cadence

1. Pull new deed records in target counties on a regular cycle — monthly or quarterly
2. Cross-reference new and existing entity owners against Secretary of State status
3. Pull franchise tax forfeiture certifications as they are published
4. Monitor probate filings for estates naming LLCs as assets
5. Track tax delinquency lists and cross-reference with entity ownership

### Maintain a Pipeline

1. Each identified lead enters a staged pipeline — initial qualification, deeper research, outreach, negotiation, contract, cleanup, closing
2. Track each lead's status, next action, and estimated timeline
3. Recognize that defunct-LLC deals move slowly — pipelines of thirty to fifty leads are normal to produce a handful of closed deals per year
4. Dead leads are not wasted — they often return in a few years when circumstances change

### Relationship Infrastructure

1. Relationships with title companies that will work on defunct-entity transactions
2. Relationships with real estate attorneys and business entity attorneys who handle reinstatements and winding-up
3. Relationships with CPAs who understand franchise tax and the financial side of entity cleanup
4. Relationships with commercial registered agent services that can provide useful information during investigations
5. A data research capability — either in-house or contracted — that can run the repetitive finding work consistently

## 1.11 Common Pitfalls in Finding and Qualifying

**Stopping at the Secretary of State status check.** An entity that shows "active" at the Secretary of State may still be in franchise tax forfeiture at the Comptroller. Check both sources.

**Assuming the deed signatory is still the right contact.** A person who signed a deed for the LLC in 2005 may have died, moved, or ceased involvement. Treat the signatory as the starting lead, not the final answer.

**Ignoring tax delinquency status.** A defunct LLC with years of unpaid property taxes may be close to tax foreclosure. Your acquisition window may be shorter than it appears.

**Underestimating the operating agreement problem.** Finding a copy of the operating agreement — or working through its absence — is often the longest single step in the acquisition. Plan for it from the beginning.

**Overlooking multi-member situations.** What looks like a sole-member LLC sometimes has additional members who were never on public filings. Investigate fully before committing acquisition capital.

**Missing the connected-entity pattern.** Sophisticated property owners often own through multiple LLCs. The member of one defunct LLC may also be the member of five others, some active and some defunct. Follow the pattern; multiple opportunities sometimes appear in one investigation.

**Failing to cross-check with probate filings.** A member who has died may have had his estate probated under his personal name in a specific county. Checking probate filings for known member names is a quick way to confirm whether heirship work is needed.

**Not documenting the finding work.** The documentation of your search for members becomes evidence in later proceedings — just like the diligent search affidavits in Chapter 30 work. Log your research as you go.

**Treating this as identical to fractured-equity heirship work.** The concepts port; the statutes do not. Do not apply Estates Code procedures to Business Organizations Code problems.

**Assuming out-of-state investors won't respond.** Out-of-state investors are frequently grateful to hear from someone offering to help resolve a stranded asset. The cold call or the well-drafted letter works more often than operators expect.

**Skipping state-variation analysis when the LLC is foreign.** A Delaware LLC registered to do business in Texas is subject to Delaware law for entity-level questions and to Texas law for property and title questions. Coordinate counsel in both states.

## 1.12 Key Takeaways

**Defunct LLCs are a fractured-equity-adjacent acquisition channel.** Stranded title, diffused or missing people with authority, and a specialized procedural toolkit most operators do not understand — the same underlying logic as the rest of this Codex, just with a different statute book.

**Texas is the illustrative jurisdiction — the strategy works everywhere.** Names of statutes change. Procedures change. Fees change. The underlying opportunity exists in every state where entities can become inactive and property can be titled to them.

**Consult qualified professionals in your state.** Real estate attorneys, CPAs, and title professionals licensed in the state where your target property sits. Do not extrapolate from Texas procedures without qualified local guidance.

**LLCs become defunct for recognizable reasons.** Franchise tax forfeiture, administrative dissolution, abandonment after death, member disputes, out-of-state investor neglect, single-purpose deals gone sideways, generational loss. Each produces a recognizable signature in the records — learn to read those signatures.

**The core finding sources are Secretary of State, Comptroller, county deed records, tax delinquency lists, and probate filings.** Cross-referencing these systematically produces the initial list of defunct-entity-held properties.

**Qualification filters heavily.** Dozens or hundreds of defunct LLCs may hold property in a given county; a handful are worth active pursuit. Property-level, entity-level, and economic qualification working together narrows the field quickly.

**Finding the people uses the Chapter 30 methodology.** The Social Breadcrumbing Method first, then the Advanced Breadcrumbing Skip Tracing Method, with specialized trails triggered by what the social work produces.

**The deed signatory, registered agent, and certificate-of-formation contacts are the natural starting points.** From those, breadcrumbing expands outward to members whose names are not in public records.

**Deceased members require heirship work.** The characteristic bridge between defunct-entity work and the Part VI heirship tools of the main Codex — many defunct LLCs have at least one deceased member, and the operator who has mastered both sides can resolve situations that stymie operators who only know one.

**Recognizable scenario patterns.** Sole-member abandonment, deceased-member stranding, out-of-state investor, estranged co-members, developer who moved on, lost-to-time entity, tax-foreclosure-pending stranding. Each implies a different acquisition approach and a different outreach strategy.

**Build a systematic pipeline.** Defined geographic footprint, recurring research cadence, pipeline tracking, and relationship infrastructure with title companies, attorneys, and CPAs who work in this area.

**Document the finding work.** Your research becomes evidence later — just like the diligent search affidavits in heirship work. Log every step, every source checked, every contact attempted, and every response received.
