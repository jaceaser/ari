This is the work. Not the strategy. Not the structure. The actual operational work of finding human beings who do not want to be found, or who are merely lost, or who are dead and have scattered their legal interests across three generations of family members who have never met each other. Every fractured-equity deal eventually runs through this chapter, because every fractured-equity deal eventually produces at least one heir who cannot be reached in five minutes. The operator who closes those deals is the one who keeps going.

## 30.1 The Work Itself

Our job in this chapter is to find people. The person we are looking for might be missing, deceased, or an heir we do not yet know by name — and sometimes we are not looking for the target at all, but for someone who knew the target, or someone who knew someone who knew the target. We find these people two ways, and we run them in this order: first **The Social Breadcrumbing Method**, which works through family, neighbors, friends, obituaries, social media, and interviews; then **The Advanced Breadcrumbing Skip Tracing Method**, which works through databases, public records, and specialized tools. Method 1 produces the intel. Method 2 runs on that intel.

I call this work breadcrumbing because that is exactly what it is. You find one crumb — a name on an obituary, a cousin who answers the phone, a neighbor who remembers the family — and that crumb leads to the next. Follow enough crumbs in the right order and you end up at a person who can sign a deed. Skip the order and you spend money and time on the wrong tools before you even know what you are looking for.

This chapter is detailed on purpose. The difference between an operator who closes fractured-equity deals and one who gives up is almost never the law. It is the willingness to chase crumbs until the person is found. Read slowly. This is the work.

## 30.2 Who We're Looking For

Before we talk about how to find them, we have to be clear about who "they" are. In fractured-equity work, "the person we're looking for" is rarely just one person, and rarely just one type of person. Here is the full taxonomy.

**The Target** — The named fractional owner on the deed. The cousin who inherited a 1/12 interest in 1987 and has not been seen since. The sibling who moved away and stopped answering the phone. The uncle who owes child support and does not want to be found. This is the obvious person — but they are only one category of "who."

**The Deceased Target** — Often the named owner on the deed is already dead. We need to confirm they died, get the death certificate, and figure out when and where. Once we confirm the death, the work does not end — it expands. Now we need to find their heirs.

**The Heirs of a Deceased Target** — When the named owner died, their interest passed to someone. Sometimes to a spouse. Sometimes to children. Sometimes to parents and siblings. Sometimes to heirs of heirs — because the first-generation heirs may have died too, and now their interests passed down another layer. A single deceased owner from the 1970s can produce twenty, thirty, fifty living heirs today. We find them all.

**Unknown Heirs** — Sometimes we do not even know who the heirs are. The deceased had a child nobody in the family acknowledges. A first marriage that ended before anyone in the current family existed. A half-sibling whose name is not on any family tree. These unknown heirs must be identified before they can be located — and identifying them is its own investigative work.

**Missing Heirs (Known by Name, Unknown by Location)** — We know their name. We know they exist. We just do not know where they are. Moved out of state decades ago. Changed phone numbers. Stopped using a fixed address. These are the bread-and-butter skip-trace targets.

**People Who Knew the Target** — When the target has vanished, the people around them often have not. A sister who has not spoken to him in twenty years still knows his ex-wife's maiden name. A neighbor from 1995 remembers where the family moved. A high school classmate stayed in touch long enough to know which state he settled in. These people are not the target — but they carry crumbs that lead to the target.

**People Who Knew Someone Who Knew the Target** — Two degrees of separation. The sister's ex-husband. The neighbor's daughter. The cousin's pastor. In families that have scattered and lost contact, the person who still has current information often is not the closest relative — it is someone farther out in the web who never lost touch. We find these people because they find the target for us.

**Family Members Generally** — Sometimes we are not even sure yet who matters. We know there is a family. We know there were children, siblings, cousins. We work the whole family tree and figure out who is who as we go. Some become targets. Some become heirs. Some become sources. Some turn out to be dead themselves. The family as a whole is a map.

**The Recently Deceased (News Obituaries)** — When a family member dies, the obituary names the surviving relatives — children, spouses, grandchildren, siblings. That obituary is one of the single most valuable intel documents in this work. Sometimes we find the target named in another family member's obituary. Sometimes we find the heirs there. Always we find the map of who is connected to whom.

**Heirs Scattered Across States and Countries** — Fractured-equity families are often scattered. One sibling in Texas, one in California, one in Chicago, one in Mexico, one in the military stationed overseas, one who moved to Alaska thirty years ago. Part of this work is recognizing that "missing" sometimes just means "moved far away and nobody followed up."

**Heirs Who Don't Know They're Heirs** — Many of the people we find have no idea they inherited anything. A grandson in Houston who never met his grandfather from Laredo. A daughter who was told her father had nothing. These heirs are easy to work with once found — because they do not have existing expectations about the property. They just know a stranger is calling them about an inheritance.

**Heirs Who DO Know and Have Hidden** — The opposite case. They know about the property. They know the family is trying to find them. They do not want to be found — usually because they owe money, have warrants, or have cut off family ties deliberately. These are harder targets, but they are findable.

## 30.3 The Two Methods

Now the framework. Everything in the rest of this chapter is organized under one of two methods. Read this section carefully — the sequence matters.

### Method 1 — The Social Breadcrumbing Method

Method 1 works through people. We start with the family and the social network around the person we are looking for, and we follow crumbs outward through human connections. The tools of Method 1 are:

1. Interviewing the family members we already have in hand
2. Working outward through known relatives — the five rings of expansion
3. Reading obituaries to map family trees and surviving relatives
4. Working social media — Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, TikTok, and the platforms the target's generation actually uses
5. Talking to neighbors at last known addresses
6. Talking to known associates — coworkers, friends, church members, members of the same social clubs and organizations
7. Following the community — the barbershop, the parish, the union hall, the veterans' post, the high school reunion committee

Method 1 is the first method because Method 1 produces the intel that Method 2 actually needs. You do not know what kind of skip trace you are running until you have talked to the family. You do not know whether to look in prisons, or in care facilities, or in Mexico, or in military records, until somebody tells you something that points you there. Method 1 generates the leads. Method 2 chases them.

### Method 2 — The Advanced Breadcrumbing Skip Tracing Method

Method 2 works through records and databases. It has two layers.

**The foundational layer** runs on every single search, no matter what. This is the baseline — free people-search sites, county records, tax rolls, voter records, genealogy databases, and (if we have access) the professional skip-trace databases like TLO, LexisNexis, and IDI. We run this on every target because it is cheap, fast, and often resolves the case by itself.

**The specialized layer** only runs when Method 1 intel tells us to run it. We do not search prison records unless a family member tells us the person has a criminal history. We do not search nursing homes and care facilities unless a family member tells us the person is in care somewhere. We do not search Mexico unless a family member tells us the person went to Mexico. We do not search military records unless a family member tells us the person served. We do not search Catholic parish records unless the family is Catholic and the search has gone international. The specialized trails are triggered by intel from Method 1 — never run as a default protocol.

That is the whole framework. Method 1 first, because Method 1 produces intel. Method 2 foundational layer on every case. Method 2 specialized layer only when Method 1 tells us where to look.

### The Sequence

1. Run the death check first — if the person is dead, the search changes entirely
2. Then Method 1 — starting with people, then expanding through the social network
3. Then Method 2 foundational layer — records, free databases, professional databases
4. Then Method 2 specialized layer — only the trails triggered by Method 1 intel
5. Document everything

## 30.4 Death Check First

Before we spend a minute trying to find someone, we check whether they are alive. If they are dead, the search changes entirely. We are no longer looking for them — we are looking for their heirs. The tools, the timeline, and the legal theory all shift.

### Why This Comes First

I have seen operators spend two weeks skip-tracing a target, only to discover in week three that the target died in 2008. Every hour they spent looking for a "missing person" was wasted — the real work was finding the heirs of a deceased person. Ten minutes at the start of the case prevents ten weeks of waste.

### Quick Death Checks

1. **Social Security Death Index (SSDI)** — free, searchable, covers most deaths since 1962
2. **Ancestry.com and FamilySearch.org** — aggregate death records, often including full dates and locations
3. **FindAGrave.com and BillionGraves.com** — crowdsourced cemetery records with dates
4. **Legacy.com and obituary aggregators** — newspaper obituaries searchable by name and location
5. **County clerk death records** — direct records from the jurisdiction
6. **Texas DSHS Vital Statistics** — Texas-specific death records
7. **Google search** with name plus "obituary" or "death" or "memorial"

### If the Person Is Dead

We pivot immediately. Get the death certificate. Confirm the date and location. Then the search target becomes the heirs — spouse, children, parents, siblings, and down through whatever generations have also died. A death in 1975 can produce decades of cascading heirship that has to be unwound family member by family member.

### If the Death Check Is Inconclusive

Sometimes the public death records are silent but family members say the person is dead. Sometimes family members say the person is alive but no records confirm it. Both situations are normal. Do not rule out either possibility until you have confirmation — run both the "alive" search (Method 1 for people) and the "dead" search (obituaries, death certificates) in parallel until one of them resolves.

## 30.5 Method 1 — Starting With People

The first move in Method 1 is to talk to the people we already have. In a fractured-equity case, we usually have at least one or two family members in hand — the person who brought us the deal, or the obvious heir we could find in five minutes. That person is our first source.

### The Foundational Interview

When you talk to your first family contact, you are not just asking about the missing person. You are mapping the whole family. Take notes. Ask these questions in every first interview:

1. Who in the family still talks to them?
2. When was the last time anyone saw or heard from them?
3. Where were they living last time anyone knew?
4. What did they do for work?
5. Were they married? To whom? Did that end? What was her name — including maiden name?
6. Do they have children? By whom? Names? Approximate ages?
7. Brothers, sisters, half-siblings? Any born outside the marriage?
8. Were the parents still alive when last anyone knew? If not, when did they die?
9. Any siblings who died? When? Did they have children?
10. Did the person ever go to jail?
11. Did the person ever move out of state or out of the country?
12. Was the person in the military?
13. Any health issues — is the person in a care facility somewhere?
14. What church did the family attend?
15. Who were the person's close friends?
16. Who would most likely know where they are today?

Every one of these questions is a potential crumb. The answers tell you which specialized searches in Method 2 to trigger later. If nobody mentions prison, you do not run prison records. If somebody mentions "he got into trouble years ago," you do.

### The Five Rings of Expansion

After the first interview, you expand outward in rings. Each ring brings new people into the conversation — and each new person is both a source and potentially a new target.

**Ring 1 — Immediate family.** Parents, siblings, children of the target. The people most likely to have current information, and also the people most likely to have been estranged from the target for their own reasons.

**Ring 2 — Extended family.** Aunts, uncles, cousins, in-laws, nieces, nephews. Often has current information when immediate family has lost contact — because extended family stayed in touch with branches that immediate family broke away from.

**Ring 3 — Former household members.** Ex-spouses, former stepchildren, former in-laws. Frequently know current information because of ongoing contact over shared children or mutual obligations.

**Ring 4 — Community and friends.** Church members, neighbors at old addresses, coworkers, drinking buddies, hunting partners, compadres. These connections often outlast family ruptures.

**Ring 5 — Distant connections.** High school classmates, military buddies, old fraternity brothers, members of social clubs. Sometimes the person who has current information is someone the family completely forgot about.

### The Opening Phone Call

When you call someone you do not know to ask about a family member of theirs, the first fifteen seconds determine whether they cooperate. Here is the script I use as a starting point:

*"Hello, is this [name]? My name is [your name], and I'm calling from [your company] in [city]. I'm trying to reach your [relationship — cousin, brother, uncle]. There is some family property business involving his name, and I'd like to speak with him directly if I can find him. This isn't collections, he isn't in trouble, and he isn't being sued. It's about a property matter that may actually benefit him. Would you happen to know how to reach him, or know someone who might?"*

Notice what this script does. It identifies you. It names the relationship — showing you have done homework. It pre-empts the three fears every family member has when a stranger calls asking about a relative: debt collection, legal trouble, and lawsuits. It frames the matter as potentially beneficial. And it asks an open-ended question that invites cooperation rather than demanding information.

### What To Do With What You Learn

Every interview produces names you did not have before. Write them down. Put them on a family tree sketch. For each new name, ask: is this person the target? An heir? A source? A deceased node that produces its own heirs? Sort as you go. A single interview can open five new leads to follow.

## 30.6 Method 1 — Obituaries

Obituaries are the highest-density intel document in this work. A well-written obituary names the spouse, the surviving children (often with current cities), the predeceased children, the siblings, the grandchildren, and sometimes even the great-grandchildren. It identifies church affiliations, military service, and employers. It is a family map, delivered to you by the family itself.

### Where to Find Obituaries

1. **Legacy.com** — aggregates obituaries from thousands of newspapers
2. **Newspapers.com** — historical newspaper archives, searchable full-text
3. **Local newspaper websites** — direct archives from specific towns
4. **Funeral home websites** — often post obituaries independent of newspapers
5. **Ancestry.com obituary collections**
6. **FamilySearch.org** — free obituary indexes
7. **Google search** with full name in quotes plus "obituary" and likely city

### Reading an Obituary Like an Intel Analyst

When you have an obituary in hand, you read it line by line:

1. **Full name of decedent** — including maiden name for women, nicknames, middle initials
2. **Date of death and place of death** — triggers records in the right jurisdiction
3. **Surviving spouse** — current name, current location often stated
4. **Children listed by name** — often with current city: "son John (Dallas), daughter Maria (Houston)"
5. **Predeceased children** — dead, but they have their own heirs
6. **Siblings** — alive ones are potential sources; dead ones have heirs
7. **Grandchildren** — next generation of heirs
8. **Church or parish named** — triggers Catholic or denominational records
9. **Military service mentioned** — triggers military records trail
10. **Funeral home named** — funeral homes keep family contact records
11. **Cemetery named** — cemetery records sometimes list next of kin
12. **Memorial contributions requested** — the named charity may have family contacts

### Obituaries for People Who Aren't the Target

You search obituaries for everyone connected to the case, not just the target. The target's mother died in 1998? Find that obituary. It will list the target as one of her surviving children, usually with the city where he was living in 1998. That is a lead even though the mother is not who you are looking for. Obituaries for parents, siblings, and cousins all map the family around your target and frequently disclose current-location information you cannot find anywhere else.

## 30.7 Method 1 — Social Media

Social media is the single biggest change in skip-tracing in the last twenty years. If your target is under sixty-five, they almost certainly have a social media footprint somewhere — and if they are over sixty-five, their children almost certainly do. Before a dime gets spent on professional databases, you work social media exhaustively.

### Platform Coverage by Generation

| Platform | Primary Age Range | Primary Value |
|---|---|---|
| Facebook | 40–75+ | Broadest coverage; family networks; "About" location data |
| Instagram | 20–45 | Photos with geotags; current neighborhood and workplace |
| LinkedIn | Working-age professionals | Current employer and location; career history |
| TikTok | 16–35 | Geotagged content; current city revealed in video metadata |
| Twitter/X | Variable | Professional and political connections |
| YouTube | All ages | Long-tail hobbyist content with personal details in comments |
| Snapchat | 15–30 | Limited public surface; family members can confirm identity |
| Nextdoor | Homeowners | Hyperlocal; reveals current neighborhood directly |
| MySpace, Classmates.com | Archived | Sometimes still useful for older searches |

### Worked Example — Finding Maria Gonzalez on Facebook

You are looking for Maria Gonzalez, last known in Houston, sister of your recorded owner. "Maria Gonzalez" returns thousands of hits. Here is how to narrow:

1. Filter by city — limit to Houston and surrounding areas
2. Cross-reference with known family names in her friend list — her brother's name is on the deed, search for her with a mutual connection to that name
3. Look at photos — the faces of relatives who attended her mother's funeral (from the obituary) may be tagged
4. Check her "About" section for her hometown — does it match the family's hometown?
5. Check her high school — does it match the school the target's obituary mentioned?
6. Look at her comments on other people's posts — does she comment on the target's posts?
7. Check who she wishes happy birthday to — family birthdays reveal family relationships

A systematic Facebook search narrows "Maria Gonzalez in Houston" from thousands of profiles to the correct one in under an hour. Once you have her profile, you have her photos, her workplace, her current neighborhood, and frequently her public-facing email and phone.

### The High School Reunion Technique

Every high school has a reunion committee. Every reunion committee has a Facebook group. Every Facebook group is full of classmates of the target — some of whom are still in touch with him. Join the group as a reunion-adjacent researcher ("I'm trying to reach [name] about family property") and you will often get three or four direct leads within a day.

### Friends-of-Friends Mapping

When you cannot find the target directly on social media, you work outward from people who would be in his network. Find his sister. Scroll her friend list. Look for anyone whose name, age, or photo matches the target. Then work from that candidate back to confirm.

### What About Privacy Settings?

Many profiles are partially private. That is fine. Even a locked profile typically shows profile photo, cover photo, city, and a friends list sample. Those four data points often confirm the identity and lead you to family members whose profiles are less locked. Privacy settings are an obstacle, not a wall.

## 30.8 Method 1 — Known Addresses and Associates

When you have a last-known address, you work that address. When you have names of former neighbors, coworkers, or friends, you work those people. This is old-fashioned fieldwork, and it still produces leads that no database will match.

### Working a Last-Known Address

1. Drive by the property if it is local — current occupants may know the target or the family
2. Check county appraisal district for current owner — former neighbor may have purchased
3. Pull USPS change-of-address hints through professional databases
4. Check utility company records (through proper channels) for forwarding addresses
5. Look up the address on people-search sites to see who else has been associated with it
6. Check voter registration records for the current occupant — they may be a relative

### Talking to Neighbors

When you can get to the old neighborhood, knock on three or four doors around the last-known address. Introduce yourself, explain the family property matter, and ask about the family that used to live there. Older neighbors in stable neighborhoods frequently remember families that moved away decades ago — and sometimes have phone numbers, because the family sent Christmas cards for years after moving.

### Associates

Associates are the people whose names come up in the target's life even though they are not family:

1. Coworkers from past jobs
2. Former business partners
3. Friends named in obituaries of family members
4. Godparents listed in the target's baptism records (if Catholic)
5. Best man or maid of honor from a wedding announcement
6. Pallbearers listed in a parent's obituary
7. Compadres — in Mexican-American families, these are legal-family-adjacent and maintain lifelong ties
8. Members of the same social club, lodge, or veterans' organization

Associates often know things that family members do not — either because they stayed in closer touch, or because the target confided in them specifically to avoid family.

## 30.9 Method 1 — Organizations and Groups

The last slice of Method 1 is the community layer. Every person is connected to groups — and the groups remember people that individuals forget.

### Churches and Parishes

If the family was religious, the church is a knowledge hub. The pastor or priest often remembers where members went when they moved. The parish secretary keeps membership rolls. The church directory from twenty years ago may list the family by name with phone numbers. Call the church, explain you are trying to reach a former member about family property, and ask if anyone there knows where the family is now.

### Unions and Trade Organizations

If the target worked in a unionized trade, the union hall keeps long records. Pensions, benefit notices, reunion invitations — the union has reason to maintain current address information for decades. For a target who was a union member twenty years ago, the local hall or the international benefit fund may have the most current address in existence.

### Veterans' Organizations

VFW, American Legion, local veterans' posts. If the target served, these organizations often have current contact information or know who does. Veterans' organizations maintain lifelong connections across relocations in ways that commercial databases often do not capture.

### Fraternal Organizations

Masons, Knights of Columbus, Elks, Shriners. Members maintain ties across relocations. A local chapter often knows where a relocated member went. These organizations have membership records and frequently publish directories.

### Hometown Associations

In Mexican-American communities, **hometown associations** (asociaciones de pueblo) maintain ties to specific villages in Mexico and keep track of who has relocated where. If the family comes from a specific pueblo, the hometown association headquartered in a U.S. city with a large population from that pueblo often has contact information for relatives on both sides of the border. This is one of the single most productive moves on the international trail.

### Community Centers, Coffee Shops, Barber Shops

In stable neighborhoods, certain locations function as community memory. The barber who has cut everyone's hair on the block for forty years often knows where families went. The coffee shop where retirees gather carries the longest institutional memory of any block in America. These are not formal sources — but they are sources. Walk in, explain what you are doing, ask who might remember the family.

### Online Communities

Beyond general social media, there are topic-specific online communities: alumni groups, military unit reunion pages, hobby forums, hometown Facebook groups, ancestry message boards. Find the community the target most likely participates in and you often find the target.

## 30.10 Method 2 — The Foundational Layer

When Method 1 has produced whatever intel it is going to produce, we move to Method 2. The foundational layer of Method 2 runs on every single search, no matter what. It is the baseline of public records and database work that applies to every target.

### Free Public Records

Start here because it is free and fast. The following records are searchable by name in most Texas counties and most other jurisdictions:

1. **County appraisal district records** — property the target owns or owned
2. **County clerk real property records** — deeds, liens, judgments with addresses
3. **County court records** — civil and criminal cases with addresses and contact information
4. **Voter registration** — current registered address
5. **Business entity filings at the Secretary of State** — if the target owns a business
6. **Professional license records** — if the target has a trade or professional license
7. **Marriage records** — reveals spouse's name and date
8. **Divorce records** — reveals ex-spouse, minor children, address at time of divorce
9. **Court dockets** — any current case with the target named
10. **UCC filings at the Secretary of State** — secured-transaction records with addresses

### Free Consumer People-Search Sites

These aggregate public records into searchable consumer-facing sites. Accuracy varies — use them to generate leads, not to finalize information:

| Site | Strengths |
|---|---|
| TruePeopleSearch.com | Addresses, phones, relatives |
| FastPeopleSearch.com | Similar coverage, different algorithm |
| ThatsThem.com | Strong on email addresses and relatives |
| Whitepages.com (free tier) | Basic contact data |
| Radaris | Aggregates social media with public records |
| ZabaSearch | Older tool, still sometimes catches what others miss |
| Spokeo (free tier) | Aggregator with good relative lists |

### Paid Consumer People-Search

Inexpensive subscriptions that give fuller data than free tiers:

1. **BeenVerified** — monthly subscription, reasonable for volume searches
2. **Intelius** — similar model
3. **Spokeo (paid tier)** — fuller reports
4. **Pipl** — stronger international coverage
5. **Whitepages Premium** — paid tier adds substantially more

### Genealogy Databases

Essential when the search involves deceased people or multi-generational heirship:

1. **Ancestry.com** — vital records, census, family trees created by other users
2. **FamilySearch.org** — free, operated by LDS, extensive international coverage
3. **MyHeritage** — strong for European and Latin American searches
4. **FindAGrave, BillionGraves** — cemetery records
5. **Fold3** — military and historical records
6. **Newspapers.com** — historical newspaper archives with full-text search

### Professional Skip-Trace Databases

If you have access — and most serious operators eventually do — professional-tier databases are the single biggest force multiplier in this work. They aggregate utility data, credit-header data, phone data, address history, and relatives/associates that consumer sites do not have.

**TLO (TransUnion TLOxp).** The workhorse. Deep coverage of address history, phones (including burner and prepaid), relatives, associates, employers, and vehicles. Monthly subscriptions typically start around $65 and scale with volume. Requires permissible purpose certification. Most professional skip-tracers use TLO as their primary tool.

**LexisNexis Accurint.** Similar category to TLO with different data sources. Strong for people with professional licenses or litigation histories. Requires permissible purpose certification.

**IDI (Interactive Data Intelligence).** Another professional-tier aggregator. Some operators prefer it for specific use cases.

Professional databases require legitimate permissible purpose under the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act and the Driver's Privacy Protection Act. For fractured-equity work — locating heirs to complete a property transaction — permissible purpose generally exists, but your intake process and documentation must establish it. Work with your attorney on compliance before subscribing.

## 30.11 Method 2 — The Specialized Layer

The specialized layer is where this chapter differs from generic skip-tracing advice. Generic guides tell you to run every specialized search on every target. That wastes money, wastes time, and rarely produces results. The right way is triggered: you only run the specialized search when Method 1 intel tells you to run it.

### The Trigger Map

Here is how each specialized trail gets triggered. If no one in Method 1 tells you any of these things, you do not run the specialized search.

| Specialized Trail | What Triggers It |
|---|---|
| Criminal trail | Family says target has been in trouble, in jail, or has warrants |
| Financial trail | Target has unresolved debts, child support obligations, IRS issues, or business liabilities |
| Employment trail | You know what the target did for work |
| Care facilities trail | Family says target is in a nursing home, assisted living, rehab, psychiatric facility, or long-term care |
| International trail | Family says target moved to Mexico, another country, or was deported |
| Catholic Church trail | Family is Catholic and search has gone international or deep-generational |
| Military trail | Family says target served in the armed forces |
| Medical trail | Family mentions serious long-term illness or extended hospitalization |

Run the triggered trails. Leave the rest alone.

## 30.12 Specialized — The Criminal Trail

When family intel tells you the target has a criminal history, the criminal justice system becomes one of the best-documented, most findable record systems in American life. People in custody have precise addresses. People on supervision have reporting requirements. People with warrants leave a trail of court dates.

### Texas Criminal Records

1. **Texas Department of Criminal Justice (TDCJ) inmate locator** — free, searchable, shows current facility and projected release
2. **Texas Department of Public Safety criminal history** — requires authorization
3. **County jail rosters** — most Texas counties publish online
4. **Municipal court records** — city-level cases with addresses
5. **District court records** — felony cases with full case history
6. **Probation and parole records** — typically not public but accessible through attorney channels

### Federal Criminal Records

1. **Federal Bureau of Prisons inmate locator** — free, national, shows current facility
2. **PACER** — federal court records, small per-page fee
3. **U.S. Marshals Service** — fugitive records (public where warrants are outstanding)

### Writing to Incarcerated Heirs

Incarcerated heirs are easy to contact — you just write a letter. Here is a sample approach:

*"Dear [name], my name is [your name], and I work with [company] in [city]. I understand you are an heir to property that belonged to [decedent's name, relationship]. I am writing to explain the situation and give you the opportunity to participate in the resolution of the family property. There is no cost to you, and you are not being sued. I will enclose a stamped envelope so you can respond. If you would prefer that I contact your attorney or a family member on your behalf, please provide their information in your reply. Thank you for your time."*

Incarcerated people often respond because receiving mail is itself valuable to them, and because the possibility of an inheritance is welcome news. Their inheritance interests can often be addressed through a power of attorney, through direct execution of documents while in custody, or through the attorney they already have for their criminal matter.

## 30.13 Specialized — The Financial Trail

When Method 1 tells you the target has unpaid debts, judgments, child support obligations, or IRS problems, the financial system contains contact information — because creditors have been chasing the same person you are chasing.

### Judgment Creditor Records

1. **County clerk judgment records** — any civil judgment against the target lists addresses and phone numbers
2. **Abstracts of judgment** — judgment liens filed in county records
3. **Post-judgment discovery filings** — sometimes list current addresses and employers

### Child Support and Family Court

1. **Texas Office of the Attorney General Child Support Division** — enforces against parents whose whereabouts are often current
2. **Family court records** — sometimes sealed, but case management frequently lists last-known addresses
3. **Modification filings** — updated addresses filed when motions are served

### Bankruptcy Records

1. **PACER federal bankruptcy records** — filings list current address, all creditors, employer, income sources
2. **Bankruptcy trustees** — sometimes willing to provide contact information for legitimate property matters

### Tax Debt and IRS Matters

IRS records themselves are confidential. You cannot request someone's address from the IRS. However, **federal tax liens are recorded in county records** — and the lien itself names the taxpayer and their last known address. If your target has a federal tax lien filed against them in any county they have lived in, you have a recorded address at a specific point in time. There are also more advanced tax-record techniques for locating missing heirs, reserved for advanced instruction, but the federal tax lien in county records is the accessible starting point.

## 30.14 Specialized — The Employment Trail

Triggered when you know what the target did for work. Employment records are one of the most reliable location sources — because employers pay people and people give employers their current addresses.

### Licensed Trades and Professions

1. **Texas Department of Licensing and Regulation** — electricians, plumbers, cosmetologists, many trades
2. **Texas Medical Board** — physicians, physician assistants
3. **Texas Board of Nursing** — RNs, LVNs
4. **Texas Real Estate Commission** — real estate license holders
5. **State Bar of Texas** — attorneys (current address of record)
6. **Texas Department of Insurance** — insurance agents
7. **Federal licensing** — aviation (FAA), maritime, federal professional credentials

### Union Membership

1. **Local union halls** — maintain member records and pension records
2. **International union offices** — for members who relocated
3. **Benefit fund administrators** — pension and health funds that need current addresses to send payments

### Former Employers

1. **HR departments** — often retain address information in old files
2. **Payroll records** — W-2 addresses, final pay addresses
3. **Company pension plans** — need current contact info to send distributions

### Self-Employed and Small-Business Targets

1. **Texas Secretary of State** — business filings, registered agent, officers
2. **DBA filings at the county clerk** — assumed name certificates
3. **Sales tax permits at the Texas Comptroller** — current address on file
4. **Occupational licenses at city level** — local trade and business licenses

## 30.15 Specialized — The Care Facilities Trail

Triggered when Method 1 tells you the target is in a nursing home, assisted living facility, rehabilitation center, psychiatric facility, group home, or other long-term care setting. These are people who are located — we know they are in care somewhere — but who cannot be reached through normal channels.

### Identifying the Facility

Start with what family members know. Even vague information narrows the search:

1. **State** — the family usually knows which state the target is in
2. **City or region** — narrows to a specific geographic area
3. **Type of facility** — nursing home vs. assisted living vs. psychiatric vs. rehab
4. **Insurance or payor** — Medicare/Medicaid recipients are in facilities that accept those programs

### Facility Databases

1. **Medicare.gov Nursing Home Compare** — searchable database of all Medicare-certified facilities
2. **State health department facility licensing databases** — Texas DADS and successor agencies
3. **Long-term care ombudsman programs** — in each state, can sometimes help reach patients
4. **VA nursing homes and VA medical centers** — if the target is a veteran

### Contacting Facility Staff

Facilities cannot confirm or deny that a specific person is a resident without HIPAA authorization. But the social work department can often take a message to a resident, if you frame it correctly:

*"Hello, this is [your name] from [company]. I am trying to reach someone who I believe may be a resident at your facility — [name], approximately [age], admitted sometime around [year]. I understand you cannot confirm or deny whether he is there. I am calling because he is an heir to family property, and his inheritance cannot be resolved without his input. If he is a resident, would you be willing to deliver a sealed letter to him or pass a message to his family contact? I am happy to provide all my information in writing and to coordinate with his social worker or with his legal representative."*

### Working with Social Workers and Administrators

Social workers inside facilities deal with exactly this kind of situation — residents with family or legal matters pending. They generally know how to handle outside inquiries without violating HIPAA, and they are often willing to facilitate contact when the purpose is legitimate and clearly communicated. A written letter from you to the social work department is the right tool:

*"Dear Social Work Department, I am writing in connection with a family property matter involving a person I believe may be a resident at your facility. I am not requesting any confidential information. I am requesting that, if this person is a resident, you pass along the attached letter to him, or to his family contact of record, or to his legal representative. Enclosed is a stamped addressed envelope for any response. If he is not a resident of your facility, please disregard this letter. My full contact information is below. Thank you for your assistance."*

### Attorney-to-Administrator Letters

When the direct social work route does not produce a response, your attorney can sometimes open the door that you cannot. An attorney letter to the facility administrator — on letterhead, referencing the legal property matter, and requesting only that the facility pass along the attorney's contact information — often produces results where a layperson's letter does not:

*"Dear Administrator, this firm represents [your name and company] in connection with a real property matter in [county], Texas. We have reason to believe that [name of target] may be a resident at your facility. We are not requesting any confidential information. We request only that, if [name] is a resident, you provide him with this letter so that he may contact us if he wishes to do so. His participation is necessary for the resolution of family property in which he has an inherited interest. Enclosed is a stamped addressed envelope. If [name] has a legal representative or guardian of record, a forwarded copy of this letter to that representative would be appreciated in lieu of direct delivery."*

## 30.16 Specialized — The International Trail

Triggered when family intel tells you the target moved to another country, was deported, or was born abroad and may have returned. In Texas fractured-equity work, the international trail is most often a Mexico trail — but Central America, the Caribbean, and occasionally other regions come up.

### Starting Points

1. What country? Family usually knows the country, often the state or region
2. What town or village? In Mexican-American families, the pueblo of origin is often well known
3. Approximate year of relocation? Narrows records
4. Did they go with anyone? Travel companions are lead sources
5. Do they still have family in that country? Almost always yes

### Mexico-Specific Resources

1. **INE (Instituto Nacional Electoral) voter records** — Mexican voter registry, accessible through legitimate research channels
2. **Registro Civil** — state-level civil registry for births, marriages, deaths
3. **RENAPO** — national population registry
4. **Municipal records at the alcaldía** — property, tax, and residency records in Mexican municipalities
5. **Mexican consulates in Texas** — maintain some records of Mexican nationals registered abroad

### The Role of the U.S. State Department

The State Department is not a skip-tracing resource. It will not run a location search on a U.S. citizen abroad at your request. The State Department's role in locating U.S. citizens overseas is limited and situation-specific:

1. **Welfare and whereabouts inquiries** — available in narrow circumstances, usually for close family, not for property-research purposes
2. **Embassy and consulate registration** — U.S. citizens can register with embassies, but the registry is not publicly searchable
3. **Passport records** — accessible only through court order or specific legal process, not through informal request

For practical purposes, the State Department is not part of your toolkit for locating an ordinary missing heir. Use it only in narrow circumstances with attorney guidance, and do not structure your search around it.

### Hometown Associations and Cross-Border Family Networks

In Mexican-American communities, **hometown associations** (asociaciones oriundas) maintain direct ties to specific villages in Mexico. If the family comes from a specific pueblo, the hometown association — usually headquartered in a U.S. city with a large population from that pueblo — often has contact information for relatives on both sides of the border. Finding and calling the hometown association is one of the single most productive moves on the international trail. In practice, the association's secretary may know the target personally.

### Immigration and Deportation Records

1. **ICE public records** — limited but sometimes reveal deportation information
2. **Immigration court records (EOIR)** — removal proceedings are sometimes public
3. **Naturalization records** — for targets who became U.S. citizens

## 30.17 Specialized — The Catholic Church Trail

Triggered when the family is Catholic and the search has gone international or deep-generational. Catholic parish records are among the best-kept vital records in the world. In many Latin American countries, parish records from the 1700s are still complete and still maintained — often better than civil records from the same period. When you are looking for an heir across generations in a Catholic family with roots in Mexico, Central America, or Southern Europe, the Catholic Church trail is the single best tool in the box.

### What Catholic Records Contain

1. **Baptism records** — date, place, child's name, parents' names, godparents' names
2. **Confirmation records** — similar detail, usually at age 12–16
3. **Marriage records** — both spouses, both sets of parents, witnesses
4. **Death records** — date, place of death, spouse, parents when available
5. **Funeral records** — burial location, family contacts
6. **Parish membership rolls** — historical lists of registered parishioners

### Why This Trail Is So Powerful

In many Mexican-American families, the people you are trying to find may not appear in any U.S. civil record — no Social Security number, no U.S. birth certificate, no U.S. marriage. But they appear in parish records from the family pueblo in Mexico. A baptism in Zacatecas in 1952 that names the child's parents and godparents is a complete family identification tool, even though the child never had an American paper trail. The Catholic Church's record-keeping is older, more continuous, and geographically broader than any civil registry you will use on the U.S. side.

### Contacting a Parish

Most Latin American parishes will respond to a respectful written inquiry in Spanish, especially when accompanied by a small donation for the parish and a self-addressed envelope or international postage. Here is a starter template in Spanish for a parish inquiry in Mexico:

*"Muy estimado Padre, le escribo para solicitar respetuosamente su ayuda en la búsqueda de registros sacramentales de mi pariente. Nombre completo: [nombre]. Fecha aproximada de nacimiento: [fecha]. Nombre de los padres: [padres]. Tipo de registro solicitado: bautismo y/o matrimonio. El motivo de esta búsqueda es una cuestión de herencia familiar en los Estados Unidos, y el encontrar estos registros es fundamental para resolver un asunto de propiedad. Adjunto una pequeña ofrenda para la parroquia y un sobre con estampilla para su respuesta. Quedo muy agradecido por cualquier información que pueda proporcionar. Que Dios le bendiga. Atentamente, [su nombre, dirección, teléfono, y correo electrónico]."*

### Archdiocesan Archives

When a specific parish cannot help — perhaps because it closed, merged, or lost records in a fire — the archdiocesan archives often have duplicates. The Archdiocese of San Antonio, the Archdiocese of Los Angeles, the Archdioceses of Mexico, Guadalajara, and Monterrey all maintain historical archives. A letter to the archive can sometimes retrieve records the local parish does not have.

### Religious Orders and Missions

Some Catholic records are held by religious orders (Franciscans, Jesuits, Dominicans) rather than diocesan structures, particularly in historical missions and rural areas. If parish-level inquiries do not produce results, inquire with the religious order that historically served the area.

## 30.18 Specialized — The Military and Medical Trails

### Military Trail

Triggered when family says the target served in the armed forces.

1. **National Personnel Records Center (NPRC)** — in St. Louis; service records accessible for certain purposes, limited for third parties
2. **VA benefits** — the VA maintains current contact info for all beneficiaries; not directly accessible to you, but VA-affiliated channels sometimes are
3. **DD-214 records** — service members frequently file their DD-214 with county clerks; these public filings often include addresses
4. **VFW, American Legion, and unit reunion associations** — social channels that often have current contact info
5. **Military locator services** — free for certain immediate-family and legal purposes
6. **Veterans' cemeteries** — for deceased veterans, burial records list next of kin

### Medical Trail

Triggered when family mentions a serious long-term illness or extended hospitalization. Direct medical records are protected by HIPAA and are not accessible to third parties. But adjacent records can help:

1. **Billing and collection records** — providers chasing payment often have current addresses
2. **Insurance records** — payors maintain current contact information, though not disclosable to outsiders
3. **Charity care and foundation records** — patients who received support from disease-specific foundations
4. **Specialized treatment centers** — for specific conditions, the patient community is small and connected
5. **Support-group and advocacy-group records** — often maintain member directories

The medical trail is usually worked indirectly — through family members, adjacent records, and community networks — not through direct medical-record inquiry.

## 30.19 The 13-Step Methodology

Here is how the two methods combine into a single operational workflow. Use this as your checklist on every case.

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| Step 1 | Identify the person and their category (target, deceased, heir, source, unknown) |
| Step 2 | Death check first (SSDI, obituaries, death certificates) |
| Step 3 | Method 1 foundational interview with family members in hand |
| Step 4 | Build initial family map from the interview |
| Step 5 | Method 1 expansion through the five rings |
| Step 6 | Obituary search for every deceased family node |
| Step 7 | Social media exhaustive search across all platforms |
| Step 8 | Method 2 foundational layer (free records, free people search, professional databases) |
| Step 9 | Identify which specialized trails Method 1 has triggered |
| Step 10 | Run triggered specialized trails only |
| Step 11 | Consolidate findings, verify against multiple sources, document |
| Step 12 | If genuinely exhausted, prepare documentation for service by publication (Chapter 31) |
| Step 13 | Keep the file open — cases that seem dead often reopen when a single new lead surfaces |

## 30.20 Documentation — The Diligent Search Affidavit

Documenting the search is not paperwork. It is the foundation for service by publication under Chapter 31, and it is the evidence that protects your title against later attack by heirs who claim they were findable. Every phone call, every email, every database run, every letter — dated, preserved, logged. If you ever need to prove you tried, your file has to do it for you.

### What the Affidavit Contains

1. Identification of the missing person and the reason they need to be found
2. Chronological summary of every search effort with dates
3. Each database searched and the result
4. Each person contacted (family, neighbors, associates) with date and result
5. Each letter sent, with copies preserved
6. Each specialized trail pursued and the outcome
7. A sworn statement that the search was diligent and, to the best of affiant's knowledge, the person cannot be located through reasonable means

### Sample Diligent Search Affidavit Excerpt

*"I, [affiant name], being duly sworn, state as follows: I have conducted a diligent search to locate [missing person name], last known to reside at [address], an heir to real property located in [county], Texas. My search included the following steps, conducted between [start date] and [end date]: (1) review of the Social Security Death Index (no match); (2) searches on TruePeopleSearch, FastPeopleSearch, ThatsThem, and BeenVerified (results yielded no current address); (3) a TLO professional database search (no current address located); (4) interview of [family member] on [date] (provided the following information: [summary]); (5) interviews of additional family members [names] on [dates] (no current location information obtained); (6) search of county property and court records in [counties] (no current address of record); (7) social media searches on Facebook, Instagram, and LinkedIn (no confirmed profile located); (8) review of Newspapers.com and Legacy.com obituary databases (no obituary located); (9) letter sent to last known address on [date], returned as undeliverable; (10) additional specialized searches triggered by family intel, including [list]. Based on all the foregoing, I have been unable to locate [missing person] and, in my judgment, the missing person cannot be located through reasonable means."*

Your attorney will polish the exact language. The essential quality is that the affidavit is specific — dated, sourced, and concrete — not generic. A diligent search affidavit that says "we searched the internet and asked the family" will not hold. An affidavit that names every database searched, every person interviewed, and every letter sent, all with dates, will hold.

## 30.21 Worked Example — The Carlos Rivera Case

Here is a composite case drawn from real files. Names and details are changed. The sequence, the decisions, and the turning points are exactly how these cases play out.

### The Starting Point

We are trying to clear title on a 12-acre parcel in Bexar County. The property was owned by Maria Rivera, who died in 1998. She had five children. Four are accounted for and willing to sign. The fifth — Carlos Rivera — has been missing from the family for twenty years. Without him, the partition cannot close. Without a clean title, the deal does not happen.

### Step 1 — Death Check

SSDI: no record. Legacy.com: no obituary under his name. FindAGrave: no burial record. Most likely alive.

### Step 2 — Method 1 Interview

We sit down with his oldest sister, Rosa. Notes from the interview: Carlos left San Antonio around 2002. He had been arrested twice before he left (drugs). Rosa thinks he may have been in prison for a while. She heard from their cousin Lupe that Carlos went to Mexico — maybe to the family's pueblo in Coahuila, maybe not. He has a son, Carlos Jr., from a relationship that ended before he left. Carlos Jr. would be about 30 now. Nobody knows where Carlos Jr. is either. The family is Catholic. Their home parish was Our Lady of Guadalupe on the West Side. Carlos served in the Army briefly in the early 1990s.

### Step 3 — What This Interview Just Triggered

- **Criminal trail** — Rosa mentioned arrests and possible prison
- **International trail** — possible move to Mexico
- **Catholic Church trail** — family parish history, possible Mexican parish records
- **Military trail** — brief Army service
- **Missing heir** — Carlos Jr., son, also unlocated

### Step 4 — Method 2 Foundational Layer

We run TLO. No current address in the U.S. since 2003. Last known Texas address was a rented apartment in San Antonio. We run TruePeopleSearch and ThatsThem. Nothing current. We pull Bexar County criminal records — three arrests between 1998 and 2002, followed by silence.

### Step 5 — Specialized Trails

**Criminal trail.** We pull TDCJ records. Carlos served a two-year sentence in a Texas state prison from 2003 to 2005. TDCJ shows he was released to a parole address in San Antonio. That address matches his last-known TLO address. After that — nothing in U.S. records.

**International trail.** Rosa mentioned a pueblo in Coahuila. We contact the hometown association for that pueblo in San Antonio. The association's secretary remembers the Rivera family — Carlos's father was a longtime member. She gives us the phone number of a cousin in Monclova, Coahuila. We call the cousin. The cousin confirms Carlos has been in Coahuila since 2005, living in a small town outside Monclova, and provides a phone number.

**Catholic Church trail.** In parallel with the international trail, we had sent a letter to the parish in the Coahuila pueblo. Three weeks later, we receive a reply — the parish confirms that Carlos participated in a godparent role at a baptism in 2019, and provides the address from the parish records. Same address as the cousin provided.

### Step 6 — Making Contact

We call the number. Carlos answers. We explain we are not law enforcement, not collections, not a process server — we are representing his family's real property matter in Texas. His mother's estate still has not been resolved, and his participation is needed. He is initially wary but agrees to a follow-up call after verifying our story with Rosa.

### Step 7 — The Second Heir — Carlos Jr.

Carlos Sr. tells us he has not spoken to Carlos Jr. in fifteen years. Carlos Jr. was raised by his maternal grandmother. The grandmother has died. Carlos Sr. thinks Carlos Jr. might be in the Dallas area. We pivot and run Method 1 / Method 2 on Carlos Jr.:

- TLO on Carlos Jr. — one hit, a Dallas address from three years ago
- TruePeopleSearch — confirms the Dallas address, with a cell phone
- Facebook — locates his profile, employment at a Dallas construction company
- LinkedIn — confirms current employer

We call Carlos Jr. He answers. He had no idea his grandmother had owned property. He was never told the family had anything. He is willing to participate.

### Step 8 — Documentation

Every step above goes into the file — interview notes with Rosa, TLO reports, TDCJ lookup, hometown association contact log, letter to the parish, parish response, phone call to the cousin, phone call to Carlos Sr., phone call to Carlos Jr. The partition suit moves forward with all heirs accounted for. The title closes clean. The deal happens.

**Total elapsed time from first interview to contact with Carlos Sr.: about six weeks. Total out-of-pocket for databases and correspondence: under $500. Value of the closed deal: substantially larger.**

## 30.22 Additional Cases — Each Illustrating a Different Trail

### Rosa Morales — Catholic Church Trail

Rosa Morales was the target. She was named on a 1962 deed in Starr County, Texas. Family said she had moved back to Mexico in the 1970s to care for her mother and never returned. No U.S. records past 1975. No Social Security activity. The family parish in Mexico was named in an uncle's obituary — San Juan Bautista in a small town in Nuevo León. We wrote to the parish in Spanish. The response came six weeks later: Rosa had died in 1991 and was buried in the parish cemetery. The parish provided the date of death and named her surviving children — three of them, still alive, all living in Monterrey. Contact information for one of them was provided by the parish priest, who had baptized her grandchildren. The full family tree for the Mexican side of the case opened up from a single letter to a single parish. A case that had been stuck for five years cleared in ninety days.

### Robert Anderson — Care Facility Trail

Robert Anderson was an 82-year-old heir on a Liberty County tract. Family said he had developed serious memory problems in his 70s and had been placed in "some kind of home somewhere in Oklahoma" by his daughter, Linda. Linda had stopped returning family calls. Method 2 foundational layer produced no current Texas address. TLO showed the last address was Linda's home in Tulsa, Oklahoma. We sent a respectful letter to Linda. No response. We used **Medicare.gov Nursing Home Compare** to pull the list of certified nursing homes in the Tulsa metro area. We drafted a social-work-department letter, made copies, and sent one to each of the larger facilities. On the sixth facility, the social worker called us back — Mr. Anderson was a resident, and Linda had formal power of attorney. The social worker facilitated a call with Linda, who turned out to have been avoiding the family for reasons unrelated to the property. Once she understood that the inheritance would actually benefit her father, she cooperated. Robert signed through his power of attorney. The case closed in four months.

### Louis Jackson — Cascade Case

Louis Jackson had inherited a 1/8 interest in a Harris County property from his father in 1972. Louis died in 1990. The question was: who are Louis's heirs? Family knew Louis had three children, but one — Anthony — had died "sometime in the 90s" in California. Anthony's children were unknown to the Texas family. We pivoted to work the chain: Louis's other two children were easy to find. Anthony's case was harder. California death records confirmed Anthony died in Los Angeles in 1996. Anthony's obituary named a wife, Patricia, and two young children. Patricia was traceable through professional databases — she had remarried and moved to Arizona. We wrote to her. She responded within a week. Her two children — Louis Jackson's grandchildren — were now in their 30s, one in Phoenix, one in Portland. Both were located within a week. Three generations of cascading heirship, all resolved through Method 1 obituary work combined with Method 2 database confirmation.

### Social-Media-Only Case

A Method 1 case that closed without Method 2 ever needing to run beyond the foundational layer. The target was a 28-year-old woman named Jennifer Ramos, heir to a small Montgomery County tract through her grandmother. The family had a falling out after the grandmother's death; Jennifer had moved away and stopped communicating. TLO had only an old apartment address. But Facebook had her wide open: current city Austin, employment at a marketing agency, photos of her at a recognizable neighborhood coffee shop. We messaged her through the marketing agency's general contact form, referencing the family property matter. She replied within 48 hours. No specialized trails ever needed to run — the entire case resolved through obituary research on the grandmother and one targeted social media contact.

## 30.23 When to Bring In a Private Investigator

At some point in this business you will hit a case that you cannot crack yourself. The target is deep-hidden. The family intel is thin. The specialized trails are triggering but not producing. The search has been running for three months. That is when you consider a private investigator.

### The PI You Want Is Not the PI Most People Think Of

The private investigator who has a storefront, a Yellow Pages ad, and a website featuring surveillance photos of cheating spouses — that is not the PI you want for this work. That PI does matrimonial surveillance and insurance fraud. He has an investigator's license and a camera, and he is fine for what he does, but he is not equipped for deep skip tracing.

The PI you want is a **retired federal investigator**. Specifically:

1. Retired FBI special agents
2. Retired Secret Service agents
3. Retired DEA, ATF, or CBP agents
4. Retired U.S. Marshals — especially former fugitive-recovery deputies
5. Retired military investigators — CID (Army), NCIS (Navy), OSI (Air Force), CGIS (Coast Guard)
6. Retired state-level major-crimes investigators — Texas Rangers, state police investigators
7. Retired intelligence-community personnel — CIA, DIA, NSA (on rare occasions, for specific specialty work)

These are people who spent twenty or thirty years actually finding people for a living — often people who were actively hiding, often internationally. They have databases, networks, and techniques that no consumer operator has access to. They are not easy to find, and they are not cheap. But when a case is worth it, they close cases nobody else can.

### How to Find One

1. **Attorney referrals** — law firms that do high-end investigations know who the good retired feds are
2. **Former federal agent professional associations** — FBI Agents Association, FLEOA, and similar
3. **State licensing boards** — some retired feds register as PIs; search by credentialing background
4. **Word of mouth** — in this business, the good ones are known to other professionals
5. **Insurance investigation firms** — some specialize in high-value investigations and employ retired federal agents

### Cost Structure

| Category | Range |
|---|---|
| Hourly rate (standard) | $75 – $200 per hour |
| Hourly rate (retired FBI / retired Marshals) | $200 – $400 per hour |
| Retainer to open a file | $2,500 – $10,000 |
| International work surcharge | Significant additional cost |
| Results-based arrangements | Rare; occasionally available for specific scopes |

### When the Cost Is Justified

- The missing heir's fractional interest is substantial
- The deal size supports $5,000 to $20,000 in investigation spend
- The heir is genuinely hidden or internationally located
- You are up against a litigation deadline where service by publication would be problematic
- The alternative is walking away from the deal entirely

### When the Cost Is Not Justified

- Small fractional interest where publication would be a better path
- Deal economics are thin
- You have not exhausted Method 1 and Method 2 yourself yet — you are trying to buy your way past work you should be doing
- The heir is likely deceased and the real question is heirs-of-heirs, which is document-intensive rather than investigator-intensive

## 30.24 Common Pitfalls

**Running specialized searches first.** Searching prison records, nursing home databases, or Mexican parish records before Method 1 has told you to — wastes time and money. Specialized trails are triggered, not defaults.

**Skipping the death check.** Spending weeks looking for a "missing person" who died years ago. Ten minutes at the start prevents ten weeks of waste.

**Interviewing only the easiest family member.** The most accessible family member often has the least current information. Work all five rings.

**Trusting a single source.** "Cousin said he's dead" is not a death record. Verify independently.

**Treating social media as optional.** For anyone under 65, social media is usually the most productive single source of location information in the entire toolkit.

**Giving up too early.** Two weeks of effort that produces no result does not mean the target is unfindable. It often means the right trail has not been triggered yet.

**Forcing a trail that was not triggered.** If no one says criminal, do not run criminal. If no one says Mexico, do not search Mexico. Specialized trails are triggered, not defaults.

**Using consumer databases and expecting professional results.** Free people-search sites generate leads. Professional databases confirm them. Skipping professional-tier access on serious cases is false economy.

**Failing to document as you go.** Documentation at the end from memory is always weaker than documentation created contemporaneously. Log every call, every email, every result on the day you make it.

**Ignoring the community layer.** Churches, unions, hometown associations, veterans' posts — the institutional memory in these organizations is enormous and almost free. It is the most underused resource in this entire chapter.

**Picking the wrong PI.** A matrimonial surveillance PI is not going to crack an international missing-heir case. Use retired federal investigators for work that requires that caliber.

**Not verifying identity before doing business.** Finding someone with the target's name is not the same as finding the target. Confirm with multiple data points — date of birth, parents' names, prior addresses — before contracting.

**Overpaying for information you could develop yourself.** Before spending $5,000 on a PI, ask whether another week of Method 1 work with the family would have produced the same lead.

## 30.25 The Ethics of This Work

Something must be said about how we conduct ourselves in this work. We are contacting people, often unexpectedly, with information about property they may not have known they had. The power imbalance is real — we have information, they do not, and what they do with our outreach will shape whether they collect inheritance that is legitimately theirs or miss it entirely.

**Tell the truth.** Identify yourself and your company. Explain the reason for the contact. Do not misrepresent yourself as family, as a lawyer, as a government official, or as anything other than what you are.

**Explain what the person owns.** Do not hide the nature of the interest, the value of the property, or the fact that they are an heir. Let them make an informed decision.

**Offer fair terms.** The fact that you found them and they could not find themselves does not entitle you to a bargain. Pay fairly for the interest you acquire.

**Respect no.** If a person says they are not interested, thank them and stop. Do not harass.

**Respect privacy.** Information you obtain in the course of this work is for the resolution of the property matter. Not for social media posts, not for gossip, not for any purpose other than what the person was told it was for.

**Protect the vulnerable.** Elderly heirs, heirs with diminished capacity, heirs in stressful circumstances deserve additional care. Involve appropriate family members and legal representatives. Slow down when necessary.

**Honor the dead.** The people whose estates we are resolving were someone's parents, grandparents, siblings. Speak respectfully about them. Handle their records with care.

Run this business right and you will look back on every deal with clean hands. Run it any other way and you will build nothing that lasts.

## 30.26 Key Takeaways

**Two methods, in sequence.** Method 1 — The Social Breadcrumbing Method. Method 2 — The Advanced Breadcrumbing Skip Tracing Method. Method 1 first, because Method 1 produces the intel that Method 2 runs on.

**Death check before everything.** Ten minutes at the start prevents weeks of wasted work.

**Method 1 is people work.** Interviews, family, friends, neighbors, social media, obituaries, community, organizations.

**Method 2 has two layers.** The foundational layer runs every time. The specialized layer runs only when Method 1 intel has triggered it.

**Specialized trails are triggered, not defaults.** Do not run prisons without a criminal intel cue. Do not run Mexico without a migration cue. Do not run care facilities without a care-placement cue. Do not run Catholic parish records without a Catholic-family cue. Do not run military records without a service cue.

**Obituaries are the highest-density intel document.** Read them line by line. They map the whole family.

**Social media is non-negotiable.** For anyone under 65, social media usually produces the single best lead in the toolkit.

**The foundational Method 2 layer runs on every case.** Free records first. Consumer people-search next. Professional databases when available.

**The right private investigator is a retired federal agent.** Not a matrimonial PI. Reserve for cases that justify the cost.

**Document everything as you go.** The diligent search affidavit is only as strong as the contemporaneous record behind it.

**Be findable to people.** Conduct this work with honesty and fair dealing. Everything in fractured-equity real estate compounds — including reputation.
