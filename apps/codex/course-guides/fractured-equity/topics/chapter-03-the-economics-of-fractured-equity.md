# The Economics of Fractured Equity

## 3.1 The Money Question

You're reading this Codex because you want to make money. Let me tell you exactly how much money this strategy produces, where it comes from, what it costs to go get it, and how to know in advance whether any given deal will be worth your time.

This chapter is the math chapter. Not heavy math. The arithmetic of this business is straightforward. What matters is that you understand every line item, every cost, every expected return, and every rule of thumb that experienced operators use to evaluate deals before committing capital.

If you internalize this chapter, you'll never waste time on a bad deal again. That alone will save you more money than most people make in a year.

---

## 3.2 The $75,000 Minimum Equity Spread Rule

I want to put this rule up front, before anything else, because it governs the whole business.

**The rule:** Don't work a deal that doesn't have at least $75,000 of equity spread at the top of the funnel.

**What "equity spread" means:** The difference between the as-is market value of the property and the total liabilities attached to it. As-is market value minus taxes owed, lien balances, tax loan payoffs, mortgage balances, and any other direct obligations that must be paid or cleared to convey marketable title.

**Equity spread is NOT the same as profit.** The spread is the best-case number. If every heir deeded to you for free, if you paid zero legal fees, if you paid zero commissions on resale — your maximum possible profit would be the equity spread. In reality, you'll spend a chunk of that spread on heir payouts, attorney fees, curative work, notary costs, junk removal, and closing costs. A $75,000 spread deal typically nets the operator $30,000 to $50,000 after all costs.

### Why $75,000 and Not Some Other Number

$75,000 is the floor where the math still works when things go wrong. Below that number, the combined costs of doing the work — even when the deal closes cleanly — start eating most of the margin. A $40,000 spread deal that takes 6 months to close, involves two heirs in different states, requires a demand letter and a partial curative action, and has a standard 6% commission on resale can end up netting the operator $8,000 to $15,000. That's wholesale margin for a lot more work.

The $75,000 floor also gives you cushion for surprises. Schedule C surprises (liens you didn't find in your diligence). A second lien that surfaces mid-deal. An heir who dies before closing and triggers another probate. A title company that requires additional curative work nobody anticipated. These happen. A $40,000 spread deal has no room to absorb them. A $75,000 spread deal still makes money even when one of them hits.

### The Rule's Exceptions

You can go below $75,000 in specific situations:

**You're new.** First three to five deals, you're learning. Taking a $55,000 spread deal early in your career is fine if the fact pattern is simple and you'll use it to build skill and confidence. Pay tuition to the business by doing smaller deals while you learn.

**Package deals.** If a $50,000 spread deal is one of four properties in a single family package, the aggregate spread might be $200,000 across all four, and the cost of doing each additional property is minimal because the research and heir conversations are shared. Small individual spreads work inside packages.

**Quick, clean, obvious.** A property with one heir who holds 100% interest and is ready to sell at a $50,000 spread, closing in 30 days with a single deed and a standard settlement — that's a $30,000 net for a month of work. Take it.

**Strategic reasons.** Sometimes you take a lower-spread deal because it's a referral opportunity, it's in a market you want to expand into, or it involves a relationship you want to build. These decisions should be deliberate, not drift.

### The Rule's Hardening

As you build your business and your pipeline fills, raise the floor. Experienced operators running 10+ deals per year often raise their minimum to $100,000 or $125,000 because their time is better spent chasing the bigger spreads. Your floor is a function of how many deals you can actually work at one time. Scarcity of your time creates the rule.

### The Discipline Behind the Rule

The number itself matters less than the discipline of having a rule. Most investors in this space don't have one. They chase every deal. They take $20,000 spread deals and discover six months later they've spent $18,000 in costs and their hourly rate was below minimum wage. The operator with a clear rule — whatever number that is — makes more money than the operator who evaluates every deal on its own without a framework.

Decide your floor. Write it down. Respect it.

## 3.3 Decomposing the Spread — From Best Case to Realistic Net

Let's take a $100,000 spread deal and walk through where the money actually goes.

### Starting Point: $100,000 Equity Spread

This is your best case. Property worth $220,000 as-is, with $120,000 in total liabilities (taxes, tax loans, mortgages). If you could close the property free and clear, pay zero heir costs, sell it with zero friction, you'd keep $100,000. You won't. Let's go through what comes out.

### Heir Payouts: $2,000 – $15,000

Most deals involve paying multiple heirs for their fractional interests. Typical payments range from $500 per heir for a small interest they don't want anyway, to $5,000 per heir for someone negotiating actively, to occasionally $10,000+ for a heir holding a meaningful interest and playing hardball.

A deal with 6 heirs, some easy, some negotiated, might cost you $8,500 total across all heir payouts. A deal with 3 cooperative heirs and one difficult one might cost $4,500. Budget a range of $2,000 to $15,000 depending on heir count and their positions.

**This deal: $8,000 in heir payouts**

**Remaining from spread: $92,000**

### Attorney Fees: $2,000 – $8,000

Almost every deal involves legal work. AOH drafting, demand letters if needed, closing packages, occasional quiet title or contribution suits. A simple deal with just AOH and closing might cost $1,500–$2,500 in attorney fees. A deal involving demand letters and some minor curative work might run $4,000–$6,000. A deal involving a full creditor-suit pipeline through judgment can run $6,000–$10,000.

**This deal: $4,500 in attorney fees (AOH, demand letter, minor curative, closing)**

**Remaining: $87,500**

### Notary and Travel Costs: $500 – $2,000

Mobile notaries charge $75–$200 per visit depending on distance and complexity. Travel for heir signings, institutional notary fees (for incarcerated heirs), and coordination costs all stack up.

**Typical deal: $800**

**Remaining: $86,700**

### Junk Removal and Turn Costs: $1,000 – $5,000

Most fractured properties need some cleanup before listing for resale. Junk removal, minor repairs, utility turn-ons, maybe a locksmith to rekey. Properties with long-term occupants or abandoned contents cost more. Raw land might cost almost nothing.

**Typical deal: $2,500**

**Remaining: $84,200**

### Schedule C Surprises: $1,000 – $10,000

These are the liens, encumbrances, or title issues that surface during the title commitment process that you didn't catch in your diligence. An old mortgage release that was never recorded. A judgment from an ex-spouse. A mechanic's lien from a 2012 roofer. A code enforcement lien nobody mentioned. These require attorney work to clear.

Not every deal has Schedule C surprises. But enough do that you should budget for them. Assume $3,000 on average across a portfolio of deals.

**Typical deal: $2,000**

**Remaining: $82,200**

### Realtor Commissions on Resale: 6% of Sale Price

You're going to pay a real estate agent to list and sell the property on the MLS. Standard commission is 6% split between buyer's and seller's agent. On a $220,000 property, that's $13,200.

This is the biggest single cost in most deals and most operators forget to budget it adequately. A property that resells for $220,000 with 6% commission means $13,200 leaves the table regardless of what else happened in the deal.

**Typical deal (on $220,000 sale): $13,200**

**Remaining: $69,000**

### Title and Closing Costs on Resale: $1,500 – $3,000

Title insurance premiums, closing fees, recording fees, minor survey costs. Usually the buyer pays most of these but the seller still has some exposure.

**Typical deal: $2,000**

**Remaining: $67,000**

### Financing Costs (if applicable): $0 – $10,000

If you used outside capital or a credit line, you have interest costs. If you paid cash from your own capital, you have opportunity cost but no line-item expense. For an operator using a credit line at 10% for 6 months on $60,000 of working capital, that's roughly $3,000.

**Typical deal with modest financing: $2,500**

**Remaining: $64,500**

### Final net profit on a $100,000 spread deal: approximately $64,500

Give or take depending on exact numbers and whether Schedule C surprises appeared. In general, on a $100,000 spread deal, expect to keep $50,000 to $70,000 net, with most deals landing in the $55,000 to $65,000 range.

### The Rule of Thumb

On a clean deal with no major surprises, expect to keep 60–70% of the starting equity spread as net profit. On a messy deal with significant curative work, demand letters, or unexpected encumbrances, expect 40–55%.

Applied to the $75,000 minimum: a $75K spread deal typically nets $30,000 to $50,000 depending on complexity. A $100K deal nets $50K–$70K. A $150K deal nets $80K–$105K. A $200K deal nets $110K–$150K. A $500K deal (yes, they happen) nets $300K+.

**The lesson:** The spread is the ceiling, not the bottom. Everything that comes out before you net is accounted for. When you underwrite deals, you're underwriting to the net — not to the spread.

## 3.4 Equity Spread vs. ARV — Why ARV Is a Dirty Word in This Business

Anyone who has been around real estate investing has heard the term ARV — After Repair Value. ARV is the expected market value of a property after you've renovated it. Flippers live and die by ARV. Wholesalers talk about ARV constantly. Most real estate courses teach ARV as the primary valuation metric.

***ARV has no place in Fractured Equity. I want you to strike it from your vocabulary.***

In this strategy, we use **as-is market value**. What the property is worth today, in its current condition, without any repairs or improvements. The number a title company would rely on. The number an MLS agent would list it for without any updates.

Here's why the distinction matters.

**ARV is a speculation.** It's a projection of what a property might sell for after repairs that might cost a specific amount and take a specific amount of time. ARV calculations have three variables that can each go wrong: the actual post-repair value, the actual repair cost, and the actual timeline. Flippers who miss on any of those three variables lose money. Flippers who miss on all three lose a lot of money.

**As-is value is a fact.** It's what the property will actually sell for today, in its current condition, on the open market. It's based on recent sales of comparable properties in similar condition. It has no projection, no renovation assumption, no variable risk.

**The strategy in this Codex is built around selling as-is, not renovating.** When you consolidate ownership of a fractured property, your typical exit is:

1. List the property on the MLS in its current condition
2. Price it at the as-is market value
3. Sell to an owner-occupant, a flipper, or a landlord who will do their own renovation
4. Close in 30–45 days from contract

You're not fixing anything. You're not putting in new kitchens. You're not dealing with contractors. Your job was to consolidate the ownership and clear the title. Once that's done, the property is sold as-is to someone whose business is renovation.

This is a critical strategic distinction. The skill you've developed — heirship research, legal process, negotiation — is valuable. The skill of renovation is a different business. You can stack them if you want, but the Fractured Equity strategy alone, ending at as-is resale, is where the reliable profit lives.

### Why Investors Who Blur ARV Into Fractured Equity Deals Lose Money

They acquire the property, consolidate title, and then instead of listing it at as-is market value, they decide "the ARV is $280,000 if I put $40,000 into it and wait six months." Suddenly they're in the renovation business. The renovation takes eight months instead of six. It costs $55,000 instead of $40,000. The final sale price comes in at $265,000, not $280,000.

The math that worked beautifully on the Fractured Equity side gets destroyed on the renovation side. They end up making the same money (or less) than if they'd sold as-is, after carrying cost and construction risk they weren't equipped to manage.

### The Discipline

When you underwrite a Fractured Equity deal, use as-is market value. Period. If you want to renovate afterward, that's a separate business decision with its own underwriting, its own risk profile, and its own return expectation. Don't confuse the two.

### The Comps You Pull

For as-is valuation, you want recent sales in the same neighborhood of properties in similar condition. A property that needs $40,000 of work should be valued against other properties that needed $40,000 of work. Your MLS agent or appraiser can help with this. Don't use ARV comps (fully renovated properties) to value fractured properties for acquisition purposes.

The as-is value is what anchors your $75,000 minimum spread calculation. As-is market value minus total liabilities equals equity spread. That's the number that matters.

## 3.5 The Package-Properties Doctrine — Where One Deal Becomes Many

I mentioned this in Chapter 1 and in the sixteen-property case study. Now let me teach it properly because it's the single most important economic multiplier in this business.

**The principle:** When you research the heirs of one fractured property, you've done 80% of the work needed to acquire every other property those same heirs have an interest in.

### How This Creates Packages

You identify a tax-delinquent property owned by the estate of John Smith, who died in 2003. You research his heirs — five children, two of whom are deceased with children of their own. Total of nine current living heirs. You spend time and money on genealogical research, obituary searches, skip tracing, and initial conversations.

Then you ask a simple question: "Did John Smith own any other properties?"

Most operators skip this question. They focus on the one property they found, do the deal, and move on.

The better move is to run John Smith's name through the county appraisal district in every county where he might have held property — not just the county where the target property sits. Also run the name through the property appraisal district in surrounding counties, neighboring states if relevant, and through probate filings to see if he was named as grantor or grantee in any other recorded transactions.

What you often find: John Smith owned three or five or fifteen other properties that were also inherited by the same nine heirs. Some rural, some in other counties, some vacant lots, some with structures. All of them now share the same ownership structure — same nine heirs, same fractional shares, same lack of consolidated authority to convey.

### Why This Creates Leverage

Once you're having a productive conversation with the nine heirs about Property A, expanding to Properties B through H costs you almost nothing additional. The heirs are already engaged. The AOH is already drafted for their family. The deed work for each additional property is standardized. The heirs sign one set of documents covering all the properties, you pay them a modest additional amount per property, and you acquire everything at once.

Your per-property acquisition cost drops dramatically. The genealogy work, which cost you maybe $1,500 in research and PI fees, is now spread across eight properties instead of one. Your sales conversation, which took weeks to establish rapport, converts eight times instead of once.

### The Economics

A single-property deal with $75,000 spread nets you $40,000. Time invested: 4 months, maybe 80 hours of your own time plus team time.

Find eight properties in the same family package, each with $40,000 spread (smaller per property but still worthwhile inside a package), total spread $320,000. Net profit across the package: approximately $180,000. Time invested: 6 months, maybe 150 hours of your own time plus team time.

The single-property deal: $40,000 in 4 months = $500/hour on your time.

The package deal: $180,000 in 6 months = $1,200/hour on your time.

The package deal is 2.4x better on an hourly basis. And your capital efficiency is also higher — you're not doing genealogy research and heir outreach eight separate times.

### What the Research Actually Looks Like

1. Search the county appraisal district by decedent's name in the target county
2. Search in surrounding counties (decedents often owned property within 100 miles of their residence)
3. Search in counties where the decedent previously lived (check obituaries for biographical clues)
4. If the decedent was involved in business, search in counties where the business operated
5. Pull property records by the decedent's SSN or date-of-birth if the appraisal district's search tool allows
6. Cross-reference with the decedent's estate filings if any probate exists

You'll often find properties the heirs themselves don't know about. A grandfather who bought a rural parcel in 1962 as an investment, never talked about it, and the family has no idea exists. When you show up with the information, the heirs are often grateful — they can't do anything with it, but they're happy for you to handle it and give them some money.

### The Package Discount

When you're acquiring multiple properties in a single conversation, the heirs often accept lower per-property payments because the aggregate is still substantial. A heir who might have demanded $5,000 for a share of a single property might accept $2,000 per property across four properties — a total of $8,000 to them, but $3,000 less per property than they would have demanded for just one.

The heirs win — they're getting more total money with less effort. You win — your per-property cost drops. Everyone leaves the table better off.

### The Operational Rhythm

After every deal, before you close the file, run the package check. Sometimes you'll find nothing and you'll move on. Sometimes you'll find one more property. Occasionally you'll find six or eight or sixteen. The occasional discovery pays for the routine effort across hundreds of deals where the decedent only owned the one property.

This is the kind of discipline that separates $200,000-per-year operators from $800,000-per-year operators. Same skill set, same heir conversations, same legal work. One of them runs the package check every time. The other doesn't.

## 3.6 Why Consistency Beats Volume

There's a pattern I've watched repeatedly in this business: operators who try to do 15 or 20 deals per year underperform operators who do 6 to 10 deals per year at higher quality.

The reason is simple. Fractured Equity deals have variable complexity. A simple deal takes 75 days and $15,000 in total costs. A complex deal can take 6 months and $50,000. An operator who tries to run 20 deals simultaneously will inevitably end up with some bad deals in the pipeline — deals that shouldn't have been worked, deals that blew up mid-process, deals that absorbed capital and attention that could have been deployed on better fact patterns.

The operator who runs 8 deals per year but runs them well often nets more than the operator running 20 deals per year who can't control the quality of the pipeline.

### What Consistency Looks Like

1. A clear rule on minimum spread (typically $75,000 as discussed)
2. A disciplined underwriting checklist applied to every deal
3. Willingness to walk away from deals that don't meet the criteria, even when sunk cost says to continue
4. A predictable monthly deal flow — 1 deal closing most months, occasionally 2 in a month, never pushing into volumes the team can't support
5. A pipeline management rhythm where old deals close before new deals start

### Income Trajectory Under Consistency

**First year (1–3 deals):** Gross profit $80,000 to $200,000, depending on deal quality and market. Most of this year is spent learning the business, making mistakes, building vendor relationships, and refining systems. Net after all business expenses (attorneys, research tools, marketing, systems, taxes) might be $50,000 to $120,000.

**Second year (4–7 deals):** Gross profit $250,000 to $500,000. Systems are working, team is forming, you're running deals more efficiently. Net after business expenses $150,000 to $300,000.

**Third year and beyond (8–15 deals):** Gross profit $500,000 to $1.5M+ at scale. Team handles most operational work, your time focuses on sourcing, highest-value negotiations, and strategic decisions. Net after business expenses $300,000 to $900,000+.

**The operators who cross $1M in annual net:** They're typically running 10–15 deals per year with an average net of $80,000 to $120,000 per deal, plus occasional package deals that add 6-figure multipliers to the year. They've invested in teams (2–5 people), systems (CRM, automation, research tools), and vendor relationships (attorneys, title officers, PIs, genealogists).

### What Breaks This

1. Taking deals below your minimum spread
2. Trying to scale faster than your systems support
3. Not running the package check, leaving multiplier profits on the table
4. Getting into renovation work on deals you should have sold as-is
5. Not firing deals that are going sideways in the first 30 days

## 3.7 Capital Requirements and the Capital Efficiency of Partial Interest

Let's talk about how much money you need to start.

### Minimum Working Capital to Begin: $20,000–$40,000

This covers:

1. Initial research and skip-trace tools ($200–$500/month)
2. Genealogy services for your first few deals ($500–$2,000 per deal)
3. Initial heir payouts ($5,000–$15,000 spread across your first deal)
4. Attorney fees for your first deal's curative work ($2,000–$5,000)
5. Holding costs between acquisition and resale (insurance, taxes, utilities, maybe $500–$1,500 per month on one property)
6. Cash reserve for surprises ($5,000–$10,000)

You can get to first close with $20,000 if you're careful and the deal is clean. $40,000 gives you enough cushion to absorb the first deal's mistakes and start the second deal without waiting for the first to close.

### Operating Capital to Run a Real Pipeline: $75,000–$150,000

At the point where you're running 3–5 deals simultaneously, you need working capital sufficient to carry multiple deals at various stages. You might have $30,000 out on four different acquisitions in process, plus operating costs for the business.

### Scaling Capital: $250,000+

At 8–15 deals per year, pipeline capital becomes significant. Some deals require larger upfront heir payouts. Some require carrying costs during extended curative work. Some involve holding inventory (completed consolidations awaiting resale in a slow market).

### The Capital Efficiency Advantage of Partial Interest

Here's why this strategy is fundamentally different from traditional real estate in capital terms.

In a traditional flip, you buy the whole property for, say, $150,000. Your capital is tied up in that property until it resells. If you have $300,000 of working capital, you can do two flips at a time, maximum.

In Fractured Equity, you're often buying partial interests. You might pay $3,000 for a 10% interest, then $4,500 for another 15%, then $6,000 for another 20%. You have $13,500 out on the property, not $150,000. Even when you finish consolidating to 100%, your total acquisition cost might be $25,000 to $45,000 on a property worth $220,000.

Your capital per deal is a fraction of what traditional real estate requires. $100,000 of working capital can support 4–6 simultaneous deals, not 2. Your capital turns faster. Your return on capital is higher.

This is why serious operators scale in this strategy faster than they can in flipping. The capital efficiency supports it.

## 3.8 Time Investment and the First-Deal Timeline

New operators consistently overestimate how fast their first deal will close and underestimate the psychological effort required to keep pushing during the quiet weeks.

### Realistic First-Deal Timeline

**Weeks 1–2:** Setting up the business. Getting tools. Building a lead list. Learning to read tax delinquency rolls and probate filings. Making your first phone calls. Almost no visible progress. You'll feel like nothing is happening.

**Weeks 2–3:** First conversations with heirs. Early genealogical research. Identifying one or two deals worth pursuing. Starting to see fact patterns make sense.

**Weeks 3–4:** First verbal commitment from an heir. Preparing the closing package. Negotiating final terms. Scheduling signing.

**Weeks 4–5:** First close. First deed recorded. First property acquired.

The operators who quit do so in Weeks 2–3 because nothing visible is happening. They can't tolerate the quiet. The ones who keep going — who trust that the phone calls and the research and the unpacking of fact patterns is the work — close their first deal by Week 5.

Your first close won't be your best deal. It'll probably be a simple fact pattern, a modest spread, with a cooperative heir or two. That's fine. The purpose of the first deal is to prove to yourself that the business works. Once you've closed one deal, the psychological barrier breaks and the second deal comes faster.

### The Second-Deal Observation

First deal to second deal typically takes 1–2 weeks. First deal to third deal typically adds another 2–4 weeks. By the time you've done three deals, you have a rhythm.

### First-Year Time Commitment

Realistic range: 15–30 hours per week for someone learning part-time, 40–60 hours per week for someone building a business full-time. The time isn't spread evenly — you'll have weeks of intense phone work, weeks of legal paperwork, weeks of waiting for service or court responses.

### Team Scaling Timeline

**Solo (deals 1–5):** You do everything yourself. Learning, mistakes, direct experience.

**First hire (deals 6–15):** Usually an operations person — someone who handles paperwork, research, and administrative tasks while you focus on calls, negotiation, and strategy.

**Second hire (deals 15–30):** Either a sales specialist (takes heir calls) or a researcher/genealogist (does the fact-pattern analysis and tree-building).

**Full team (30+ deals):** Sales team, operations team, genealogy, deal management, legal coordinator.

## 3.9 The Deal Math Worksheet — Your Daily Evaluation Tool

By the end of this Codex you'll have a deal math worksheet in Appendix D that you'll fill out on every deal before committing capital. For this chapter, here's the simplified version so you understand the mental model.

### Top Section: The Spread Calculation

1. As-is market value: $______
2. Minus: Delinquent taxes: $______
3. Minus: Tax loan payoff (if any): $______
4. Minus: Mortgage balance: $______
5. Minus: Other direct liens: $______
6. **Equity spread: $______**

If the spread is below $75,000, stop here unless one of the exceptions applies.

### Middle Section: Estimated Costs

1. Estimated heir payouts: $______ (estimate based on heir count and sophistication)
2. Estimated attorney fees: $______ (estimate based on deal complexity)
3. Estimated notary/travel: $______
4. Estimated junk removal/turn costs: $______
5. Estimated Schedule C surprise budget: $______ (typically $2,000)
6. Realtor commissions on resale (6% of sale price): $______
7. Title and closing costs on resale: $______
8. Financing costs (if applicable): $______
9. **Total estimated costs: $______**

### Bottom Section: The Net

1. Equity spread: $______
2. Minus: Total estimated costs: $______
3. **Estimated net profit: $______**

### Decision Rule

1. If estimated net is $30,000+ and the fact pattern is manageable: pursue
2. If estimated net is $15,000–$30,000 and it's a simple fact pattern: consider, but only if pipeline is slow
3. If estimated net is below $15,000: pass
4. If estimated net is negative: obviously pass

You'll find that most deals you look at don't pass this evaluation. That's normal. You're looking for the 1 in 10 or 1 in 15 opportunities that do. Don't force a deal that doesn't fit the math.

## 3.10 The Income Trajectory — What to Expect Year by Year

I want to ground you in what to realistically expect. Not what's possible at the highest end, not what the worst operators produce, but what a competent operator who applies the discipline in this Codex can expect.

### Year 1: Building the Foundation

1. 1–3 closed deals
2. Gross profit: $80,000–$200,000
3. Net after business expenses: $50,000–$120,000
4. Mostly learning, making mistakes, establishing vendor relationships
5. The operator is still working alone or with minimal help

### Year 2: Establishing the Business

1. 4–7 closed deals
2. Gross profit: $250,000–$500,000
3. Net after business expenses: $150,000–$300,000
4. Systems forming, first team member hired, deal process standardizing
5. The operator is spending more time on calls and strategy, less on paperwork

### Year 3: Scaling

1. 8–12 closed deals
2. Gross profit: $500,000–$1,000,000
3. Net after business expenses: $300,000–$600,000
4. Team of 2–4 people, CRM and automation in place, consistent pipeline
5. The operator is spending time on highest-value negotiations and recruitment

### Year 5+: Mature Operation

1. 10–20 closed deals per year with occasional package deals
2. Gross profit: $750,000–$2M+
3. Net after business expenses: $450,000–$1.2M+
4. Team of 4–8 people, operational systems optimized
5. The operator is focused on strategic decisions, highest-value deals, and potentially expanding into multiple markets

### The Operators Who Exceed This Trajectory

Some operators scale faster and higher. They hit $1M in Year 2 or $2M in Year 3. Usually because they came into the business with significant real estate experience, capital, or team infrastructure that accelerated their ramp. These are exceptions, not the baseline.

### The Operators Who Fall Short

More common. They get stuck at Year 1 numbers for two or three years because they never fully commit to the discipline — the $75K floor, the package check, the consistent pipeline, the team-building. They do 2 or 3 deals a year forever and wonder why the business doesn't scale.

The difference is execution discipline, not market conditions or luck.
