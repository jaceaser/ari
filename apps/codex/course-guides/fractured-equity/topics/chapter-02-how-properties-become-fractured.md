# How Properties Become Fractured

## 2.1 Why This Chapter Matters

Before you can acquire fractured properties, you need to understand how they got fractured in the first place.

This isn't academic. It's practical. When you pull a tax delinquency list and see 400 properties, you need to be able to look at the owner names and dates and guess — with reasonable accuracy — which properties are fractured and which are just ordinary delinquencies. When you call an heir for the first time, you need to know enough about how their family's property ended up stuck that you can ask the right questions without sounding like an idiot. When you underwrite a deal, you need to know what category of fracture you're dealing with because each category has its own resolution pathway, its own cost, and its own timeline.

Every fractured property in this country got fractured through one of a small number of pathways. I'm going to walk you through each one. By the end of this chapter you'll recognize these patterns from across a room.

---

## 2.2 Death Without a Will — The Single Biggest Source of Fractured Equity

More than half of American adults die without a will. Some estimates put it closer to two-thirds. When that happens, the decedent's property — including any real estate they owned — passes under the laws of intestate succession in the state where the property is located.

Intestate succession is the default. It's what the law does when a person doesn't leave written instructions. And intestate succession, applied to real estate, is the single largest machine in America for fracturing property ownership.

Here's why.

A decedent dies intestate leaving real property and a surviving spouse and three kids. Depending on the state and the specific property (community vs. separate, homestead vs. non-homestead), the law dictates a specific split of ownership. Maybe the surviving spouse takes 50% and the three kids split the other 50% — 16.67% each. Now four people own the property. None of them alone can sell it. All four have to agree, sign deeds, and attend closings.

Ten years later, one of those kids dies intestate with two children of her own. Her 16.67% now splits between those two children — 8.33% each. Now five people own the property: the surviving spouse, two of the original kids, and two grandchildren.

Fifteen years later, the surviving spouse dies intestate. Her 50% passes to her three children — but one of them is already dead, so her 16.67% passes to the two grandchildren (in addition to their mother's original share). Now the ownership structure is:

1. Original kid #1: 33.33% (his original 16.67% plus his share of his mother's 50%)
2. Original kid #2: 33.33%
3. Grandchild #1: 16.67% (her mother's 8.33% plus her share of the grandmother's 50%)
4. Grandchild #2: 16.67%

Four living owners, each with an odd fractional share, and we haven't even factored in any remarriages, adopted children, or children from prior relationships. If the original decedent had outside children — kids from a prior marriage, kids the surviving spouse didn't know about — the math gets even more tangled.

This is how a single piece of property that was "the family house" in 1975 becomes an ownership nightmare by 2025. Nobody set out to make it complicated. The law just applied itself, quietly, at each death.

### Why Intestate Families Don't Clean It Up

You might wonder why these families don't just probate the estate, sign some paperwork, and consolidate ownership in one person. The answer is almost always one of these:

**Nobody knows they need to.** Most families don't realize that when someone dies without a will, property doesn't automatically go to the obvious person. They assume Mom's house now belongs to "the kids" collectively in some informal way, and nobody ever writes it down or records it.

**The cost scares them off.** Formal probate costs $2,500 to $10,000 or more depending on the state and the complexity. Families without significant assets often skip probate entirely, letting the property sit in an informal limbo.

**There's nothing to fight over yet.** As long as Mom's house is sitting there, nobody is fighting. The fighting starts when someone wants to sell, someone else doesn't, and the family discovers they can't sell because nobody has the authority.

**They don't speak to each other.** Family estrangement is the hidden fuel of fractured equity. When siblings don't talk, they can't coordinate to sell. When they can't coordinate, the property sits, taxes accumulate, and eventually it ends up on a delinquency list.

**Some heirs have moved away and lost touch.** Twenty years after the original death, one of the heirs lives in another state and hasn't thought about the property in a decade. Another heir is in prison. Another has mental health issues. Another died without anyone notifying the family.

Every intestate death without probate creates potential fracture. Every generation of intestate deaths without probate stacks the fractures higher. The properties you'll find on your deal lists are often the result of 40, 60, or 80 years of compounding intestate succession that nobody ever cleaned up.

## 2.3 Wills That Were Never Probated

The second major source of fractured property is wills that exist but never got filed for probate.

A person writes a will. They put it in a drawer. They die. The family finds the will, reads it, sees that Mom left everything to the oldest son. Everybody agrees that's fine. They hand the house keys to the oldest son and move on with their lives.

Here's the problem: a will that has never been admitted to probate has no legal effect on real property. Under Texas Estates Code § 256.003 and equivalent laws in other states, a will typically must be admitted to probate within four years of the decedent's death. After four years, admission becomes much harder and in many cases impossible.

If the will was never admitted, the property passes by intestate succession — as if the will had never existed. So the oldest son the family gave the keys to? He doesn't actually own the property. The property is owned by whoever the intestate succession laws say inherits — which is usually all the decedent's children equally, plus possibly a surviving spouse.

Now layer fifteen years on top of that. The oldest son has been living in the house, paying taxes, thinking he owns it. His siblings have scattered, and most assume the same thing — Mom's will left the house to him. In reality, each sibling still owns an undivided interest in the property. The will did nothing. The family's informal agreement did nothing. Legally, the property is jointly owned by all the children of the original decedent, most of whom don't know they own it.

When you come along, the oldest son will tell you with complete certainty that he owns the property. He'll be wrong. The AOH you research will show the actual legal owners under intestate succession — him plus all his siblings. That gives you a strategic choice: acquire from the siblings (who don't know they own anything and will often sign for small payments), acquire from the oldest son if he's willing, or both.

**The unprobated-will situation creates a specific strategic reality:** the person in possession may believe they own the whole property when, legally, they may own only a fractional interest under intestate succession. Your job is not to exploit that misunderstanding. Your job is to identify the actual ownership, communicate clearly what interest is being conveyed, and buy only on terms that are fair, accurate, and fully documented. In practice, that means you acquire the seller's actual interest, disclose the nature of that interest in the transaction documents, and then determine whether the remaining heirs are willing to sell as well.

The opportunity is not in confusing people. The opportunity is in doing the research others did not do, identifying the actual ownership structure, and creating a lawful path to consolidation. That keeps the deal ethical, defensible, and insurable — which is exactly how this business should be done.

## 2.4 Wills That Were Probated but Never Closed

Some estates get probated but never formally closed. The personal representative files the will, gets letters testamentary, and then… stops. Maybe they meant to distribute the property and never got around to it. Maybe there were debts they couldn't resolve. Maybe they died themselves before finishing the work.

An open probate with an unclosed administration creates a specific legal situation: the estate still owns the property, the appointed executor still has authority to act on behalf of the estate, and the beneficiaries have rights to eventual distribution but don't yet hold title.

This is actually one of the best fact patterns in the business, because it creates an opportunity for what I'll teach in Chapter 18 as the administrator/executor single-signature shortcut. When a probate is open and an executor is in place, that executor can convey the estate's property in one signature — regardless of how many beneficiaries are scattered below. You don't need twelve heirs to sign; you need one executor. The rest of the work happens inside the estate.

I'll go deep on the mechanics in Chapter 18. For now, just understand that an open, unclosed probate is a category of fracture that has a specific and powerful resolution pathway available to you.

## 2.5 Multiple Generations of Unsettled Estates — The Compounding Effect

The worst (and most profitable) fact patterns involve two, three, or four generations of unsettled estates stacked on top of each other.

A property originally deeded to a couple in 1958. Husband dies intestate in 1982. No probate. Property passes under intestate succession to wife plus four children. Wife dies intestate in 2001. No probate. Her half of the property plus any share she held from husband's death now passes under intestate succession. Two of the original four children have died in the intervening years — one in 1995 with three children, one in 2018 with four children and a second spouse. The intestate succession calculations for each death layer on top of the previous ones.

By the time you encounter this property in 2024, the ownership might look like:

1. Two surviving original children: shares calculated from the intestate deaths of both parents
2. Three grandchildren from the 1995 death: shares calculated from their mother's share plus her portion of grandmother's death
3. Four grandchildren plus one surviving spouse from the 2018 death: more complex calculations
4. Potentially a posthumous adjustment if any of the deaths triggered different intestate rules depending on when they occurred

You're looking at nine to twelve current owners, each with a fractional share requiring careful math to determine. Nobody in the family has ever calculated these shares. Nobody has recorded anything. The property has simply sat there, passing through each intestate death silently, while the tax bill came addressed to the original owners from 1958 and nobody paid it.

These are the deals that create $100,000 to $200,000 paydays. The complexity that overwhelms most investors is exactly what creates the discount to the market. You paid maybe $40,000 across ten scattered heirs to consolidate a property worth $280,000. The spread exists because almost no one else in the real estate industry can even diagnose what's going on, much less solve it.

Chapter 13 will teach you the fact-pattern analysis methodology for untangling these. By the time we finish Part III of this Codex, you'll look at a four-generation fact pattern and see it as a structured problem, not chaos.

## 2.6 Outside Children, Prior Marriages, and Blended Families

Outside children — children from a relationship other than the decedent's current marriage — are the single most common fact-pattern detail that flips an ownership analysis. They're also the most commonly overlooked.

Here's the rule in Texas (and the logic is similar in other community property states): when a decedent dies intestate leaving a surviving spouse plus any child who is not also the surviving spouse's biological or adopted child, the decedent's share of the community property does not pass to the surviving spouse. It passes to all the decedent's children — including the outside children — in equal shares.

Read that twice. One outside child flips the rule completely. The surviving spouse, who most people assume "gets everything," gets nothing beyond her own half of the community. The decedent's half goes entirely to his children, and the children from the prior relationship are on equal footing with the children from the current marriage.

### How Outside Children Create Fractured Properties

**The decedent's family doesn't know about the outside children.** This happens all the time. A man has a child with someone before his marriage, loses contact, marries someone else, builds a life. His widow and his kids with her assume they're the heirs. They're not. The forgotten child — who may not even know the decedent was their parent — is a legal heir under intestate succession.

**The outside children know but the "inside" family doesn't.** The outside child knows perfectly well who their father was. They may have maintained distant contact or been aware of the death but didn't come forward. When an investor does proper genealogical research, the outside child surfaces.

**The outside children have died and their own descendants are the heirs.** Twenty years after the decedent's death, the outside child may be deceased. Her children — the decedent's grandchildren, who may have no connection to the rest of the family at all — are now the legal heirs.

### Why This Matters for Your Business

When you find a property where the title analysis seems simple ("husband died, property went to his wife, wife died, property went to her three kids"), always ask whether the husband had children from a prior relationship. If he did, the whole analysis changes. The wife never inherited what the family thinks she inherited. The property actually has additional owners nobody has accounted for.

This is where careful genealogical research creates competitive advantage. Other investors look at the deed, the obituary, and the family tree as told by one surviving heir. You pull marriage records, look for prior marriages, check for divorce decrees, search Social Security death records for children who might not appear in current obituaries. That work uncovers outside heirs who represent both ownership interests you need to acquire and leverage you can use in negotiations with other heirs.

## 2.7 Defective Conveyances in the Chain of Title

Not every fractured property has a complex family situation. Some have clean families but broken chains of title. A deed somewhere in the property's history was defective, and the defect has never been cured, and now the current family can't sell because no title company will insure it.

Common defects:

**Missing signatures.** A married person conveyed property without the required spousal signature. In some states — and specifically on homestead property in Texas — both spouses must sign on a conveyance even if only one spouse is on the deed. A deed signed by only one spouse on a homestead property is defective and may fail to convey.

**Wrong legal description.** The deed describes the property incorrectly — wrong lot number, wrong survey reference, conflicting metes and bounds. A title examiner comparing the deed to the county records will flag it.

**Grantor with no authority.** The person who signed didn't actually own the property, or didn't have the authority they claimed to have. This happens with executors who sign in individual capacity instead of representative capacity. It happens with corporate officers whose authority to convey wasn't documented. It happens with powers of attorney that didn't authorize real estate transactions.

**Forged signatures.** A deed signed by someone other than the named grantor. Sometimes intentional fraud, sometimes family members signing for absent relatives "to save time." Either way, the deed is void to the extent of the forgery.

**Deed never recorded.** A deed that was signed but never filed with the county. Until it's recorded, it's ineffective against subsequent purchasers without notice. Decades later, the deed may be lost or the grantor may be deceased, making the transfer impossible to re-execute.

**Tax sale defects.** A property was sold at tax auction, but the notice requirements weren't properly met, or the redemption period wasn't properly honored, or the sale itself had procedural flaws. Later examination reveals the tax sale is vulnerable to challenge.

**Dissolved entity conveyances.** A corporation or LLC conveyed property before or after its dissolution without proper winding-up procedures.

When a family tries to sell a property with a defective conveyance somewhere in the chain, the title company stops the deal. The defect has to be cured — usually through a corrective deed, a quiet title action, or in some cases an affidavit that establishes the intent of the original transaction. These cures take time, money, and legal sophistication that ordinary families don't have. The property sits unsold.

You step in. Your attorneys know how to handle defective conveyances — whether through correction deeds cooperatively executed, quiet title actions litigated through court, or adverse possession claims supported by long-term occupation. You pay the family a discount because the title is broken. You fix the title. You resell at market. The spread is the cost of fixing the problem nobody else could fix.

Chapter 7 covers deed mechanics, Chapter 10 covers quiet title, and Chapter 35 covers defective deeds and chain problems in detail. For now, just understand that defective conveyances are a specific category of fracture with specific resolution pathways.

## 2.8 Properties Held by Defunct or Dissolved Entities

Real estate is sometimes owned by corporations, LLCs, nonprofit organizations, trusts, or religious associations. When those entities continue operating, the ownership is clean. When those entities dissolve, forfeit their corporate status, or lose track of their governance, the real estate becomes functionally stuck.

**Administrative forfeiture** happens when an entity fails to file required state reports, pay franchise taxes, or maintain its registered agent. The Secretary of State forfeits the entity's right to do business. The entity technically still exists but can't transact cleanly until the forfeiture is cured.

**Voluntary dissolution** happens when the owners of an entity formally wind it up. If the wind-up includes proper transfer of assets to the owners, everything's fine. If the wind-up is incomplete — the entity dissolved but the real estate was never deeded out — the real estate sits in a dissolved entity that has no active governance.

**Administrative dissolution** happens when a state dissolves an entity for long-term failure to file or pay. Similar to forfeiture but more terminal.

**Abandoned entities** are entities whose owners have lost interest or died. The corporation has no active board, no active members, no active operations. The property sits.

**Religious and nonprofit entities** are particularly prone to this because governance is often informal and records are poorly maintained. A small church bought a property in 1952 when its founding pastor was young. The pastor died in 1985. The church dwindled to a few elderly members. By 2010, there's no functioning board, no current records, and the property sits titled to "First Baptist Church of [Town]" with no living person clearly authorized to convey.

### How This Creates a Deal for You

Defunct entities require specialized approaches. You can't just have "the LLC" sign a deed — there's no active manager to sign. You need to either:

1. Reinstate the entity, restore its governance, and have properly-authorized officers convey
2. Work with the last known officers or members under wind-up authority
3. Pursue the conveyance through a court proceeding that establishes authority
4. In certain cases, acquire through a combination of heirship (if the entity owners died) and entity wind-up procedures

The complexity keeps competitors out. The bonus chapters at the end of this Codex — Chapters 36-38 on Defunct LLCs and Chapters 39-40 on Dormant Trusts — walk through the specific mechanics for each type of defunct entity.

## 2.9 Tax-Delinquent Properties with Complicated Ownership

Every county in America publishes a tax delinquency list. Most investors look at those lists and see the delinquent tax balance as the deal — buy the property for the back taxes, fix it up, sell it.

The real operators look at the delinquency list as a diagnostic.

A tax-delinquent property with a single current owner is usually just a distressed owner who can't afford the taxes. That's a straightforward situation — you negotiate with the owner to buy at a discount before the auction, or you buy at the auction itself.

A tax-delinquent property where the owner on the tax roll has been dead for five or fifteen years is a fractured property. The reason the taxes aren't being paid isn't that the owner can't afford it. The reason is that nobody has the authority to write the check. The estate never distributed. The heirs are scattered. Nobody wants to be the one to pay because they'd be paying on everyone's behalf without any guarantee of being reimbursed.

When you see the same decedent's name on the tax roll for twenty years after their death, you're looking at a fractured property almost by definition. Someone has to have died. Someone has to have failed to probate. Heirs are somewhere, unaware or uncooperative.

Tax delinquency is the pressure that brings these properties to the surface. The family may have ignored the property for years. Once the county schedules a foreclosure auction, suddenly the issue forces itself into view. Heirs start talking to each other — often for the first time in years. Someone might scramble to pay the taxes and save the property, but without a single responsible party, the scramble usually fails. The property goes to auction, often unsold because of the ownership problems, and the cycle continues.

This is your entry point. The deadline pressure of a scheduled tax sale creates urgency in a family that has ignored the property for a decade. When you call an heir and explain that the property is 45 days from auction, suddenly they pay attention. The technique is to arrive at that conversation already having done the heirship research, already holding a plan, and already able to offer a clean exit from the problem they've been avoiding.

## 2.10 Life Estates That Block the Remainder

A life estate is a specific form of property ownership where one person (the life tenant) has the right to use the property for the rest of their life, and another person or group (the remainderman or remaindermen) has the right to take possession at the life tenant's death.

Life estates arise in a few ways:

**By deed.** A parent deeds property to their children while reserving a life estate for themselves. This is a common estate planning technique.

**By will.** A will leaves property to one person for life, with the remainder to someone else at death.

**By operation of law.** In some states, a surviving spouse inherits a life estate in certain portions of a deceased spouse's separate property when the deceased spouse had outside children. This is a major source of life estates you'll encounter in practice — the life estate wasn't created on purpose; it was created automatically by the intestate succession statute.

**Lady bird deeds (enhanced life estate deeds)** are a specialized form used primarily in Texas and Florida. The grantor retains a life estate with full powers to sell, mortgage, or cancel during life, with the remainder passing at death. Covered in Chapter 7.

### Why Life Estates Fracture Properties

A life tenant and a remainderman together hold all the ownership interests in a property. Neither can sell alone. If Grandma has a life estate and her three children hold the remainder, none of them individually can convey marketable title. Grandma can't sell without the kids' signatures on a release of the remainder. The kids can't sell while Grandma is alive without her release of the life estate.

Life estates can create stuck properties when:

**The life tenant refuses to release.** Grandma is 78 and wants to stay in the house forever. The kids can't do anything until she dies, which could be 20 years.

**The life tenant can't be found.** The life tenant moved, lost contact, or disappeared. The remaindermen can't cure title because they can't get the release.

**The remaindermen disagree.** Three kids hold the remainder; two want to sell when Grandma dies, one doesn't. Even after Grandma's death, they can't act in unison.

**The life tenant is committing waste.** Grandma hasn't paid taxes in four years. The property is deteriorating. The remaindermen want to act but have limited options because they can't displace a living life tenant.

Chapter 28 teaches the waste doctrine approach to extinguishing life estates when the life tenant has stopped paying taxes. It's a powerful pathway for deals where Grandma won't release voluntarily but also won't pay the taxes that are accumulating in her name.

## 2.11 Missing and Unlocatable Heirs

Some fractures don't come from complex family structures or defective conveyances. They come from a specific heir who has gone missing.

A family might have a clean ownership picture — one decedent, five heirs, no complications — except that one of those five heirs moved to Oregon in 1994 and hasn't been heard from in three decades. The other four want to sell. The property can't be sold cleanly without the missing heir's signature or without a legal procedure that works around the absence.

Missing heirs fall into categories:

**Deliberate estrangement.** The heir chose to cut off the family and doesn't want to be found. They may have changed their name, moved to another country, or simply gone silent.

**Mental illness or substance use.** The heir's condition has caused them to lose contact with family. They may be homeless, institutionalized, or cycling through hospitals and shelters.

**Incarceration.** The heir is in prison. They can be located through correctional records, but contact requires specific protocols.

**International residence.** The heir moved to another country years ago. Tracking them requires international genealogical research.

**Presumed dead but not legally declared.** The heir hasn't been heard from for more than seven years. In Texas and most states, statutory procedures exist to have them declared presumed dead for inheritance purposes, but these procedures haven't been used.

**Actually dead but unrecorded.** The heir died in another state, or abroad, and the death was never communicated to the family. They may have left descendants of their own who are now the actual heirs.

Chapter 32 covers the full playbook for missing heirs, including the Texas presumed-death procedure (Estates Code §§ 551.001–551.106), the five legitimate pathways for international heir scenarios, private investigator deployment for homeless and incarcerated heirs, and the indemnification-based approaches for deals where some heirs simply cannot be located despite diligent search.

## 2.12 Adverse Possession Situations

Some fractured properties involve someone who has been using the property for decades without holding a clean title to it. This is the territory of adverse possession.

A property's original owners died fifty years ago without probating. A distant relative moved onto the property sometime in the 1970s — maybe a grandson, maybe a boyfriend of one of the heirs, maybe someone who just started using the land with the family's informal acceptance. They've paid the taxes ever since. They've fenced the property, maintained it, lived there or farmed it.

Legally, they don't own the property. The chain of title runs through the original decedent's heirs, who are scattered and largely unaware. But under adverse possession statutes, long-term open and notorious possession of a property creates a potential claim to title — subject to meeting specific statutory requirements.

Texas has multiple adverse possession variants with different requirements:

1. **3-year statute:** Requires color of title (a deed that looks like it conveys but has a defect)
2. **5-year statute:** Requires color of title, recorded deed, tax payment, and peaceable possession
3. **10-year statute:** Peaceable and adverse possession for 10 years; no color of title required but 160-acre cap unless enclosed
4. **25-year statute:** Peaceable and adverse possession for 25 years; the strongest variant with no tax payment or color of title required

An adverse possessor who has met all the elements for long enough has a legal claim to title — but that claim typically requires a quiet title action in court to be converted into marketable title. Until they complete that process, the property remains in legal limbo: the recorded owners still hold title on paper, the adverse possessor has a statutory claim, and no title company will insure a sale.

### How This Creates Deals

You identify a property with a 25-year occupant who has paid taxes for decades but doesn't hold a deed. You either:

1. Acquire from the original title holders (the heirs of the original owner, wherever they are) and use their interests to quiet title against the occupant
2. Acquire from the occupant directly and pursue quiet title on their adverse possession claim
3. In some cases, acquire from both sides and quiet title through consolidation

Chapter 31 covers adverse possession as both sword and shield in detail. The key insight for this chapter is simply that long-term possession without clean title is a category of fracture that creates its own kind of deal opportunity.

## 2.13 The Property That Has Every Fracture at Once

I want to close this chapter by walking you through a fact pattern that has every kind of fracture stacked on top of each other, because these are the deals that define careers.

Property originally deeded to a married couple in 1957. Husband had a child from a prior marriage before he met his wife; neither his wife nor his wife's children ever knew. Husband died intestate in 1984. No probate. Under Texas community property rules, because of the outside child, his half of the community passed to his children — his outside daughter (now middle-aged and living in another state, unaware she's an heir) and his three children with his wife. His wife took only her own half.

Wife remarried in 1989. New husband moved into the property. Wife died intestate in 2003 without children from the second marriage. No probate. Her interest in the property passed under intestate succession — depending on when exactly she died and the specifics of the property, some to her surviving second husband (in life estate, if the property was separate property) and some to her children.

Second husband stayed in the property as either a life tenant (by operation of law) or an occupant without clear legal right. Second husband never paid property taxes after 2005, believing the wife's children were responsible. Second husband died in 2018, leaving behind a handwritten note claiming the property as his own under adverse possession. His heirs now also potentially claim an interest.

Meanwhile, the original husband's outside daughter died in 2015, leaving two children who are the current legal heirs to her share. The original husband's three children with his first wife have had various life events: one died in 2009 with two children of his own (who are the current heirs to his share), one lives in the property intermittently and has been paying partial taxes since 2020, and one lives out of state and hasn't engaged with the property in thirty years.

Total current legal heir count, if properly researched: approximately nine to twelve people, including the outside daughter's children in another state who have no idea they're heirs to anything, the grandchildren of the deceased son, the second husband's heirs who may or may not have a valid adverse possession claim, and the two in-state children who are the primary point of contact.

Tax delinquency: $31,000. Scheduled foreclosure auction: 67 days out. Property as-is value: $195,000. Chain of title: has a 1994 deed that was signed by the wife without the second husband's required spousal signature, creating a defect in the chain.

Fractures present: ownership (scattered across 9-12 heirs), title (defective 1994 deed), possession (intermittent occupancy plus potential adverse possession claim), payment obligation (failed since 2005), plus an outside child the family never knew about, plus multiple generations of intestate deaths without probate, plus a scheduled tax sale creating time pressure.

This is what a rich fact pattern looks like. It's also exactly the kind of property nobody else in the real estate industry will touch. The as-is market value after consolidation is $195,000. The combined cost of acquisition, title curative, legal fees, and closing costs will likely land in the $55,000 to $75,000 range depending on how many heirs need to be bought out vs. forced out through legal process. Net profit: $100,000 to $125,000.

By the time you finish this Codex, you'll look at that fact pattern and see the solution pathway in about ten minutes. You'll know which acquisitions to pursue first, which heirs to research most aggressively, which legal mechanisms to deploy and when, and how to time the tax sale pullback. The complexity that paralyzes other investors will feel to you like a puzzle with a known solution method.
