## 5.1 Where We Are

Bonus Chapter 39 addressed finding dormant trusts and the people behind them — trustees, successor trustees, beneficiaries, grantors, and heirs of deceased parties. This chapter addresses the mechanics of actually acquiring the property through or from the trust. The conversations with trustees and beneficiaries. The diligence required before contracting. The deal structures available. The court proceedings that may be needed to produce clear authority. The title work. The closing mechanics. And the post-closing steps.

Trust acquisitions are typically more procedurally varied than defunct-LLC acquisitions. Defunct-LLC work has a relatively standardized cleanup path through the Comptroller and the Secretary of State. Trust work depends heavily on the specific trust instrument, the current posture of the trustees and beneficiaries, and whether court intervention is needed. No two trust deals look exactly alike.

Texas is the illustrative jurisdiction throughout. The Texas Trust Code (Texas Property Code, beginning at § 111.001) and related provisions provide the framework. Trust proceedings in Texas are generally handled in district courts, though certain matters go to probate courts. Every other state has its own trust law and court structure. If you are working elsewhere, engage a trust and estate attorney licensed in that state and have them translate the framework in this chapter to your jurisdiction.

## 5.2 First Contact With Trustees and Beneficiaries

The first conversation in a trust acquisition is different from the first conversation in a defunct-LLC acquisition. In an LLC case, you are usually talking to someone who knows they are a member even if they have forgotten the details. In a trust case, you are often talking to someone who has only fragmentary knowledge of their role — a successor trustee who was named years ago and never served, a beneficiary who was told as a child that a grandparent had set something aside. The conversation has to educate without overwhelming, inform without pressuring, and open the door to a longer relationship.

### Contacting the Trustee

When the trustee named on the deed or in a recorded memorandum of trust is still living and reachable, the trustee is your first contact. The opening:

*Hello, this is [your name] with [your company]. I believe you are named as trustee of the [trust name], which holds a property at [address]. I work with trustees whose trusts have become inactive — where the trust still holds property but the administration has not been updated in a while. I'm not with the state or any enforcement agency. I buy properties in this kind of situation and I handle the work that goes into bringing the trust administration current so the sale can happen cleanly. Would you be open to a brief conversation about your specific situation? Whatever we discuss, you would want to run it by your own attorney before committing to anything.*

Trustees vary widely in their knowledge and posture. Some are family members who were named as successor trustees but never served — they may not even know a trust exists or that they have a role. Others are attorneys or trust professionals who know the trust well. Others are beneficiaries who were also named as trustees. The first conversation establishes which category the trustee is in and calibrates the rest of the outreach accordingly.

### Contacting Beneficiaries

When the trustee cannot be reached, is deceased, or is unwilling to engage, the beneficiaries are the next avenue. Beneficiaries often have authority — either individually or collectively — to initiate the actions needed to address the trust's dormancy. The opening:

*Hello, this is [your name] with [your company]. I believe you are a beneficiary of the [trust name] created by [grantor's name]. The trust holds a property at [address], and the administration appears to be inactive. I work with beneficiaries in this kind of situation. I buy properties held by inactive trusts and handle the work required to bring the trust into a position to sell. I wanted to reach out because in many cases beneficiaries are not aware of their interest until something brings it to their attention. Would you be open to a brief conversation about your situation? You would want to verify anything I tell you with your own attorney before acting on it.*

Beneficiaries who had no knowledge of their interest are often the most receptive — you are delivering news about money they did not know they had a claim to. Beneficiaries who have been waiting for resolution are often grateful for a partner. Beneficiaries who have an emotional attachment to the property or its history may be more cautious; respect their pace and give them time.

### When the Grantor Is Still Living

For revocable living trusts where the grantor is still alive, the grantor may retain the ability to revoke the trust or direct the sale. The conversation in these cases goes directly to the grantor if possible. If the grantor has lost capacity, the capacity-and-representative-signing framework from Chapter 32 of the main Codex applies.

### When Beneficiaries Have Died

When key beneficiaries have died, their heirs stand in their shoes for most purposes. The conversation is similar to finding heirs of deceased members in LLC cases — respect the family's loss, explain the situation clearly, and indicate that the inheritance resolution may require some estate or heirship work alongside the trust work.

### Multiple Parties

Most trusts involve multiple parties — a trustee, two or three beneficiaries, sometimes remainder beneficiaries who take at trust termination. Reaching them in the right order matters. Generally:

1. Start with the most reachable person who has the most authority — often a sole trustee who is also a beneficiary
2. Use that first contact to get introductions to the others
3. Avoid cold contact with multiple parties simultaneously — it creates confusion and sometimes suspicion
4. Let the person you first reached vouch for you to the rest of the group

Sequence is not a formality here. It is a tactical decision that controls whether the group coalesces around a shared resolution or fractures into competing camps before you have established any trust.

## 5.3 Preliminary Diligence Before Contracting

Between first contact and the purchase contract, you complete the diligence picture. Trust diligence is more paper-intensive than LLC diligence because the trust instrument is private and often has to be pieced together.

### Trust Document Diligence

1. Locate the trust instrument itself — the complete original if possible, certified or reliable copy otherwise
2. Identify all amendments or restatements of the trust
3. Locate any separate documents the trust instrument references (schedules of property, lists of beneficiaries, funding documents)
4. Review the trust for the key terms — trustee succession, beneficiary identification, termination conditions, power of sale, requirements for modification or termination, and any specific provisions governing real property
5. Identify whether the trust is revocable or irrevocable
6. Identify whether the trust is a living trust (inter vivos) or a testamentary trust (created by a will)

If no copy of the trust instrument can be located through the trustee or beneficiaries, check probate records (some testamentary trusts are on file), recorded memoranda of trust, and attorney records if the drafting attorney can be identified. A trust cannot be administered — or sold out of — without someone producing the governing instrument.

### People Diligence

1. Confirm the current status of each named trustee — alive, dead, serving, resigned, reachable, unreachable
2. Confirm the current status of each named beneficiary — alive, dead, reachable, unreachable
3. For deceased beneficiaries, identify whether the estate has been administered and whether heirship determination is needed
4. Identify any named successor trustees and their status
5. For charitable trusts, identify the charitable beneficiaries and their current status

People diligence in trust cases is genealogical work. Apply the same breadcrumbing and genealogical research techniques from Chapter 30 of the main Codex. Deceased trustees and beneficiaries create gaps in the authority chain that must be traced before you can close. Do not assume everyone named in a trust from fifteen years ago is still alive.

### Property Diligence

1. Full chain of title — how the property came into the trust and any conveyances within or out
2. Current property tax status, delinquencies, any tax foreclosure calendar
3. Recorded liens, mortgages, mechanics liens, judgment liens, HOA liens
4. Current appraised value and estimated market value
5. Physical condition and occupancy status

The property's tax calendar is an urgency signal. If delinquent taxes are approaching the foreclosure threshold, the timeline for trust cleanup has to be compressed or the deal structure has to account for the tax situation before closing.

### Court Record Diligence

1. Search state and federal court records for prior proceedings involving the trust, the trustee in their trustee capacity, or any of the beneficiaries
2. Search probate records for the estate of the grantor and of any deceased trustees or beneficiaries
3. Identify any pending litigation or pending probate matters that could affect the trust

Prior proceedings can be either a help or a hindrance. A previously opened probate for the grantor's estate may have determined heirship questions that are now settled. An open adversarial proceeding among beneficiaries is a red flag that turns what looks like a routine trust deal into contested litigation. Always run court records before signing.

### Tax Diligence

1. Identify whether the trust has been filing required federal income tax returns (Form 1041)
2. Identify any accumulated trust-level tax obligations
3. For grantor trusts, understand how trust income has been reported historically

Trust-level tax delinquency does not automatically block a sale, but it affects how the closing is structured and what the beneficiaries receive. The CPA has to model the tax picture before you negotiate the purchase price.

## 5.4 Deal Structuring

The deal structure depends on who has authority to act, what the trust instrument allows, and whether court intervention will be needed. Several structures are common.

### Pathway 1 — Trustee-Led Sale

The cleanest structure when available. The trust has a living, serving trustee with clear authority under the trust instrument to sell real property. The trustee, acting in their trustee capacity, executes a sale of the property to the acquirer. Characteristics:

1. Trustee is alive, identified, and willing to serve
2. Trust instrument grants the trustee clear authority to sell — either expressly or through general trust-management powers
3. Any required beneficiary consents can be obtained
4. No court intervention is required
5. Trustee signs the deed in trustee capacity, proceeds go into the trust, trust distributes to beneficiaries per the trust instrument

Pathway 1 is the fastest and least expensive structure when the facts support it. The practical constraint is finding it. A dormant trust often lacks a trustee willing and able to serve. If one is available, move quickly.

### Pathway 2 — Successor Trustee Appointment Through the Trust Instrument

When the named trustee is gone (died, resigned, unable to serve) and the trust instrument names a qualified successor who is alive and willing to serve, the successor takes over without court action. Characteristics:

1. Trust instrument names a successor trustee and specifies how succession occurs
2. The named successor accepts the appointment through whatever mechanism the trust prescribes — often a written acceptance of trustee duties
3. The successor trustee then proceeds as in Pathway 1
4. Documentation of the succession is prepared for the title company to show authority
5. Sometimes a court order is still needed — even when the trust names a successor, title companies occasionally require judicial confirmation of the succession to be comfortable insuring

When a named successor is available and willing, prepare the acceptance documentation carefully and take it to the title company early. Get their underwriter's position on insurability before you invest additional time and money in the deal.

### Pathway 3 — Court-Appointed Successor Trustee

When no named successor is available — all named successors have died, resigned, or are unreachable — a court must appoint a successor trustee. Under Texas law, this is typically done in a district court under the Texas Trust Code. Characteristics:

1. Application for appointment of successor trustee is filed, typically by a beneficiary or another party with standing
2. Notice is given to beneficiaries and any other interested parties
3. Court appoints a qualified successor — often a beneficiary, sometimes a professional trustee, sometimes a neutral attorney
4. The newly appointed successor trustee then proceeds to sell the property as in Pathway 1
5. Adds time and cost but produces clear judicial authority that title companies typically accept

Pathway 3 is the workhorse of dormant-trust acquisitions. It takes six to ten months in Texas for an uncontested proceeding. The timeline must be factored into both your underwriting and any purchase contract outside-closing-date provision.

### Pathway 4 — Trust Modification or Termination

Some trust situations are best resolved by modifying the trust's terms or terminating it entirely. Grounds for modification or termination under Texas law include:

1. The purposes of the trust have been fulfilled or have become illegal or impracticable
2. The circumstances not anticipated by the grantor justify modification to carry out the grantor's intent
3. All beneficiaries consent and the modification is not inconsistent with a material purpose of the trust
4. The trust lacks sufficient assets to justify its continued administration
5. Other grounds provided by the Texas Trust Code or case law

Modification or termination typically requires court action — a proceeding initiated by the trustee, a beneficiary, or another interested party, with notice to all affected parties and sometimes to the Texas Attorney General for charitable trusts. The resulting court order provides authority for the specific action — distributing property to beneficiaries, selling property and distributing proceeds, or whatever the modification specifies.

In a dormant trust where the grantor's purposes have long since passed and continuing administration serves no function, trust termination with immediate property sale can be the cleanest outcome available. The court is often receptive to uncontested termination petitions when all beneficiaries support them.

### Pathway 5 — Acquiring Beneficiary Interests

Instead of (or in addition to) working with the trust itself, the acquirer can purchase the beneficial interests from the beneficiaries. This structure is analogous to the Pathway 2 membership-interest acquisition in defunct-LLC work. Characteristics:

1. Beneficiaries sell their interests in the trust to the acquirer
2. The acquirer becomes the beneficiary (or one of the beneficiaries)
3. As beneficiary, the acquirer can then consent to trust termination, direct the trustee to sell, or pursue other resolutions
4. Trust instrument may restrict beneficial interest transfers — some trusts have spendthrift provisions that prevent beneficiaries from transferring their interests
5. Spendthrift provisions are a significant constraint; under Tex. Prop. Code § 112.035, a spendthrift provision can block voluntary transfers by a beneficiary

This pathway is less commonly used than the others because of spendthrift provisions and the practical difficulty of becoming a beneficiary in place of the original. When available, it can be the cleanest path in specific situations — particularly in trusts without spendthrift restrictions where all beneficiaries are willing to sell.

The due-diligence step on Pathway 5 is reading the trust instrument for spendthrift language before any pricing conversation with the beneficiaries. Starting that conversation and then discovering the interest cannot be transferred wastes everyone's time and sometimes sours the relationship for other pathways.

### Combined and Hybrid Approaches

Many deals combine elements of multiple pathways. For example, the acquirer may initiate a court proceeding to appoint a successor trustee (Pathway 3), with the court-appointed successor then proceeding with a trustee-led sale (Pathway 1) after the appointment is final. Or the acquirer may obtain beneficiary consents (elements of Pathway 5) while also coordinating a trust termination (Pathway 4) to produce the cleanest possible title outcome. The attorney and the specific facts determine which combination fits.

The combinations are not shortcuts — they are responses to specific fact patterns. A deal with a willing beneficiary who lacks a successor trustee may require Pathway 3 for the appointment and Pathway 1 for the sale. A deal with all-consenting beneficiaries and a terminated grantor purpose may use elements of Pathway 4 with a beneficiary-interest consent executed as a conditions precedent. Get your trust attorney into the deal structure conversation before you commit to a purchase agreement.

## 5.5 Price and Terms

The purchase price and deal terms have to account for the specific costs and timeline of trust cleanup, which often exceed defunct-LLC cleanup costs because of the likelihood of court involvement.

### Backing Price Out From Exit

The same underwriting discipline applies as in defunct-LLC work:

1. Estimated exit value
2. Less required profit margin
3. Less rehab or improvement costs
4. Less holding costs during cleanup and exit
5. Less trust-cleanup costs (attorney fees for trust proceedings, court costs, ad litem fees if applicable, CPA fees)
6. Less heirship-related costs if deceased beneficiaries require heirship work
7. Less title insurance and closing costs
8. Equals the maximum price available to pay to the trust, the beneficiaries, or the sellers

The trust-cleanup-cost line is where most operators underestimate. Court proceedings cost money. Ad litems cost money. A CPA who knows trust taxation is not a generic accountant. Title companies with trust experience sometimes charge higher premium rates because of the added underwriting work. Run specific estimates — not ranges pulled from memory — for the specific deal before you sign a purchase agreement.

### Who Gets Paid

Depending on the pathway, the payee differs:

1. **Pathway 1 and Pathway 2** — funds go to the trust, which then distributes to beneficiaries per the trust instrument
2. **Pathway 3** — funds go to the trust under the newly appointed trustee, who distributes per the trust instrument or per the court's order
3. **Pathway 4** — funds flow per the terms of the modification or termination order
4. **Pathway 5** — funds go directly to the beneficiaries in exchange for the assignment of their beneficial interests

Who receives the funds affects tax treatment, the need for 1099 reporting, and the documentation required at closing. Coordinate with the CPA on how the funds flow should be structured for the specific deal.

### The Purchase Agreement

The purchase agreement contains the same core elements as in defunct-LLC work but adapted to the trust context:

1. Identification of the parties — the trust, the trustee in trustee capacity, the beneficiaries if they are selling beneficial interests
2. Description of what is being sold — real property in Pathways 1 through 4, beneficial interests in Pathway 5
3. Purchase price and allocation of costs
4. Conditions precedent — successor trustee appointment (if needed), court approval (if needed), beneficiary consents, clear title, trust instrument confirmation
5. Representations and warranties — trust authority, beneficiary identification, no undisclosed claims, chain of title
6. Indemnification provisions for residual risks
7. Escrow and holdback provisions for any amounts held after closing
8. Timing milestones and outside closing dates

The outside-closing-date provision is critical in trust deals. Court timelines are estimated, not guaranteed. Build in buffer time, and include an extension mechanism — at the acquirer's election, for a defined extension fee or without fee if the delay is caused by court scheduling — so a deal does not collapse because a court hearing got continued by thirty days.

## 5.6 Court Proceedings When Needed

Court involvement distinguishes trust work from LLC work. Many dormant-trust cases require a court order at some stage — to appoint a successor trustee, to approve a trust modification or termination, to construe ambiguous trust terms, or to resolve a dispute among trustees and beneficiaries. This section walks through what typically happens when a court proceeding is part of the acquisition.

### Where Trust Proceedings Are Filed

In Texas, trust proceedings are typically filed in the district court of the county where the trust is being administered (often the county where the trustee resides or where the trust property is located). Some trust matters — particularly those involving testamentary trusts arising from a decedent's will — are filed in probate court. Your attorney will identify the correct venue for the specific case.

The county matters because local court practice varies. Some district courts are experienced with trust proceedings and move them quickly. Others are not. An attorney with local court knowledge advises you on the likely timeline based on the specific judge and court administration in that county.

### Application for Successor Trustee Appointment

When the trust lacks a current trustee and no named successor is available, the typical proceeding is an application to appoint a successor trustee. The application identifies:

1. The trust and its essential terms
2. The circumstances under which the trust lacks a trustee
3. The beneficiaries and their identities
4. The proposed successor trustee and why that person is qualified
5. A request that the court appoint the proposed successor

Notice is given to the beneficiaries. If any beneficiary objects, the court may hold a hearing to resolve the objection. If no objection is raised, the court typically enters the order appointing the successor trustee. The newly appointed successor then has judicial authority to act for the trust going forward.

Who is proposed as the successor trustee matters. A beneficiary who is willing to serve is often the court's first choice — they already have an economic stake in the outcome and are motivated to move the administration forward. When no beneficiary is available or qualified, a professional trustee or an attorney can be appointed.

### Application to Modify or Terminate the Trust

When the trust's purpose has become impossible or impractical, or when other grounds for modification or termination exist, the trustee or a beneficiary files an application asking the court to modify or terminate the trust. The application identifies:

1. The trust and its essential terms
2. The grounds for modification or termination
3. The proposed modification or the proposed distribution on termination
4. The beneficiaries and the effect of the proposed action on them
5. The grantor's intent, to the extent ascertainable, and how the proposed action fits with that intent

Notice is given to all beneficiaries, to the grantor if living, and often to the Texas Attorney General if the trust has charitable purposes. The court considers the application and, if satisfied, enters an order authorizing the modification or termination. The order provides the authority for the subsequent sale and distribution.

Building a strong record on grantor intent matters. Courts are not supposed to rewrite a trust on a whim — they are supposed to carry out the grantor's intent in circumstances the grantor could not anticipate. A modification petition that articulates the original purpose, explains how circumstances have changed, and shows why the proposed modification best serves the original intent is far more likely to succeed than one that simply asks the court to fix a problem without explaining why the fix fits the purpose.

### Application to Construe Trust Terms

When the trust instrument is ambiguous or when there is genuine uncertainty about its meaning, the trustee or a beneficiary can ask the court to construe the trust — to declare what the terms mean. A construction order provides authority to act consistent with the court's interpretation. This is useful when the trust's provisions are unclear about key questions like the scope of the trustee's sale power, the identity of contingent beneficiaries, or the effect of changed circumstances.

Construction proceedings are often underused by operators who jump straight to modification or termination. In some cases, an ambiguous provision about sale authority can be resolved through construction in ninety days at a fraction of the cost of a full modification proceeding.

### Declaratory Judgment on Trust Matters

Under the Uniform Declaratory Judgments Act (Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code Chapter 38), a court can declare the rights, duties, and legal relations of parties interested in a trust. This is a broad tool that can resolve trust questions without requiring full-blown litigation. Common uses include confirming the identity of beneficiaries, confirming trustee authority, and resolving disputes among trust parties without contested trial proceedings.

### Ad Litem Attorneys

In some trust proceedings, the court appoints an attorney ad litem to represent parties who are not able to represent themselves — unknown beneficiaries, minors, beneficiaries under disability. The ad litem investigates, reports to the court, and often participates in negotiating the final terms. The same framework applies here as in the unknown-heir litigation covered in Chapter 33 of the main Codex. Cooperating with the ad litem produces better outcomes than treating them as an obstacle.

The practical approach is to provide the ad litem everything you would provide the court — the trust instrument, your diligence file, the proposed deal structure, the pricing rationale, the title work. Ad litems who receive a complete, transparent package move faster and are more likely to recommend approval. Ad litems who feel like they are pulling information out of reluctant parties dig deeper and take longer.

### Timeline of Court Proceedings

| Proceeding Type | Typical Timeline |
|---|---|
| Uncontested successor trustee appointment | 60 to 120 days |
| Uncontested trust modification or termination | 90 to 180 days |
| Contested proceedings | 6 to 24 months or longer |
| Appeals | Substantial additional time |

These are working estimates for Texas. Other states vary significantly. Build court timeline estimates into every purchase contract with enough outside-date buffer to survive an uncontested proceeding that runs long. If a proceeding goes contested, reassess the entire deal economics before committing further resources.

## 5.7 Title Work on Trust Transactions

Title insurance on trust-held property requires additional underwriting compared to typical real property transactions. The title company needs to be satisfied that the person executing the deed has the legal authority to do so and that no competing claims exist that would defeat the conveyance.

### What Title Companies Review

1. The trust instrument itself — sometimes a complete copy, sometimes a certification of specific provisions
2. Any amendments or restatements of the trust
3. Evidence of the trustee's authority — either the trust's express grant or a court order
4. For successor trustees, documentation of the succession (trust instrument provisions, written acceptance of duties, court order if applicable)
5. Beneficiary consents if the trust requires them for a sale
6. Any court orders providing authority for the specific transaction
7. Confirmation that the trust has not been revoked or terminated in a way that would affect trustee authority
8. Evidence that required notices have been given to interested parties

Bring the title company into the deal as early as the preliminary diligence phase. Their underwriter needs time to review the trust instrument and identify what additional documentation they require. A title company that says at the end of a six-month court proceeding that they need something different than what you produced has cost you months and legal fees. A title company that tells you exactly what they need on day one gives you a documentation target that drives the entire proceeding.

### Memorandum of Trust for Closing

Even when no memorandum of trust has been previously recorded, the title company often requires one to be prepared and recorded as part of closing. Under Tex. Prop. Code § 114.086, a memorandum can summarize the trust's essential terms and the trustee's authority without disclosing the full trust instrument. The memorandum provides the public record evidence the title company needs.

The memorandum of trust is a protective document not just for the title company but for future title searches. A deed executed by a trustee whose authority appears only in a private trust document creates a gap in the chain. A recorded memorandum closes that gap and protects against later challenges to the conveyance.

### Affidavits and Certifications

The trustee typically signs an affidavit or certification at closing confirming:

1. The trust is in existence and has not been revoked
2. The trustee is the current trustee and has authority
3. The trust instrument authorizes the sale
4. Any required beneficiary consents have been obtained
5. No other person has a claim to the property that would defeat the conveyance

The trustee's affidavit is a central closing document. The title company may provide a specific form for the trustee to execute. Review it carefully before the trustee signs — the representations in that affidavit bind the trustee, and anything in the affidavit that turns out to be inaccurate creates both title exposure and personal liability for the trustee. A trustee who signs an affidavit saying all beneficiaries have consented, when in fact one beneficiary's heir has not been located, has a problem.

### Court Orders as Title Support

When the acquisition has involved a court proceeding — for successor trustee appointment, trust modification, or trust construction — the court's final order is part of the title file. Title companies generally accept properly entered court orders as strong evidence of authority, reducing the need for other forms of proof.

The combination of a properly entered court order plus a trustee's affidavit plus a recorded memorandum of trust typically produces an insurable title package. If a title company will not insure that package, the question is whether the problem is the title company's inexperience with trust transactions or a genuine title defect. Work with your trust attorney to distinguish the two — and change title companies if the issue is inexperience.

### Working With the Right Title Company

As with defunct-LLC work, not all title companies are equipped or willing to insure trust transactions. Title companies with trust experience:

1. Know what documentation they need and ask for it systematically
2. Have underwriters comfortable with trust authority questions
3. Can commit to insurability early in the deal process
4. Coordinate effectively with trust attorneys
5. Do not withdraw late in the process

Developing relationships with two or three title companies that handle trust transactions is essential infrastructure for this work. Those relationships are built before the first deal closes — through referrals from trust attorneys, through introductions at local estate-planning bar events, and through honest conversations with title company commercial divisions about your deal types and volume.

## 5.8 Closing the Real Property Transaction

With all authority questions resolved, title work completed, and contingencies satisfied, the transaction closes. The mechanics vary by pathway.

### Closing Mechanics — Pathway 1 (Trustee-Led Sale)

1. The trustee executes the deed in trustee capacity — typically as "[Name], Trustee of [Trust Name]"
2. The memorandum of trust is recorded if not previously recorded
3. The trustee's affidavit of authority is recorded or retained with the title file
4. Purchase funds flow through the title company or escrow agent
5. Funds are disbursed — attorney and CPA fees, title insurance, recording, any liens being cleared, and the net to the trust
6. The trust then distributes to beneficiaries per the trust's terms and applicable tax requirements
7. The deed records; title insurance issues to the acquirer

### Closing Mechanics — Pathway 2 or 3 (Successor Trustee)

Similar to Pathway 1, but with additional documentation:

1. The successor trustee's authority is documented — through the trust instrument's succession provisions, written acceptance, and (if applicable) the court's order appointing the successor
2. The successor trustee executes the deed in trustee capacity
3. All other mechanics mirror Pathway 1

For Pathway 3, the court's appointment order is the centerpiece of the authority package. Get a certified copy of the order from the clerk, include it in the title file, and confirm with the title company that they have reviewed and accepted it before scheduling the closing date.

### Closing Mechanics — Pathway 4 (Modification or Termination)

1. The court's order authorizing the modification or termination is the central authority document
2. If the order directs sale by the trustee and distribution of proceeds, the closing mirrors Pathway 1 with the court order attached as authority
3. If the order directs distribution of the property to beneficiaries followed by sale by beneficiaries, the closing involves two conveyances — from the trust to the beneficiaries, and then from the beneficiaries to the acquirer
4. All orders, memoranda, and supporting documents are recorded or retained as the title company requires

The two-conveyance structure in Pathway 4 doubles some of the closing costs — two deeds, two sets of recording fees, two transfer tax events in states that have them. Model those additional costs into your underwriting before you choose the structure.

### Closing Mechanics — Pathway 5 (Beneficiary Interest Acquisition)

1. The beneficiaries execute assignments of their beneficial interests to the acquirer
2. The assignments are delivered in exchange for the purchase funds
3. The acquirer, as the new beneficiary, may then work with the trustee to direct a sale of the property out of the trust, or to terminate the trust with distribution to the new beneficiary (the acquirer), or to hold the property in the trust going forward
4. Subsequent property-level closing, if any, follows the applicable pathway above

Pathway 5 has two phases: the interest acquisition and the subsequent property action. The interest acquisition closes relatively quickly when all beneficiaries cooperate and no spendthrift provisions exist. The subsequent property action then follows — which may require a successor trustee appointment (Pathway 3) or a modification or termination proceeding (Pathway 4) before the property itself can be conveyed.

### The Closing Statement

The closing statement documents the flow of funds. For trust transactions, typical items include:

| Line Item | Notes |
|---|---|
| Gross purchase price | Total consideration to the trust or beneficiaries |
| Attorney fees (trust-side work) | Trust proceeding, contract negotiation, closing |
| Court costs and ad litem fees | If court proceedings were involved |
| CPA fees | Trust tax work and closing-year return |
| Title insurance premium and endorsements | Often higher than standard residential |
| Recording fees | Deed, memorandum of trust, supporting documents |
| Prorated property taxes | Credit to acquirer for accrued delinquency |
| Liens or encumbrances being paid off | Mortgages, judgment liens, HOA arrears |
| Net proceeds to trust or beneficiaries | After all deductions |
| Acquirer's total cash-to-close | Purchase price plus acquirer-paid costs |

Review the closing statement with your attorney and CPA before the closing date. Trust closings are more complex than standard residential closings, and discrepancies in the allocation of costs or proceeds can create tax or legal problems that surface later.

## 5.9 Post-Closing Cleanup

### Trust Administration Going Forward

1. If the trust continues to exist (Pathway 5 where the acquirer becomes the beneficiary), the trustee continues administration under the trust's terms
2. If the trust is terminating (Pathway 4 with termination, or final distribution following a sale), the trustee completes final administration — distribution of proceeds, filing of final tax returns, notice to any remaining creditors
3. Any residual trust obligations are resolved, and the trust ceases to exist

The trustee's final administration duties after a sale are real obligations, not formalities. Final distributions to beneficiaries that are made without settling trust-level obligations — tax liabilities, creditor claims, outstanding professional fees — can create personal liability for the trustee if the trust has insufficient assets to cover those obligations after distribution.

### Tax Reporting

1. The trust's final tax return is filed for the year of termination
2. Beneficiaries receive Schedule K-1 forms reporting their share of trust income and distributions
3. 1099 reporting may be required depending on how funds flowed
4. The acquirer's CPA handles any tax implications on the acquirer's side

The K-1 and 1099 obligations are not the acquirer's responsibility in most trust structures — those are the trustee's and the trust's obligations. But the acquirer should confirm those obligations are being handled, because a beneficiary who receives an unexpected tax bill with no K-1 to support it may come back to the deal looking for an explanation. Coordinate with the trustee's CPA to confirm the reporting is completed.

### File Retention

Retain the complete deal file indefinitely. Trust transactions can produce questions long after closing — a previously unknown beneficiary who surfaces, a dispute among heirs of deceased beneficiaries, a question about the validity of the court proceedings. At minimum the file should include:

1. All trust documents — original or certified copy of the trust instrument, amendments, restatements
2. Entity and people research — genealogical work, death records, heir searches
3. Correspondence with trustees and beneficiaries
4. Purchase agreement and amendments
5. All court filings and orders if court proceedings were involved
6. Notices given to interested parties
7. Title commitment, policy, endorsements
8. Closing documents — deed, memorandum of trust, trustee's affidavit, closing statement
9. Tax documents

"Indefinitely" means indefinitely. A beneficiary's unknown heir appearing a decade after closing and asserting a claim to the property is not a fantasy scenario — it has happened, and the deals where it happened with complete files resolved cleanly while the deals with incomplete files became expensive litigation.

### Property Transition

Parallel to the trust-side cleanup, the property itself transitions to the acquirer's operation — insurance, utilities, property tax address updates, lease review, and any planned rehab or management work. The property-transition checklist from Chapter 21 of the main Codex applies here. The trust mechanics do not change the operational requirements for the property once title is clear.

## 5.10 Realistic Timeline and Cost

Trust acquisitions typically run longer and more expensively than defunct-LLC acquisitions because of the likelihood of court involvement and the greater document complexity.

### Timeline

| Pathway | Estimated Timeline |
|---|---|
| Pathway 1 — cooperative living trustee with clear authority | 90 to 180 days |
| Pathway 2 — successor trustee under trust instrument | 120 to 210 days |
| Pathway 3 — court-appointed successor trustee | 6 to 10 months |
| Pathway 4 — court-approved modification or termination | 6 to 12 months |
| Pathway 5 — beneficiary interest acquisition only | 90 to 270 days plus follow-on action |
| Cases with deceased beneficiaries requiring heirship work | Add 90 to 180 days |
| Contested proceedings | Add 6 months to 2 years |

These ranges assume competent legal representation and a cooperative court. Build purchase contract outside-closing-date provisions from the upper end of the range, not the middle. The deals that go smoothly close before the deadline. The deals that hit turbulence need the buffer.

### Cost

| Scenario | Professional Fees and Closing Costs |
|---|---|
| Simple trustee-led sale | $5,000 to $20,000 |
| Court-appointed successor trustee | $12,000 to $40,000 |
| Trust modification or termination proceeding | $15,000 to $60,000 |
| Complex contested proceedings or extensive heirship work | $40,000 to $150,000+ |
| Ad litem fees (when appointed) | $2,000 to $10,000 typical |
| CPA fees for trust tax work | $2,000 to $10,000 |

These ranges are order-of-magnitude and highly fact-specific. Obtain precise estimates from your attorney and CPA before committing to the deal structure. The estimate-before-signing discipline is the single most important underwriting habit in this asset class — trust deals where the cleanup costs ran twice the estimate because the operator did not get upfront fee agreements are deals where the profit evaporated or flipped to a loss.

## 5.11 Exits at a Glance

Once the property is acquired, the same standard real estate exits available for any acquired property are available. Match the exit to the property, the market, and your capital position.

1. **Wholesaling** — assign the contract to an end buyer before closing, where feasible given the cleanup complexity
2. **Wholetailing** — close, minimal cleanup, quick retail resale
3. **Fix and flip** — rehabilitate and resell at a higher price
4. **Rehab and hold** — rehabilitate and keep as a rental
5. **Buy and hold as-is** — rent in current condition
6. **Owner financing the resale** — sell with seller financing
7. **Partition exit** — when the trust held a fractional interest, use partition to convert to cash
8. **Land-banking** — hold long-term for development or assemblage
9. **Refinance out** — after seasoning, refinance to recover acquisition capital

Trust-acquired properties are often older, more neglected, and in need of more rehab work than defunct-LLC properties, because the trust's inactive administration frequently coincides with property neglect. This can favor fix-and-flip and rehab-and-hold exits that are equipped to handle substantial rehab work. Underwrite to a specific exit and walk from deals that do not pencil at that exit's economics.

Wholesaling trust deals requires a sophisticated end buyer who understands that they are acquiring a property that recently cleared a trust-administration proceeding and that the title work will look different from a standard fee-simple purchase. Having a bench of such buyers in advance makes wholesaling trust deals feasible. Selling to a buyer who does not understand trust title chains creates re-trade risk if their title company has objections.

## 5.12 Key Takeaways

**Trust acquisitions are more varied than LLC acquisitions.** Every deal depends on the specific trust instrument, the current posture of trustees and beneficiaries, and whether court intervention is needed. No two look exactly alike.

**First contact educates without pressuring.** Trustees and beneficiaries often know only fragments of the situation. Explain clearly, respect their pace, invite independent legal review.

**Preliminary diligence before contracting.** Locate and analyze the trust instrument. Confirm status of all trustees and beneficiaries. Handle deceased beneficiary heirship questions. Review property and tax picture. Run court record searches.

**Five pathways cover most cases.** Trustee-led sale, successor trustee under the trust instrument, court-appointed successor trustee, trust modification or termination, and beneficiary interest acquisition. Combinations are common.

**Texas Trust Code is the Texas framework.** Tex. Prop. Code § 111.001 and following provisions. Every state has its own trust law; engage local counsel.

**Price backs out from exit.** Including specifically trust-cleanup costs, court costs, ad litem fees, heirship work, title insurance, and closing costs. Obtain fee estimates before signing.

**Court proceedings are more common than in LLC work.** Successor trustee appointments, trust modifications, trust terminations, trust construction, declaratory judgments. Budget time and cost accordingly.

**Title insurance requires careful documentation.** Trust instrument, authority evidence, memorandum of trust, trustee affidavit, court orders where applicable. Work with title companies that handle trust transactions and engage them on day one.

**Closing mechanics differ by pathway.** Trustee-signed deed, successor-trustee-signed deed, court-ordered conveyance, or beneficiary interest assignments followed by subsequent property action.

**Post-closing trust administration completes the work.** Final trust distribution, final tax returns, beneficiary K-1s, file retention indefinitely.

**Timeline runs 90 days to 12+ months.** Simpler cases at the low end; cases with court proceedings or heirship issues at the higher end. Build contracts from the upper estimate.

**Standard real estate exits apply.** Match to property characteristics. Trust-acquired properties often favor rehab-heavy exits due to deferred maintenance during the dormancy period.

**State variation is significant.** Trust law varies more than entity law in some respects. Local counsel in the target state every time.

**Coordinate specialized professionals.** Trust and estate attorney, real estate attorney, CPA familiar with trust taxation, probate attorney for heirship matters, and title companies with trust experience — all assembled before the first deal enters diligence.
