## 14.1 What Underwriting Actually Is

Underwriting is the process of converting a classified deal into a specific acquisition plan with specific numbers.

The matrix from Chapter 13 answers: Is this a deal? Underwriting answers: If I pursue this deal, what am I actually committing to? How much capital. How much time. How much risk. What's the expected return. What happens if things go worse than expected.

Most real estate investors underwrite poorly or not at all. They look at a property, think "there's money there," and dive in. Then they overpay heirs because they didn't calculate fractional shares properly. They run out of capital mid-deal because they didn't budget curative costs. They hold the property longer than planned because they didn't factor in underwriter timelines. Each of these mistakes eats deal profit.

Serious operators underwrite every deal before committing capital. They know, within a reasonable margin, what the deal will cost and what it will produce. They don't always hit their estimates perfectly — surprises happen — but they're close enough that most deals clear their targeted net.

This chapter teaches you the underwriting methodology. By the end, you should be able to look at any matrix-classified deal and produce a complete underwriting analysis in 30-60 minutes.

---

## 14.2 The Underwriting Output

Underwriting produces a deliverable — an underwriting report that summarizes the analysis. For simple deals, this is a half-page document. For complex deals, it might be 3-5 pages.

Every underwriting report contains:

1. **Deal identification** — property, decedent, matrix code
2. **Value analysis** — ARV, as-is value, equity spread
3. **Heir allocation** — each heir's fractional share with verification
4. **Payout structure** — what you'll offer each heir
5. **Cost breakdown** — all acquisition and holding costs
6. **Revenue projection** — resale proceeds net of costs
7. **Net profit estimate** — both target and downside scenarios
8. **Capital requirements** — cash needs throughout the deal
9. **Timeline estimate** — expected duration with key milestones
10. **Risk analysis** — what could go wrong and how much it costs
11. **Decision recommendation** — pursue / pursue with conditions / pass

This document is your business case. It's what justifies committing capital. It's what you refer back to when the deal is in progress and you're deciding whether to accept new demands or terms.

## 14.3 Value Analysis — What Is This Property Actually Worth

The first piece of underwriting is establishing value. Without accurate value estimates, every other number is wrong.

### ARV (After-Repair Value)

The amount the property would sell for on the retail market if it were in good condition. This is the end-buyer value — what a family buying a finished home would pay.

How to estimate:

- **MLS comparable sales** in the same neighborhood, 3-12 months old
- **Zillow estimates** (Zestimate) — useful as a secondary reference
- **Real estate agent opinions** — if you have an agent relationship, a CMA (comparable market analysis) is free or low-cost
- **Professional appraisals** — $500-$800, reliable for important deals
- **County appraisal district** — reflects tax-assessed value; often 70-90% of market value; useful as a floor

For underwriting purposes, use a conservative ARV. If comps support $340K, use $325K. Surprises in the real estate market usually run downward.

### As-Is Value

What the property would sell for in its current condition to an investor buyer (not a retail buyer). Usually 60-80% of ARV for properties needing significant work.

How to estimate:

- **Investor-buyer comps** — recent sales of comparable-condition investment properties
- **Discount from ARV** — typical 20-40% depending on condition
- **Direct investor inquiries** — what would local flippers or buy-and-hold investors pay today

### Equity Spread

ARV minus all known encumbrances (mortgages, tax liens, judgments) minus selling costs equals equity spread. This is the maximum theoretical value available to be captured by an operator who acquires all interests.

**Equity spread formula:**

> ARV − Mortgages − Tax liens − Other liens − Closing costs − Realtor commissions = Equity spread

This is NOT your profit. It's the pool from which heirs, attorneys, curative work, holding costs, and your profit all come.

## 14.4 Property Condition Assessment

ARV depends on property condition. You need to assess condition before trusting an ARV estimate.

### Condition Categories

| Category | Description | ARV Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Move-in ready** | Habitable, clean, updated systems, no major defects | Full retail comp value |
| **Cosmetic updates needed** | Functional but dated — paint, flooring, minor repairs | 5-15% haircut |
| **Significant rehab needed** | Major systems, kitchens, bathrooms | 20-40% haircut if sold as-is |
| **Gut rehab required** | Down-to-studs remodel | 40-60% haircut if sold as-is |
| **Teardown** | Land value only — structure adds nothing | Land value only |

### How to Assess Condition

- **Drive-by inspection:** Exterior condition, roof, landscaping, general impression
- **Interior inspection if possible:** Direct access by arrangement with heirs
- **Code enforcement records:** Documented violations indicate condition
- **Utility records:** Disconnected utilities suggest vacancy and possible damage
- **Neighbor information:** People who live nearby often know the property's history
- **Aerial imagery:** Google Earth, Zillow, county GIS for roof condition and yard state

### Assumptions When You Can't Inspect

Some deals proceed without interior access. Assume:

1. Working systems: 50/50 odds
2. Major damage from vacancy: 30% odds if vacant 2+ years, higher with longer vacancy
3. Missing fixtures (copper, HVAC, appliances): 40% odds if vacant, higher in certain neighborhoods
4. Animal damage or squatter damage: 15-25% odds with long vacancy

Budget conservatively when condition is unknown.

## 14.5 Heir Allocation — The Math

From Chapter 12, you have the calculated fractional shares for each heir. Now apply the economics.

### Heir Share Math

For each heir:

> **Heir's fractional share × Equity spread = Heir's theoretical entitlement**

Example: 8 heirs each entitled to 1/8 of equity spread. Equity spread = $200K. Each heir's theoretical entitlement = $25K.

But heirs don't get their full theoretical entitlement. Several factors reduce what they actually receive:

1. **Operator profit.** Obviously, you don't pay out 100% of the spread. Your profit comes from buying below theoretical entitlement.
2. **Cost absorption.** Some costs (attorney fees, curative work) are carved out of the spread before heir payouts. Heirs effectively bear proportionate share.
3. **Risk adjustment.** Heirs accept less than theoretical entitlement because the operator is absorbing all the execution risk. They're trading certainty for discount.
4. **Time preference.** Heirs take money now rather than wait potentially years for full entitlement through sale of the property. The discount is the price of immediacy.

### Typical Heir Payout Ratios

| Deal Type | Heir Payout Ratio |
|---|---|
| Clean simple deal | 50-65% of theoretical entitlement |
| Moderate complexity deal | 40-55% of theoretical entitlement |
| Complex deal | 30-45% of theoretical entitlement |
| Very complex deal (every-fracture type) | 20-35% of theoretical entitlement |

The more complex the deal, the lower the heir payout percentage because:

1. More capital and time at risk
2. Higher probability of execution failure
3. More work required per dollar of eventual profit
4. Greater need to offset costs from the spread

### Worked Example

$200K equity spread. 6 heirs each entitled to 1/6 = $33,333.

Target heir payout ratio in this deal: 45% (moderate complexity).

Each heir offered: $33,333 × 45% = $15,000.

Total heir payouts: $15,000 × 6 = $90,000.

Remaining $110,000 of spread covers:

1. Attorney fees: ~$8,000
2. Curative work: ~$5,000
3. Realtor commission: ~$15,000 (if ARV $250K and commission 6%)
4. Title and closing: ~$3,000
5. Holding costs: ~$6,000 (6 months of taxes, insurance, utilities)
6. Rehab if any: variable
7. Operator profit: residual

If rehab is $30,000, operator profit is $110,000 − $8,000 − $5,000 − $15,000 − $3,000 − $6,000 − $30,000 = **$43,000 net.**

This is E2 economics. Classify and decide accordingly.

## 14.6 Payout Structure — How You Actually Pay Heirs

Heirs don't all get paid the same way. The structure matters for both economics and relationship management.

### Payout Timing

- **At signing:** Small portion ($500-$2,000) to secure commitment
- **At recording of deed:** Majority of payout (70-80%)
- **At final close:** Remainder conditional on completion

### Why Stage Payments

1. Heirs have skin in the game to cooperate with later paperwork
2. Deal doesn't collapse if one heir takes money and disappears
3. Schedule C requirements can be addressed without renegotiating
4. Your capital isn't fully committed until title is clear

### When to Pay More Upfront

1. Hostile heirs who need to be bought out to prevent disruption
2. Heirs in financial distress where upfront payment is the key motivation
3. Heirs who have alternative offers from competitors
4. Deals with urgency (scheduled tax sale, etc.)

### Payout Forms

- **Cashier's check:** Most common, heir can deposit quickly
- **Wire transfer:** For larger amounts or distant heirs
- **Personal check:** Fine for small payouts, clears slower
- **Cash:** Rarely used due to documentation and legal concerns

### Documentation

Every payout is documented with:

1. Receipt signed by the heir
2. Recorded deed from the heir (for the substantial payment portions)
3. Release of interest language

Keep copies of everything. This is your audit trail.

## 14.7 Cost Breakdown — All the Money Leaving Your Pocket

Chapter 3 introduced the cost categories. Now apply them to underwriting with specific numbers.

### Acquisition Costs

1. Heir payouts (from Section 14.5 calculation)
2. Attorney fees for AOH or JDH: $1,000-$8,500 depending on complexity
3. Title company curative work fees: $500-$5,000
4. Recording fees and court filing fees: $500-$2,000
5. Notary and travel: $500-$3,000 depending on heir locations
6. Payoff of mortgages and liens: variable based on encumbrances

### Holding Costs (Per Month)

1. Property taxes (monthly accrual): varies by property value
2. Insurance: $50-$200/month depending on property
3. Utilities if maintained: $100-$400/month
4. HOA dues if applicable: $30-$500/month
5. Maintenance and lawn care: $50-$300/month
6. Security/monitoring: $0-$200/month

Multiply monthly holding by expected holding period.

### Rehab Costs (If Applicable)

| Rehab Level | Cost Range |
|---|---|
| Light rehab (paint, flooring, systems check) | $5,000-$20,000 |
| Moderate rehab (kitchens, bathrooms, roof) | $20,000-$60,000 |
| Heavy rehab (gut renovation) | $60,000-$150,000+ |

Get contractor estimates before finalizing budget.

### Selling Costs

1. Realtor commission: 5-6% of sale price
2. Title insurance (seller pays): ~0.5-1% of sale price in Texas
3. Closing costs (seller portion): 1-2% of sale price
4. Seller concessions (if typical in market): 1-3% of sale price

### Schedule C Surprise Budget

Build in 5-10% of spread as a contingency for unexpected requirements. The surprises always come.

### Financing Costs (If Using Leverage)

1. Private money interest: 10-14% annually, typically
2. Hard money interest: 10-14% annually
3. Points and fees: 2-4% of loan amount
4. Refinance costs if holding period extends

## 14.8 Timeline Estimate — How Long This Will Take

Different deals have different natural timelines. Estimating timeline affects capital carrying costs.

### Typical Timelines by Matrix Type

| Matrix Classification | Expected Timeline |
|---|---|
| Simple intestate (O1, H1-H2, clean) | 60-90 days from first contact to close |
| Multi-heir coordination (O2, H2-H3, clean) | 90-150 days |
| Contested estate (O2-O3, C2-C3) | 5-8 months |
| Multi-generational (O3-O4, H3-H4) | 6-12 months |
| Every-fracture (O4 T3 P2 Y4) | 9-18 months |

### Key Milestones in the Timeline

| Window | Activity |
|---|---|
| Week 1-4 | Research, analysis, first heir contacts |
| Week 4-8 | Negotiations with heirs, deals signed |
| Week 8-12 | Heirship documentation (AOH, JDH filing, etc.) |
| Week 12-20 | Title cleanup, schedule C requirements |
| Week 20-28 | Preparation for sale, listing, marketing |
| Week 28-36 | Retail sale, closing |

Longer timelines apply when complexity increases.

### What Drives Timeline Extension

1. Heirs slower to respond or sign than expected
2. Missing heirs requiring skip tracing
3. JDH proceedings taking 4-6+ months
4. Curative work requiring quiet title action
5. Underwriter requirements changing mid-deal
6. Contested claims from previously-unknown heirs

### Underwriting a Conservative Timeline

Take your base estimate and add 25-40%. If base is 6 months, underwrite 7.5-8.5 months. The extra time isn't always used, but when surprises happen, you're not surprised.

## 14.9 Capital Requirements — What You Actually Need in the Bank

Different stages of the deal require cash. Plan for each.

### Initial Capital (First 30-60 Days)

1. Initial heir good-faith deposits: $500-$2,000 × number of heirs
2. Attorney retainer: $2,000-$5,000
3. Title company initial work: $500-$2,000
4. Research costs (records, skip tracing, genealogy): $500-$2,000
5. Initial property securement if applicable: $0-$5,000

**Subtotal:** $3,500-$18,000+ in first 30-60 days before you even have deeds in hand.

### Acquisition Capital (Days 60-180)

1. Main heir payouts: 70-80% of total heir payout budget
2. Attorney fees for AOH/JDH: $2,000-$8,500
3. Continued property maintenance and taxes: variable
4. Curative work: variable

**Subtotal:** Substantial portion of heir payout budget, which for the sample deal above was $90,000. So $60,000-$75,000 in this phase.

### Completion Capital (Days 180-Closing)

1. Final heir payouts: 20-30% of heir payout budget
2. Payoff of mortgages and liens
3. Closing costs

**Subtotal:** $20,000-$30,000 for the sample deal, plus lien payoffs which vary significantly.

### Rehab Capital (If Applicable)

- Spent in bursts throughout the rehab period
- Usually $5,000-$30,000 per month during active construction

### Resale Capital (Held in Reserve)

- Holding costs until retail sale
- Seller concessions
- Commission paid at closing

## 14.10 Financing the Deal — When to Use Leverage

Most serious operators use some form of leverage to scale their deals. Understanding when and how is critical.

### Cash-Only Deals

1. Small deals with total capital need under your available cash
2. Deals where speed matters more than leverage efficiency
3. Situations where your own cash is cheaper than alternatives

### Private Money

1. Borrowed from individuals (doctors, dentists, retirees, family, business associates)
2. Interest rates: 8-14% typically
3. Flexible terms you negotiate
4. No formal underwriting process
5. Relationship-dependent

### Hard Money

1. Borrowed from commercial hard money lenders
2. Interest rates: 10-14%
3. Points: 2-4%
4. Terms: 6-12 months typically
5. Formal underwriting: ARV, rehab budget, operator experience
6. Faster closing than traditional lending

### Business Lines of Credit

1. Borrowed from banks against your business's credit
2. Interest rates: 7-12%
3. Flexible draw and repayment
4. Requires established business history

### Traditional Bank Loans

- Rarely work for this business (too complex, too short timelines)
- Exception: commercial bank loans for operators with substantial track records

### Leverage Math

Assume $200,000 total capital need on a deal with $50,000 projected net.

**Scenario 1: All cash.**

- Capital tied up: $200,000 for 8 months
- Opportunity cost if that capital could earn 10% elsewhere: $13,333
- Net profit after opportunity cost: **$36,667**

**Scenario 2: $50,000 cash + $150,000 at 12% for 8 months.**

- Interest cost: $150,000 × 12% × 8/12 = $12,000
- Points (2%): $3,000
- Total financing cost: $15,000
- Capital tied up (your money): $50,000 for 8 months
- Opportunity cost at 10%: $3,333
- Net profit: $50,000 − $15,000 − $3,333 = **$31,667**

Cash-only wins on this deal by a small margin. Leverage makes sense when you have multiple deals running simultaneously and your cash is constrained.

## 14.11 Downside Scenario — What Happens When Things Go Wrong

Every underwriting should include a downside analysis. What does the deal look like if things go worse than expected?

### Typical Downside Scenarios to Model

**Scenario D1: 20% cost overrun.** Heir payouts run higher than target (hostile heir demands more). Curative work runs longer than expected. Net drops by 20% of variable costs.

**Scenario D2: Extended timeline.** Holding costs extend 3-6 months longer than estimated. Capital carrying costs increase. Net drops by additional 5-15%.

**Scenario D3: Retail sale price below estimate.** Market softens or property doesn't show well. Sale price 10-15% below ARV estimate. Net drops significantly (sensitivity is high).

**Scenario D4: Title cleanup fails.** Quiet title required instead of AOH. Additional $5,000-$8,000 in legal fees. 3-5 additional months of timeline. Net drops by that amount plus extended holding costs.

**Scenario D5: Unknown heir surfaces.** Previously unidentified heir emerges during title review. Requires renegotiation or buyout. Additional $10,000-$30,000 in costs. Possible deal collapse if heir is hostile.

**Scenario D6: Catastrophic — heir dispute becomes litigation.** Contested claims lead to court proceedings. Legal fees $10,000-$40,000. Timeline extends 12-24 months. Deal may still close but at much-reduced profit.

### Underwriting to Downside

Calculate your net in Base Case and in Downside Scenarios D1-D3 (commonly occurring). If the deal still produces $30K+ net in the downside scenarios, it's a solid deal. If downside scenarios drop you below break-even, you're taking substantial risk.

### The "Walk-Away" Price

Calculate what you can pay that would produce acceptable profit in the downside case. This is your true maximum — don't go higher no matter how the negotiation flows.

## 14.12 The Underwriting Report — Putting It All Together

For every deal you pursue, produce a written underwriting report. For small deals, this is a single page. For complex deals, 3-5 pages.

### Sample Report Structure

**Page 1: Executive Summary** — Property address, decedent identification, matrix code, equity spread estimate, total acquisition cost estimate, expected net profit, timeline estimate, capital requirement, recommendation (pursue / pursue with conditions / pass)

**Page 2: Value Analysis** — ARV analysis with supporting comps, as-is value analysis, condition assessment, equity spread calculation

**Page 3: Heir Allocation** — Heir list with fractional shares, verification sources, payout structure, total heir payout budget

**Page 4: Financial Detail** — Acquisition cost breakdown, holding cost projection, rehab budget if applicable, selling cost projection, total cost summary, net profit calculation

**Page 5: Risk and Scenario Analysis** — Downside scenarios modeled, key risks and mitigations, walk-away price, timeline risks

### Why the Written Report Matters

1. Forces disciplined thinking
2. Documents assumptions for later review
3. Enables team discussion and approval
4. Provides reference during deal execution
5. Post-deal review captures variance for learning

## 14.13 Underwriting in Practice — The Sample Deal

Let me walk through a complete underwriting for a real sample deal.

### Deal Summary

**Property:** 234 Maple Street, a fictional address in a San Antonio suburb

**Decedent:** John Smith, died intestate 2018

**Matrix Code:** O2 Y2 / H2 C1 L1 OC:No / CH1 EN1 TI1 / $3 E3

### Value Analysis

1. ARV: $285,000 (based on 4 comparable sales 2-8 months old in neighborhood)
2. As-is: $210,000 (estimated)
3. Property condition: Cosmetic updates needed, working systems

### Encumbrances

1. Delinquent property taxes: $19,400
2. No other liens

### Equity Spread Calculation

| Line Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| ARV | $285,000 |
| Minus realtor commission (6%) | −$17,100 |
| Minus closing costs | −$3,000 |
| Minus delinquent taxes | −$19,400 |
| Minus title policy | −$1,500 |
| **= Equity Spread** | **$244,000** |

### Heir Allocation

6 heirs identified through research, each entitled to 1/6 = $40,667 theoretical entitlement.

Target heir payout ratio: 45% (moderate complexity deal, well-researched)

Per heir offer: $40,667 × 45% = $18,300

Total heir payouts: $18,300 × 6 = $109,800

### Cost Breakdown

| Cost Category | Amount |
|---|---|
| Heir payouts | $109,800 |
| Attorney fees (AOH) | $900 |
| Title company fees (curative) | $800 |
| Recording and court fees | $400 |
| Notary and travel | $1,500 |
| Minor rehab budget | $12,000 |
| Holding costs (4 months) | $3,200 |
| Schedule C surprise reserve | $3,000 |
| **Subtotal acquisition and prep** | **$131,600** |

Plus:

- Delinquent taxes at closing: $19,400
- Closing costs and commission: $20,100

**Total costs from spread: $171,100**

### Net Profit

> Equity Spread $244,000 − Total costs $171,100 = **$72,900 net**

(This compares to the initially-estimated E3 classification; actual net is at the low end of E3 but still clearly E3.)

### Capital Requirements

| Phase | Window | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| Good faith deposits + attorney retainer + research | Week 1-4 | $8,000 |
| First wave of heir payouts (70%) | Week 4-12 | $76,900 |
| Attorney fees, curative work, second wave of heir payouts | Week 12-20 | $38,000 |
| Final heir payouts + rehab start | Week 20-28 | $40,000 |
| Rehab completion + holding + pre-sale | Week 28-32 | $5,000 |
| Taxes, commission, closing costs | At closing | Paid from proceeds |

**Peak capital requirement: ~$160,000** (before resale proceeds come in)

### Timeline

| Phase | Window |
|---|---|
| Research and initial contacts | Week 1-4 |
| Heir negotiations, signed agreements | Week 4-10 |
| AOH preparation and recording | Week 10-14 |
| Title company review, Schedule C resolution | Week 14-18 |
| Rehab | Week 18-24 |
| Listing and sale | Week 24-28 |
| Closing | Week 28-32 |

**Total: 8 months from first contact to close.**

### Risk Analysis

**D1 (cost overrun):** If heir payouts run 15% higher than target, costs increase by $16,500. Net drops to $56,400. Still E2/E3 acceptable.

**D2 (timeline extension):** Additional 2 months adds $1,600 in holding. Net drops to $71,300. Acceptable.

**D3 (sale price 10% below ARV):** Sale at $256,500 instead of $285,000. Revenue drops by $28,500, commission also drops. Net drops to ~$47,000. Still acceptable.

**D5 (unknown heir surfaces):** Additional $15,000 in payout. Net drops to $57,900. Still acceptable.

Walk-away analysis: The deal produces acceptable profit in all common downside scenarios. Pursue.

### Decision: PURSUE

Action plan:

1. Begin heir contacts immediately
2. Target first signed agreement within 3 weeks
3. File AOH once 4 of 6 heirs committed
4. Schedule closing target for approximately month 8

This underwriting report took approximately 40 minutes to produce after initial research was complete. That 40-minute investment prevents expensive mistakes and provides the roadmap for executing the deal.

## 14.14 Common Underwriting Mistakes

New operators consistently make predictable underwriting mistakes. Watch for these:

### Mistake 1: Underestimating Heir Count

Deals initially classified H1 often become H2 or H3 after deeper research. If your base underwriting was based on 4 heirs and you actually have 8, you're either paying double the heir payouts or negotiating harder discounts than planned.

**Mitigation:** Underwrite to estimated upper bound of heir count, not best estimate.

### Mistake 2: Over-Optimistic ARV

Using the highest comparable sale rather than the median. Not accounting for property condition. Assuming a hot market will continue.

**Mitigation:** Use conservative ARV. Discount for any condition questions.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Capital Carrying Costs

If your cash is tied up for 8 months at an opportunity cost of 10%, that's a real cost that should appear in your underwriting.

**Mitigation:** Include opportunity cost in calculations when using cash. Include financing cost when using leverage.

### Mistake 4: Missing the Schedule C Budget

Expecting title cleanup to be free. When the title company identifies additional requirements, the operator scrambles for budget.

**Mitigation:** Always include 5-10% of spread as Schedule C reserve.

### Mistake 5: Underestimating Timeline

Assuming best-case timelines and not budgeting carrying costs for realistic timelines.

**Mitigation:** Base underwriting on realistic timelines, not best-case.

### Mistake 6: Ignoring Walk-Away Price

Entering negotiations without a predetermined maximum. Paying more than the deal supports because the negotiation got emotional or pressurized.

**Mitigation:** Calculate walk-away price before negotiating. Don't exceed it.

### Mistake 7: Not Underwriting the Downside

Only modeling the base case. Being surprised when things go worse than expected.

**Mitigation:** Model D1 through D5 for every deal. Know your numbers in all scenarios.

## 14.15 Portfolio-Level Underwriting

Beyond individual deals, experienced operators underwrite their whole portfolio.

### Questions to Ask at Portfolio Level

1. How much capital is currently tied up in deals?
2. What's my weighted average expected net across open deals?
3. How am I distributed across matrix types (simple vs. complex)?
4. What's my pipeline at each stage?
5. Am I taking appropriate risk across my deals?

### Portfolio Diversification

1. Don't have all deals in one county
2. Don't have all deals with same fracture type
3. Don't have all deals at same stage
4. Don't have all deals at same capital size

### Portfolio Capital Management

1. Keep reserves for Schedule C surprises across all deals
2. Maintain relationships with multiple capital sources
3. Track cash flow timing — when does each deal produce liquidity?
4. Plan capital deployment across the quarter

As your business grows from 1-2 deals per year to 10-20 per year, portfolio underwriting becomes as important as individual deal underwriting.

## 14.16 Key Takeaways for Operators

Before moving to Chapter 15, internalize these principles:

**Underwriting converts matrix classification into specific numbers.** Every deal needs both.

**The underwriting report is your business case.** Write it.

**Value analysis comes first.** Without accurate value estimates, everything else is wrong.

**Heir allocation is the math.** Theoretical entitlement × payout ratio = actual offer.

**Every cost category must be modeled.** Acquisition, holding, rehab, selling, financing, contingency.

**Timeline estimates affect capital carrying costs.** Be realistic.

**Capital requirements have phases.** Plan for each stage of the deal.

**Leverage has benefits and costs.** Model both sides when using financing.

**Downside scenarios are essential.** If the deal only works in base case, it's too risky.

**Walk-away price prevents overpaying.** Calculate it before negotiating.

**Common underwriting mistakes are avoidable.** Apply discipline.

**Portfolio-level thinking matters as you scale.** Individual deals are only part of the picture.
