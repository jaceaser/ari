## 15.1 The Multiplier That Changes Everything

I've talked throughout this Codex about the Package-Properties Doctrine — the principle that once you've found one fractured property owned by a family, there are often more. This chapter is where I teach you how to actually find them.

Here's the economic reality: a single property deal with $60,000 net profit is a good deal. A family package of four properties — all owned by the same decedent or estate, all fractured, all acquirable through the same research and heir relationships — is a $240,000 project with marginal additional effort.

The work to acquire properties 2, 3, and 4 is not four times the work of acquiring property 1. You've already done the heir research. You've already built the relationships. You've already got the attorney retained and the title company engaged. The marginal cost per additional property is dramatically lower than the cost of the first property. Which means the net profit per additional property is dramatically higher.

This is the single most underutilized technique in fractured equity investing. Most operators close one deal, cash their check, and move on to the next decedent. They leave three or four properties sitting on the table because they never looked. Package-Properties Research teaches you to look, and to look systematically.

By the end of this chapter, you'll know exactly how to search, what sources to use, how to prioritize which properties in a package to pursue first, and how to adjust your economics for multi-property packages.

## 15.2 Why Multi-Property Estates Are So Common

Before we talk about technique, understand why packages exist in the first place.

People who owned one real estate property often owned more than one. This is especially true for the demographic that produces fractured equity deals — older Texans, rural property owners, immigrant families, entrepreneurs, and people who accumulated modest wealth over their lifetimes.

Common patterns that produce multi-property estates:

**The primary residence plus rental property pattern.** A family lives in one house; they rented out a second one for income. When the owners died, both properties remained in the estate unprobated.

**The primary residence plus family land pattern.** An urban residence plus rural land inherited from parents. The rural land was never sold because nobody in the family actively used it but nobody wanted to let it go. When the owners died, both parcels entered the fractured pool.

**The multi-unit investor pattern.** Small-scale landlords who accumulated 3-8 properties over their working life. Sometimes these are all rental houses in the same neighborhood. Sometimes they're a mix of houses, small commercial buildings, and vacant lots.

**The developer or contractor pattern.** Someone in the real estate trades who ended up owning multiple properties — inventory they were going to develop, speculative holdings, work-in-progress. When they died, the portfolio froze.

**The estate-planning failure pattern.** A family with substantial real estate that never executed proper estate planning. Multiple properties, multiple family members, no wills, no administration.

**The multi-generational accumulation pattern.** Property owned across generations. Grandma owned one house. She died, and her son inherited. Son owned his own house plus Grandma's house. Son died. His two children inherited both houses. One of them died. Now three houses and more heirs than anyone can track.

In every one of these patterns, when you find one property, you're probably looking at a piece of a larger estate. Your job is to find the rest.

## 15.3 The Research Workflow — How to Actually Find Package Properties

Here's the systematic search methodology. Follow it for every promising deal.

### Step 1: Confirm the Decedent's Full Legal Identity

Before you search, lock down exactly who you're searching for:

1. Full legal name including middle name and any known variations
2. Maiden name if applicable
3. Approximate dates of birth and death
4. Social Security number if you have access (from death records)
5. Last known addresses throughout the decedent's life
6. Spouse's full legal name (if any)

You need this precision because real estate records are notoriously inconsistent with names. "John Smith" might appear in county records as "John A. Smith," "J.A. Smith," "John Smith Jr.," "Johnny Smith," or various other combinations. Your search has to catch all variants.

### Step 2: Search the County Appraisal District

Go to the county appraisal district (CAD) website for the county where your known property is located. Most Texas CADs offer owner-name searches.

Search strategies:

1. Exact full name match
2. Last name only with first-initial variations
3. Common name variations (Johnson vs. Johnston, etc.)
4. Spouse's name (property may be titled to the spouse)
5. Any prior maiden or married names

Every property returned belongs to someone with that name. Your job is to determine which ones are actually your decedent's.

### Step 3: Expand to Surrounding Counties

People own property in multiple counties more often than you'd think. Expand your search to:

1. Adjacent counties (easy to own property across a county line)
2. Counties where the decedent previously lived
3. Counties where the decedent had family roots
4. Counties mentioned in any will or estate document
5. Counties in the decedent's employment history (business properties)

Each additional county costs 10-20 minutes of search time. Cast a wide net.

### Step 4: County Clerk Grantor Index Searches

The CAD tells you current ownership. The County Clerk's grantor/grantee index tells you everything the decedent ever acquired or transferred. Search for the decedent as grantee (someone who received property).

This reveals properties the decedent bought over their lifetime. Some of those they may have sold before death. Others may still be in the estate.

### Step 5: Probate Court Searches

If the decedent had a probate case, the estate inventory filed in probate (if required) lists all real property. Pull the inventory for every probate case involving the decedent.

Even better, check probate cases for:

1. The decedent's spouse (who may have estate property)
2. The decedent's parents (inherited property may have been retained)
3. The decedent's predeceased siblings or children (property from their estates may have been inherited)

### Step 6: Texas Secretary of State Searches

If the decedent owned property through entities (LLCs, corporations, trusts), search the Secretary of State website for:

1. Entities registered in the decedent's name
2. Entities where the decedent was a registered agent, member, or officer
3. Dissolved entities that may still have had property in them when they dissolved

Defunct entities with real estate are the topic of Chapters 36-38. For package research, even identifying that an entity existed opens new research paths.

### Step 7: Genealogical Family-Pattern Searches

If the decedent came from a multi-generational family, check CAD records for:

1. The decedent's parents (whose property may have passed to the decedent)
2. The decedent's grandparents (especially for rural land)
3. Siblings who may have predeceased

Multi-generational family land is a particularly rich source of package opportunities. A 3-acre tract in a rural county may have been in the family for 80 years, with ownership now scattered across 15-25 descendants who all inherited fractional interests.

### Step 8: Cross-Reference with Other Research

If you've been doing heir research (Chapter 12), you've already been examining obituaries and genealogy. Re-read the obituary with property-search eyes:

- "Survived by his son John, who lives in the old family home on County Road 411..." — that's a different property to investigate
- "Preceded in death by his brother Robert, who farmed the property in Floresville..." — rural family property
- "The family asks that memorials be directed to the church in lieu of flowers..." — often reveals the town where family history is rooted

### Step 9: Tax Delinquency Cross-Check

If you found your primary deal through a tax delinquency list, run the decedent's name through the tax delinquency databases for other counties you're searching. If they're behind on taxes for one property, they're probably behind on others. This both confirms ownership and signals fracture.

## 15.4 Tools and Services That Accelerate Package Research

Some tools are particularly useful for this work.

**DataTree (by First American):** Integrated title data with owner-name search across counties. Subscription-based, $100-$300/month depending on access level. Shows property ownership, recording history, and sales data.

**PropertyRadar:** Real estate investor-focused database. Strong for owner-name searches and for building targeted lists. $100-$200/month.

**TitlePro247:** Title plant access with full document images. $60-$150/month. Particularly useful for recording-history research.

**LexisNexis Courtlink / Public Records:** Very comprehensive but expensive ($500+/month). Used by professional researchers for difficult cases.

**Ancestry.com:** The historical newspaper collection and family tree records can reveal multi-generational property patterns. $50/month for a subscription.

**Texas CAD websites:** Free, varies by county quality. Some counties have excellent cross-search tools; others have barely usable databases.

**Secretary of State's SOSDirect:** Texas entity search. $1 per search or subscription pricing. Used heavily for defunct entity research.

For most operators, a combination of DataTree (or PropertyRadar) plus direct CAD searches plus SOSDirect access will cover the majority of package research needs.

## 15.5 Verifying That Found Properties Are Actually the Decedent's

Finding properties with matching owner names is the easy part. Confirming they're actually your decedent's is the research step that matters.

### Red Flags That Suggest Matching Names but Different People

1. Very common name (Maria Garcia, John Smith) without distinguishing details
2. Property location inconsistent with decedent's known residences or family ties
3. Ownership dates that don't match the decedent's life (property acquired after death)
4. Different middle name or name variation that doesn't match known aliases

### Verification Techniques

**Cross-reference dates:** Match acquisition dates to the decedent's known life events.

**Check previous addresses:** Does this property's address appear in the decedent's history?

**Trace grantors:** Who sold the property to this "John Smith"? Does it match family or business patterns?

**Check co-grantees:** If acquired with a spouse or co-owner, does that match the decedent's spouse or associates?

**Tax roll details:** Sometimes CAD records include phone numbers, prior owner details, or property use that helps confirm identity.

**Obituary cross-check:** Does the obituary mention property in that location or county?

### When You Can't Definitively Confirm Identity

Proceed cautiously. In some cases, you can approach the research from the property side — research the current ownership history to determine whether it plausibly connects to your decedent's family. If confirmation is impossible, the property may be worth referring to an attorney for a formal title examination before you commit to pursuing it.

## 15.6 The Package Worksheet

For every deal with potential package properties, build a package worksheet. This is the document that summarizes what you've found and guides your acquisition strategy.

Standard package worksheet elements:

### Decedent Identification

1. Full name and known variations
2. Birth/death dates
3. Residence history
4. Spouse and primary family structure
5. Probate case status (if any)

### Property Table

For each identified property:

1. Address and legal description
2. County
3. Current owner as listed on CAD
4. Assessed value
5. Delinquent taxes (if any)
6. Known mortgages or liens
7. Estimated market value
8. Property condition (if known)
9. Confirmed or suspected (confidence level of identification)

### Heir Implications

1. Does this property have the same heirs as the primary deal?
2. Are there unique heirs for this property (e.g., specific bequests)?
3. Total heirs across all properties

### Economic Projections

1. Equity spread per property
2. Cumulative spread for the package
3. Estimated per-property acquisition cost
4. Cumulative acquisition budget
5. Projected net per property
6. Total projected package net

### Acquisition Sequencing

1. Which property to pursue first (usually highest spread or most time-sensitive)
2. Which to acquire together vs. separately
3. Timeline implications for each

## 15.7 The Economics of Packages

Package acquisitions have different economics than single-property deals. Understanding the differences is essential.

### Cost Advantages of Packages

**Shared attorney fees.** Once you've retained an attorney and begun a JDH or AOH process, additional properties in the same estate often require only incremental legal work. A JDH that determines the heirs of a decedent can cover multiple properties in the same filing. Marginal cost per additional property: $500-$1,500 instead of $3,500-$8,500.

**Shared title company engagement.** Once your title company is working the decedent's estate, they can examine multiple properties through the same engagement. Marginal cost per additional property often 30-50% less than a standalone property.

**Shared heir relationships.** Your conversations with heirs can cover all properties in the package. One round of negotiation, one set of signatures, one set of deeds (though one deed per property). Total heir-relationship effort for 4 properties is often only 30-50% more than for 1 property.

**Shared research.** Heir research is per-decedent, not per-property. Your Chapter 12 research covers all properties.

**Shared closing infrastructure.** Once you're set up for one closing, additional closings are incremental.

### Cost Differences from Single Deals

**Heir payouts scale with property count.** Each property has its own equity spread. Heirs get paid per property, not once for the whole estate. Your total heir payout budget multiplies by property count (adjusted for efficiency of package negotiation).

**Closing costs per property.** Title insurance premiums, recording fees, and closing costs scale with number of properties.

**Capital requirements scale.** You need capital for each property's acquisition, held until each property's resale.

**Timeline can extend or compress.** Simpler packages (all properties similar) can all close together. Complex packages may require serial closings.

### Per-Property Economic Analysis

Run Chapter 14's underwriting methodology for each property in the package. The per-property analysis tells you:

1. Which properties meet your economic floor
2. Which should be prioritized
3. Which should be excluded from the package (too marginal)
4. Which should get more aggressive negotiation

Not every property in a package is worth acquiring. Sometimes you'll find 4 properties and decide 3 are strong deals, 1 is marginal, and you pursue only the 3.

## 15.8 Heir Negotiation in Multi-Property Packages

When you're negotiating with heirs across a package, the structure changes.

### The Basic Package Offer

"Here's what I'm offering for your interest in all of the properties combined. I want to acquire your entire inherited interest in the estate, not one property at a time."

This structure is cleaner for heirs who want a single payment and a single decision. It's also cleaner for you because it prevents heirs from playing properties off against each other ("I'll sign the city property for $X but hold out on the rural land for more").

### Property-by-Property Structure

Sometimes it's better to negotiate each property separately. Situations where this makes sense:

1. Different heirs inherited different properties (e.g., will gave the farm to one child, the house to another)
2. Some properties are marginal and you're not committed to acquiring them
3. Heirs have different emotional attachments to different properties
4. Some properties have complexities that require separate handling

### The "All or Nothing" Negotiation

For some heirs, make it clear that you're acquiring the package or nothing. This prevents partial-package situations where you've acquired 60% of the estate but can't complete the rest. The alternative is that you walk away entirely.

### Mixed Approaches

Often the best approach combines structures. Offer a lump-sum package price but break it down per property. Allow for individual property negotiations on properties where heirs have special interest. Be flexible on structure while holding firm on total package economics.

### Negotiation Sequencing

Pick your lead property. Negotiate the most important property first — usually the highest-value, lowest-complexity property. Once that's committed to, the other properties often fall into place with heirs who want to complete the transaction efficiently.

## 15.9 The Pre-Signing Disclosure Obligation

When you're negotiating a package, you have an ethical and practical obligation to disclose to heirs what you know.

### What to Disclose

1. The full list of properties you've identified
2. Your basis for believing each property is part of the estate
3. Your estimated value for each property
4. Your total package offer with per-property breakdown

### Why You Disclose

Heirs can't make informed decisions about their own interests without knowing what they have. An operator who negotiates a favorable package by concealing information is vulnerable to later challenge — by disappointed heirs, by attorneys, by courts. A package acquired through concealment is less stable than a package acquired through disclosure.

### Exception — When Properties Are Speculative

If a property is only possibly part of the estate and you're still investigating, you can disclose it conditionally: "I'm researching whether this property in Uvalde County was also your grandfather's. If it turns out to be, we can include it in this offer, but I haven't confirmed it yet."

### Documenting Disclosure

Written communication with heirs should reference all identified properties. Your written offers should list them. This protects you in later disputes.

## 15.10 The Package Acquisition Timeline

Packages take longer than single deals but not proportionally longer.

### Typical Timeline for a 4-Property Package

1. **Weeks 1-6:** Comprehensive research — all properties identified, confirmed, valued. Heir research completed.
2. **Weeks 6-12:** Package offer negotiations with heirs. Signed agreements on all properties.
3. **Weeks 12-20:** Heirship documentation (AOH or JDH covering all properties).
4. **Weeks 20-28:** Title clearance on each property. Schedule C items resolved per property.
5. **Weeks 28-36:** Rehab (if needed) and sale preparation.
6. **Weeks 36-44:** Retail sales beginning. First property closes.
7. **Weeks 44-60:** Remaining property sales complete.

Total timeline: 9-15 months for a 4-property package versus 6-8 months for a typical single deal.

### Economic Impact of Extended Timeline

Capital is committed longer. Holding costs accumulate. But the net profit expansion usually more than offsets the timeline cost.

### Staging Sales

Don't necessarily sell all properties at once. Staged sales over 3-6 months produce better market results (each sale markets independently), spread realtor workload, and improve cash flow. First sale pays off acquisition capital. Second sale produces net profit. Third and fourth sales are pure profit.

## 15.11 Examples of Package Deals

Here are realistic examples of what packages look like in practice.

### Package Example 1: The Urban Residential Package

**Decedent:** Roberto Martinez, died intestate 2009 in Bexar County. Research revealed he owned:

1. Primary residence in San Antonio: ARV $195,000
2. Rental house in San Antonio (3 blocks away): ARV $145,000
3. Rental house in San Antonio (different neighborhood): ARV $170,000

**Total ARV:** $510,000. **Heirs:** 4 children, all adult, all cooperative. All properties tax-delinquent for 6-8 years.

**Package economics:**

| Line Item | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Combined equity spread | $380,000 |
| Heir payouts (50% ratio applied to $380K) | ($190,000) |
| Attorney/title/curative (shared across properties) | ($6,500) |
| Holding costs over 14 months | ($18,000) |
| Realtor commissions on 3 sales | ($30,000) |
| Minor rehab on all three | ($22,000) |
| **Operator net profit** | **$113,500** |

Versus single-property analysis: if the operator had only pursued the primary residence, net would have been ~$35,000. **Package multiplied net by 3.2x.**

### Package Example 2: The Rural Land Package

**Decedent:** Mary Thompson, died intestate 2015 in Kerr County. Research revealed ownership of:

1. Residential home in Fredericksburg: ARV $285,000
2. 42-acre rural tract inherited from her father in 1978: market value $310,000 (undeveloped)
3. 8-acre parcel adjacent to the 42-acre tract: market value $68,000

**Total ARV:** $663,000. **Heirs:** 2 adult children, estranged from each other. No outside children.

**Complications:** The 42-acre tract was in her family name for three generations. The 8-acre parcel had a boundary dispute with a neighbor that had to be resolved through survey and agreement before sale.

**Package economics after resolution of boundary dispute:**

| Line Item | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Combined spread | $495,000 |
| Heir payouts (40% ratio, adjusted down for complexity) | ($198,000) |
| Attorney fees (JDH plus boundary resolution) | ($12,000) |
| Title company fees | ($4,500) |
| Survey costs | ($3,500) |
| Holding costs over 11 months | ($9,000) |
| Realtor commissions | ($39,000) |
| **Operator net profit** | **$229,000** |

Single-property focus on the house alone would have produced ~$65,000 net. **Package multiplied net by 3.5x.**

### Package Example 3: The Multi-Generational Family Land

**Decedent sequence:** Grandfather died 1962. Grandmother died 1978. Three children — one died 1995, one died 2008, one still living. Properties identified:

1. 160-acre farm in Floresville: market value $850,000
2. Residential house on 2 acres: market value $210,000
3. Additional 38-acre tract: market value $220,000

**Total:** $1,280,000 in three properties. **Heirs:** 19 total across three generations, including 6 grandchildren of the earliest death, 4 great-grandchildren, and several hostile parties.

**Required:** JDH for each of the three deaths. Combined proceeding recommended.

**Package economics:**

| Line Item | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Combined spread | $990,000 |
| Extensive heir payouts (25% ratio given complexity, 19 heirs) | ($247,500) |
| Attorney fees (triple JDH, complex) | ($38,000) |
| Attorney ad litem fees | ($6,500) |
| Publication costs | ($1,200) |
| Title company curative work | ($15,000) |
| Holding costs over 22 months | ($28,000) |
| Realtor commissions | ($80,000) |
| **Operator net profit** | **$573,800** |

This is the career-defining deal category. Single-property focus on the highest-value property alone would have produced ~$180,000. **Package multiplied net by 3.2x.**

## 15.12 When Packages Are Not Worth Pursuing

Not every package makes sense. Some situations where you pursue only one property:

**When secondary properties have low equity spread.** If the primary property has $200K spread and the secondary properties have $20K spreads each, the secondary properties aren't worth the work. Focus resources on the primary.

**When secondary properties are in distant jurisdictions.** Property in a county where you don't have attorney relationships, title company access, or local knowledge has to justify the infrastructure investment. Sometimes the economics don't support expanding.

**When heirs for secondary properties are different or hostile.** If the secondary property has a different set of heirs (e.g., property inherited from a different family line), the relationship-building effort effectively doubles. Sometimes better to focus.

**When secondary property has unique complications.** A secondary property with severe title defects, tenant occupants with adverse possession claims, environmental issues, or similar problems may not be worth curing for the package value add.

**When timeline for secondary properties would delay primary sale.** If acquiring secondary properties would delay sale of the primary by months, the opportunity cost may not justify it. Close the primary, then circle back to secondaries separately.

## 15.13 Post-Primary-Close Package Continuation

Sometimes you don't identify all package properties until after the primary deal is already in progress. What do you do when a second property surfaces mid-deal?

### If Discovered During Acquisition

Add it to the ongoing negotiation if heirs are still willing. Execute a supplementary acquisition agreement. Track it through the same title process.

### If Discovered During Closing

Usually too late to add to the current closing. Close the primary, then pursue the newly-identified property as a follow-up transaction. Your existing heir relationships make the follow-up easier.

### If Discovered After Primary Closing

Pursue as a follow-up. You have:

1. Existing heir relationships
2. Existing documentation of the decedent's identity
3. Existing attorney/title company relationships
4. Credibility from having closed the primary deal

Follow-up acquisitions often close faster and more smoothly than cold-start acquisitions because trust has been established.

### The Ongoing Research Principle

Even after closing a deal, periodically search for additional properties owned by the same decedent. Sometimes older properties surface that weren't initially found — because they were in obscure counties, in old entity names, or in variations of the decedent's name that weren't initially searched.

## 15.14 Key Takeaways for Operators

Before moving to Part IV, internalize these principles:

**Multi-property estates are common.** If a decedent owned one property, they often owned more.

**Systematic search is required.** CADs, adjacent counties, grantor index, probate records, Secretary of State, genealogical patterns. Miss none of these.

**Verification is critical.** Finding properties with matching names doesn't confirm the match. Verify identity through cross-references.

**Package economics are better than single-property economics.** Shared infrastructure costs make marginal properties profitable. Total package net often 3x+ single-property net.

**Disclosure to heirs is required.** Show heirs all properties you've identified. Document your disclosure. This prevents later challenges.

**Acquisition sequencing matters.** Lead with the most valuable, least complex property. Use that momentum to close the rest.

**Not every package is worth complete pursuit.** Marginal secondary properties can be skipped. Focus resources on properties meeting the economic floor.

**Staged sales work better than simultaneous sales.** Market each property independently. Sales 3-6 months apart optimize market results and cash flow.

**Ongoing research pays off.** Even after closing, continue periodic searches for additional properties of past decedents you've worked with.

**Packages are the multiplier that transforms this business.** Operators who master package research produce 2-4x the annual net profit of operators who work single deals.
