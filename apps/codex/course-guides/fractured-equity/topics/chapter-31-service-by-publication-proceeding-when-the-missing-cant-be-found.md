## 31.1 When the Search Fails

Chapter 30 taught you how to find missing people. Most of the time, the Bread Crumbing Skip Tracing Method works — ninety-nine percent of targets surface eventually. But sometimes they don't. The cousin nobody's heard from since 1998. The half-brother whose name is known but whose current life is genuinely untraceable. The estranged grandchild whose mother refused to even provide a first name at the funeral.

When your search genuinely fails, the legal system provides a mechanism to proceed without them: service by publication. You publish notice of the legal action in a newspaper. The court appoints an attorney to represent the absent party's interests. The case moves forward. A judgment binds the missing party even though they never appeared.

This chapter teaches that procedure. The legal requirements under Texas Rules of Civil Procedure. The application you'll file. The affidavit of diligent search that comes from Chapter 30. The publication requirements. The attorney ad litem appointment. The cost and timeline. How to ensure your judgment actually sticks.

Service by publication isn't a shortcut. It's the last resort when genuine search has failed. Used correctly, it closes deals that would otherwise be stuck forever. Used sloppily, it produces judgments that get unwound later — creating title problems that haunt properties for decades. This chapter shows you how to do it correctly.

## 31.2 The Legal Foundation

Service by publication is governed by specific rules with specific requirements. Understanding the legal foundation matters because courts scrutinize these applications carefully.

### Texas Rules of Civil Procedure Rule 109

The primary rule governing service by publication in Texas civil cases. Key requirements:

1. Applicant must swear that defendant's residence is unknown AND that defendant is a transient person
2. OR applicant must swear that defendant is absent from state or is a non-resident
3. AND applicant must swear that due diligence has been used to locate defendant
4. The affidavit must state specifically what search efforts were made
5. Courts may require additional investigation before granting

### Texas Rules of Civil Procedure Rule 114

Governs the mechanics of publication once authorized: where published, how often, for how long, and what the citation must contain.

### Texas Rules of Civil Procedure Rule 116

Addresses service by publication for defendants whose names are unknown. Critical for unknown heir situations (covered more in Chapter 33).

### Texas Rules of Civil Procedure Rule 244

Requires appointment of attorney ad litem to represent defendants served by publication. This is crucial — the court appoints a lawyer to represent the missing party's interests. You'll pay this lawyer. The lawyer will review the case, sometimes object, and ultimately sign off on the judgment.

### Texas Estates Code Provisions

For probate-related proceedings (heirship determinations, estate administration), additional publication requirements apply:

- **Texas Estates Code § 51.053** — notice by publication in heirship proceedings
- **Texas Estates Code § 202.053** — specific requirements for judicial declaration of heirship
- **Texas Estates Code § 51.055** — unknown heirs of decedent

### Due Process Foundations

Beyond specific Texas rules, service by publication has constitutional due process implications. The U.S. Supreme Court has held that publication alone may be insufficient when the defendant's address is actually known or easily discoverable. The leading case is *Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank* (1950), which established that notice must be "reasonably calculated" to reach the defendant.

**Practical implication:** if you knew or could have easily found the defendant's address, publication won't save the judgment from attack. That's why Chapter 30 documentation matters so much — you must prove genuine diligence before publication is appropriate.

## 31.3 When Publication Is the Right Tool

Service by publication isn't the answer to every missing-heir problem.

### Appropriate Situations

1. You've genuinely exhausted the Chapter 30 bread crumbing methods
2. Documentation supports diligent search
3. The deal economics support publication costs and timeline
4. The absent party's interest is fractional enough that attorney ad litem fees won't destroy deal economics
5. The legal proceeding is one where publication is permitted (partition, quiet title, heirship determination, etc.)
6. You need a binding judgment against the absent party for title to be clear

### When Publication ISN'T the Answer

**You haven't really searched hard enough.** Courts deny publication applications when search was superficial. Complete the Chapter 30 methodology first.

**The absent party's address is actually known.** If you know where they are but they won't respond, that's different. You need personal service or other direct methods, not publication.

**Deal economics too small.** Publication costs $200–$800. Attorney ad litem adds $500–$2,500. Attorney time on the motion adds $1,500–$3,500. Total publication costs often exceed $2,000–$6,000. Deals with thin margins can't absorb this.

**Time pressure doesn't fit.** Publication takes 28+ days minimum, often 3–6 months total with ad litem process. Deals with faster deadlines need different approaches.

**Better alternatives exist.** Sometimes direct contact through known relatives works. Sometimes settlement with other heirs resolves things. Consider all options before publication.

## 31.4 The Application Process

Your attorney files the application for service by publication. Here's what it requires.

### The Motion or Application

Filed in the court where the underlying case is pending (partition case, heirship proceeding, quiet title, etc.). Contents include:

1. Identification of the case and parties
2. Identification of the specific party to be served by publication
3. Statement that party's residence is unknown, or that party is a non-resident absent from Texas
4. Statement that due diligence has been exercised to locate
5. Request for order authorizing publication
6. Proposed citation text
7. Designation of newspaper for publication

### The Affidavit of Diligent Search

This is the heart of the application. It's the sworn statement documenting your search efforts — directly drawing from the documentation you prepared in Chapter 30.

**What the Affidavit Must Cover**

1. Identity of the affiant (you or your agent)
2. Relationship to the case
3. Identity of the person sought
4. What is known about the person (last address, last employment, family connections)
5. Specific databases searched with dates
6. Social media searched with dates
7. Interviews conducted (names, dates, outcomes)
8. Specialized searches attempted (criminal, financial, institutional, international)
9. Any leads followed and their results
10. Conclusion that person cannot be located despite diligent effort

**Quality Matters**

Weak affidavits get denied. Strong affidavits get granted. A good affidavit reads like a detective's report — specific names, dates, sources, and dead ends. A weak affidavit reads like a form: *"I searched various databases and was unable to locate defendant."* Courts reject the weak kind.

Your Chapter 30 documentation feeds directly into this affidavit. The more thorough your search, the stronger your affidavit. This is why documentation-as-you-go matters.

### The Proposed Citation

The citation is the actual notice that will be published. It must contain:

1. Name of the court
2. Case number
3. Names of parties
4. Brief description of the nature of the suit
5. Property description if property is involved
6. Specific notice to the named defendant
7. Date by which defendant must appear or answer
8. Consequences of failure to appear

### Newspaper Designation

Publication must be in a newspaper. Which newspaper:

1. Generally a newspaper of general circulation in the county where case is pending
2. Sometimes additional publication in the county of the defendant's last known residence
3. Newspapers must be approved for legal notices
4. Your attorney typically works with specific newspapers regularly

## 31.5 The Court Review

Judges scrutinize these applications. Here's what they look at:

### Judge's Checklist

1. Does the affidavit show genuine effort, not boilerplate?
2. Are specific searches described with dates and results?
3. Did the searcher pursue obvious leads?
4. Is there evidence the defendant's address was actually unknown after real search?
5. Does the citation contain required elements?
6. Is the publication newspaper appropriate?

### Common Reasons Applications Get Denied

**Boilerplate affidavits.** Generic statements without specifics. Courts see hundreds of these and reject them.

**Inadequate search.** Applicant clearly didn't try hard enough. Obvious databases skipped.

**Address actually available.** Judge runs a quick search and finds the defendant themselves. Application dismissed, attorney embarrassed.

**Wrong procedure.** Publication sought when personal service would work, or when address is actually known.

**Improper citation.** Required elements missing from proposed publication.

### When Application Is Granted

1. Court enters order authorizing publication
2. Order specifies publication requirements
3. Order appoints attorney ad litem for absent party
4. Attorney begins coordination with newspaper
5. Clock starts on publication period

## 31.6 The Publication Requirements

Once authorized, publication must follow specific rules.

### Frequency and Duration

- Under Texas Rules of Civil Procedure, publication is typically once per week for 4 consecutive weeks
- Some proceedings require longer periods
- Calculated from first publication date
- Gives absent party time to learn of suit and respond

### Newspaper Qualifications

1. Must be a newspaper of general circulation
2. Must be published at regular intervals
3. Must be approved to carry legal notices
4. Generally must be published in the county where case is pending

### Proof of Publication

1. Newspaper provides affidavit of publication
2. Includes dates of publication
3. Includes tear sheet or copy of published notice
4. Filed with the court as proof service was completed

### Publication Costs

- Varies by newspaper and length of notice
- Typical range: **$200–$800** per publication run
- Paid to the newspaper directly
- Your attorney coordinates
- Verify current rates with specific newspapers

### After Publication Completes

1. Defendant has period to answer (typically until Monday following 42 days after service-complete date)
2. If defendant doesn't answer, attorney ad litem represents their interests
3. Case proceeds to disposition

## 31.7 The Attorney Ad Litem

Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 244 requires the court to appoint an attorney ad litem when service is by publication. Understanding this role matters greatly.

### Who the Attorney Ad Litem Is

The attorney ad litem is a licensed Texas attorney appointed by the court to represent the absent party's interests. They are **NOT your attorney**. They are adverse to you. Their job is to protect the missing person's rights in their absence.

### The Attorney Ad Litem's Duties

1. Review the pleadings to understand the case
2. Attempt to contact the absent party through any means available
3. Investigate the facts of the case
4. File answer on behalf of absent party
5. Appear at hearings to represent absent party's interests
6. Review proposed judgments for fairness
7. Object to anything that would unfairly prejudice absent party

### What Attorneys Ad Litem Actually Do

In most cases, the attorney ad litem:

1. Accepts the appointment and reviews the file
2. Attempts their own search for the absent party (sometimes finds them, sometimes doesn't)
3. Files a general denial on behalf of the absent party
4. Reviews proposed judgment
5. Signs off if terms appear reasonable
6. Objects if terms appear to unfairly prejudice absent party
7. Collects their fee from the party who requested publication (that's you)

### Attorney Ad Litem Costs

- Court-appointed, but **paid by the party requesting publication**
- Typical range: **$500–$2,500** depending on case complexity and attorney rates
- Can be higher in complex cases or high-value properties
- Paid at conclusion of case or as court orders
- Usually included in your budget

### Working Productively with Attorney Ad Litem

Don't treat the attorney ad litem as an adversary even though they technically represent the opposing position. Cooperation produces better outcomes:

1. Provide requested information promptly
2. Share your Chapter 30 search documentation
3. Explain the deal structure and why it's fair
4. Address concerns they raise about absent party's interests
5. Recognize their professional obligation — they have to do their job

A productive attorney ad litem relationship produces smooth case resolution. An adversarial one produces objections, delays, and potentially unwound judgments.

### When the Attorney Ad Litem Objects

Sometimes the ad litem raises concerns. Common issues:

- Purchase price appears below market value
- Attorney ad litem wants additional search attempts
- Proposed judgment terms seem unfair
- Procedural defects in application or publication

When this happens, you have options:

1. Address the concern directly (raise the purchase price, conduct more search)
2. Negotiate a modified judgment acceptable to ad litem
3. Litigate the dispute if necessary
4. In worst case, withdraw and attempt different approach

## 31.8 The Cost Budget

Service by publication involves multiple cost components. Budget carefully.

### Typical Publication Case Budget

| Item | Cost Range |
|---|---|
| Attorney fees for publication motion | $1,500–$3,500 |
| Publication costs (newspaper) | $200–$800 |
| Attorney ad litem fees | $500–$2,500 |
| Additional attorney time during case | $1,000–$3,000 |
| Court filing fees and costs | $100–$300 |
| **TOTAL** | **$3,300–$10,100** |

This is for a single party served by publication within an existing case. Multiple parties or complex cases cost more.

### When Costs Make Sense

1. Property ARV significantly exceeds acquisition plus legal costs
2. Absent party's interest is meaningful but you have other interests already
3. Other paths to acquisition have failed
4. Deal economics support the total legal spend

### When Costs Don't Justify

1. Small-value property where legal costs consume profit
2. Absent party's interest is tiny (1/32, 1/64) — attorney ad litem may refuse to sign off
3. Too many missing parties multiply costs

## 31.9 The Timeline

Service by publication takes time. Plan accordingly.

### Typical Timeline

| Phase | Time |
|---|---|
| Attorney prepares application and affidavit | Weeks 1–2 |
| Court reviews and grants | Weeks 2–4 |
| Publication runs (4 consecutive weeks typically) | Weeks 4–8 |
| Answer period expires | Weeks 8–14 |
| Attorney ad litem review and engagement | Weeks 14–20 |
| Case disposition, judgment entry | Weeks 20–24 |
| **Total: approximately 4–6 months** from initial application to final judgment | |

### What Can Extend Timeline

1. Contested applications (court denies initially, requires more search)
2. Complex cases with multiple missing parties
3. Attorney ad litem conducts their own search
4. Ad litem objections that require resolution
5. Appeals or motions to vacate later

## 31.10 Getting Notice to the Missing Party

Beyond required publication, diligent operators take additional steps to notify absent parties.

### Why Additional Notice Matters

1. Strengthens due process showing
2. Protects judgment against later challenge
3. Ethical — you're trying to give them actual notice, not just legal notice
4. Sometimes produces unexpected appearance that resolves case

### Additional Notice Methods

1. Certified letter to last known address (even if old)
2. Letters to family members known to potentially be in contact
3. Letters to former employers
4. Social media messages through verified accounts (even if unanswered)
5. Letters to attorneys who previously represented them
6. Notice to last known institution if institutionalized
7. Obituary-style notice in their last known city's newspaper

### Document Everything

Every additional notice attempt should be documented:

- Date, method, address, subject
- Returned mail preserved
- Social media messages screenshot-preserved
- Added to the overall search file

## 31.11 What the Judgment Accomplishes

Once your case concludes after proper publication, the resulting judgment binds the absent party.

### Binding Effect

1. Judgment binds the absent party as if they had been personally served
2. Title transfers or partition orders are enforceable
3. Quiet title decrees clear absent party's potential claims
4. Heirship declarations establish who inherited what

### Title Insurance Implications

1. Most title companies will insure based on proper publication judgments
2. Some require specific waiting periods before insuring
3. Complex cases may require additional underwriting
4. Work with title companies familiar with publication cases

### Potential Challenges to Judgment

The absent party can potentially challenge the judgment if:

1. Diligent search wasn't actually performed (judgment voidable)
2. Procedural defects in publication
3. Address was actually known and personal service was possible
4. Fraud in the application

Most challenges must be brought within specific time periods (typically 2 years). **Strong procedural compliance defeats most challenges.**

## 31.12 Special Situations

Several specific situations require different handling.

### When the Missing Person Appears During Case

Sometimes during the publication process or shortly after, the missing person surfaces. This happens more than operators expect — the attorney ad litem's search may find them, or family members alert them after seeing publication, or random circumstances bring them to light.

1. Stop the publication procedure
2. Move to personal service
3. Proceed with normal case management
4. Sometimes this produces voluntary settlement

### When the Missing Person Is Actually Dead

Your search should have found this (SSDI/DMF is Step 1 in Chapter 30). But if death is discovered during publication case:

1. Publication against deceased person is moot
2. Must identify deceased's heirs
3. Amend pleadings to name correct parties
4. May need separate heirship determination for the deceased

### Multiple Missing Parties

When multiple parties are missing, publication procedure applies to each:

1. Separate affidavits for each missing party
2. Each receives publication
3. Attorney ad litem may represent one or multiple
4. Costs multiply per missing party

### Service on Corporate Entities

Sometimes the missing party is actually a defunct corporate entity (relevant for defunct LLC work in Chapters 36–38). Different procedures apply:

1. Texas Business Organizations Code has specific provisions
2. Registered agent service if agent still exists
3. Publication if no registered agent
4. Additional procedures for forfeited entities

### International Parties

When the missing party is believed to be abroad:

1. Hague Convention procedures may apply (for parties in signatory countries)
2. Service through foreign authorities takes significant time
3. Publication often still required as backup
4. Attorney with international experience essential

## 31.13 Practical Example — A Publication Case

Here is a realistic case walked from application to judgment.

### Background

- **Property:** 3-bedroom home in San Antonio, ARV $185,000
- **Decedent:** Died 2010 intestate
- **Known heirs:** 5 children, 4 located and cooperating
- **Missing heir:** Son Miguel, last heard from 2002
- **Operator position:** Has acquired 4/5 interests, needs resolution for Miguel's 1/5

6-week search following Chapter 30 priority order:

|---|---|---|
| Step 1 | SSDI check | No death record |
| Step 2 | Family intelligence | Last known Dallas 2000, drug problems |
| Step 3 | Social media | No trace |
| Step 4 | Free records | Dallas arrest record 2005, TX property record 2007 (foreclosed) |
| Step 5 | Consumer databases | Multiple Miguels, age/location uncertain |
| Step 6 | Interviews | Sister had Dallas phone from 2006 (disconnected) |
| Step 7 | Criminal trail | Dallas County arrests stopped 2007 |
| Step 7 | Financial trail | No bankruptcy, no recent judgments |
| Step 7 | Employment trail | Former employer gone, no records |
| Step 8 | TLO professional search | No current information found |
| Step 9 | Institutional indicators | None |
| Step 10 | Not applicable | Not Latin American extended family |
| Step 11 | PI hired ($2,800) | No additional information found |

**Conclusion:** Diligent search fails. Proceeding to Step 12 — Service by Publication.

### Filing the Partition Case

1. Attorney files partition action in Bexar County District Court
2. All 5 heirs named as defendants (including Miguel as missing)
3. 4 cooperating heirs to be served personally (formality)
4. Miguel to be served by publication

### The Publication Application

1. Filed 30 days after main case filing
2. Affidavit of diligent search includes entire Chapter 30 documentation

### Publication Runs

- San Antonio Express-News designated
- First publication: Week 5

- Court appoints local attorney Elizabeth Martinez

### Case Resolution

- Partition case proceeds with 4 cooperating heirs and Martinez representing Miguel

5. Title insurance obtainable because procedure was clean

## 31.14 When Publication Fails

Sometimes publication-based cases don't work out.

### Application Denied

Court denies publication application:

1. Typically due to weak affidavit or inadequate search
2. Response: address concerns and refile, or conduct more search
3. Sometimes indicates need to reconsider whole approach

### Missing Person Surfaces

Usually positive but complicates case:

1. Must pivot to personal service
2. Publication efforts wasted (but documented)
3. Deal may restructure based on their appearance

### Attorney Ad Litem Major Objections

Ad litem concludes deal is unfair:

1. May require significant price increase
2. May require additional search before signing off
3. Case may not close on original terms
4. Sometimes deals become uneconomic

### Judgment Later Unwound

Sometimes a missing party surfaces years later and challenges judgment:

1. Must show procedural defect or inadequate search
2. Can cloud title or require additional settlement
3. Procedural compliance is your protection
4. Title insurance often covers these situations

### Court Scrutiny Increases

Some courts have become more demanding about publication applications due to abuses by other operators. Response:

1. More thorough affidavits required
2. Additional search steps may be requested
3. Ad litem appointments more rigorous
4. Judgments more carefully scrutinized
5. Affects timeline and cost projections

## 31.15 Ethical Dimensions

Service by publication has specific ethical considerations that operators must internalize.

### The Notice Reality

Let's be honest: publication rarely actually reaches the missing party. Four weeks in a newspaper they don't read? They probably never see it. The legal fiction is that publication provides notice; the practical reality is that most absent parties never know the case happened.

This isn't a reason to avoid publication when legitimately needed. It's a reason to:

1. Take additional notice efforts seriously (beyond just newspaper)
2. Ensure genuinely fair terms since they're not present to negotiate
3. Hold their share of proceeds properly
4. Facilitate their appearance if they ever surface
5. Document everything thoroughly

### Fair Value When Absent

The absent party can't negotiate. They can't accept or reject terms. They can't push for better prices. This creates an asymmetry that ethical operators address through:

1. Paying fair market value for their interest, not minimum extraction
2. Allowing attorney ad litem meaningful review
3. Accepting reasonable ad litem objections
4. Holding sale proceeds in a way that protects their right to claim later

### The Attorney Ad Litem Relationship

1. Don't pressure ad litem to rubber-stamp your deal
2. Respect their duty to represent absent party's interests
3. Provide them everything they need to do their job well
4. Accept that they may push back on terms
5. Recognize they're protecting you too — their approval makes judgment more defensible

### The Long-Term View

Publication-based acquisitions can come back to haunt operators who handled them poorly:

1. Missing parties who later surface and challenge
2. Title problems that emerge years later
3. Reputation damage in the community
4. Court scrutiny on future applications

**Clean procedure, fair dealing, and thorough documentation protect long-term interests.**

## 31.16 Coordinating with Other Tools

Publication often works in combination with other tools.

### With Partition Actions

Common combination. Partition files against all heirs; known heirs served personally; missing heirs served by publication. Case proceeds to resolution with ad litem representing missing heirs' interests.

### With Heirship Declaration

Judicial declaration of heirship with unknown or missing parties. Publication to reach unknown heirs. Ad litem represents unknown heirs. Final order establishes heirship definitively. (Chapter 33 covers this in detail.)

### With Quiet Title

Clearing title where ownership claims are unclear. Publication against parties with potential claims. Final quiet title decree clears property.

### With Probate Administration

Estate administration involving missing heirs. Publication as part of probate notice. Administrator proceeds to complete estate despite absent heirs.

### The Multi-Step Sequence

1. Chapter 30: Exhaustive search fails
2. Chapter 31: Publication authorized and completed
3. Chapter 33: Heirship or quiet title resolves underlying question
4. Chapter 34: Deal structured to account for any residual risk

Together these tools handle even the most complex missing-heir scenarios.

## 31.17 Key Takeaways for Operators

- **Service by publication is Step 12 — the last resort.** Only after Chapter 30 bread crumbing genuinely fails.
- **Legal foundation is Texas Rules of Civil Procedure** Rules 109, 114, 116, 244, plus probate-specific rules in Estates Code.
- **Due process limits apply.** You can't use publication if defendant's address is actually known or easily findable.
- **The affidavit of diligent search** draws directly from Chapter 30 documentation. Quality documentation produces strong applications.
- **Courts scrutinize applications carefully.** Boilerplate gets denied. Specifics with dates and sources get granted.
- **Publication is 4 consecutive weeks** typically. Newspaper of general circulation in county where case pending.
- **Attorney ad litem is required** under TRCP 244. Court-appointed lawyer represents absent party's interests. You pay.
- **Work productively with ad litem.** Provide information, respect their duty, address concerns. Adversarial relationships produce worse outcomes.
- **Cost budget: $3,300–$10,100 typical** for single-party publication within existing case.
- **Timeline: 4–6 months** from application to final judgment typical.
- **Additional notice efforts beyond publication** strengthen procedure and improve ethical posture.
- **Judgment binds absent party** as if personally served, but can be challenged if procedure defective.
- **Special situations require adjustment:** missing person surfaces, deceased parties, multiple missing, corporate entities, international parties.
- **Fair value when absent** is ethical imperative. The absent can't negotiate.
- **Coordinates with partition, heirship, quiet title, probate.** Rarely stands alone.
