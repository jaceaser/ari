## 24.1 The Quiet Tool

Of all the leverage tools in Part V, contribution suits are the quietest. They don't force sales. They don't trigger sheriff's auctions. They don't appear in probate court. They're civil lawsuits for money — straightforward, procedural, often boring.

But they're also remarkably powerful in specific situations.

**Contribution suits create judgments.** Judgments become liens. Liens attach to property interests. Property interests you want to acquire. That chain — from money owed, to judgment rendered, to lien created, to acquisition possible — is the heart of the contribution strategy.

**Contribution suits establish leverage without drama.** A partition action is a declaration of war. A contribution suit is a business accounting. The counterparty takes them differently. Settlements are often easier.

**Contribution suits recover actual money.** You paid taxes on a property your co-owner should have partially paid. You fix a roof that protected both your interests. You pay insurance that covers both. Legally, you're entitled to recover their share. Contribution suits make that happen.

**Contribution suits open doors.** Once a contribution judgment exists, multiple paths open. Collection. Lien attachment. Coordination with partition. Pressure for voluntary settlement. One tool, multiple uses.

This chapter teaches you contribution suits comprehensively. The legal foundation. When they apply. How to document the claim. The procedural mechanics. How to use contribution judgments strategically. How they combine with other tools.

Not every operator will use contribution. But operators who don't understand the tool miss leverage opportunities that would be productive.

## 24.2 The Legal Foundation

Contribution claims arise when one co-owner pays more than their proportional share of expenses that benefit all co-owners. The legal basis rests on three pillars.

### Common Law Equitable Principle

Co-tenants (joint owners) owe each other certain duties related to shared property. When one co-tenant discharges a debt or obligation that benefits all, the others must contribute their proportional share. This principle dates to English common law and is firmly established in Texas jurisprudence.

### Statutory Recognition

Various Texas statutes recognize and codify aspects of contribution:

1. Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code Chapter 32 — general contribution principles
2. Texas Property Code references in partition and co-tenant provisions
3. Case law developed over decades establishing specific applications

### The Basic Rule

A co-tenant who pays more than their proportional share of the following is entitled to contribution from the other co-tenants for their proportional share:

1. Necessary taxes and assessments
2. Necessary insurance
3. Necessary repairs (with limitations)
4. Encumbrances affecting the shared property
5. Necessary expenses for preservation of the property

### What's "Necessary"

The key word is **"necessary."** Luxuries, improvements, or optional expenditures may not be recoverable. Contribution is for expenses that preserved the property or discharged obligations that would have affected all owners.

**Examples of typically recoverable expenses:**
- Property taxes (always necessary)
- Mortgage payments on pre-existing liens
- Repairs preventing further damage (leaks, structural)
- Insurance premiums
- Emergency repairs (HVAC failure, plumbing burst)
- Code compliance expenditures

**Examples of typically NOT recoverable (without agreement):**
- Cosmetic improvements
- Upgrades beyond necessary repair
- Additions or renovations
- Improvements benefiting only one owner

### Proportional Share

Each co-owner's contribution obligation is proportional to their ownership interest. If you own 25% and pay $10,000 in taxes, the other owners collectively owe you $7,500 (75% of $10,000). Each owner owes according to their share.

## 24.3 When Contribution Applies

Several common fact patterns trigger contribution claims.

**Pattern 1: Taxes Paid on Shared Property**

You own a 1/4 interest along with three other co-owners. Property taxes come due. Other owners don't pay. To prevent tax sale, you pay the full amount.

Your claim: 3/4 of what you paid, from the other three owners (1/4 each).

**Pattern 2: Insurance on Shared Property**

Same property structure. Insurance is needed to protect everyone's interest. Other owners don't contribute. You pay the premium.

Your claim: 3/4 of the premium from the other owners.

**Pattern 3: Necessary Repairs**

Roof is leaking. If not repaired, property will be damaged further, harming everyone's interest. Other owners won't contribute. You pay for the repair.

Your claim: 3/4 of the repair cost from the other owners.

**Pattern 4: Discharge of Lien**

A lien exists against the property that threatens foreclosure. If foreclosure happens, all owners lose their interest. Other owners won't pay. You pay off the lien.

Your claim: 3/4 of the payoff amount from the other owners (plus potential interest).

**Pattern 5: Mortgage Payments**

Monthly mortgage payments on a pre-existing loan. Other owners don't contribute. You continue paying to prevent foreclosure.

Your claim: Each payment contributes to the recovery amount; accumulates over time.

**Pattern 6: Recurring Expenses**

HOA dues, utilities (if you're the only user paying but all benefit from preservation), ongoing maintenance. Other owners don't contribute.

Your claim: Ongoing accumulation of amounts paid.

## 24.4 The Strategic Value Beyond Money

A contribution suit is rarely just about recovering money. The strategic value is often the judgment itself and what it enables.

**Strategic Benefit 1: Creates Judgment**

Once you win a contribution suit, you have a judgment. In Texas, judgments become liens against any real property owned by the debtor in the county where the judgment is recorded (with proper abstracting procedures per Texas Property Code § 52.001 et seq.).

This means: The co-owner who didn't contribute now has a judgment lien against their property interest — including their interest in the very property you share.

**Strategic Benefit 2: Creates Collection Rights**

Judgments enable collection procedures: wage garnishment (with limitations in Texas), bank account levy, property execution, and more. These collection tools apply pressure on the debtor.

**Strategic Benefit 3: Creates Basis for Other Actions**

A judgment supports additional tools:
- Creditor-suit-to-sheriff's-sale (Chapter 26) needs a judgment creditor; you can become that creditor
- Partition with accounting often works more efficiently with a judgment already in place
- Settlement negotiations benefit from your established legal claim

**Strategic Benefit 4: Forces Counterparty Attention**

An heir ignoring calls about selling will respond to a lawsuit. Service of process gets attention. Court dates get attention. Attorney involvement gets attention. Contribution suits force engagement even when voluntary attempts fail.

**Strategic Benefit 5: Establishes the Narrative**

When you reach trial or settlement, you're not just an investor trying to acquire property — you're someone who's been carrying financial burden that others should have shared. Courts and juries view this favorably.

## 24.5 Documenting the Claim

Before filing, document everything thoroughly.

### Financial Records Needed — For Each Expense Paid

1. Receipt or invoice showing amount and purpose
2. Proof of payment (check, wire confirmation, credit card statement)
3. Date paid
4. Description of what the payment was for
5. Connection to the property (address, parcel ID, account number)

### For Recurring Expenses

1. Complete records across all payments
2. Pattern of payments over time
3. Failure of others to contribute (not always documented easily)

### For Necessity

1. Evidence that the expense was necessary to preserve or protect property
2. Expert opinions if helpful (structural engineer, code compliance officer)
3. Consequences if not paid (tax sale, foreclosure, further damage)

### Demands for Contribution

1. Written notifications to other owners that expense was coming or paid
2. Requests for contribution
3. Their responses or non-responses
4. Documentation of continued non-payment

### Example of Good Demand Letter

> "Dear [Co-Owner],
>
> I am writing regarding [property address]. As you know, we are co-owners of this property. Property taxes of $[amount] are due on [date]. Based on your proportional interest of [percentage], your contribution is $[amount].
>
> Please remit payment to [me/tax authority] by [date]. If I do not receive your contribution, I may be forced to pay the full amount to prevent tax sale, at which point I will expect reimbursement of your proportional share.
>
> Please contact me at [number] if you have questions or need to discuss payment arrangements.
>
> Sincerely, [Your name]"

This documents: notification of obligation, specific amount requested, deadline for response, consequence of non-action, offer to discuss. Save all responses (or evidence of non-response).

### Ownership Records

1. Deed showing your interest and others' interests
2. Affidavit of Heirship if applicable
3. Any estate/probate documents relevant to ownership
4. Title work establishing the ownership percentages

## 24.6 The Demand Letter Requirement

Before filing, consider the formal demand letter. Some jurisdictions require it; in Texas, it's not strictly required for contribution but is strongly advised.

**Creates clear record:** A formal demand letter creates a dated record of your claim, the amounts owed, and notice to the debtor. This strengthens your case if litigation becomes necessary.

**Triggers counterparty response:** Formal letters often get responses that informal communications don't. Sometimes the response includes payment, proposed settlement, or at least engagement.

**Opportunity to resolve without litigation:** Many claims settle after demand letters. The counterparty realizes the seriousness and either pays or negotiates.

**Required by some attorneys/courts:** Some attorneys require demand letters before filing. Some courts look favorably on pre-litigation demands as evidence of good faith efforts to resolve.

### What to Include

1. Formal identification of parties
2. Legal basis for claim
3. Specific amounts owed
4. Supporting documentation references
5. Deadline for response
6. Consequences of non-response

### Attorney-Drafted vs. Self-Drafted

If you plan to litigate, have your attorney draft the demand letter. It establishes the legal framework and preserves options. If you're trying to resolve informally first, a well-drafted personal letter may suffice.

### Timeline

Typically give 30 days for response. Shorter periods seem aggressive; longer periods suggest lack of seriousness.

## 24.7 Filing the Contribution Suit

When demand letters and negotiation fail, filing proceeds.

### Jurisdiction

State court in the county where:
1. Property is located (for claims related to the shared property)
2. Debtor resides (alternative venue)
3. Contract was formed (for agreed obligations)

Most contribution cases go in the county where the property is located.

### Court Level

**Small Claims Court:** For amounts under $20,000 (check current limits). Simpler procedure. Often proceed without attorneys. Faster resolution.

**County Court:** Mid-range amounts. More formal procedure. Attorney representation typical. Fuller discovery process.

**District Court:** Larger amounts. Complex cases. Full litigation procedure. Appeals available.

Choosing the right court level depends on size of claim and complexity. For simple contribution claims under $20,000, small claims may be efficient. For larger claims or those involving multiple parties, higher courts with more formal procedure make sense.

### Necessary Parties

Who must be named in the lawsuit:

**Primary defendants:** Co-owners from whom contribution is sought.

Other potentially interested parties:
- Spouses of co-owners (for community property considerations)
- Other parties with claims against the property
- Lienholders whose rights might be affected
- Any administrator or executor if estate is involved

### The Petition/Complaint

Your initial filing includes:
1. Identification of parties
2. Statement of facts (ownership, expenses paid, non-contribution)
3. Legal theories (common law contribution, specific statutes)
4. Specific amounts sought
5. Request for judgment
6. Request for costs and (possibly) attorney fees

### Service of Process

Each defendant must be properly served. Service can be:
1. Personal service by constable or process server
2. Service by certified mail (limited applicability)
3. Service by publication (for missing defendants)

Improper service voids the case. Use qualified process servers.

## 24.8 Defending Against Common Counterarguments

Defendants often raise specific arguments against contribution claims. Know each one before you file.

**Defense 1: "The Expense Wasn't Necessary"**

Defendant claims the expense was optional, not necessary. You improved when you didn't need to.

Your response: Document necessity. Show consequences of non-payment. Provide expert testimony if helpful. Distinguish between repair and improvement.

**Defense 2: "I Didn't Agree to This Expense"**

Defendant says they never agreed to incur the expense, didn't know about it, and shouldn't owe.

Your response: Necessary expenses don't require pre-approval. Show prior demand letters or notifications. Document attempts at communication. Legal duty of co-tenants to contribute regardless of specific agreement for necessary expenses.

**Defense 3: "You Benefited More Than I Did"**

Defendant argues you used or controlled the property, so they shouldn't have to pay for what benefited you.

Your response: Contribution is based on ownership interest, not use. If you had use, that's a separate accounting (partition includes this). Document that expenses protected all owners' interests equally.

**Defense 4: "I Don't Have the Money"**

Defendant claims inability to pay.

Your response: Inability to pay isn't a defense to liability. Judgment establishes debt regardless of ability to pay. Collection follows separately. May lead to lien attachment on property interest.

**Defense 5: "The Statute of Limitations Has Run"**

Defendant argues your claim is too old. Texas statute of limitations for most contribution claims is 4 years from the date of payment.

Your response: Document when payments were made. Each payment starts its own limitations period. Continuing breach arguments for ongoing expenses. Tolling arguments if applicable.

**Defense 6: "I Was Never Really a Co-Owner"**

Defendant contests ownership interest itself.

Your response: Produce deed, AOH, probate documents. Establish heirship with evidence. May need to add quiet title claim. Your attorney addresses legal theories.

## 24.9 The Accounting Component

Contribution cases often involve accounting — a formal financial reconciliation between co-owners.

### What Accounting Reveals

1. All payments made by each co-owner
2. All benefits received by each co-owner (rents collected, property use)
3. Net position of each co-owner
4. Final amount owed

### When Accounting Matters

**Simple contribution:** Just recovery of expenses paid. Straightforward calculation.

**Complex cotenancy:** Multiple years of operations, rents collected, expenses paid, improvements made, losses suffered. Full accounting needed.

**Rents and profits:** If one co-owner has been collecting rents without sharing, that's part of the accounting. Credit for expenses they paid, but owe the others their share of rents.

**Ouster situations:** If one co-owner excluded others from use, there may be fair rental value claims. Document exclusion and value.

**Improvements:** Generally not recoverable without agreement, but if improvement benefited all, some recovery may be possible. Complex analysis.

**Partition with accounting:** Partition actions (Chapter 25) usually include accounting. Contribution claim may be part of partition case rather than separate suit.

## 24.10 Interest and Costs

Your recovery may include more than the principal amount.

**Pre-Judgment Interest**

Texas allows pre-judgment interest on contribution claims. Typically at statutory rate (6% simple per year for non-contract claims, higher for contracts). Calculated from date of each payment to date of judgment.

**Post-Judgment Interest**

Once judgment is entered, interest accrues at post-judgment rate until paid. Currently around 5% per year (check current statutes).

**Court Costs**

Filing fees, service fees, deposition costs, and other court costs are typically recoverable by the prevailing party.

**Attorney Fees**

Generally not recoverable in contribution cases unless:
1. Specific statute allows it
2. Contract between parties provides
3. Bad faith by defendant

Texas follows the "American Rule" — each party pays their own attorney fees unless exception applies.

## 24.11 Settling Contribution Claims

Most contribution cases settle. Settlement structures vary.

**Straight Payment Settlement:** Defendant pays agreed amount, case dismissed. Simplest structure.

**Payment Plan:** Defendant pays over time. Agreed payments with timeline. Judgment may remain pending full payment.

**Property Transfer:** Defendant transfers their interest in shared property as settlement. Becomes the acquisition path.

This is often the strategic goal. You went into contribution knowing the property interest was more valuable than the cash recovery. Settlement by property transfer gives you what you really wanted.

**Offset Against Other Obligations:** If there are mutual obligations (you owed them, they owed you), settlement offsets.

**Combination Settlement:** Partial payment plus partial property transfer. Payment plan plus additional terms.

### Settlement Agreement Terms

1. Mutual release of claims
2. Specific consideration
3. Timeline for performance
4. Default provisions
5. Confidentiality (often desired)
6. Property transfer mechanics (if applicable)

## 24.12 Using the Judgment Strategically

Once you have a judgment, strategic options expand well beyond simple collection.

**Option 1: Collect the Money**

Traditional collection. Garnishment, levy, execution on non-exempt assets. Texas has significant homestead and personal property exemptions that limit collection against individuals.

**Option 2: Record as Lien**

Abstract the judgment per Texas Property Code § 52.001 et seq. Judgment becomes a lien against defendant's real property in the county where recorded.

Judgment lien attaches to:
- Defendant's interest in the shared property
- Any other real property defendant owns in the county
- Future real property acquired during the life of the lien

**Option 3: Leverage for Settlement**

Active judgment creates pressure for settlement. Defendant may now offer property transfer or other resolution to eliminate the judgment.

**Option 4: Coordinate with Partition**

If partition action is planned or pending, the contribution judgment integrates. Your interest in the partition proceeds includes the judgment amount due from defendant.

**Option 5: Set Up Creditor-Suit Sequence**

With a judgment, you're now a judgment creditor. This opens the creditor-suit-to-sheriff's-sale pipeline (Chapter 26) if appropriate.

**Option 6: Hold the Judgment**

Judgments are typically good for 10 years in Texas, renewable. Sometimes holding the judgment is the best strategy — defendant may need to deal with it eventually (home sale, refinance, etc.).

## 24.13 The Multi-Party Contribution Scenario

When multiple co-owners haven't contributed, the case structure becomes more complex.

### Scenario: 5 Co-Owners, 4 Haven't Contributed

You own 20%. Four other co-owners each own 20%. You've paid $50,000 in qualifying expenses.

Your claim: $40,000 (80% of $50,000) total, $10,000 from each non-contributing co-owner.

**Naming All Parties:** Include all four non-contributors as defendants. Each has separate liability for their share.

**Service and Procedure:** Each defendant served separately. Each may appear separately or jointly. Each may have different defenses.

**Default Options:** If some defendants default (don't respond), judgment can enter against them for their share. Other defendants continue in the case.

**Settlement Dynamics:** Some defendants may want to settle; others may want to fight. Your attorney negotiates each separately.

**Proportional vs. Joint Liability:** Each defendant is typically liable for their proportional share, not joint and several liability. If one defendant can't pay, their share isn't automatically shifted to others.

Multi-party cases are more complex but not disproportionately so. The economies often favor addressing all non-contributors in one proceeding.

## 24.14 Practical Example — A Complete Contribution Case

Let me walk through a complete contribution case from facts to judgment.

### The Situation

Your aunt died in 2018 leaving a 4-bedroom home in San Antonio. You and your three siblings inherit as 1/4 tenants each. Property is rental; tenant has been paying $1,400/month since 2015.

Your oldest sister has been collecting the rent and handling operations. She's deposited rents in her personal account. When you've asked for accounting and your share of rents, she's evaded the question.

You've been paying property taxes ($4,200/year), insurance ($1,200/year), and occasional repairs ($1,500 in 2022, $2,800 in 2023). Total over 3 years: $16,800 in direct expenses, plus your 3/4 share of uncollected rents.

### The Math

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Taxes/insurance/repairs paid (3 years) | $16,800 |
| Your 25% share of that | $4,200 |
| Contribution owed by other 3 siblings | $12,600 ($4,200 each) |
| Rents collected by sister (3 yrs × $1,400 × 12 mo) | $50,400 |
| Your share of rents (25%) | $12,600 |
| Each other sibling's rent share (25%) | $12,600 each |

### Initial Contact

You call your sister about the accounting and your share. She blows you off. You write a formal letter requesting accounting and contribution. She ignores it. You write a final demand letter giving 30 days to respond. Nothing.

### Attorney Consultation

You consult an attorney. They confirm:
1. Clear case for contribution on expenses paid
2. Clear case for rents collected (accounting)
3. Statute of limitations protects most claims (within 4 years)
4. Multi-party case against all three siblings
5. Potential judgment for $12,600 in contribution + $12,600 in rents = $25,200 (from sister who collected rents)
6. Plus $4,200 from each of two other siblings for their unpaid contribution

### Filing the Case

Your attorney files in Bexar County District Court:
1. All three siblings as defendants
2. Claims for contribution and accounting
3. Specific amounts claimed
4. Request for judgment, interest, costs

Filing fee: $400. Attorney retainer: $3,500.

### Service and Responses

All three siblings are served. Responses:
- **Sister who collected rents:** Hires attorney, files answer disputing amounts
- **Brother 1:** Defaults (ignores the lawsuit)
- **Brother 2:** Hires attorney, files answer contesting some expenses

### Discovery Phase (Months 2–6)

Your attorney conducts:
1. Document discovery (bank records, tax records, property records)
2. Depositions of siblings
3. Expert accounting review

Sister's bank records reveal full rent collection pattern. Brother 1's default continues.

### Settlement Discussions (Month 7)

Brother 2's attorney proposes settlement: pay $4,000 to resolve his part. You accept; claim against him dismissed.

Sister's attorney proposes comprehensive settlement: pay $20,000, release all claims. After negotiation, you agree to $22,500. Her payment resolves her part.

Brother 1 remains in default.

### Default Judgment (Month 8)

Court enters default judgment against Brother 1 for $4,200 plus interest and costs.

### Final Results

| Party | Resolution | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| Sister | Settlement | $22,500 |
| Brother 2 | Settlement | $4,000 |
| Brother 1 | Default judgment | $4,200 (collection pending) |

### Strategic Outcome

Total recovered: $26,500 in cash, plus $4,200 judgment.

But more importantly, you now have: cleared up the rent accounting, established fair compensation, and a judgment against Brother 1 that could support acquisition of his 25% interest. When you later approach Brother 1 about selling his interest, you have leverage: his judgment or his interest. Most likely outcome — he sells his 25% interest to satisfy the judgment, likely at a discount.

### Time and Cost

- Total timeline: 8 months
- Total legal costs: $7,500
- Net recovery plus judgment: $34,000 equivalent
- Net cash from contribution case alone: $26,500

## 24.15 When Not to Use Contribution

Contribution isn't always the right tool.

**The amounts are small.** If you've paid $500 in expenses the others should share, a contribution suit makes no sense. The legal costs exceed the recovery.

**Defendants are judgment-proof.** Defendants with no assets, on social security, bankrupt. A judgment against them is just paper.

**Relationships matter more than recovery.** Family relationships sometimes worth more than the legal recovery. Sometimes absorbing the loss preserves relationships that have other value.

**Better tools exist.** If partition action addresses the same issues more comprehensively, use partition instead. Contribution and partition can coordinate, but sometimes one tool is sufficient.

**Claims are weak or time-barred.** Statute of limitations has run. Documentation is poor. Necessity is questionable. Don't file weak cases.

**You haven't actually paid.** Contribution requires you to have paid. If you haven't paid yet, you can't seek contribution (though you might have other claims).

## 24.16 Interaction with Other Tools

Contribution often coordinates with other leverage tools.

**Contribution + Partition**

Partition actions typically include accounting. Contribution claim becomes part of partition case rather than separate lawsuit. Sometimes cleaner to combine both in one action than to file separately.

**Contribution + Creditor-Suit**

Judgment from contribution sets up creditor-suit (Chapter 26). Your judgment makes you a judgment creditor with specific rights to initiate that sequence.

**Contribution + Tax Sale**

If property goes to tax sale because co-owners didn't contribute, you may have both contribution and ownership claims. Complex but possible. Document payment history regardless of outcome.

**Contribution + Quiet Title**

Sometimes quiet title is needed to establish ownership interests before contribution can proceed. Parallel or sequenced actions depending on how contested the ownership question is.

**Contribution + Voluntary Acquisition**

Contribution judgment creates settlement pressure. Counterparty may agree to sell interest at discount to resolve judgment. Achieves acquisition through what started as a money claim.

## 24.17 Key Takeaways for Operators

**Contribution is the quiet leverage tool.** Less dramatic than partition or creditor-suit, but often more efficient.

**Legal foundation is co-tenant duties.** Common law plus statutory recognition in Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code.

**Pattern applies when one co-owner pays more than their share of necessary expenses.** Taxes, insurance, necessary repairs, lien discharge, mortgage payments.

**"Necessary" is the key word.** Luxuries, improvements, optional expenditures typically not recoverable.

**Proportional liability** — each co-owner owes based on their ownership percentage.

**Strategic value exceeds the money.** Judgment creates lien, opens collection, supports other actions, forces engagement.

**Documentation is critical.** Financial records, necessity evidence, demand letters, communication records. No paper trail means a weak case.

**Demand letters are strongly advised.** Create record, trigger response, opportunity for resolution.

**Jurisdiction and court level depend on amounts.** Small claims through district court. Match the court to the claim size.

**Multi-party cases** are common and manageable. Each defendant separately liable for their share.

**Common defenses** include necessity challenges, no-agreement arguments, statute of limitations, ownership disputes. Prepare for each before you file.

**Interest, costs, sometimes fees** add to recovery. Pre-judgment interest runs from each payment date.

**Most cases settle.** Often through payment, sometimes through property transfer — which was the real goal all along.

**Judgments create strategic options.** Collection, lien, leverage, coordination with other tools. The judgment itself is the prize, not just the cash it commands.

**Don't use contribution when** amounts are small, defendants judgment-proof, relationships matter more, better tools exist, claims weak.

**Contribution coordinates with other tools** — partition, creditor-suit, quiet title, voluntary acquisition.
