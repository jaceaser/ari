A quiet title action is the nuclear option of title curative — and sometimes the only option. It's a lawsuit filed in district court that asks the court to declare the plaintiff the rightful owner of a specific property, extinguishing all competing claims.

When you file a quiet title action, you're not just cleaning up paperwork. You're asking the legal system to formally adjudicate who owns the property. Every potential adverse claimant — every heir, every judgment creditor, every person who might have a claim — must be named in the lawsuit and given the opportunity to come forward and assert their claim. Anyone who doesn't respond after proper notice loses their claim against you.

## When It's Required

Some title defects can be resolved with documents. A missing deed can be replaced with a correction deed. An outdated lien can be released with an affidavit. But some problems can't be fixed without a court order:

- **Unknown heirs** who couldn't be personally served on an affidavit of heirship
- **Adverse possession claims** where someone has been using the property under a color of title claim
- **Gaps in the chain** more than 20-30 years old that can't be bridged with available documents
- **Tax sale title** where the pre-sale chain is too messy for a title company to insure

In any of these situations, a quiet title action is the path to clean, insurable title.

## The Process

The quiet title petition names everyone who might have a claim: known heirs, unknown heirs (served by publication), holders of recorded encumbrances, adjacent landowners with potential overlap claims. The court issues notice to all named parties. Each has a window to respond.

If no one responds, the court enters a default judgment declaring your title. If someone does respond and asserts a competing claim, the case turns into litigation — which is why thorough due diligence before filing matters. You want to know about competing claims before they show up in an answer.

Once the judgment is entered, you record it in the county property records. From that point forward, the judgment is your title evidence. Title companies will insure based on it. Lenders will lend against it. Buyers will accept it.
