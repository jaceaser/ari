## Why the First 30 Days Matter

The day title transfers, everything changes. You're no longer a speculator hoping to acquire a property. You're the owner. That means you're liable for the property, responsible for securing it, accountable for code violations, on the hook for taxes and utilities, and racing the clock on your holding costs.

Operators who excel at the first 30 days have these outcomes:

1. Property secured within 48 hours
2. Occupants handled within 14 days
3. Utilities transferred within 7 days
4. Initial assessment complete within 10 days
5. Clear plan for next phase by day 20
6. Property fully prepared for next action by day 30

Operators who neglect the first 30 days have these outcomes:

1. Property broken into, stripped, or vandalized
2. Occupants become entrenched and harder to remove
3. Code violations and lien letters arrive
4. Utilities shut off creating mold, freezing damage, or worse
5. Discovery of surprises that weren't visible before closing
6. Holding costs compounding without progress

This chapter is the operational playbook for the first 30 days. Not the philosophy of property management — the hands-on procedures that turn a fractured-equity acquisition into a property you can actually do something with.

## Day One — The Immediate Priorities

The day closing completes, before you do anything else, three things happen.

### Priority 1: Confirm Ownership Legally

1. Receive confirmation that deed is recorded
2. Obtain stamped copy of recorded deed
3. Confirm title policy is issued (usually within a few days)
4. Note recording information in your deal file

You can't really do the rest until you own it legally. Everything that follows depends on this.

### Priority 2: Secure the Property Physically

1. Change the locks immediately (or have it scheduled for the first available time)
2. Board up any broken windows or entry points
3. Post "No Trespassing" signs
4. Take photos of property condition at time of acquisition
5. Document any occupants present

A property that sits unsecured for even a few days can develop problems: squatters moving in, theft of fixtures, damage from weather, or unauthorized access.

### Priority 3: Establish Operator Presence

1. Place a note or contact sheet at the property if occupants are present
2. Put your name on the mailbox if applicable
3. Establish a communication channel with any occupants
4. Document your presence with dated photos

This matters legally. You want evidence that you've taken possession.

## Dealing With Occupants

Many fractured equity properties have occupants. Not all occupants are the same. Your response depends on who they are.

### Category 1: Heirs Living in the Property

An heir or family member has been living in the property, often since the decedent's death. They may or may not have signed a release. This is the most common occupant situation.

**If the occupant signed a release:** They've already agreed to sell their interest. Whether they'll leave voluntarily depends on how the deal was structured. Some deals allow them to stay for a negotiated period; others require them to vacate at closing.

**If the occupant didn't sign:** They still may have heir rights. If you acquired only partial interest (say, 5 of 6 heirs), the non-signing heir may have legal right to continue occupying.

### Category 2: Non-Heir Family Members

A girlfriend, boyfriend, adult child of the decedent's partner, or other family member who isn't legally an heir but has been living there.

Their legal rights are usually weaker than heirs', but they may have equitable claims (contribution to maintenance, promises from decedent, etc.).

### Category 3: Tenants

Someone was renting from the decedent or one of the heirs. Rental relationships vary widely — written leases, verbal agreements, month-to-month arrangements.

Legal status depends on the lease terms and state landlord-tenant law.

### Category 4: Squatters

Someone occupying without any legal right. Moved in after the decedent's death without permission.

Removable through various legal processes, but specific procedures must be followed.

### Category 5: Friends or Family of Squatters/Occupants

Additional people who arrived after the primary occupant. May or may not have legal status of their own.

## The Heir-Occupant Playbook

When an heir is living in the property, managing their removal requires care.

### Scenario A: Heir Signed Release and Agreed to Vacate at Closing

Most straightforward. Heir should have moved out by closing date. If they haven't:

1. Document their continued presence
2. Remind them of agreement (in writing, keep copies)
3. Allow a brief grace period (7-14 days) if reasonable
4. If they still don't leave, proceed with eviction

### Scenario B: Heir Signed Release but Was Given Time to Vacate

Many deals include a leaseback or occupancy agreement. Heir remains in property for a defined period (30-90 days typically) to transition.

1. Confirm terms of agreement
2. Honor the agreement's deadline
3. Document any terms violations
4. Plan for their departure timing

### Scenario C: Heir Didn't Sign Release and Still Has Interest

You've acquired partial interest only. Non-signing heir may have legal right to occupy their interest's equivalent. This requires Chapter 25 (Partition) or Chapter 26 (Sheriff's Sale Pipeline) strategies, not eviction.

### Scenario D: Heir Signed Release but Now Refuses to Leave

They signed, took the money, but are squatting in the property they sold you. Frustrating but addressable.

1. Eviction action for breach of occupancy terms
2. Documentation of signed release and consideration received
3. Attorney handles per state landlord-tenant law

### The Heir-Occupant Conversation

Before any legal action, try direct conversation:

"I know this is hard. You grew up in this house. I want to make this as smooth as possible. We agreed you'd be out by [date]. Can we talk about what you need to make that happen?"

Sometimes heirs need practical help — moving assistance, storage for family items, a little more time. Providing that can save months of legal process.

### What Not to Do

1. Don't use threats or intimidation
2. Don't change locks while they're inside (illegal in most states)
3. Don't shut off utilities to force them out
4. Don't harass them with repeated unannounced visits
5. Don't escalate to physical confrontation

These tactics create legal liability for you and rarely work.

## Eviction Procedures for Texas Properties

When conversation fails, eviction is the legal remedy.

### Texas Eviction Basics

Texas eviction is called a **"forcible detainer"** action, governed by Chapter 24 of the Texas Property Code and Rules 510-510.13 of the Texas Rules of Civil Procedure.

### Step 1: Notice to Vacate

Texas Property Code § 24.005 requires written notice before eviction. For tenants under lease, the notice period is typically 3 days (shorter if lease specifies). For former heir-occupants, your attorney may recommend longer notice.

Notice must:

1. Be in writing
2. Identify the property
3. State reason for vacancy demand
4. Specify deadline to vacate
5. Be delivered per statute (personal delivery, leaving at premises, or certified mail)

### Step 2: Filing Eviction Petition

If occupant doesn't leave after notice, file eviction petition in Justice of the Peace court for the precinct where property is located.

Petition includes:

1. Property description
2. Relationship to occupant (purchaser of property, not landlord)
3. Demand for possession
4. Request for hearing

Filing fee is typically $100-$200.

### Step 3: Service of Petition

Constable or private process server delivers petition to occupant. Occupant gets time to respond (typically 6-10 days).

### Step 4: Eviction Hearing

Justice of the Peace holds hearing. Both sides present case. Judge rules on possession.

If you win, judge issues writ of possession. Occupant has 5 days to vacate voluntarily.

### Step 5: Constable's Removal

If occupant still doesn't leave, constable executes writ of possession. Removes occupant and their belongings. You take possession.

Total timeline: 3-6 weeks from notice to removal, assuming no complications.

### Complications

1. Occupant files appeal (delays process 15-30 days)
2. Occupant files bankruptcy (automatic stay delays eviction)
3. Occupant claims ownership interest (requires different legal action)
4. Judge rules against you (forces different approach)

Cost: Typically $1,500-$4,000 in attorney fees plus filing fees.

## The Squatter Problem

Squatters — people occupying without any legal right — are different from heir-occupants but require similar procedures.

### Identifying Squatters

1. Not named in title chain
2. No lease or rental agreement
3. No permission from prior owner
4. Arrived after decedent's death
5. Often moving in as news of decedent's death spreads

### Texas Approach

Squatters in Texas are technically trespassers but evicting them requires the same forcible detainer process as tenants. You can't just call police and have them removed (in most cases).

### Why Squatters Are Tricky

Once someone has been in the property for any period, courts generally require eviction process rather than self-help removal. Police will often decline to intervene in what they view as a civil matter.

### Prevention

The best squatter strategy is prevention. Secure the property immediately upon acquisition. Visit regularly. Make it clear the property is owned and managed.

### If Squatters Are Present at Closing

Begin eviction process immediately. Every day they occupy strengthens their position.

## Tenant Handling

When your property has actual tenants (as opposed to heir-occupants), different rules apply.

### Step 1: Identify Lease Terms

Was there a written lease? What were the terms? Expiration date? Rent amount? Deposit held?

### Step 2: Continue or Terminate

Under Texas law, a new owner generally takes subject to existing leases unless the lease specifies otherwise. You can:

**Continue the lease:** Tenant stays, pays rent to you, lease runs its course. Good if tenant is paying well and you want the income.

**Terminate per lease terms:** If lease allows termination on sale, execute per terms.

**Wait out lease:** If lease expires soon, wait rather than terminate.

### Step 3: Notify Tenant

Provide written notice of ownership change:

1. Your identity as new owner
2. Where to send rent payments
3. Contact information
4. Any changes to lease terms (within allowed limits)

### Step 4: Transfer Deposit

Texas Property Code § 92.105 requires security deposit transfer to new owner at closing. Get deposit from prior owner if possible. If not, you may be liable for refund at end of lease.

### Step 5: Decide on Keeping Tenant

Factors:

1. Rent level vs. market
2. Tenant's payment history
3. Property condition maintenance
4. Your plans for property

If you plan to sell retail, a month-to-month tenant is easier to manage than a long-term lease. If you plan to hold as rental, a good tenant with stable history is valuable.

## Property Security Measures

Beyond locks and boards, comprehensive property security includes:

### Physical Security

**Locks:** Rekey or replace all exterior locks. Deadbolts on all exterior doors. Sliding door locks. Window locks.

**Doors:** Repair damaged doors, install sturdy replacements for compromised ones.

**Windows:** Board broken windows. Install window alarms if needed.

**Lighting:** Exterior lights on timers or motion sensors. Bright bulbs that actually illuminate.

**Gates/fences:** Repair damaged fences. Secure gates with locks.

**Alarms/cameras:** Basic alarm system ($200-$500). Camera system ($100-$300 for basic setup). Signs alone deter opportunistic thieves.

### Regular Presence

**Drive-bys:** Visit property 2-3 times per week minimum. Check for changes, intrusion signs, new occupants.

**Neighbors:** Meet neighbors. Give them your contact info. Ask them to call if they see problems.

**Lawn/yard:** Keep yard maintained. Overgrown property signals vacancy to criminals.

**Mail:** Stop mail delivery or have it forwarded. Stacked mail signals vacancy.

### Documentation

**Photos:** Take exterior and interior photos regularly. Date-stamp everything.

**Logs:** Maintain visit log. Note anything unusual.

**Incidents:** Document any break-ins, damage, or suspicious activity immediately. Police reports for anything significant.

## Utility Management

Utilities at acquired properties require specific handling.

### Priority Utilities

**Water:** Crucial. Water shutoff creates freeze damage in winter, vacancy signals year-round. Either transfer water service to your name or ensure it remains on through another arrangement.

**Electricity:** Needed for security systems, pumps, climate control. Transfer service or arrange alternative.

**Gas:** If applicable to property. Heating in winter is important for preventing freeze damage.

**Sewer/trash:** Usually automatic with utility account. Transfer to your name.

### How to Transfer

1. Contact each utility company
2. Establish new account in your name (or your entity's name)
3. Provide proof of ownership (recorded deed, title policy)
4. Pay deposit if required
5. Continue service without interruption

### Timing

Ideally transferred at closing or within 3 days. Gap between old owner's service ending and your service beginning creates problems.

### Vacant Property Rates

Some utilities offer vacant property rates — lower service levels for properties not occupied. Useful for properties you're holding empty before rehab or resale.

### Monthly Costs

Budget $150-$400 per month for basic utilities on vacant property. This is holding cost that needs to be factored into deal economics.

### What Happens if Utilities Are Off

1. Water: Pipes may freeze in winter, fixtures may deteriorate
2. Electric: No security system, no sump pump, no climate control
3. Gas: No heating, freeze damage in winter
4. All of these create property damage that increases eventual rehab costs

Pay the utilities. It's cheaper than the damage.

## Insurance

The day you own the property, you need insurance. The prior owner's policy (if any) is gone.

### Types of Property Insurance

**Vacant property insurance:** Specialized product for properties without occupants. Standard homeowner's policies often won't cover vacant properties beyond 30-60 days. Typically $500-$2,000/year for basic coverage. Higher deductibles than owner-occupied. Limited perils covered.

**Dwelling fire policy (DP-1, DP-2, DP-3):** Simpler coverage for properties that may be vacant or rental. DP-1: Basic coverage, limited perils. DP-2: Broader perils. DP-3: Open perils (most comprehensive of the DP series).

**Landlord policy:** If property has tenants. Property coverage, liability coverage, loss of rents coverage.

### Selecting Coverage

Factors:

1. How long you'll own the property
2. Whether you'll occupy/rent/vacant hold
3. Replacement cost vs. actual cash value
4. Deductible amount

### What to Cover

1. Dwelling structure
2. Other structures (garages, sheds)
3. Liability for injuries on property
4. Loss of use (if applicable)
5. Vandalism
6. Named perils (or open perils with broader coverage)

### Skipping Insurance

Some operators gamble without insurance on short-hold properties. This is risky:

1. Fire destroys property: total loss
2. Injury on property: personal liability exposure
3. Theft: no recovery
4. Weather damage: out of pocket

A $1,000 annual premium protects against $200,000 potential loss. The math supports coverage.

## Property Tax Management

Property taxes continue to accrue. Managing them properly matters.

### Confirm Tax Status

1. Pull tax records for property
2. Confirm all prior taxes were paid at closing (delinquent taxes should have been addressed)
3. Verify current tax bill amount
4. Note next payment due date

### Payment Timing

Texas property taxes are due by January 31 of the following year. Penalties and interest begin February 1.

1. Pay before January 31 to avoid penalties
2. For shorter holds, plan payment timing around sale
3. For longer holds, consider escrow payment monthly

### Appeals and Protests

If you think the assessment is too high:

1. Texas property tax appeals have specific deadlines (usually May 15)
2. File protest with appraisal district
3. Present evidence supporting lower value
4. Can reduce taxes significantly if successful

### Special Situations

**Homestead exemption:** Prior owner may have had exemption. Transfers don't retain exemption. Factor higher taxes into holding costs.

**Agricultural exemption:** Special use assessments may need review.

**Over-65/disabled exemptions:** Don't transfer to new owner.

### For Flip Properties

Since you're likely to sell within 6-12 months, you may only pay taxes once (at resale, prorated). But if you hold longer, budget for full annual tax.

## The Initial Property Assessment

Now that the property is yours and secured, do a comprehensive assessment.

### Exterior Assessment

**Roof:** Age, condition, leaks, missing shingles, sagging areas, gutters

**Siding/walls:** Damage, rot, paint condition, structural concerns

**Windows:** Broken, boarded, single vs. double pane, frames rotted

**Doors:** Condition, security, frames intact

**Foundation:** Cracks, settling, drainage issues

**Landscape:** Overgrowth, dead trees, drainage, fence condition

**Driveways/walkways:** Cracks, settling, tripping hazards

**Outbuildings:** Sheds, garages, carports — condition, safety

### Interior Assessment — Kitchen

1. Cabinets condition
2. Countertops
3. Appliances (working? present?)
4. Plumbing under sinks
5. Flooring

### Bathrooms

1. Toilets, tubs, sinks functional
2. Tile condition
3. Grout and caulk
4. Ventilation (fans working)
5. Plumbing leaks

### Bedrooms

1. Flooring
2. Walls (holes, stains, damage)
3. Closets intact
4. Windows operable
5. Door hardware

### Living Areas

1. Flooring
2. Ceiling (water stains indicate roof issues)
3. Walls
4. Fireplace if present
5. Windows

### Systems — HVAC

1. System type (central, window units, heat pump)
2. Age and condition
3. Working or broken
4. Ductwork condition
5. Thermostat

### Electrical

1. Panel age and condition
2. Outlet count and placement
3. Grounding status
4. Code compliance
5. Any obvious issues (burning smell, flickering lights)

### Plumbing

1. Pipe material (copper, PEX, galvanized, cast iron)
2. Fixture condition
3. Water pressure
4. Drain operation
5. Water heater age and condition

### Safety

1. Smoke detectors present and functional
2. Carbon monoxide detectors if gas appliances
3. Railings on stairs
4. Trip hazards
5. Structural concerns

### Document Everything

1. Photos of each area
2. Notes on conditions
3. Rough estimates for repairs needed
4. Priority ranking

This assessment drives your next-phase decision.

## The Next-Phase Decision

After initial assessment, decide what to do with the property.

### Option 1: Quick Flip (Wholesale)

Sell the property as-is to another investor. No rehab. Fastest exit.

- **Advantages:** Fastest capital recovery, lowest risk, minimal work
- **Disadvantages:** Lower profit than other exits
- **Best for:** Properties needing heavy rehab, when you want velocity

### Option 2: Retail Flip (Rehab and Sell)

Complete rehab, list for retail sale to end buyer.

- **Advantages:** Highest total profit potential
- **Disadvantages:** Requires rehab capital, longer timeline, execution risk
- **Best for:** Properties in good neighborhoods with moderate rehab needs

### Option 3: Hold as Rental

Rehab to rental standard, keep for cash flow.

- **Advantages:** Passive income, appreciation, tax benefits
- **Disadvantages:** Ties up capital, ongoing management
- **Best for:** Cash-flowing properties in rental-friendly areas

### Option 4: Hold Short-Term for Appreciation

Minimal rehab, hold for market appreciation before sale.

- **Advantages:** Low work, potential market gains
- **Disadvantages:** Market risk, holding costs, capital tied up
- **Best for:** Appreciating markets, operators with patient capital

### Option 5: BRRRR (Buy, Rehab, Rent, Refinance, Repeat)

Rehab to rental standard, rent, refinance to pull capital, use capital for next deal.

- **Advantages:** Builds portfolio, recovers capital
- **Disadvantages:** Requires rehab and refinance expertise
- **Best for:** Operators building rental portfolios

### Decision Factors

1. Your capital available
2. Your rehab capability/capacity
3. Market conditions
4. Property condition
5. Neighborhood trajectory
6. Your overall business model

Make this decision early. It drives everything else.

## Initial Rehab Decisions

Even if full rehab isn't planned, some initial work is almost always worthwhile.

### Must-Do Items (Regardless of Exit Strategy)

**Clean out property:** Remove trash, abandoned possessions, debris

**Basic security:** Locks, boards on broken windows

**Safety:** Smoke detectors, no major hazards

**Utilities functional:** Water not leaking, electrical not dangerous

**Stop active damage:** Roof leaks, plumbing leaks, any ongoing deterioration

### Should-Do Items for Most Exits

**Basic cleaning:** Make property presentable for showings or inspections

**Minor exterior:** Lawn mowed, weeds pulled, curb appeal improved

**Lock/secure outbuildings:** Don't give thieves easy targets

**Deal with obvious pest issues:** Rodents, wasps, termites if visible

### Avoid Doing (Until Decision Is Final)

1. Cosmetic upgrades
2. Fixture replacement
3. Paint before you know who's next occupant
4. Major system replacement if not immediately necessary

## Clean-Out Strategy

Many fractured equity properties come with substantial personal property left behind.

### The Situation

Decedent's possessions. Family photos. Furniture. Clothes. Kitchen items. Sometimes vehicles. Sometimes valuable items (jewelry, documents, heirlooms).

### Legal Status

Generally, personal property in a house belongs to the estate. Your deed conveys real estate, not personal property. Personal property ownership depends on:

1. Was it conveyed with the sale? (rare but possible)
2. Did family already remove what they wanted?
3. Did heirs sign off on abandoned items?
4. What does state law say about abandoned property?

### The Practical Approach

**Step 1: Photograph everything.** Before touching anything, photograph all rooms. Documentation protects you.

**Step 2: Allow heir access.** Give heirs reasonable opportunity to retrieve personal items. Typically 14-30 days. Document the offer and their response.

**Step 3: Identify potentially valuable items.** Documents (wills, deeds, birth certificates), family photos and albums, jewelry, firearms, legal papers. These should be preserved and offered to family specifically, even if they've otherwise declined.

**Step 4: Clean out.** If family doesn't retrieve within reasonable time: hire junk removal company ($300-$2,000 depending on volume), or rent dumpster ($300-$500) and do yourself. Donate usable items (Goodwill, Salvation Army). Properly dispose of hazardous materials (paint, chemicals, batteries).

### What to Keep

1. Documents that may relate to property (appliance manuals, warranties)
2. Keys (to property, outbuildings, anything locked)
3. Property records you find (tax documents, mortgage papers)

### What to Be Careful With

**Firearms:** Handle per state law, may require police involvement

**Drugs (legal or illegal):** Don't take possession

**Evidence of criminal activity:** Document and call police

**Biological hazards:** Hire proper cleanup services

## Code Violations and Municipal Issues

Inherited code violations are common on fractured equity properties.

### Identifying Issues

1. Check municipal records for open violations
2. Visit property and note visible issues
3. Talk to code enforcement about any pending matters
4. Request status of any pending actions

### Common Violations

1. Overgrown lawn or vegetation
2. Abandoned vehicles on property
3. Trash or debris
4. Structure not weatherproof (boarded windows, open doors)
5. Fence not repaired
6. Animal feces or hoarder-level accumulation
7. Unsafe structures

### Your Exposure

You own it now. Violations become your problem. Outstanding fines from prior owner may remain against the property as liens.

### Resolution

1. Address active violations quickly (usually means cleaning, securing, maintaining)
2. Negotiate with code enforcement on older violations
3. Pay any fines that haven't been contested
4. Document compliance

### Timeline Concerns

1. Some violations have daily accruing fines
2. Addressing quickly prevents escalation
3. Extended neglect can lead to condemnation or demolition

### Working With Code Enforcement

Municipal inspectors are usually reasonable when they see the new owner actively addressing issues. Show up in person. Introduce yourself. Explain you just acquired the property. Ask what they need to resolve violations. Usually cooperative.

## Surprises and How to Handle Them

After acquisition, surprises happen. Some common ones:

### Hidden Damage

Mold discovered. Foundation crack found. Termite damage exposed. Roof worse than expected.

Response:

1. Assess seriousness (professional if needed)
2. Determine repair cost
3. Adjust next-phase plan if needed
4. Factor into your deal profitability analysis
5. Learn for future underwriting

### Unknown Occupants

Person you weren't told about living in the property.

Response:

1. Identify their legal status
2. Follow appropriate procedure (eviction if needed)
3. Document everything
4. Don't confront aggressively

### Title Issues Discovered

Something title commitment missed. Someone asserting interest you didn't know about. Error in deed.

Response:

1. Contact title company immediately (title insurance should cover covered defects)
2. Contact your attorney
3. Document the issue
4. Don't compound by making hasty decisions

### Environmental Hazards

Asbestos, lead paint, oil tank, contaminated soil, meth lab residue.

Response:

1. Get professional assessment
2. Understand remediation cost
3. Consult attorney about disclosure obligations
4. May need to adjust strategy significantly

### Property Condemned or Scheduled for Demolition

Municipality has active proceedings.

Response:

1. Contact municipality immediately
2. Attorney involvement critical
3. Explore stay of demolition during your cure efforts
4. May need to prioritize remediation aggressively

## Key Takeaways for Operators

Before moving to Chapter 22, internalize these principles:

**The first 30 days determine success or failure.** Neglect in this period creates expensive problems.

**Day one priorities:** Confirm ownership, secure physically, establish presence.

**Occupants require different responses depending on their legal status.** Heirs, family members, tenants, and squatters each need tailored approaches.

**Heir-occupant conversations work better than legal threats.** Attempt direct communication first.

**Eviction procedures take 3-6 weeks and $1,500-$4,000.** Build this into timeline and budget when needed.

**Property security is immediate and ongoing.** Locks, lights, visits, neighbors.

**Utility management prevents damage.** Transfer service, don't let utilities lapse.

**Insurance is mandatory, not optional.** Vacant property policies are available and worth the cost.

**Property taxes continue accruing.** Stay current.

**Initial assessment drives the next-phase decision.** Do it thoroughly, early.

**Five next-phase options exist:** wholesale, retail flip, rental, short-term hold, BRRRR. Pick based on your situation.

**Clean-out requires care.** Legal process, family access, proper disposal.

**Code violations are your problem now.** Address quickly to prevent escalation.

**Surprises happen. Have a response framework.** Don't panic; evaluate, consult, plan.

**Records matter.** Maintain comprehensive files from day one.

## What's Next
