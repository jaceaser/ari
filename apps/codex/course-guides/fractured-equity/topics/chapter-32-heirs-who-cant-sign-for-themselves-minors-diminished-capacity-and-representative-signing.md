## 32.1 The Problem

Chapter 30 taught you how to find missing heirs. Chapter 31 taught you how to proceed when the search fails. This chapter addresses a different situation: you found the heir, you know exactly where they are, and they are a real living person — but they cannot legally sign a deed.

A minor child cannot sign. An adult under guardianship cannot sign on their own. An adult whose cognitive capacity has been diminished by dementia, stroke, severe mental illness, or brain injury may or may not be able to sign depending on the specifics. In each of these situations, the interest exists, the person exists, and the transaction is still possible — but signing has to happen through a legally recognized representative, not through the heir personally.

This chapter walks through the four main categories of heirs who can't sign for themselves, the legal mechanisms that allow someone else to sign on their behalf, the ethical constraints that apply to working with vulnerable people, and the practical steps to close deals without crossing any line that would unwind your title or create criminal exposure. The theme throughout is that these transactions are possible, but they require more care, more documentation, and sometimes more patience than transactions with heirs who are fully competent adults.

The stakes of getting it wrong are high. A deed signed by a person who lacked capacity is voidable — and sometimes void outright. A deed signed by someone claiming to hold power of attorney who actually didn't is a nullity. A transaction involving an elderly person that looks like exploitation can produce criminal liability under elder-abuse statutes. Do this work carefully, with attorney involvement at every step, and with full documentation of every decision.

## 32.2 Four Categories of Heirs Who Can't Sign

**Category 1 — Minors (Under 18)**

A person under the age of eighteen in Texas cannot execute a deed, a contract for the sale of real property, or most other binding legal instruments on their own behalf. The inheritance interest exists — minors can and do inherit real property — but the interest is held for them, and any transaction involving that interest requires court-approved representative signing. There is no informal workaround. Parents cannot simply sign for their children unless a court has authorized them to do so.

**Category 2 — Adults Under Guardianship**

When a court has formally adjudicated an adult as needing a guardian — because of intellectual disability, severe mental illness, advanced dementia, traumatic brain injury, or other condition — the guardianship order specifies who can act on that person's behalf and for what purposes. A guardian of the estate has authority over property; a guardian of the person has authority over medical and personal decisions. Some guardianships are full (the ward cannot act at all in the relevant domain); some are limited (the ward retains some authority, and the guardian has authority only in specified areas). You must read the actual guardianship order — not rely on what family members tell you — to understand who can sign for what.

**Category 3 — Adults with Diminished Capacity but No Guardian**

The most difficult category. An adult who has dementia, has had a stroke, is in late-stage illness, or otherwise has significantly reduced cognitive capacity, but who has never been formally adjudicated by a court and has no guardian. Legally, this person is still presumed competent. Practically, they may or may not be able to execute a deed at any given moment. This category demands the most care and the most attorney involvement. It is also the category most ripe for abuse, and the category where the elder-exploitation statutes have the sharpest teeth.

**Category 4 — Adults with Existing Powers of Attorney**

An adult who, while competent, executed a durable power of attorney authorizing someone else to act on their behalf. A properly executed durable power of attorney survives the principal's loss of capacity — that is what "durable" means. If the POA is valid, includes authority over real property transactions, and has been executed under Texas statutory form (Texas Estates Code Chapter 751), the agent can sign the deed. This is often the cleanest path when an elderly heir has lost capacity.

## 32.3 Capacity to Execute a Deed

Before walking through each category, we need a working understanding of what "capacity" means legally. Capacity is the legal threshold a person must meet in order for their signature on a deed to be valid.

**The Three Elements of Capacity**

Under Texas law and the general common law, capacity to execute a deed requires the signer to understand three things at the moment of signing:

1. **The nature of the transaction** — that they are transferring ownership of real property
2. **The consequences of the transaction** — that after signing, they no longer own what they previously owned
3. **The parties involved and the property** — who is receiving the interest, and what property they are transferring

This is a lower threshold than you might assume. A person with early-stage dementia can often meet all three elements on a good day. A person with mild cognitive impairment almost always can. A person in late-stage Alzheimer's typically cannot. The legal test is situation-specific, not diagnosis-specific.

**Capacity Is Not Binary, and It Fluctuates**

A critical legal and practical point: capacity is not a switch that is either on or off. It fluctuates, sometimes within the same day. A person with moderate dementia may be fully lucid at 10:00 a.m. and completely confused by 8:00 p.m. — a phenomenon called "sundowning." A person recovering from a stroke may have capacity on medication and not off it. A person with schizophrenia may have capacity when well-medicated and not during an episode.

The law recognizes this. A deed signed during a genuine lucid interval, with proper documentation, is valid even if the same person lacked capacity the day before and lacks capacity the day after. This is one of the most important facts in this chapter: capacity at the moment of signing is what matters, and that moment can be documented, protected, and made to stand up to later challenge.

**Who Decides Whether Capacity Existed**

At the moment of signing, the notary has an independent duty to refuse a signing if the notary reasonably believes the signer lacks capacity. The notary's duty is independent — the notary is not just there to stamp the document. A notary who signs a deed for someone who was obviously not tracking the conversation has exposure of their own.

After the fact, capacity can be challenged in court by anyone with standing — usually other heirs, a guardian appointed later, or family members who claim the signer was exploited. The party asserting the deed's validity generally carries some burden of showing capacity existed, which is why contemporaneous documentation matters so much. If capacity is ever challenged, the evidence you produce about the signing day — the medical documentation, the witnessing, the attorney involvement — determines whether the deed survives.

**The Presumption**

Texas law presumes adults are competent until a court says otherwise. That presumption is rebuttable — evidence of diminished capacity can overcome it — but the default is capacity. This matters for practical reasons: a grown adult with no guardianship order has the legal right to sign, and you cannot refuse to deal with them simply because you are worried they might be challenged later. What you can and should do is document carefully, involve an attorney, and make sure the circumstances of the signing support the presumption.

## 32.4 Category 1 — Minors

Minors inherit all the time. A grandfather dies intestate with three adult children and two minor grandchildren (children of a deceased fourth child). The minor grandchildren inherit their deceased parent's share. Now the real property has minor owners. To sell or partition that interest, you need a court-authorized representative to sign for the minors.

**The Legal Tools for Minors**

**Guardian of the Estate of the Minor.** A court-appointed guardian with authority over the minor's property. The guardian is typically a parent, but can be another adult. Appointment requires a separate guardianship proceeding in the county where the minor resides. Once appointed, the guardian can execute documents on the minor's behalf — but for real property transactions, most Texas guardianships require additional court approval of the specific transaction.

**Court-Appointed Temporary Guardian for a Specific Transaction.** When a full guardianship is not warranted but a single transaction needs to close, courts sometimes appoint a temporary guardian or a guardian ad litem for the specific purpose of reviewing and executing the sale. This is faster and cheaper than a full permanent guardianship.

**Court Approval of Minor's Transaction under Tex. Prop. Code.** In some circumstances, a minor's interest can be transferred with court approval through a specific statutory procedure, with the proceeds held in a restricted account until the minor reaches majority.

**UTMA Custodian (Limited Applicability).** A Uniform Transfers to Minors Act custodian has authority over gifts made to a minor under that specific framework. UTMA generally does not apply to real property interests acquired through intestate succession, but if a property interest was specifically transferred into a UTMA arrangement, the custodian can act.

**The Process**

The typical sequence for a minor's interest:

1. Identify whether a guardian of the estate has already been appointed for the minor
2. If yes — review the order to confirm scope of authority, then proceed with guardian's signature and any required court approval of the specific sale
3. If no — decide whether to pursue full guardianship (slower, more expensive, broader authority) or temporary/ad litem guardianship (faster, narrower, transaction-specific)
4. Coordinate with the parent or natural guardian, who will almost always be the applicant or a central figure in the guardianship proceeding
5. Budget for court approval of the transaction itself — the judge will review whether the sale is in the minor's best interest
6. Arrange for the minor's share of proceeds to be deposited into a registry of the court, a restricted account, or a structured instrument that protects the minor until majority

**Cost and Timeline**

| Path | Approximate Cost | Approximate Timeline |
|------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Temporary or ad litem guardianship for a transaction | $1,500–$4,000 attorney fees | 60–120 days |
| Full guardianship of the estate | $3,000–$8,000 | 6–12 months |
| Court approval of the specific transaction | $500–$1,500 additional | One additional hearing |
| Registry of court or restricted account management | Nominal ongoing fees | Ongoing until majority |

**Ethics with Minors**

A minor's inheritance is protected specifically because the minor cannot protect it themselves. The judge reviewing the transaction is going to ask whether the sale price is fair, whether the minor's share is being properly preserved, and whether any adult involved in the transaction — including a parent guardian — has conflicts of interest. Expect scrutiny. Price the minor's share at true pro-rata fair market value, not a discount. Be prepared to document comparable sales, appraisals, and the reasonableness of any expenses deducted from the minor's proceeds.

## 32.5 Category 2 — Adults Under Guardianship

An adult under guardianship is a person whom a court has found unable to manage their own affairs. The guardianship order is the controlling document. Every step of your work with this heir's interest flows from what that order actually says.

**Reading the Guardianship Order**

Your attorney needs a certified copy of the guardianship order, not a summary and not the family's description. The order will specify:

1. **The ward** — the person whose interests are being protected
2. **The guardian** — the person authorized to act for the ward
3. **Scope of guardianship** — full or limited
4. **Type of guardianship** — guardian of the estate (property authority), guardian of the person (personal/medical authority), or both
5. **Specific authority granted** — what the guardian can and cannot do without additional court approval
6. **Bond requirements** — whether the guardian has posted a bond and in what amount
7. **Reporting requirements** — what the guardian must report to the court and when
8. **Court approval requirements for transactions** — whether sales of real property require court authorization

Do not assume. Different orders have different terms. A guardian who has full authority in one case may have narrow authority in another.

**Types of Guardianship in Texas**

**Full Guardianship.** The ward has been found unable to manage any significant affairs. The guardian has broad authority, though real property sales typically still require court approval under the Texas Estates Code.

**Limited Guardianship.** The ward retains specified rights; the guardian's authority is limited to specific areas. Increasingly common as courts recognize that people with disabilities often retain significant decision-making capacity in many areas of their lives.

**Guardianship of the Estate Only.** The guardian has authority over property and finances; the ward retains authority over personal and medical decisions. This is the type of guardianship that authorizes signing deeds.

**Guardianship of the Person Only.** The guardian has authority over personal and medical decisions; the ward retains authority over property. If the ward has a guardian of the person but not of the estate, the ward themselves still signs deeds — but careful capacity review is warranted because a guardianship of the person often signals underlying cognitive concerns.

**Temporary Guardianship.** Short-term, specific-purpose guardianship, often used during emergencies or pending permanent appointment.

**Court Approval for Real Property Sales**

Under Texas Estates Code provisions governing guardianships, sales of real property held by a ward typically require court approval even when the guardian has general authority. The process involves:

1. Filing an application in the probate or guardianship court with jurisdiction
2. Notice to interested parties
3. Typically an appraisal or other evidence of fair market value
4. Hearing and court order authorizing the sale
5. Compliance with any terms the court imposes (minimum price, method of sale, reporting on proceeds)

This is not a rubber stamp. Courts take seriously their duty to protect wards from transactions that favor buyers over the ward's interests. Come prepared with fair market evidence and clean terms.

**Working with a Guardian**

Guardians are typically either family members or professional guardians (attorneys or guardianship specialists). Family-member guardians are emotionally invested and sometimes inexperienced with the paperwork. Professional guardians know the process but will be scrupulous about fees, court approval, and the ward's best interest. In both cases, allow time for careful process. Do not push for shortcuts. A guardianship transaction is not the place to apply pressure.

**When the Ward's Interest Is Small**

If the ward's fractional interest is modest, sometimes the transaction cost of going through court approval exceeds the value of the interest. In those cases, options include:

1. Leaving the ward's interest unaddressed and acquiring the other heirs' interests only — taking on the ward as a fractional co-owner
2. Partition by sale, which forces a court-supervised resolution and handles the ward's interest as part of the broader partition
3. A buyout structured to make the ward's portion attractive enough to justify guardian effort, including the court approval process

## 32.6 Category 3 — Diminished Capacity, No Guardian

This is where the most care is needed. The heir is an adult, living in the community or in a care setting, with reduced cognitive function but no guardianship order. Legally presumed competent. Practically, maybe. You have to make good decisions under genuine uncertainty — and you have to make those decisions with full attorney involvement, because the consequences of getting it wrong range from a voidable deed to a criminal charge.

**First — Is There an Existing Power of Attorney?**

Before assuming this category applies, check whether the heir executed a durable power of attorney while fully competent. Many elderly people did, years before cognitive decline began. If a valid durable POA exists covering real property transactions, this case is actually Category 4 — the agent signs, and diminished capacity does not block the transaction.

Ask the family. Ask adult children. Ask whether any attorney handled elder-law or estate-planning work for the heir. A POA drafted in 2012 for someone whose cognition declined in 2022 is still good, and it solves the problem entirely.

**Second — Assess Whether Capacity Exists at This Moment**

If no POA exists, the question becomes whether the heir has capacity to execute the deed themselves. This assessment is a legal and clinical judgment — not something you make on your own. The factors that support a finding of capacity:

1. The heir can describe who they are, where they live, and who their family members are
2. The heir understands they inherited property from a specific person
3. The heir understands that signing will transfer their interest to someone else
4. The heir understands they will receive payment in exchange
5. The heir can articulate, in their own words, why they are agreeing to the transaction
6. The heir is not being prompted or coached through the conversation by anyone else
7. The heir's answers are consistent across multiple conversations at different times

**The Lucid-Interval Path**

When capacity is present — genuinely, not manufactured — the heir can sign. Best practice for a lucid-interval signing:

1. **Obtain a medical evaluation from the heir's physician** within a few days of the signing, confirming the heir's current capacity and understanding of the transaction
2. **Schedule the signing during the heir's best time of day** — typically morning, before any afternoon or evening cognitive decline
3. **Have an independent attorney present** — not the attorney of the buyer, but one retained specifically for the heir, who can confirm the heir's understanding and voluntariness
4. **Use an independent notary**, not one affiliated with the buyer
5. **Record the conversation** in which the heir describes the transaction in their own words (with consent)
6. **Have at least two disinterested witnesses**, ideally someone the heir has known for years
7. **Document everything in the file** — the medical report, attorney involvement, notary review, witness statements, transaction records

**When Capacity Is Absent or Doubtful**

If the heir cannot meaningfully articulate what is happening, or if capacity is only present for brief unpredictable windows, the lucid-interval path is not available. Your options then are:

1. Pursue a guardianship proceeding — the family applies, a guardian is appointed, and then the transaction proceeds under Category 2
2. Use the statutory healthcare-surrogate or next-of-kin framework if applicable to the specific situation (but note: healthcare surrogates generally do not have authority over real property transactions)
3. Walk away from the heir's specific interest and work with the other heirs only, potentially pursuing partition
4. Wait — if the heir's condition is temporary (e.g., post-surgical, rehabilitation, medication adjustment), capacity may return

**What You Cannot Do**

**You cannot have the heir sign when you know capacity is absent.** This is not a gray area. A deed executed by someone you know lacks capacity is invalid, and arranging such a signing exposes the investor, the notary, and potentially the attorney to civil liability and possible criminal exposure under elder-exploitation statutes.

**You cannot "notary-shop" for a lucid moment.** Taking an heir with significant cognitive impairment from notary to notary until one signs it off is a pattern that elder-protection investigators recognize immediately. Prosecutors pursue these cases aggressively.

**You cannot rely on a family member's assurance that "she knows what she's doing."** The family member may have their own conflict of interest. Independent legal and medical review is required.

**You cannot coach the heir on what to say.** The attorney and notary need to hear the heir describe the transaction in their own words. A coached answer is a red flag that indicates capacity is actually absent.

**Elder Financial Exploitation Statutes**

Texas and every other state have criminal statutes targeting the exploitation of elderly or vulnerable adults. The offenses typically include misapplication of property, financial exploitation, and theft from an elderly person. Penalties range from state jail felonies to first-degree felonies depending on amounts involved. Civil exposure adds treble damages and attorney fees in some statutes.

Do not let the apparent simplicity of signing a deed obscure how seriously law enforcement takes these cases when capacity turns out to have been absent. Protect yourself with documentation and independent professional involvement on every transaction that touches this category.

## 32.7 Category 4 — Heirs with Valid Powers of Attorney

When an heir executed a valid durable power of attorney before losing capacity, the agent named in the POA can sign the deed. This is often the cleanest resolution when capacity is uncertain — the legal authority already exists, authorized by the heir themselves while they could authorize it.

**Validity Requirements**

Under Texas Estates Code Chapter 751 (Durable Power of Attorney Act), a valid POA requires:

1. Written document
2. Signed by the principal
3. Acknowledged before a notary (or meeting alternative statutory execution requirements)
4. Language indicating the power is durable — i.e., survives the principal's loss of capacity — OR executed as a statutory durable POA under the Texas form
5. The principal had capacity at the time of signing (a POA executed by someone who already lacked capacity is invalid)
6. The power over real property specifically granted — either through a general power of attorney that includes real property, or through specific real-property authority language

**Statutory Durable Power of Attorney Form**

Texas provides a statutory form under Tex. Est. Code § 752.051 that, when used, creates a clear authority framework. The form allows the principal to check off specific areas of authority. Real property transactions are one of the listed authorities. If the executed POA uses this form and real property authority is selected, the agent's authority is clear. POAs that depart from the statutory form can still be valid but require more careful review.

**Documenting the POA's Validity**

Your attorney needs to review:

1. **Certified or original copy of the POA** — not a photocopy of uncertain provenance
2. **The principal's identity at the time of execution** — evidence that the principal actually signed
3. **The principal's capacity at the time of execution** — contemporaneous evidence that the principal understood what they were signing
4. **Whether the POA is still in effect** — POAs can be revoked, can expire, or can have become inoperative if the named agent has died or resigned
5. **Scope** — does the specific language grant authority over this specific transaction?

**Title Insurance on POA Transactions**

Title companies scrutinize POA-executed transactions carefully. They typically require:

1. Original or certified copy of the POA
2. Affidavit from the agent affirming the POA is in effect and has not been revoked
3. Sometimes a physician's statement regarding the principal's current status
4. Sometimes recording of the POA in the county real property records
5. Occasionally an indemnification from the agent, the buyer, or both

Work with your title company early in the transaction. Identify exactly what documentation they will require. Get the documentation assembled before closing rather than scrambling at the last minute.

**When POA Is Not Enough**

Even a valid POA is sometimes not the right tool:

1. If the principal is now competent again (recovered from stroke, changed medications), the principal should sign themselves — using the POA when the principal can sign is unnecessary and looks suspicious
2. If the agent has a conflict of interest (agent is buying from principal, or agent's family is benefiting), most POAs and the Texas statute require special handling
3. If the agent is named in the transaction as a co-heir, self-dealing concerns arise that your attorney must address
4. If the principal has died after the POA was executed but before the current transaction, the POA has terminated by operation of law — a POA does not survive the principal's death, and the principal's heirs or estate must now act

## 32.8 Attorney Ad Litem Representation

In certain proceedings, the court appoints an attorney ad litem — a lawyer whose job is to protect the interests of a person who cannot adequately protect themselves. For heirs who can't sign for themselves, the attorney ad litem often appears in:

1. Guardianship proceedings — representing the proposed ward
2. Probate and heirship proceedings — representing unknown or absent heirs, or known heirs under disability
3. Service-by-publication cases — representing the served-by-publication party
4. Partition proceedings involving minor or vulnerable co-tenants

**The Ad Litem's Role**

The attorney ad litem is not your attorney. They are not the family's attorney. They represent the protected person's interests and owe that person full loyalty. A good ad litem will:

1. Interview the protected person (when that is possible)
2. Review the transaction or proceeding for fairness
3. Raise concerns with the court when the transaction does not serve the protected person's interests
4. Negotiate improvements to terms — higher price, better protection of proceeds, additional safeguards
5. Ultimately advise the court on whether to approve the transaction

**Working with the Ad Litem**

Treat the ad litem as a professional doing their job, not as an obstacle. Provide requested information promptly. Respond to concerns substantively. Pay the fee without dispute. Ad litems who feel respected and well-informed produce better outcomes for everyone, including the investor. Ad litems who feel obstructed or disrespected report concerns to the court, and those reports can derail transactions.

**Cost**

| Context | Approximate Fee |
|---------|----------------|
| Transaction-focused appointments | $500–$2,500 |
| Contested proceedings or complex situations | Up to $5,000 or more |
| Who pays | The party requesting the proceeding (that's you) |
| Recovery | Sometimes taxed as costs and recovered from transaction proceeds |

## 32.9 The Notary's Independent Duty

In every category above, the notary has an independent duty at the signing. The notary is not just affixing a stamp. Under Texas law and the notary regulations that accompany it, the notary must:

1. Confirm the signer's identity
2. Confirm the signer is acting freely and voluntarily
3. Confirm the signer understands what they are signing
4. Refuse to notarize if any of the above are in doubt

**What This Means for Your Transaction**

A notary who notarizes a deed for someone who is obviously disoriented, who is being prompted through answers by a family member, or who cannot explain what they are signing — has violated their duty. A deed so notarized can be challenged, and the notary can face regulatory action or worse. Your attorney and your title company will want to use notaries who take their duty seriously. That is actually protective of your transaction, not against it — a notary who documents a careful review contemporaneously makes your title stronger, not weaker.

**Selecting a Notary for Sensitive Signings**

1. Use a notary who is experienced with elder-law signings, estate-planning signings, or guardianship work
2. Avoid mobile notaries paid by volume who do not slow down for capacity review
3. Consider having the attorney bring a notary the attorney has worked with before
4. For signings in care facilities, the facility may require their own notary to be present — coordinate in advance

## 32.10 Medical Documentation

When capacity may be at issue, contemporaneous medical documentation is the strongest evidence you can gather. A capacity evaluation by the heir's physician — dated within a reasonable window around the signing — documents that a medical professional who knows the patient assessed capacity at a relevant time.

**What to Request**

Work with the heir's physician (or, if needed, a consulting physician like a geriatric psychiatrist) to obtain:

1. A written statement describing the heir's current cognitive status
2. A specific statement addressing capacity to execute legal documents
3. A statement addressing whether the heir understands the specific transaction
4. Notes on any conditions that affect capacity and whether those conditions are stable, improving, or declining
5. Information about medications and their effect on cognition

**Protecting Against Privacy Concerns**

Medical records are protected under HIPAA. The heir (or their authorized representative) needs to release the records specifically. Work with the attorney and with the family to obtain appropriate HIPAA-compliant releases. Do not attempt to obtain medical records through back channels — that creates its own legal exposure and undermines the value of the documentation you do obtain.

**When Medical Documentation Says Capacity Is Absent**

If you obtain a physician's statement and it concludes the heir lacks capacity, do not ignore it. Do not shop for a different opinion. The physician who knows the patient is usually right. Shift the transaction to the appropriate path — guardianship, POA if one exists, or walking away from the heir's specific interest.

## 32.11 Signings in Care Facilities

When the heir is in a nursing home, assisted living, rehabilitation, or a psychiatric facility, the signing happens in the facility. This introduces additional considerations.

**Facility Cooperation**

1. Coordinate with the facility's social work or administration in advance
2. Ask about any facility policies regarding legal signings by residents
3. Schedule at a time the resident is typically alert and capable
4. Request a private room for the signing rather than conducting it at bedside in a shared room
5. Bring attorney, notary, and witnesses — the facility typically will not provide these

**HIPAA Considerations in Facility Signings**

Facility staff cannot disclose resident information without authorization, but they also cannot actively prevent a resident from meeting with outside attorneys and signing documents on their own behalf. The resident has the right to conduct their own legal affairs — what they do not have, if they lack capacity, is the legal effectiveness of those signings. So the facility may cooperate with the logistics without confirming the resident's medical status, and your capacity documentation has to come from the resident's own physician (with the resident's authorization).

**The Facility's Potential Concerns**

Facilities are sometimes wary of outside parties conducting signings with residents because they have seen genuine exploitation cases. A respectful, well-documented, attorney-led process reassures the facility. An aggressive, poorly-documented process triggers alarm, often including the state long-term-care ombudsman or adult protective services. Conduct yourself in a way that makes the facility an ally, not an adversary.

**Post-Signing Verification**

After the signing, facility social workers sometimes follow up with the resident to confirm they understood what happened. If the resident, in that follow-up, is unable to explain the transaction or expresses confusion about it, the facility may report the matter. The best protection against this outcome is to conduct the signing such that the resident genuinely did understand it — not to avoid facilities that conduct follow-ups.

---

## 32.12 Ethical Lines That Cannot Be Crossed

This chapter's subject matter attracts bad actors. The opportunity to acquire real estate from people who cannot advocate for themselves is real, and it tempts some operators into crossing lines that end careers and put people in prison. The following practices are not gray areas:

**Paying below pro-rata fair value for the vulnerable heir's interest.** Every heir in every category above is entitled to the same per-interest value as every other heir in the same property. Discounting the vulnerable heir's share because they won't know the difference is textbook elder financial exploitation. Pay the same per-interest price to the guardian, the agent, or the ad litem that you pay to the competent adult heirs.

**Using forged or backdated powers of attorney.** A POA document created after the principal lost capacity is a nullity, and producing it knowingly is fraud. A POA backdated to appear to have been executed earlier is forgery. Both produce serious criminal exposure.

**Notary shopping.** Taking an impaired signer to multiple notaries until one signs it off is a recognized pattern of elder exploitation. Do not do it.

**Paying family-member "consultants" under the table to deliver the heir's signature.** When a family member is compensated by the buyer to produce a signature from a relative who cannot fully advocate for themselves, both the family member and the buyer have exposure under conspiracy, exploitation, and theft statutes.

**Sole-heir misrepresentation with the vulnerable heir conveniently unrepresented.** An affidavit of heirship that names only the heirs who cooperated, and omits the heir who could not advocate, is perjury — a third-degree felony under Texas law. Your title is also unwound when the omitted heir's family asserts their interest.

**Pressuring family members into not reporting concerns.** Intimidating family members who raise concerns about a vulnerable heir's participation is its own form of elder-protection violation in many jurisdictions.

**The Test**

Ask yourself before every transaction in this category: if the state attorney general's elder-protection unit reviewed this file six months from now, would the file support the conclusion that every participant acted properly? If yes, proceed. If anything in the file gives you pause, fix the problem before closing.

---

## 32.13 When to Walk Away

Not every deal involving a vulnerable heir is closable. Know the signs that tell you to stop:

1. Capacity is genuinely absent and no valid POA exists
2. Guardianship is needed but family members refuse to pursue it
3. A family member insists on "managing" the heir's signing in ways that look coached or coercive
4. The heir's interest is small and the process cost is larger than the interest
5. An attorney you trust advises against proceeding
6. A title company refuses to insure the transaction because of capacity concerns
7. A notary refuses to notarize because of capacity concerns
8. An attorney ad litem objects and the objection is substantively correct
9. Adult protective services or a long-term care ombudsman has opened an inquiry

Walking away from one deal is always cheaper than being the subject of an elder exploitation investigation. Leave the heir's interest alone, work the rest of the deal with the competent heirs, and live to invest another day.

## 32.14 Worked Examples

### Example 1 — The Minor Grandchildren

A Harris County tract is owned 1/4 each by four heirs, two of whom are minor grandchildren (ages 8 and 11). The grandchildren's parent — the decedent's adult child — had predeceased the decedent. The grandchildren inherited their deceased parent's share. The two adult heirs (aunts of the minors) want to sell. The minors' surviving parent (widowed spouse of the deceased co-heir) is willing to pursue guardianship of the minors' estates.

**The path:** Apply for limited guardianship of the estate for each minor, with the surviving parent as guardian. Apply for court approval of the sale. Close the transaction with the guardian signing and proceeds deposited in restricted accounts for each minor.

**Timeline:** Approximately six months.

**Attorney cost:** Approximately $6,000 total for the guardianship work and transaction approval.

**Result:** The transaction closes, the minors' shares are preserved in restricted accounts, and the property title is clean. The key decision that made this work was the surviving parent's willingness to file — without that cooperation, the path would have been a longer and more expensive petition by a third party, or a partition action structured to require court-supervised resolution of the minors' shares.

### Example 2 — The Existing Durable POA

An 84-year-old heir in Brazos County owns a 1/6 interest in a 40-acre tract. She has moderate dementia. In 2015, when she was fully competent, she executed a Texas statutory durable power of attorney appointing her daughter as agent. The POA includes real property transaction authority. She now lives with the daughter.

**The path:** Attorney obtains certified copy of the POA, obtains agent affidavit affirming the POA is in effect, and coordinates with the title company. The daughter signs as agent.

**Timeline:** Transaction closes cleanly, with less than two weeks added to the process.

**Cost to address the capacity question:** Approximately $500 in attorney time to review the POA and assemble documentation.

**Result:** The POA created a decade earlier solves the entire problem. The lesson: always ask about estate planning documents before assuming Category 3. A valid POA collapses Category 3 into Category 4, which is dramatically cleaner and faster.

### Example 3 — The Lucid-Interval Signing

A 78-year-old heir in Dallas County has mild-to-moderate dementia — good mornings, confused evenings. No guardianship. No POA. Her adult children are divided: two want to sell, one does not.

**The path:** Consulting geriatric psychiatrist evaluates her capacity at 10:00 a.m. and documents that she understands the nature of the transaction, the consequences, and the parties. Independent attorney retained specifically for her — at buyer's expense — reviews the transaction with her, documents her understanding, and recommends she proceed only if that is genuinely her wish. Signing held at 10:30 a.m. with the attorney, two disinterested witnesses (one a longtime neighbor), and an experienced elder-law notary present. Her own words about the transaction are recorded (with her consent and the consent of everyone present). Everything is documented in the transaction file.

**Result:** The dissenting child later raises concerns. The contemporaneous documentation — the geriatric psychiatrist's written evaluation, the independent attorney's engagement letter and notes, the notary's journal entry with a description of the capacity review, the recorded statement, the two witness declarations — withstands challenge. The deed holds.

**The lesson:** The lucid-interval path is not a workaround. It is a legitimate path when capacity genuinely exists. But you must document as if every element will be challenged, because any element might be.

### Example 4 — When Walking Away Was Right

A 91-year-old heir in a nursing home in Wichita County owns a 1/8 interest in a rural tract. No guardianship. No POA. Family has not visited in two years. The investor conducts an initial visit with a nephew who claims to speak for her.

**What the visit shows:** The heir cannot name the property. She cannot describe her family. She is actively confused about the conversation. When asked what she owns, she asks the nephew to answer for her.

**The nephew's position:** She "has good days" and wants to push for a signing on one of those days.

**What happens:** The attorney declines to proceed. The title company confirms they will not insure. The investor walks away, pursues partition with the other seven heirs, and acquires a 7/8 undivided interest in the property without the 1/8.

**The alternative:** Pushing through a capacity-doubtful signing — using an unverified "good day" as cover — would have risked elder-exploitation exposure that was not worth the value of a 1/8 rural interest. The investigation, the criminal exposure, the reputational damage, the attorney fees: all of it costs more than the 1/8 was worth.

**The lesson:** Walking away is not failure. It is correct decision-making.

## 32.15 Documentation

Every transaction in this chapter's categories needs a documentation file that could withstand later scrutiny. At minimum:

| Document Category | What It Includes |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Representative authority | Guardianship order, POA, court appointment |
| Authority scope | Specific review confirming authority covers this transaction; court approval order if required |
| Medical documentation | Contemporaneous physician statement on capacity, specific to this transaction |
| Attorney involvement | Independent attorney's engagement, review, and notes; ad litem report if applicable |
| Notary records | Notary's journal entry; any supplemental notes on capacity review process |
| Transaction records | How price was determined; comparable evidence of fair market value |
| Communication records | Who spoke with whom, when, about what |
| Signing records | Who was present, in what capacity; what the signer said in their own words |

Retain this file for the life of the property interest at minimum. Claims involving vulnerable heirs can surface years after the transaction. The file is your defense.

## 32.16 Common Pitfalls

**Assuming a parent can sign for a minor without court authority.** They cannot. Parental authority over minor children does not extend to executing deeds on behalf of the minor's inherited property interest. A court-appointed representative is required.

**Treating an old POA as automatically valid.** POAs can be revoked, can terminate on the principal's death, can become inoperative if the agent is unavailable, and can be invalid from the start if the principal lacked capacity at execution. Attorney review every time.

**Assuming a guardian has all the authority the family says they have.** The guardianship order controls. Read it. Real property sales typically require separate court approval even when the guardian has general authority.

**Relying on family member assurances about capacity.** The family member may have a conflict of interest, may not have seen the heir recently, or may have a biased perspective. Independent medical and legal evaluation is required whenever capacity is at issue.

**Overlooking the attorney ad litem.** In guardianship and publication cases, the ad litem has real authority and real duties. Treat the ad litem as a central player, not a formality.

**Skipping medical documentation in marginal capacity cases.** A physician's statement costs a few hundred dollars and produces strong protection. Skipping it to save money is false economy — the protection is worth orders of magnitude more than the cost if the deed is ever challenged.

**Using the same notary on every signing involving impaired signers.** A notary who sees the same buyer bringing impaired signers repeatedly is a notary who is going to be investigated. Use experienced notaries appropriate to each specific situation.

**Pushing a transaction the attorney advises against.** Your attorney is telling you something the file shows. Listen.

**Failing to anticipate title company scrutiny.** Title companies are careful about these transactions because they are the ones who insure the result. Engage them early. Provide documentation proactively. Address concerns before closing, not at the closing table.

**Trying to handle this work without specialized counsel.** General practitioners can handle normal real estate transactions. Transactions involving minors, wards, and heirs with diminished capacity benefit from attorneys who handle elder law, guardianship, or probate work regularly.

## 32.17 Key Takeaways

**Four categories of heirs who can't sign for themselves:** minors, adults under guardianship, adults with diminished capacity but no guardian, and adults with existing powers of attorney. Each has a different path.

**Capacity is not binary and it fluctuates.** A person with moderate dementia can have capacity at 10 a.m. and lack it by 8 p.m. Contemporaneous documentation of the signing moment is what makes the deed hold.

**The three elements of capacity:** understanding the nature of the transaction, understanding the consequences, and understanding the parties and property.

**Adults are presumed competent.** Texas presumes adult capacity unless a court has found otherwise. Proceed with respect for that presumption, but also with careful documentation.

**Minors require court-authorized representative signing.** Parents cannot informally sign for minor children's inherited property interests. A guardian of the estate, with court approval of the specific transaction, is the required path.

**Guardianship orders control.** Every guardianship is different. Read the specific certified order. Do not assume scope from what family members say.

**Durable POAs are the cleanest path when available.** A valid POA executed while the principal was competent solves the diminished-capacity problem without court involvement. Always ask before assuming none exists.

**The lucid-interval path requires proof.** When an heir with diminished capacity signs during a lucid interval, you must document medical, legal, notary, and witness evidence of capacity at the moment of signing.

**The notary has independent duties.** Notaries are not passive stamp-affixers. They must confirm identity, voluntariness, and understanding — and refuse to notarize when any of these is doubtful.

**Attorney ad litems protect vulnerable parties.** Treat them as professionals doing important work, not as obstacles. Feed them information. Respond to concerns. Pay their fees.

**Elder exploitation statutes are serious.** The ethical lines are not negotiable. Exploitation exposes the investor to civil liability and criminal charges that end careers.

**Pay fair value to vulnerable heirs.** Pro-rata, no discount, no shortcut. The guardian, agent, or ad litem receives the same per-interest price you offer every other competent heir.

**Know when to walk away.** When capacity is genuinely absent, when guardianship will not be pursued, when the attorney advises against proceeding — leave the interest alone and work the rest of the deal.

**Documentation is defense.** Every transaction in this category needs a file that withstands scrutiny years later. Build it as you go. Retain it for the life of the interest.
