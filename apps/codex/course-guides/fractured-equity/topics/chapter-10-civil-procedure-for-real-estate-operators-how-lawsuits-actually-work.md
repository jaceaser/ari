## 10.1 Why You Need to Understand Civil Procedure

In earlier chapters, I've referenced lawsuits: contribution suits, partition actions, quiet title actions, creditor suits, sheriff's sales. You don't need to be an attorney. Your lawyer does the legal work. But you do need to understand what happens procedurally — when petitions are filed, how service works, what a default judgment looks like, how writs of execution convert judgments into property, and what a bankruptcy automatic stay does to your deal.

Operators who don't understand civil procedure can't manage their attorneys well. They don't know when to push for faster filings, when to ask about service progress, when to schedule hearings, or when a deal is in trouble. They let attorneys set timelines that don't match their business needs, pay for wasted hearings, and lose months on procedural delays that a more engaged operator would have caught.

This chapter gives you the operator's working knowledge of civil procedure. Not law school. Just enough to run your cases as a business, manage your attorneys effectively, and understand what's happening in the courthouse while your deals move through the system.

Texas Rules of Civil Procedure apply to state court cases in Texas. Federal court uses a different set of rules — the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. For most of your work, Texas state courts are where everything happens, so the focus here is Texas procedure with brief notes on federal procedure where relevant.

---

## 10.2 Which Court Handles What

Different courts handle different types of cases. Knowing which court is the right venue saves you time and money.

### District Courts (Texas)

Courts of general jurisdiction. Handle:

1. Civil cases over $500 in controversy (with some exceptions)
2. Title suits (quiet title, trespass to try title)
3. Partition actions
4. Contract disputes
5. Tort claims
6. Most commercial matters

Most of your litigation — contribution suits, partition, quiet title — will be in district court.

### County Courts at Law (or Statutory County Courts)

Similar jurisdiction to district courts in most Texas counties, with some differences based on local statutes. Handle many of the same types of cases as district courts in counties that have them.

### Probate Courts

Specialized courts (in larger counties) or the probate sections of district courts (in smaller counties). Handle:

1. Probate of wills
2. Administration of estates
3. Heirship proceedings (including JDH)
4. Guardianships
5. Trust disputes
6. Mental health commitments

For probate-related matters (muniment of title, JDH, administrator appointments), probate court is the venue.

### Justice Courts (JP Courts)

Limited jurisdiction courts. Handle:

1. Small civil claims (up to $20,000 in Texas)
2. Eviction proceedings (forcible entry and detainer)
3. Truancy
4. Class C misdemeanors

Eviction actions — which you'll rarely initiate but may encounter when acquiring occupied properties — go through JP courts, then potentially to county court on appeal.

### County Courts (Constitutional County Courts)

Handle limited civil matters (up to $20,000) plus some probate duties in counties without separate probate courts. Varies significantly by county.

### Federal District Courts

Handle federal cases — federal question jurisdiction, diversity jurisdiction, federal statutory claims. For most fractured equity work, federal court isn't relevant. The exception is bankruptcy court (a unit of the federal district court system), which becomes relevant when an heir or cotenant files bankruptcy.

### Bankruptcy Courts

Specialized federal courts handling bankruptcy cases. Relevant to your deals because an **automatic stay** (see Section 10.13) halts most legal proceedings against a bankruptcy debtor.

## 10.3 The Petition — How Lawsuits Start

A Texas lawsuit begins when the plaintiff files a petition with the court clerk.

### What the Petition Contains

1. Identification of the court
2. Names and addresses of all parties (plaintiffs and defendants)
3. Statement of the facts giving rise to the claim
4. Legal theories the plaintiff is asserting
5. Specific relief requested (damages, property, declaratory judgment, etc.)
6. Prayer for costs and attorney's fees (if applicable)
7. Verification (sworn statement) if required for the type of case
8. Signature of the attorney (or pro se plaintiff)

### Filing

Petition is filed with the court clerk, along with the filing fee ($300–$500 for most civil cases, higher for some specialty courts). The clerk stamps it as filed and assigns a case number.

### Service on the Defendant(s)

After filing, the plaintiff must serve each defendant with a citation (the court's notice of the lawsuit) and a copy of the petition. Service mechanics are critical and discussed in Section 10.5.

### Operator's Perspective

For contribution suits, partition actions, quiet title actions, and the other kinds of cases you'll bring, your attorney will draft the petition. Your job is to:

1. Provide accurate factual information
2. Identify all parties who should be defendants
3. Specify the relief you're seeking (what result do you want?)
4. Review the petition before filing for accuracy
5. Pay the filing fees

Petitions are amendable. If facts change or the court requires additional detail, amended petitions can be filed. But the original petition sets the scope of the case, so get it right the first time when possible.

## 10.4 Parties — Who's In the Lawsuit

A civil case has plaintiffs (those suing) and defendants (those being sued). Getting the parties right is critical.

### Required Parties

In some cases, specific parties must be joined as defendants. In a partition action, every co-owner must be a party. In a quiet title action, every person claiming or potentially claiming an interest must be a party. Missing a necessary party can invalidate the judgment or leave title issues unresolved.

### Diligent Search

Texas Rules of Civil Procedure require plaintiffs to identify and serve all known defendants. When a defendant's address isn't known, plaintiffs must conduct a diligent search before seeking alternative service.

What "diligent search" means in practice:

1. Skip tracing through databases
2. Address verification through voter registration, DMV, utilities
3. Social media investigation
4. Inquiries with known relatives
5. Last-known-employer contact
6. Postal service inquiries
7. In complex cases, private investigator work

Document this search. Courts will ask for proof of diligence before allowing alternative service like publication.

### Interested Parties and Interveners

Some cases allow non-party persons with potential interests to intervene. For example, in a partition action, a creditor with a lien on one cotenant's interest might intervene to protect their lien. Interveners become parties subject to the court's jurisdiction.

### Pro Se Parties

Parties who represent themselves (without attorneys) are **pro se.** Most of the heirs you'll deal with in your cases will be represented by attorneys if they respond at all. Some respond pro se. Pro se defendants often miss deadlines, make procedural errors, and end up in default — which affects your strategy.

## 10.5 Service of Process — How Defendants Learn They're Being Sued

Service is the formal legal act of delivering the citation and petition to the defendant. Without proper service, the court doesn't have jurisdiction over the defendant, and any judgment obtained is void.

### Methods of Service in Texas

**Personal service.** A process server, constable, or sheriff personally delivers the citation and petition to the defendant. This is the gold standard and required if possible.

**Certified mail, return receipt requested.** Plaintiff mails the documents via certified mail. The defendant signs for them. Service is effective on receipt.

**Substituted service.** When personal service has been unsuccessful, the court may authorize alternative methods — leaving the documents with a person of suitable age at the defendant's residence, posting on the door, etc. Requires a motion and court order.

**Service by publication.** When the defendant cannot be located despite diligent search, the plaintiff can obtain permission to serve by publication — publishing notice of the lawsuit in a newspaper in the county for a specified period. Used primarily in in rem actions (affecting property rather than personal rights).

**Service on corporations and entities.** Served through the registered agent on file with the Secretary of State, or through other statutorily-permitted methods.

### Operator's Role

Your attorney manages service. Your job is to provide addresses, identify possible alternative locations, and approve the costs of process servers or publication.

### Timeline

1. Personal service: Can be accomplished in days if the defendant is locatable
2. Certified mail: 1–3 weeks for receipt and return
3. Substituted service: 2–6 weeks after court authorization
4. Service by publication: 6–12 weeks (publication period plus return)

### Why Service Matters to Your Strategy

Heirs you can't find can't sign deeds. But heirs you can't find can also be served by publication — which allows the case to proceed against them, potentially resulting in default judgment extinguishing their interest or clearing title against them. **Service by publication is one of your tools for dealing with missing heirs.** Chapter 32 covers this in depth.

## 10.6 The Answer — The Defendant's Response

Once served, the defendant must respond within a specific period or risk default.

### Texas Deadline

Defendants must file a written answer by 10:00 a.m. on the Monday following the expiration of 20 days after the service date (the "Monday following" rule). Failure to answer by this deadline allows the plaintiff to seek a default judgment.

### Content of the Answer

A basic answer contains:

1. Identification of the case and parties
2. General denial of the plaintiff's allegations (the standard Texas practice)
3. Affirmative defenses (specific legal defenses to the claims)
4. Any counterclaims or cross-claims
5. Signature and contact information

### Why Defendants Sometimes Don't Answer

1. They don't understand the process
2. They can't afford an attorney
3. They're overwhelmed by the situation
4. They don't think the suit has merit and ignore it
5. They're out of state and don't realize service was completed
6. They have other problems taking priority

### What Happens When the Defendant Doesn't Answer

The plaintiff becomes eligible to seek a default judgment (Section 10.8).

## 10.7 Discovery — Getting Information from the Other Side

Once both sides are in the case, Texas allows discovery — the formal exchange of information between parties. Discovery tools:

**Written interrogatories:** Written questions one party sends to the other, with written answers due within 30 days.

**Requests for production:** Requests for documents, records, or other tangible items. The other party must produce them or object.

**Requests for admission:** Requests that the other party admit or deny specific facts. Facts admitted or not denied are established for purposes of the case.

**Depositions:** In-person questioning of witnesses or parties under oath. Transcribed by a court reporter. Typically used for important witnesses.

**Expert witness disclosures:** If either side plans to use expert testimony, experts must be identified and their opinions disclosed.

### Discovery in Fractured Equity Cases

Most of your cases settle or end in default before substantial discovery occurs. When discovery does happen:

1. Interrogatories and requests for production on family history, property records, and claims
2. Depositions of specific witnesses if disputes require detailed testimony
3. Expert testimony only in unusual cases (appraisal disputes, defective deed analysis)

Budget for discovery if your case is contested. Each discovery exchange costs $500–$2,000 in attorney time.

## 10.8 Default Judgment — When Defendants Don't Show Up

Default judgment is one of the most important concepts for operators because it's how many of your cases actually end.

### What It Is

When a defendant fails to answer a properly-served lawsuit within the required time, the plaintiff can move for default judgment. The court reviews the file, confirms proper service, and if appropriate, enters judgment in favor of the plaintiff — typically giving the plaintiff whatever relief was requested in the petition.

### Requirements for Default

1. Defendant was properly served
2. Deadline to answer has passed
3. No answer has been filed
4. Petition adequately states a claim for the relief sought

### Process

Plaintiff files a motion for default judgment, often accompanied by:

1. Affidavit confirming service
2. Copy of the return of service
3. Military status declaration (confirming the defendant isn't in active military service, which would trigger special protections)
4. Proposed judgment reflecting the relief requested

Court reviews and, if everything is proper, signs the judgment.

### Why Default Judgments Matter to Your Business

Many of your cases will end in default. Heirs scattered across states may not respond. Heirs who don't have money for attorneys may ignore service. Heirs who are elderly, confused, or uninterested may simply not act.

When a cotenant defaults in a contribution suit, you obtain a money judgment that can be executed against their property interest — leading to a sheriff's sale where you can acquire their interest at the amount of the judgment.

When a defendant defaults in a quiet title action, the court can enter judgment clearing title against them — effectively extinguishing their potential claims.

### Risks of Default Judgments

**Motion to set aside.** Defendants can move to set aside a default judgment within 30 days of entry by showing good cause. Courts often set aside defaults when defendants show they missed deadlines for understandable reasons.

**Bill of review.** After 30 days, defendants can challenge a default judgment only through a bill of review (see Section 10.11). Harder to prevail but possible.

**Due process challenges.** A defendant can challenge whether service was truly proper. If service was defective, the default judgment can be voided even years later.

Your attorney should ensure service is bulletproof before pursuing default, because a set-aside default wastes all the time and money spent.

## 10.9 Summary Judgment — Resolving Cases Before Trial

Summary judgment is a procedure where one party asks the court to decide the case (or a specific issue) based on the written record, without a trial.

### When It's Used

When the material facts aren't in dispute and the case can be decided as a matter of law. For example, if defendants have admitted all relevant facts and the only remaining question is how the law applies, summary judgment is appropriate.

### Process

Moving party files a motion for summary judgment, supported by affidavits and evidence. Non-moving party has a period (typically 7–21 days) to respond. Court rules on the motion, sometimes after a hearing.

### In Fractured Equity Cases

Summary judgment is used occasionally — particularly in quiet title actions where the facts are clear and unopposed, or in contribution actions where the defendant has admitted the expenses. Full trials are rare in this business because most cases settle, default, or are resolved on motion.

## 10.10 Trial — When Cases Actually Go to Trial

Very few civil cases actually go to trial. In Texas, trial rates for civil cases are under 5% — most cases settle, default, or are resolved on motion. In fractured equity specifically, trials are rare.

### When Trials Happen

1. Contested title or heirship disputes that can't be resolved on motion
2. Partition proceedings where commissioners have to determine how to divide property
3. Damages disputes in contribution cases
4. Will contests and probate disputes

### Types of Trial

**Jury trial:** Jury decides factual disputes. Available in most civil cases. Costs more, takes longer, adds uncertainty.

**Bench trial:** Judge decides both facts and law. Faster, less expensive, more predictable.

Most Texas fractured equity cases are bench trials because they involve technical legal issues rather than disputed facts that juries are good at deciding.

### Cost of Trial

Even a simple bench trial can cost $10,000–$25,000 in attorney time, expert fees, and court costs. For most fractured equity deals, trial costs erase the deal's profit. Settlement is usually the right economic answer.

## 10.11 Post-Judgment Motions and Appeals

Even after judgment, cases aren't always over.

**Motion for new trial:** Losing party can file within 30 days of judgment. Grounds include: newly discovered evidence, misconduct, insufficient evidence, or errors of law. Rarely granted.

**Appeal:** Losing party can appeal to the Court of Appeals within 30 days of judgment (90 days if certain post-judgment motions are filed). Appeals don't usually involve new evidence; they're about whether the trial court correctly applied the law. Appeals take 12–24 months and cost $15,000–$40,000+.

**Bill of Review:** After 30 days post-judgment, a direct attack on a judgment can only be made through a bill of review. The party must show:

1. Meritorious defense to the underlying claim
2. Failure to assert the defense was not due to their own fault or negligence
3. Prevented from asserting it by fraud, accident, or mistake

Bills of review are very hard to win. Courts strongly prefer the finality of judgments.

### Operator Implications

Once you have a judgment, enforce it promptly. Appeal windows close in 30 days. After that, judgments are stable and can be collected on through writs of execution (Section 10.12).

## 10.12 Writs of Execution — Turning Judgments Into Property

A writ of execution is how you convert a money judgment into cash or property.

### What a Writ of Execution Is

A court order directing the sheriff or constable to seize specific property belonging to the judgment debtor and sell it to satisfy the judgment. The proceeds of the sale go to the judgment creditor up to the amount of the judgment; any excess goes back to the debtor.

### Process

1. Judgment creditor obtains a final money judgment
2. Judgment creditor files an application for writ of execution with the court
3. Clerk issues the writ
4. Writ is delivered to the sheriff or constable
5. Sheriff/constable identifies and seizes property of the debtor
6. Sheriff/constable conducts a sheriff's sale (auction)
7. Winning bidder receives a sheriff's deed
8. Proceeds distribute to creditor (up to judgment amount) with excess to debtor

### Types of Property Subject to Execution

1. Non-exempt real estate (not homestead)
2. Non-exempt personal property
3. Bank accounts (by garnishment, a related procedure)
4. Wages (through wage garnishment, limited by federal and state law)
5. Any fractional interest the debtor has in real property

### Texas Exemptions

Texas has broad exemptions protecting debtors' property from execution. Homestead is fully exempt. Tools of trade, household furnishings, and some other categories are partially exempt.

### In Fractured Equity Deals

The writ of execution is the enforcement mechanism that converts a contribution judgment into acquisition. You obtain a judgment against an uncooperative cotenant for their unpaid share of taxes/expenses. You get a writ of execution. The sheriff seizes and sells the cotenant's fractional interest in the very property the judgment arose from. At the sheriff's sale, you bid your judgment amount (through a "credit bid" where you bid your judgment rather than cash). You acquire the cotenant's interest for the judgment amount.

This is the creditor-suit-to-sheriff's-sale pipeline that Chapter 26 covers in detail. The writ of execution is the pivotal document.

## 10.13 The Sheriff's Sale — Where Execution Happens

When a writ of execution is issued against real property, the sheriff conducts a public auction.

### Notice Requirements

1. Notice posted in public places for a specified period (typically 20 days)
2. Notice published in a newspaper
3. Notice mailed to the debtor and any lienholders

### Location and Time

Sheriff's sales in Texas are conducted on the first Tuesday of each month, outside the county courthouse, typically between 10:00 a.m. and 4:00 p.m.

### The Auction

1. Sheriff announces the sale
2. Starting bid is typically a minimum amount set by the sheriff (often $0 or a small starting amount, or sometimes the amount of the judgment)
3. Participants bid openly
4. Highest bidder wins
5. Payment typically required immediately (cash, cashier's check, or other approved form)

### Credit Bid for Judgment Creditors

When you're the judgment creditor, you have a powerful advantage: you can **credit bid** — bid the amount of your judgment without actually paying cash. Your bid is credited against your judgment. If you win, you receive the sheriff's deed for your credit bid amount.

**Example:** Your judgment is $18,000. Other bidders max out at $12,000. You credit bid $12,001. You win. You receive a sheriff's deed transferring the debtor's interest to you. Your judgment is now satisfied up to $12,001; you have a remaining unsatisfied judgment of $5,999.

### Title Resulting from Sheriff's Sale

The sheriff's deed transfers whatever interest the debtor had in the property. Not more, not less. If the debtor had a 1/6 fractional interest, you acquire a 1/6 fractional interest — which may be exactly what you want in a fractured equity deal.

### Redemption Rights

In some situations, the debtor or other parties have a right to redeem the property by paying the judgment amount plus costs within a specified period. Check with your attorney about redemption rights applicable to your specific situation.

## 10.14 Temporary Restraining Orders and Injunctions

Sometimes you need the court to order someone to stop (or start) doing something on an emergency basis. TROs and injunctions are the tools.

### Temporary Restraining Order (TRO)

1. Emergency order issued without a full hearing
2. Typically granted ex parte (without notice to the other side)
3. Short duration (usually 14 days, extendable)
4. Used when immediate irreparable harm will occur without the order

### Temporary Injunction

1. Follows the TRO after a hearing
2. Lasts until trial on the merits
3. Requires notice and hearing
4. Higher evidentiary standard than TRO

### Permanent Injunction

1. Entered at the end of the case
2. Permanent unless modified
3. Based on full trial on the merits

### Uses in Fractured Equity

1. TRO to stop a scheduled tax sale when the foreclosing entity has procedural defects
2. TRO to prevent a cotenant from selling a property unilaterally
3. Injunction to prevent waste by a life tenant
4. Injunction to preserve the property pending partition

### Costs

TROs are expensive: $2,000–$5,000 in attorney fees for the motion and hearing. Injunctions with full hearings cost $5,000–$15,000+.

### Bond Requirements

When a plaintiff obtains a TRO or injunction, the court often requires a bond to protect the defendant in case the order is wrongfully granted. Bond amounts vary based on potential harm.

## 10.15 Bankruptcy Stays — The Deal-Stopper

When any defendant or property owner involved in your deal files bankruptcy, a powerful federal rule takes effect immediately: the **automatic stay.**

### What the Automatic Stay Is

Under **11 U.S.C. § 362**, when a bankruptcy petition is filed, an automatic stay takes effect that prevents:

1. Commencement or continuation of lawsuits against the debtor
2. Enforcement of judgments against the debtor
3. Acts to obtain possession of property of the bankruptcy estate
4. Acts to create, perfect, or enforce liens against property of the estate
5. Setoffs and similar remedies

### Practical Impact on Your Deals

1. If a defendant files bankruptcy after being served, your lawsuit is stayed. No further proceedings without court approval.
2. If a judgment debtor files bankruptcy, you cannot execute on the judgment. The sheriff's sale is stopped.
3. If a property owner files bankruptcy, you cannot acquire the property without bankruptcy court approval.
4. Even if you're in the middle of closing, if the seller files bankruptcy, the closing is halted.

### How to Respond

1. Confirm the bankruptcy filing is legitimate
2. Retain a bankruptcy attorney (your real estate attorney probably isn't one)
3. File a motion for relief from stay if you have grounds
4. Participate in the bankruptcy proceeding to protect your interests

### Motion for Relief from Stay

You can seek court permission to proceed with your lawsuit or acquisition despite the stay. Grounds:

1. Cause, including lack of adequate protection
2. Debtor has no equity in the property and the property isn't necessary for reorganization
3. Other specified bankruptcy rules

### Timeline Impact

A bankruptcy stay can delay your deal by 6 months to several years. Most deals don't survive that. The operators who do preserve value are the ones who engage bankruptcy counsel early and actively protect their interests.

## 10.16 Attorney's Fees and Costs

Understanding how attorney's fees work in civil litigation helps you budget and negotiate.

### The American Rule

Generally, each party pays their own attorney's fees. The winner doesn't automatically get their fees paid by the loser.

### Exceptions (When Fee-Shifting Applies)

1. Contract provisions allowing recovery of attorney's fees
2. Specific statutes authorizing fee recovery
3. Frivolous lawsuit penalties
4. Sanctions for misconduct

### Texas Fee-Shifting Statutes Relevant to Operators

**Texas Civil Practice & Remedies Code § 38.001:** Allows recovery of attorney's fees in contract claims (including some real estate cases)

**Texas Property Code § 22.001 et seq.:** Fee provisions in quiet title actions

**Uniform Declaratory Judgments Act:** Allows fees in declaratory judgment actions

### Court Costs

Filing fees, service fees, jury fees, and similar costs are typically awarded to the prevailing party. These are modest amounts ($500–$2,000 typically) compared to attorney's fees.

### Strategic Fee Decisions

When fee-shifting is available, litigation economics change. A defendant facing the prospect of paying your attorney's fees if they lose is more likely to settle. Include fee claims in your petitions when statutes or contracts allow.

## 10.17 Key Takeaways for Operators

Before moving to Part III, internalize these principles:

**You don't need to be a lawyer.** But you need to understand procedure well enough to manage your attorneys and your cases.

**Venue matters.** District court for most civil cases. Probate court for estate-related matters. JP court for evictions. Know where each type of case belongs.

**The petition starts everything.** Get it right. Identify all parties. Request the specific relief you need. Your attorney drafts it, but you approve it.

**Service is critical.** Improper service voids judgments. Document diligent search. Use publication when necessary.

**Default judgments are where many cases end.** Many of your cases will resolve without the defendant ever appearing. Make sure service is bulletproof.

**Writs of execution convert judgments into property.** The pipeline from contribution judgment → writ of execution → sheriff's sale → acquired interest is one of the most important mechanics in this business.

**Credit bidding is a superpower.** When you're the judgment creditor at a sheriff's sale, you can bid your judgment amount without cash. This is how you acquire interests from debtors who don't have money to buy you out.

**Bankruptcy stays stop everything.** Learn to recognize them immediately. Get bankruptcy counsel when needed. Some deals can't survive a long stay.

**TROs and injunctions are tools for specific situations.** Not every deal needs them, but know they exist and what they cost.

**Most cases settle or default.** Trials are rare. Plan accordingly.
