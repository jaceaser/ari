## 25.1 The Most Important Leverage Tool

If you had to pick one leverage tool to master, pick this one.

Partition actions are the legal right to force the sale of jointly-owned real property when co-owners can't agree on what to do with it. Under Texas law, any cotenant — any owner of a fractional interest — has the absolute right to compel partition. No minimum ownership percentage required. No excuse needed. No permission from other owners necessary.

This is a powerful right. Think about what it means practically:

1. You own a 10% interest in a property. The other 90% won't sell voluntarily. Partition forces a sale, and you recover your 10%.
2. You've acquired interests from 5 of 7 heirs. The last 2 won't engage. Partition forces resolution, and you either buy them out in the process or acquire the whole property at the forced sale.
3. You bought a single heir's 25% interest cheaply, knowing that partition is your exit strategy. Partition delivers that exit.

Partition is why fractional interest acquisitions work at all. Without partition, a 25% interest would be essentially worthless — you couldn't live in it, couldn't sell it, couldn't really do anything with it. Partition gives the fractional interest its value by creating a forced exit mechanism.

This chapter teaches partition comprehensively. The statutory framework under Texas Property Code Chapter 23. The two forms — partition in kind and partition by sale. When each applies. How to file. How to manage the case through discovery and hearing. How settlements typically work. How to handle counterclaims. How partition coordinates with other tools.

Master this chapter and you master the single most important legal mechanism in fractured equity.

## 25.2 The Legal Foundation

Texas Property Code Chapter 23 — "Partition of Real Property" — establishes the statutory framework. Key provisions:

**§ 23.001 — Right of Partition**

> "A joint owner or claimant of real property or an interest in real property or a joint owner of personal property may compel a partition of the interest or the property among the joint owners or claimants under this chapter and the Texas Rules of Civil Procedure."

This is the foundational grant. Any cotenant can file. No ownership threshold. No defenses based on size of interest.

**§ 23.002 — Jurisdiction**

District court has jurisdiction over partition actions involving real property, regardless of value of the property or interest.

**§ 23.003 — Partition of Property in Several Tracts**

Tracts located in different counties can generally be handled in a single action where plaintiff has proper venue.

**§ 23.004 — Partition by Sale**

Establishes when partition by sale is appropriate — primarily when physical partition would be impractical or would result in harm to the parties' interests.

**Texas Rules of Civil Procedure Rules 756–771**

Detailed procedural rules for partition actions. Covering pleadings, parties, commissioners, reports, sales, and related matters.

### The Foundational Principle

The right to partition is nearly absolute in Texas. Courts have little discretion to deny partition. The only real questions are:

1. What method — in kind or by sale?
2. What accountings are due between the parties?
3. How are the proceeds distributed?

Not usually: "Should partition happen?"

That distinction matters enormously for operators. You are not asking for a favor. You are asserting a right. The legal posture of a partition plaintiff is fundamentally different from a plaintiff asking a court to grant discretionary relief. Courts do not weigh the equities of whether partition should occur — they determine how it proceeds.

## 25.3 Partition in Kind vs. Partition by Sale

Two forms of partition exist. Understanding which applies matters greatly.

### Partition in Kind (Physical Division)

The property is physically divided among cotenants. Each cotenant receives a specific portion reflecting their interest.

**Example:** 100 acres of farmland with 4 cotenants at 25% each could be partitioned in kind by dividing into four 25-acre parcels.

**When Partition in Kind Applies**

1. Property can be physically divided without significant loss of value
2. Each portion can stand alone as useful property
3. Divisions can be approximately equal, or accountings can equalize them
4. Physical partition doesn't prejudice any cotenant

**Common for:**
- Large acreage
- Multiple parcels (each parcel to different cotenant)
- Rural properties with natural dividing lines
- Commercial properties with divisible units

### Partition by Sale

The property is sold as a whole and proceeds divided among cotenants per their interests.

**When Partition by Sale Applies**

Texas Property Code § 23.004(a) establishes that partition by sale is appropriate when:

1. Property cannot be divided in kind without prejudice to owners
2. Physical partition would substantially diminish value
3. Property is residential and can't be meaningfully divided
4. Market conditions favor sale over division

**Common for:**
- Single-family homes
- Small residential lots
- Commercial buildings with unified function
- Properties where physical division would destroy value

### The Typical Fractured Equity Case

Most fractured equity properties are single-family homes that can't be physically divided. Partition by sale is the applicable method.

**Why This Matters for Operators**

1. Partition by sale is simpler procedurally
2. Results in actual cash distribution
3. You can bid at the sale to acquire full ownership
4. Holdouts can't prevent the sale, only negotiate terms
5. Clean exit for all parties

Understanding which form applies before filing shapes your entire case strategy. If you're dealing with a 3-bedroom house in San Antonio, you're filing for partition by sale. If you're dealing with a 400-acre ranch with 6 heirs and natural creek lines that create logical boundary divisions, partition in kind may be on the table. Know the difference before you engage counsel.

## 25.4 When Partition Is the Right Tool

Not every fractured equity problem calls for partition.

### Partition Is Right When — You Own a Fractional Interest

You need to actually have an interest in the property. Acquired through deed from heirs, administrator's deed, contribution judgment attachment, or other means. A prospective interest isn't enough.

### Voluntary Negotiation Has Failed

You've exhausted reasonable voluntary efforts. Offers made, responses sought, reasonable terms proposed. Without this documented history, partition feels premature and some judges react accordingly. The documented negotiation history also protects you ethically and strategically — it demonstrates that partition is the last resort, not the opening move.

### The Deal Economics Support Litigation Costs

Partition costs $12,000–$35,000+. The deal profit potential needs to justify this cost. Run the numbers honestly before filing. If the property's net profit after all costs lands at $20,000, a potential $30,000 legal bill destroys the deal. The economics have to work even in a worst-case legal spend scenario.

### You're Prepared for Timeline

8–24 months typical. Capital committed throughout. Other deals proceeding during litigation. Partition is not a quick flip tool. It is a sustained legal engagement that requires your attention and your attorney's active management.

### You Can Bid at the Sale or Accept Market Proceeds

At the forced sale, you either buy the property at market (using combined cash and your credit for your existing interest) or you take cash for your interest. Know your preferred outcome before filing. These are different strategies and they shape how you approach settlement.

### Partition Is Wrong When:

**You don't *actually own* an interest yet.** Prospective buyers can't partition. You need to complete acquisition first.

**Voluntary negotiation is working, just slowly.** Don't abandon working channels for litigation that may not produce better results.

**Deal economics *too* small.** $30,000 in legal fees for a $20,000 profit potential is backwards.

**You're not prepared for the process.** Litigation requires sustained attention. Don't file if you can't manage it.

**Better tools exist.** Contribution judgment might accomplish the same outcome more cheaply. Voluntary acquisition with improved terms might work. Consider alternatives before committing to litigation. Chapter 24's contribution suit is almost always worth attempting before partition because it costs less, resolves faster, and often prompts settlement on its own.

## 25.5 Setting Up the Partition Case

Before filing, prepare thoroughly.

### Establish Your Ownership Interest Clearly

Document the chain of title proving your fractional interest:

1. Deed from heirs (showing what percentage conveyed)
2. Affidavit of Heirship documenting decedent's heirs
3. Probate documents if applicable
4. Your recorded deed showing current ownership

Title company review may be helpful to confirm your interest before filing. If your title to the fractional interest has any cloudiness, resolve it before you bring suit. You cannot successfully pursue partition on a disputed ownership claim.

### Identify All Other Cotenants

Every cotenant must be named as a defendant. Missing parties create problems:

1. Judgment doesn't bind non-parties
2. Future claims possible from missing cotenants
3. Title issues after partition sale

Complete heirship research ensuring all cotenants identified. For unknown heirs, Part VI (Chapters 30–34) covers procedures. A partition judgment that binds only the parties you served is worth far less than one that binds all cotenants. Do not cut corners on heir identification before filing.

### Prepare Ownership Schedule

Document each cotenant's interest:

1. Who they are
2. How they acquired their interest
3. What percentage they own
4. Current status (alive, deceased, missing, incompetent)

This schedule becomes the backbone of your petition and the court's eventual distribution order. Inaccuracies at this stage cause problems throughout the case.

### Assess the Property

1. Current fair market value
2. Condition affecting value
3. Income history if any
4. Any existing liens or encumbrances

Get an appraisal if value is uncertain. Partition often involves court-appointed appraisals anyway. Knowing the value going in helps you evaluate settlement offers against the likely auction outcome.

### Prepare Accounting

If there are accounting issues between cotenants — rents collected, expenses paid, improvements made — prepare your accounting claim. Partition often includes accounting. If you've been paying taxes on a property for two years before filing, that contribution claim belongs in this case.

### Consider Related Actions

Should other claims be combined with partition?

1. Contribution claims for expenses paid
2. Accounting for rents or profits
3. Quiet title for clouded interests
4. Specific performance on any agreements

Filing multiple claims in one action is often efficient. Combining related claims avoids parallel litigation, reduces overall costs, and usually produces more comprehensive settlements.

## 25.6 The Partition Petition

Your attorney files the partition petition. Required elements:

### Parties Identification

1. Plaintiff (you) as cotenant
2. All defendants (other cotenants)
3. Any other necessary parties

### Property Identification

1. Legal description
2. Street address
3. Tax parcel number
4. County

### Ownership Statements

1. Your interest and how acquired
2. Each defendant's interest and how acquired
3. Supporting documentation (deeds, AOH, probate)

### Factual Allegations

1. Property is jointly owned
2. Cotenants cannot agree on disposition
3. Partition is appropriate remedy

### Request for Relief

1. Partition of the property
2. Method (in kind or by sale) requested
3. Accounting if applicable
4. Distribution of proceeds
5. Costs and fees if applicable

### Specific Prayer for Relief

> "WHEREFORE, Plaintiff prays that this Court enter judgment ordering partition of the Property by sale, with proceeds distributed to the cotenants in accordance with their respective interests..."

The prayer for relief should be specific about the method you're seeking. Asking for partition by sale when the property is clearly a single-family home is appropriate — the court will confirm it's the correct method, but requesting it directly moves the case along more efficiently.

## 25.7 Service and Initial Response

Every defendant must be properly served.

### Personal Service

Process server or constable delivers petition and citation to defendant personally. Most reliable method. Use personal service whenever defendants can be located — it eliminates service challenges and starts the response clock running cleanly.

### Service by Mail

Certified mail with return receipt. Can be used in limited circumstances.

### Service by Publication

For defendants who cannot be located despite diligent efforts. Published in newspaper for specified period. Required for missing heirs (Chapter 31 covers this in detail). Service by publication is slower and produces a weaker default judgment — defendants served by publication can later seek to set aside default more easily than those personally served.

### Typical Timeline

1. Filing to service: 2–6 weeks
2. Service to response deadline: 20 days under Texas Rules

### Defendant's Responses

**Answer Admitting Ownership:** Most common response. Defendant confirms they're a cotenant and asserts any counterclaims or defenses.

**Answer Denying Ownership:** Defendant disputes that they have any interest (or that you have any interest). This raises threshold issues that must be resolved before partition can proceed.

**Affirmative Defenses:** Common defenses include statute of limitations, laches (undue delay), agreement against partition (rare but possible), and specific equitable defenses.

**Counterclaims:** Defendant asserts their own claims — contribution claims against you, accounting claims, trespass or other tort claims, their own partition counter-petition.

**Default:** If a defendant doesn't respond within the deadline, default judgment can be sought. A defaulting defendant loses rights to participate but may retain their ownership interest subject to the partition. The partition proceeds against their interest even without their active participation.

## 25.8 The Discovery Phase

If defendants appear, discovery follows.

### Written Discovery

1. Interrogatories (written questions)
2. Requests for production (document requests)
3. Requests for admission (admit or deny statements)

### Depositions

Oral questioning under oath. Typically:
1. Each cotenant
2. Key witnesses (appraisers, family members with knowledge)

### Document Collection

1. Property records
2. Financial records (taxes paid, rents collected, expenses)
3. Communication records
4. Heirship documentation

### Expert Discovery

1. Property appraisals
2. Accounting experts
3. Title experts if disputes

### Discovery Timeline

1. Opens 30 days after answer filed
2. Typically runs 3–9 months
3. May be extended for complex cases

### Discovery Disputes

Common disputes:
- Documents not produced
- Interrogatories not answered
- Depositions refused
- Scope of discovery

Court resolves disputes through motions to compel and protective orders. These can add time and cost. Defendants who want to delay partition sometimes use discovery disputes as a stalling mechanism. Efficient case management — your attorney pushing the case forward on schedule — is the counter.

Discovery often serves a secondary strategic purpose: it surfaces information that clarifies settlement value. After depositions and appraisals, both sides know the facts. Uncertainty reduces. Settlement becomes more rational. Many cases that seemed stuck before discovery resolve cleanly after it.

## 25.9 The Commissioner Process

For partition in kind cases, Texas Rules of Civil Procedure 758–768 establish a commissioner process.

### Commissioners

Three disinterested persons appointed by the court to make the actual physical division.

### Commissioner Duties

1. Inspect the property
2. Research values
3. Propose division
4. Report to court

### Commissioner Report

1. Description of proposed divisions
2. Justification for equity
3. Any equalization payments needed
4. Recommendations

### Court Review

Court reviews the report, hears objections, confirms or modifies. Final order implements the division.

### When This Matters

In fractured equity cases with single-family homes, the commissioner process is rare because partition by sale is the typical method. But for properties that can be divided in kind — acreage, multiple parcels, rural land with natural boundary lines — commissioners are the mechanism through which the division actually gets made. Understanding their role matters when you're working with rural or multi-parcel properties where physical division is on the table.

## 25.10 The Partition by Sale Process

More common in fractured equity. Court orders the property sold.

### The Sale Order

Court enters an order directing:

1. Property to be sold (not divided)
2. Method of sale (public auction typical, sometimes private sale)
3. Minimum bid, if any
4. Terms of sale
5. Distribution of proceeds

### Public Auction

Most common. Public notice given. Auction at courthouse or property. Highest bidder wins.

- **Pro:** Market-driven price
- **Con:** Sometimes goes below fair value
- **Pro:** Clean procedure with finality

### Private Sale (Broker-Listed)

Court may order property listed with a broker, sold at market. Less common but possible for residential.

- **Pro:** Typically achieves fair market value
- **Con:** Slower process
- **Pro:** Familiar to real estate market

### Negotiated Sale (Rare)

Court approves sale to a specific buyer at a specific price. Only with all parties' consent, usually.

### The Bidding Strategy

At partition sales, you have a structural advantage: you can bid using your existing interest as credit.

**Example:** You own a 40% interest. Property sells for $200,000 at partition sale. You bid $200,000.

- Your $200,000 bid is applied: $80,000 credit for your 40% interest (no cash needed for that portion)
- $120,000 cash required for the 60% you don't own

**Net cash required: $120,000 to acquire full ownership.**

**Compared to Non-Cotenant Bidders**

Other bidders must bring full cash. Your credit advantage lets you bid higher than competitors without risking more cash. A competitor who wants to bid $200,000 needs $200,000 in cash. You only need $120,000 to place the same winning bid. This structural edge can be decisive at auction.

### This Is Why Partition Is Strategically Valuable

It lets you acquire full ownership using the leverage of your existing partial interest. The partial interest you acquired voluntarily — often at a discount — becomes the credit that reduces your cash requirement at the forced sale. The whole system compounds.

## 25.11 Distribution of Proceeds

After sale, proceeds distribute per interests — with adjustments.

### Gross Proceeds

Sale price received at auction or private sale.

### Deductions

1. Sale costs (auctioneer, broker commission, advertising)
2. Unpaid taxes and liens
3. Court costs
4. Commissioner fees (if applicable)
5. Attorney fees if awarded

### Net Proceeds

Gross minus deductions.

### Distribution by Interest

Each cotenant receives their percentage:

| Cotenant | Interest | Net Proceeds | Share |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | 40% | $205,000 | $82,000 |
| B | 30% | $205,000 | $61,500 |
| C | 20% | $205,000 | $41,000 |
| D | 10% | $205,000 | $20,500 |

### Accounting Adjustments

If accounting claims have been determined:

1. Cotenant owed contribution gets their share plus the contribution amount
2. Cotenant who owes receives their share minus the owed amount
3. Net distributions reflect all claims

### Example Calculation

Property sells for $240,000.

Sale costs: $20,000.

Unpaid liens: $15,000.

**Net proceeds: $205,000.**

4 cotenants at 25% each:

- Base share: $51,250 each

Adjustments: Cotenant A paid $8,000 in taxes that should have been split; owed contribution of $6,000 from the other 3. A gets +$6,000, others -$2,000 each.

**Final distribution:**

| Cotenant | Base Share | Adjustment | Final |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | $51,250 | +$6,000 | $57,250 |
| B | $51,250 | -$2,000 | $49,250 |
| C | $51,250 | -$2,000 | $49,250 |
| D | $51,250 | -$2,000 | $49,250 |

**Total: $205,000. Balances.**

The accounting adjustment built into this example is exactly why combining contribution claims with partition in a single case is efficient. Rather than pursuing two separate lawsuits, one proceeding resolves both the forced sale and the accounting between cotenants.

## 25.12 Settlement Dynamics

Most partition cases settle. Why and how:

### Why They Settle

**Cost of continuing exceeds settlement:** Legal fees mounting on both sides. Everyone eventually wants resolution.

**Uncertainty of final sale price:** At public auction, sale price is unpredictable. Parties prefer certainty.

**Relationship preservation:** Even broken families sometimes prefer settlement to final forced sale.

**Time value:** Extended litigation delays everyone's access to funds.

### Settlement Forms

**Plaintiff Buyout:** You (the partition plaintiff) buy out the defendants' interests at an agreed price.

> "Plaintiff shall pay Defendants $[amount] in exchange for Defendants' conveyance of their interests. Upon completion, partition case is dismissed."

This is the common outcome and often your preferred result — you get the property, they get paid.

**Defendant Buyout:** Defendants buy your interest. Rare in fractured equity but possible if defendants want to keep the property.

**Third-Party Sale:** All parties agree to sell to a specific third party, divide proceeds by agreement. Avoids public auction.

**Private Auction Among Parties:** All cotenants bid against each other in a private process. Highest bidder wins and pays others.

### Settlement Timing

1. **Early settlement (pre-discovery):** Less common, but possible when parties want quick resolution
2. **Post-discovery settlement:** Most common. Parties know facts, legal positions clarified, decisions easier
3. **Mediation settlement:** Formal process with neutral mediator. Often productive
4. **Pre-trial settlement:** Common. Trial risks become more apparent
5. **Post-judgment settlement:** Even after partition is ordered, parties can agree on how it executes

### Your Settlement Positioning

**Opening position:** Often higher than your true goal. Gives negotiating room.

**Best alternative to negotiated agreement:** What happens if you don't settle? Forced sale at auction. Sometimes this favors you (you can bid); sometimes it doesn't (auction prices may be below market).

**Bottom line:** What's the minimum acceptable outcome? Be clear with yourself before settlement discussions begin.

**Trade-offs:** Money vs. speed. Certainty vs. potential upside. Relationship considerations.

The settlement dynamic in partition is structurally different from voluntary negotiation. In voluntary negotiation, the holdout heir has leverage — they can refuse indefinitely. In partition litigation, time and costs work against both sides roughly equally, and the endpoint (forced sale) is certain. That changes the negotiation math. Defendants who would never engage during voluntary outreach become motivated negotiators when their own attorney starts billing.

## 25.13 The Homestead Complication

Texas homestead law affects partition in specific situations.

### The Homestead Principle

Texas homestead law provides strong protections for owner-occupied residential property, including limits on forced sale for debts. But those protections were designed against creditors — not against other co-owners exercising partition rights.

### Does Homestead Prevent Partition?

Generally, no. Partition among cotenants is not the same as creditor forced sale. Even homestead property can be partitioned among cotenants. The courts have consistently held that the homestead protection doesn't immunize a cotenant from the partition rights of other cotenants.

### Surviving Spouse's Homestead Right

Under Texas Constitution Article XVI § 52 and related provisions, a surviving spouse may have homestead rights in property owned by the decedent and heirs. These rights can limit partition during the spouse's lifetime. This is a real complication in estate scenarios where the decedent's surviving spouse is still living in the property and asserting homestead rights.

### Minor Children's Homestead Rights

Minor children of the decedent may have homestead rights that affect partition. These rights are taken seriously by Texas courts and may delay or condition partition in specific circumstances.

### Current Occupant Issues

If one cotenant is living in the property as their homestead, partition complicates their housing situation. Courts may take this into account in the timing and terms of the partition order, though they generally will not deny partition outright.

### What This Means for Operators

1. Research homestead claims before filing partition
2. Expect homestead-based defenses in cases where a family member occupies the property
3. Some properties require specific strategies around homestead — phased timing, structured settlements that account for the occupant's relocation needs
4. Attorney review is essential when homestead complications are present — this is not an area to navigate without experienced counsel

## 25.14 Partition Involving Estates

When the property is still in a decedent's estate, partition interacts with probate.

### If Probate Is Open

1. Administrator or executor has authority over estate property
2. Partition typically waits for administration to complete
3. Or partition names the administrator as a party

### If Probate Is Closed

1. Heirs own the property directly
2. Standard partition applies
3. Each heir is a cotenant with the right to be served and participate

### If No Probate Has Been Opened

1. Heirs still own via statutory succession
2. Partition can proceed with heirs as parties
3. Affidavit of Heirship may be needed to establish interests before filing

### Interactions

1. Partition and probate can run in parallel
2. Sometimes probate administration is more efficient — an administrator with authority to sell may be able to act without litigation
3. Administrator may have authority to sell without partition
4. Complex estates may require multiple proceedings

The estate-partition interaction is common in fractured equity because the whole space exists where decedents died without clear administration. Know whether probate is open or closed, who the administrator is if one exists, and whether the administrator has independent authority to sell. Sometimes a quick call to the estate's attorney reveals that partition isn't necessary — the administrator will sell voluntarily.

## 25.15 Counterclaims and Cross-Claims

Defendants sometimes add their own claims.

### Common Defendant Claims

**Contribution claims against plaintiff:** Defendant claims you owe them contribution for expenses they've paid.

**Accounting claims:** Defendant wants an accounting of rents or other income collected from the property.

**Ouster claims:** Defendant claims they were excluded from property use — that you denied them access or enjoyment.

**Quiet title claims:** Defendant wants specific title issues resolved in their favor before partition proceeds.

**Partition counter-petition:** Sometimes a defendant files their own partition requesting different terms or a different method.

**Waste claims:** Claims that you or others caused property damage that diminished value.

### How to Handle

1. Your attorney manages these procedurally. Do not try to handle them without counsel.
2. **Combine if possible:** Better to resolve all claims in one case than have separate proceedings.
3. **Separate if necessary:** If counterclaims are too complex or factually distinct, they may be severed to separate cases.

### Settlement Implications

Counterclaims affect settlement dynamics. Sometimes you can settle combined claims more efficiently than separately — a global settlement that resolves partition, contribution, and accounting in a single payment is often cleaner than three separate negotiations. Sometimes the opposite — a defendant's counterclaim muddies the partition and makes settlement harder to structure.

## 25.16 Practical Example — A Partition Case Through to Completion

Let me walk through a complete partition case.

### The Property

3-bedroom home in San Antonio. ARV $235,000. 6 cotenants (heirs of decedent who died 2018).

### Your Position

You've acquired 4 of 6 heir interests through voluntary purchases. You own 4/6 = 66.67% interest. Remaining 2 heirs (brothers) won't engage despite multiple attempts over 14 months.

### Decision to File

Deal economics support partition costs. Property values are rising — further delay is costly. Voluntary options exhausted.

### Attorney Consultation

Attorney confirms:
- Clear ownership structure
- Partition available under § 23.001
- Partition by sale likely (single-family home can't be physically divided)
- Typical costs $15,000–$25,000
- Typical timeline 10–14 months

Attorney retainer: $7,500. Additional fees billed monthly.

### Filing the Case

Bexar County District Court.

- Plaintiff: You
- Defendants: The two non-cooperating brothers
- Requested: Partition by sale, distribution per interests

Filing fee: $400.

### Service

Both brothers served within 3 weeks.

### Initial Responses (Month 2)

Brother 1 hires attorney, files answer. No counterclaims. Contests value, seeks delay.

Brother 2 defaults (doesn't respond).

### Discovery (Months 3–7)

- Appraisal ordered: $232,000 (confirms your ARV estimate)
- Written discovery exchanged with Brother 1
- Deposition of Brother 1

Brother 1's position emerges: wants a buyout at a higher price than you've offered.

### Settlement Discussions (Month 8)

Brother 1's attorney opens at $40,000 for his 1/6 interest ($232,000 × 16.67% = $38,667 "mathematical" price).

Your position: $22,000 (considering costs, uncertainty, and typical partition discount).

Negotiation over 60 days. Settlement at $30,000 for his interest.

Brother 2 remains in default.

### Default Judgment on Brother 2 (Month 9)

Court enters default against Brother 2. His 1/6 interest remains in the partition case but he forfeits his right to contest.

### Settlement Documentation (Month 10)

1. Brother 1 conveys his interest via Release of Interest for $30,000
2. Motion to dismiss partition against him
3. Continuing partition against Brother 2

### Resolution of Brother 2's Interest (Months 10–12)

Options:
1. Proceed to partition sale (court-ordered auction)
2. Make final offer to Brother 2 for late settlement

You authorize attorney to make a final offer: $18,000 for his interest.

Brother 2 appears through attorney. Accepts offer.

Both interests acquired.

### Final Outcome

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Brother 1 settlement | $30,000 |
| Brother 2 settlement | $18,000 |
| Legal costs (partition) | $18,500 |
| Timeline from filing | 12 months |

### Deal Economics

| Component | Amount |
|---|---|
| First 4 heirs (voluntary) | ~$85,000 |
| Last 2 heirs (partition leverage) | $48,000 |
| Legal costs (partition) | $18,500 |
| Total acquisition | ~$155,000 |
| Property ARV | $232,000 |
| Net potential (after rehab/resale) | ~$55,000–$65,000 |

### Strategic Assessment

Without partition, the deal was stuck at 66.67% ownership. Partition unlocked the remaining interests. The cost was substantial but the deal economics absorbed it. Net result: successful acquisition of a property that otherwise would have been lost to continued inaction.

Note what actually happened here: partition never reached the courthouse steps for an auction. The threat of the forced sale — and the ongoing legal costs for both sides — drove both brothers to settle. Brother 1 settled at month 8 after discovery clarified his position. Brother 2 settled at month 12 when he finally appeared through counsel. The auction was the gun on the table. It didn't fire, but everyone knew it would.

## 25.17 When Partition Fails

Sometimes partition doesn't work as planned.

### Failure Mode 1: You Lose the Case

Rare but possible. Title issues you didn't anticipate. Ownership disputes going against you. Procedural failures.

**Response:**
- Understand why you lost (sometimes appealable)
- Adjust strategy for remaining acquisition paths
- Reduce losses and move on

### Failure Mode 2: Settlement Values Exceed Your Economics

Cotenants demand more than the deal supports. Settlement at the demanded level destroys your profit.

**Response:**
- Proceed to auction if feasible
- Accept sale at auction price even if lower than voluntary settlement
- Sometimes auction results favor you — outside bidders compete, and market price emerges from the auction itself

### Failure Mode 3: Forced Sale Price Too Low

Public auction yields a poor price. You receive less than expected for your interest.

**Response:**
- Bid yourself to ensure a fair price — use your credit advantage
- Consider better property preparation before auction to attract bidders
- Consider private sale alternative with court approval

### Failure Mode 4: Extended Timeline

Case drags beyond the expected timeline. Costs mount. Opportunity cost increases.

**Response:**
- Push your attorney to efficient case management — motion discipline, discovery compliance, settlement pressure
- Consider settling sooner to reduce costs even if terms are slightly worse
- Accept sunk costs and focus on outcome rather than what you've already spent

### Failure Mode 5: New Complications Emerge

Additional heirs surface. New liens discovered. Property damage occurs. Any number of unexpected issues arise during litigation.

**Response:**
- Adjust case strategy
- Possibly amend petition
- Settle if new facts change the economics — a deal that made sense before a $30,000 lien discovery may not make sense after

## 25.18 Key Takeaways for Operators

Before moving to Chapter 26 (Creditor-Suit Pipeline), internalize these principles:

**Partition is the most important leverage tool.** Master it or leave money on the table.

**Legal foundation is Texas Property Code Chapter 23.** Any cotenant has the right to compel partition. No minimum interest. No excuse needed.

**Two forms exist:** partition in kind (physical division) and partition by sale. Most fractured equity cases use partition by sale.

**Partition by sale is the operator's friend.** You can bid using your existing interest as credit, gaining advantage over outside bidders.

**Setup requires thorough preparation.** Document ownership, identify all cotenants, assess property, prepare accounting claims.

**The petition must include** all parties, property description, ownership statements, factual allegations, and specific requests for relief.

**Service must be proper.** All defendants served, publication for missing defendants.

**Discovery follows if defendants appear.** Written discovery, depositions, documents.

**Commissioner process** applies to partition in kind cases. Three appointed persons propose division.

**Partition by sale generates proceeds** that distribute per interests, adjusted for accounting claims.

**Most cases settle.** Plaintiff buyout, defendant buyout, third-party sale, or private auction are common outcomes. The lawsuit is the forcing function for the settlement.

**Homestead complications exist.** Research before filing. Attorney review essential.

**Estates interact with partition.** Open probate may require administrator involvement. Closed estates allow standard partition.

**Counterclaims are common.** Your attorney manages. Combine or separate as efficient.

**Bidding strategy at sale uses your interest credit.** Major advantage over outside bidders.

**Partition can fail.** Understand failure modes. Adjust strategy. Focus on outcomes, not sunk costs.

**Timeline is 8–24 months.** Plan capital and attention accordingly.

**Costs are $12,000–$35,000.** Deal economics must absorb this in your underwriting — not as a surprise after filing.
