## 18.1 Why This Chapter Changes Everything

Of all the techniques in this Codex, the Administrator/Executor Single-Signature Shortcut is the one that most dramatically changes the economics and timeline of a deal.

In a normal fractured equity deal, you negotiate with every heir individually. Six heirs means six conversations, six signatures, six notaries, six payments, six Releases of Interest. If any one of those heirs gets cold feet, lawyers up, moves, dies, or just stops returning calls, the deal is stuck.

In a single-signature deal, you negotiate with one person — the appointed administrator or executor of the estate — who has the legal authority to convey the entire estate's interest in the property. One conversation. One signature. One notary. One payment. One deed.

The economics transform:

- **Timeline:** 3-6 months instead of 6-12 months
- **Legal costs:** $3,000-$6,000 instead of $8,000-$15,000
- **Heir payout structure:** One check from the estate, distributed by the administrator rather than you chasing 6-15 separate payments
- **Risk:** Single point of failure instead of coordinating multiple signers
- **Title:** Cleaner chain because estate conveys directly rather than collecting fragments

Not every deal qualifies for this shortcut. The conditions have to be right. But when they are, it transforms the business. An operator who closes two complex multi-heir deals per year can close five or six single-signature deals in the same period with the same capital.

This chapter teaches you how to identify when this shortcut is available, how to structure the transaction, how to approach the administrator or executor, what protective provisions you need, and how to handle the specific procedural requirements.

## 18.2 The Legal Foundation

Before diving into technique, understand the legal foundation.

Texas Estates Code § 401.001 et seq. governs independent administration of estates. Under independent administration, the personal representative (executor for testate estates, administrator for intestate) has broad authority to act on behalf of the estate without ongoing court supervision.

Specifically, **Texas Estates Code § 402.051** grants the independent executor or administrator the power to:

1. Sell real and personal property of the estate
2. Make contracts on behalf of the estate
3. Pay debts
4. Distribute property to beneficiaries or heirs
5. Take any other action that a court-supervised representative could take with court approval

This means: when properly appointed, an independent administrator or executor can convey estate property to you through a single signature, without requiring any of the individual heirs to sign.

### Supporting Statutes

1. **Texas Estates Code § 402.052** — independent representatives may sell estate property without court order
2. **Texas Estates Code § 403.001** — specific powers regarding real property
3. **Texas Estates Code § 101.003** — the estate of a decedent vests in the heirs or beneficiaries at the moment of death, subject to the powers of the personal representative during administration

### The Key Concept

During independent administration, the personal representative holds the authority to convey property that the estate owns. The heirs/beneficiaries have equitable interests but don't need to sign for the representative's conveyance to be valid. This is what makes the single-signature shortcut possible.

## 18.3 When This Shortcut Is Available

The single-signature shortcut requires one of these conditions:

### Condition 1: An Independent Administration Is Already Open

If the estate has been opened for independent administration (with an executor or administrator already appointed by a probate court), and the administration is still active, the personal representative can convey without additional heir involvement.

This is the ideal scenario. The heavy lifting — probate filing, appointment, court supervision — was done by the family or a prior attorney. You just need to identify the personal representative and engage them.

### Condition 2: An Estate Can Be Opened for Independent Administration

If no probate has been filed but the legal structure allows it, a probate case can be opened specifically for the purpose of this transaction. This requires:

1. A willing heir (or creditor) to petition for appointment
2. Court approval
3. All other heirs to consent (for intestate estates)
4. A will directing independent administration (for testate estates)

This path takes 2-6 months and costs $3,000-$8,000 in legal fees but still saves substantial time and money compared to individual heir acquisitions.

### Condition 3: A Court Order Grants Specific Authority

In some cases, the court can authorize a specific transaction without opening a full administration. This is less common but exists in some probate fact patterns.

### When the Shortcut Is NOT Available

1. The family refuses to cooperate in opening an administration
2. Heirs refuse to consent to independent administration
3. The court requires dependent administration (contested estates, minor heirs without guardian consent, etc.)
4. No will directs independent administration and heirs can't agree
5. The property was never in a decedent's estate (e.g., held in an LLC)

In these cases, you work with heirs individually under the Chapter 16-17 methodology.

## 18.4 Identifying the Opportunity — The First Signals

How do you know early whether a deal might support single-signature?

### Strong Signals (Likely Qualifies)

1. Probate case exists in court records for the decedent
2. "Independent executor" or "independent administrator" named
3. Estate is actively being administered (documents being filed)
4. Heirs mention "the executor" or "the administrator" when you talk to them
5. Will was filed and admitted to probate with independent executor named
6. Attorney of record is handling estate matters

### Moderate Signals (May Qualify)

1. Heirs all cooperative but no administration opened
2. Will exists but hasn't been probated
3. Large estate where administration makes sense
4. Multiple properties where consolidated administration is efficient

### Weak or Negative Signals

1. Estate openly contested
2. Heirs won't talk to each other
3. Dependent administration required
4. Administration closed years ago
5. Will contests pending

### Research Techniques

- **Check probate court records** for the decedent
- **Search for filed wills** in the county where decedent died
- **Ask heirs directly** during first calls whether there's an executor or administrator
- **Check attorney filings** — attorneys handling estate matters often file appearances
- **Talk to the Probate Clerk** — they can tell you whether a case is open

## 18.5 Scenario A — Administration Already Open

When there's an active independent administration, your path is clear:

### Step 1: Confirm the Representative's Authority

Pull the probate court records:

1. Order appointing the representative
2. Letters Testamentary (for executors) or Letters of Administration (for administrators)
3. Will (if any) with powers conferred
4. Any court orders restricting authority

You need to verify:

1. The person you're dealing with is actually the appointed representative
2. Their authority hasn't been revoked
3. The administration is still open (not closed)
4. The property in question is within the administration

### Step 2: Contact the Representative

Make first contact similarly to how you'd contact an heir (Chapter 16 methodology), but with different framing:

> "Hi [name], my name is [your name] from [company]. I'm calling because I understand you're the executor of [decedent]'s estate. I've been looking at [property address], which I believe is part of the estate. Do you have a few minutes to discuss?"

### Step 3: Build Rapport and Understand the Situation

The representative may be:

1. An heir themselves (most common — often the oldest child)
2. A trusted family member or friend
3. A professional fiduciary
4. An attorney

Each has different perspectives and incentives. An heir-representative has emotional connections to the property. A professional fiduciary is focused on efficient liquidation. Engage accordingly.

### Step 4: Present the Opportunity

> "My company acquires estates in exactly this situation. Rather than the family having to wait months or years for the estate to sell on its own, we can close on the property in 60-90 days with cash. The estate gets a clean sale, the debts get paid, the heirs get their distribution, and you can close out the administration efficiently."

The key selling points for the representative:

- **Efficiency:** Their job is to close the estate. Your offer helps them do that.
- **Liquidity:** Estate needs cash for debts, taxes, distributions
- **Certainty:** No listing, no showings, no inspection contingencies
- **Speed:** Retail sales on estate property often drag for months

### Step 5: Structure the Transaction

This is a standard Purchase and Sale Agreement (Type 2 from Chapter 17) with the estate as seller. Some specific provisions:

- **Seller identification:** "[Name], as Independent Executor of the Estate of [decedent], Cause No. [probate case number]"
- **Court file reference:** Reference the probate case to establish authority
- **Representative's warranty:** Warrants their authority to sell and that administration is active
- **Estate warranty of title:** Standard real estate warranties
- **Distribution provision:** Not your concern — the estate distributes proceeds to heirs

### Step 6: Verify and Close

Before closing:

1. Title company independently verifies the representative's authority
2. Letters Testamentary or Letters of Administration are current (some jurisdictions require renewals)
3. No court orders restrict the sale
4. All required notices have been given
5. Estate creditors have been addressed

The title company issues the policy based on the representative's authority. The heirs don't sign.

### Typical Deal Economics

On a property with $200,000 equity spread and 6 heirs:

- **Traditional multi-heir approach:** $95,000 heir payouts (50% × entitlement × 6 heirs), $8,000 legal, 8-month timeline
- **Single-signature approach:** $140,000 purchase price to estate (negotiated), $3,500 legal, 3-4 month timeline

The estate approach often pays heirs slightly more in aggregate because the representative negotiates on behalf of all heirs as a group and captures some of the complexity discount. But your costs are lower, timeline is shorter, and capital velocity is dramatically better.

## 18.6 Scenario B — Opening an Administration for the Transaction

When no administration is open but the legal structure allows opening one, this becomes a multi-step transaction.

### Step 1: Identify a Willing Petitioner

Someone has to petition for appointment. Usually an heir, occasionally a creditor (see Part V). The petitioner becomes the prospective administrator or executor.

Who makes a good petitioner:

1. An heir with good relationships with other heirs
2. Someone local to the court
3. Someone willing to serve
4. Someone without conflicts (e.g., not a hostile party in family disputes)

### Step 2: Get Heir Consent for Independent Administration

For intestate estates, all heirs must consent to independent administration. This requires:

1. Signed consent forms from each heir
2. Each heir affirming the proposed administrator
3. Waiver of bond if applicable

This is where the single-signature shortcut has a hybrid element — you still need initial consent from heirs, but only for the administrative appointment, not for each individual property conveyance.

### Step 3: File the Application

Your attorney files in probate court:

1. Application for Letters of Administration (intestate) or Probate of Will and Letters Testamentary (testate)
2. Heir consent forms
3. Proof of publication (if required)
4. Bond filing or waiver

### Step 4: Attend the Hearing

Usually brief. Court reviews the application, swears in the proposed representative, issues Letters.

### Step 5: Receive Letters

Letters Testamentary or Letters of Administration are issued. This is the document that proves the representative's authority. Multiple certified copies are needed — one for each institution that will need proof (title company, county clerk, etc.).

### Step 6: Execute the Transaction

Purchase and Sale Agreement signed by the representative. Conveyance by deed from the estate. Title company closes.

### Typical Timeline

| Phase | Weeks |
|---|---|
| Identify petitioner, obtain heir consents | 1-4 |
| Attorney prepares and files application | 4-8 |
| Hearing held, Letters issued | 8-12 |
| Purchase agreement signed, title work, closing | 12-16 |

Total: 3-4 months when everything goes smoothly.

### Typical Costs

| Item | Cost |
|---|---|
| Attorney fees for probate | $3,500-$6,500 |
| Court filing fees | $500-$1,000 |
| Bond premium (if required) | varies |
| **Total** | **$4,500-$8,500** |

Compared to the alternative of chasing 6-15 individual heir signatures, this is often cheaper and faster.

## 18.7 Structuring the Representative's Compensation

One critical element: how does the representative get paid?

### Under Texas Law

Independent administrators and executors are entitled to reasonable compensation for their services, typically 5% of amounts received and paid out by the estate (Texas Estates Code § 352.002 and related provisions). In testate estates, the will may specify different compensation.

### In the Deal Context

When you're acquiring from an estate, you're typically not paying the representative's compensation directly. Their compensation comes from the estate's assets — meaning, from the proceeds of the sale to you.

### But Here's Where It Gets Interesting

If the representative is also an heir (very common), they have a dual interest:

1. As heir, they want a distribution
2. As representative, they earn compensation for administering

Smart deal structuring recognizes this:

1. The purchase price to the estate gets split between creditor payments, administrator compensation, and heir distributions
2. The representative has an incentive to maximize the overall proceeds (benefits all heirs) AND to close efficiently (benefits the administration they're managing)
3. Your offer addresses both

### Negotiating with the Representative

> "The estate needs cash to pay debts, pay administrative costs including your compensation, and distribute what's left to the heirs. My offer is designed to cover all of that with enough left for a meaningful distribution. Let me walk through the math with you..."

Show the representative:

1. Your purchase price
2. Their compensation as representative
3. Estimated debt and administrative costs
4. Remaining amount available for heir distribution
5. What each heir would receive

This transparency builds trust and helps the representative sell the deal to the other heirs.

## 18.8 The Hybrid Deal — Administrator Plus Some Heir Cooperation

Not every single-signature deal is purely single-signature. Sometimes you structure as:

**"Administrator-Led with Heir Confirmations"**

1. Administrator executes the main purchase agreement
2. Heirs sign individual consent forms or acknowledgments
3. Title company treats administrator's signature as primary authority
4. Heir consents provide additional assurance

### When to Use

1. Title underwriter requires additional heir consent
2. Some heirs have special concerns that administrator wants documented
3. Estate has complications that benefit from individual heir acknowledgment
4. Risk management for edge cases

### The Structure

The purchase agreement is between you and the estate, signed by the administrator. Additionally, each heir signs a "Consent and Ratification" document that confirms:

1. They acknowledge the administrator's authority
2. They consent to the sale
3. They release any claims against the property that would survive the administrator's conveyance

This hybrid approach:

1. Maintains the efficiency of single-signature
2. Adds belt-and-suspenders protection
3. Satisfies conservative title underwriters
4. Documents heir awareness and consent

## 18.9 When the Representative Is the Problem

Sometimes the administrator or executor is uncooperative, incompetent, or self-dealing. The shortcut breaks down.

### Common Problems

**The uncooperative representative:** Representative refuses to engage, refuses to sell, or demands unreasonable terms. Sometimes this is strategic (they want the property for themselves). Sometimes it's dysfunctional (they don't know what to do).

**The incompetent representative:** Representative doesn't understand their role, doesn't respond timely, can't make decisions. The administration stalls.

**The self-dealing representative:** Representative is trying to buy the property from the estate themselves, or arrange a sweetheart deal with a friend, or otherwise benefit personally at the expense of other heirs.

**The absent representative:** Representative was appointed but has stopped functioning — moved, become unable to act, or just disengaged.

### Your Options

**Option 1: Work around the representative by going to the heirs directly.** Return to individual heir acquisition. Slower, more expensive, but bypasses the stuck representative.

**Option 2: Petition for removal.** Under Texas Estates Code § 404.003 and related provisions, a representative can be removed for cause. This requires filing a petition in probate court. Requires time (2-4 months) and cost ($3,000-$8,000).

**Option 3: Petition for a new representative.** If the current representative resigns or is removed, a new one can be appointed. You work with the new representative.

**Option 4: Walk away.** Sometimes the juice isn't worth the squeeze. If the deal economics don't support the extra work, pass.

### When Self-Dealing Is Suspected

If you believe the representative is trying to acquire the property for themselves below fair value, you may have standing as an interested party to object. Consult your attorney. Heir-beneficiaries have stronger standing, but an outside operator with a demonstrated interest can sometimes intervene.

## 18.10 Title Company Considerations for Single-Signature Deals

Title underwriters treat single-signature estate deals specifically. Know what they're looking at.

### Documents the Title Company Will Need

1. Certified copies of Letters Testamentary or Letters of Administration (current, usually within 60 days)
2. Probate court order appointing the representative
3. Will (if testate) with independent executor language
4. Heir consent forms (if required)
5. Publication notices (if required)
6. Creditor notice affidavits
7. Administrator's or executor's deed (standard form with specific authority recitations)

### What Underwriters Verify

1. Appointment is valid and current
2. Authority extends to the specific property being conveyed
3. All required notices have been given
4. No pending challenges to the administration
5. Statutory waiting periods have run where applicable
6. All statutory requirements for independent administration have been met

### Specific Concerns Underwriters May Raise

**Administration too young:** Some underwriters want at least 6 months since appointment before they'll insure an estate sale (to allow creditor claim period to run)

**Contested administration:** If challenges are pending, underwriters will typically decline coverage

**Specific bequest issues:** If the will has specific bequests naming the property, the representative's ability to sell may be limited

**Bond status:** Some underwriters want to see current bond in place (if bond was required)

### Indemnification and Holdback

For some estate deals, title underwriters may require:

1. An indemnification from the estate against creditor claims
2. A cash holdback at closing until the statutory creditor period runs
3. Additional documentation beyond standard estate sales

These are negotiable with the title company but should be understood before closing.

## 18.11 The Representative's Liability Concerns

The administrator or executor has personal liability exposure if they do the deal wrong. Understanding their concerns helps you address them.

### What the Representative Worries About

1. Getting sued by an heir who claims the sale was too low
2. Getting sued by creditors who claim the sale harmed their recovery
3. Being personally liable for estate debts that weren't properly paid
4. Fiduciary duty violations
5. Tax liability for the estate

### How to Address These Concerns

**Documented fair value:** Provide the representative with documentation supporting that your offer is fair: comparable sales analysis, appraisal or broker's opinion, condition report if applicable, market analysis. This protects the representative against claims that they undersold.

**Court approval option:** In some cases, even under independent administration, the representative can petition the court for approval of a specific transaction. This gives them additional protection. Your attorney can help structure this if needed.

**Professional advisor documentation:** Encourage the representative to work with their own attorney and/or accountant. Don't try to save them this cost — it reinforces the fiduciary process and gives them additional cover.

**Creditor notification compliance:** Make sure statutory notice requirements have been met before closing. This is typically the administrator's job but affects your deal.

### Addressing Representative's Concerns in the Contract

Your purchase agreement should include:

1. Acknowledgment that the representative is acting in their fiduciary capacity
2. Warranties by the representative only as to their authority, not title warranties that require the estate to defend
3. Indemnification from the estate to the representative for the sale (separate from indemnification to you)
4. No personal liability language protecting the representative from personal claims

## 18.12 When the Representative Is Also an Heir

The most common single-signature scenario involves a representative who is also an heir. This is usually the oldest child acting as administrator or executor.

### Benefits

1. They have personal interest in maximizing estate value (benefits them as heir)
2. They usually have relationships with other heirs
3. They may have direct knowledge of the property and family history
4. They're motivated to close the estate efficiently

### Complications

1. Dual fiduciary-beneficiary role requires care to avoid conflicts
2. Other heirs may suspect self-dealing
3. Representative may have emotional attachment to the property
4. Family dynamics can complicate representative's judgment

### Best Practices

1. Make the pricing transparent to all heirs (through the representative)
2. Document the fair market value analysis
3. Have heirs acknowledge the sale (even if not required)
4. Keep the transaction arms-length
5. Don't engage in side deals with the representative that benefit them over other heirs

### The Acknowledgment Approach

Even when not strictly required, having all heirs sign an acknowledgment that they're aware of the sale, its price, and the distribution they'll receive is a powerful risk-management tool. It prevents post-sale challenges from heirs claiming they weren't informed.

## 18.13 Single-Signature for Multi-Property Packages

When an estate has multiple properties (Chapter 15), the single-signature shortcut scales beautifully.

### The Compounding Benefit

1. One administrator handles the sale of all properties
2. One probate administration covers all transactions
3. Shared legal costs across multiple properties
4. Faster closing of the entire package

### Structural Options

**Option 1: Single purchase agreement for all properties.** One document covering all estate properties, signed by the administrator, with the estate conveying all properties to you.

**Option 2: Separate agreements per property.** Each property has its own agreement, all signed by the administrator. Useful when properties have different conditions, values, or complications.

**Option 3: Sequenced closings.** Agreements signed simultaneously but closings staged over weeks or months. Manages cash flow and allows orderly marketing of properties you'll resell.

### Typical Economics for a 4-Property Estate Package Under Single-Signature

| Factor | Single-Signature | Multi-Heir |
|---|---|---|
| Representatives negotiated with | 1 | 4-8 heirs |
| Total legal costs | $5,000-$8,000 | $12,000-$25,000 |
| Timeline | 4-6 months | 8-12 months |
| Heir distribution | After all closings, distributed once | Staggered per property |

This is how some operators scale to $500K+ annual net profit on modest capital — the efficiency of single-signature combined with the volume of packages.

## 18.14 Practical Example — A Complete Single-Signature Deal

Let me walk through a complete single-signature deal from start to finish.

### Background

**Decedent:** Thomas Henderson, died testate 2019, Bexar County. Will named his daughter Sarah as independent executor. Estate includes:

1. Primary residence: $245,000 ARV
2. Second property (rental): $180,000 ARV
3. Vehicle and personal property

Will divides estate equally among 3 children. Administration is active (opened in 2020).

### How You Found It

Tax delinquency list flagged the properties (2-year delinquency). Research revealed the probate case. Court records identified Sarah Henderson as Independent Executor.

### Initial Contact

Your first call to Sarah:

> "Hi Sarah, my name is Charles from REI Labs. I'm calling because I'm looking at a couple of properties that appear to be part of your father Thomas's estate. I understand you're serving as the executor. Do you have a few minutes to discuss?"

Sarah engages. She explains the family has been struggling to sell the properties, they don't have capital to pay the back taxes, and she's been stalled. She's interested in hearing about an efficient path to close.

### The Initial Conversation Covers

1. Confirmation of the probate case and her authority
2. The property conditions and current market
3. Estate debts and obligations
4. The heirs' preferences
5. Timeline constraints

### The Offer Structuring

You underwrite the deal (Chapter 14 methodology):

**Property 1 (residence):**

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| ARV | $245,000 |
| Repair needs | $15,000 |
| Equity spread | $185,000 |
| Proposed purchase price | $135,000 |

**Property 2 (rental):**

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| ARV | $180,000 |
| Repair needs | $8,000 |
| Equity spread | $140,000 |
| Proposed purchase price | $98,000 |

**Total package offer: $233,000**

### The Representative's Calculation

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Purchase price | $233,000 |
| Less delinquent taxes | ($23,000) |
| Less estate administrative costs (including executor compensation) | ($14,000) |
| Less creditor claims | ($8,000) |
| **Available for heir distribution** | **$188,000** |
| Per heir (3 heirs) | $62,667 |

### Sarah's Perspective

> "That's more than we've been able to get doing it ourselves. Let me talk to my siblings."

### The Agreement

Single Purchase and Sale Agreement covering both properties. Signed by:

1. Sarah Henderson, in her capacity as Independent Executor of the Estate of Thomas Henderson
2. You, as buyer

Executed with standard option period (30 days), contingencies, and closing timeline.

### Parallel Acknowledgments

The other two heirs (Sarah's siblings) sign Consent and Acknowledgment documents:

1. Acknowledging the sale
2. Confirming their understanding of the price and their expected distribution
3. Ratifying Sarah's authority to sell

This isn't strictly required under independent administration, but provides protection.

### Title Work

Your title company:

## 18.15 Key Takeaways for Operators

Before moving to Chapter 19, internalize these principles:

**The single-signature shortcut transforms deal economics.** Faster timeline, lower cost, cleaner title when conditions are right.

**Three scenarios trigger the shortcut:** existing independent administration, openable administration, or specific court authorization.

**Identify the opportunity early.** Check probate court records for every deal. Many more deals qualify than operators realize.

**Legal foundation is Texas Estates Code § 401-402.** Independent representatives have broad conveyance authority.

**When administration exists, engage the representative directly.** Verify authority, build rapport, structure the transaction as standard purchase agreement with estate as seller.

**When administration doesn't exist but could be opened, consider the path.** 2-4 months and $4,500-$8,500 legal costs often saves more than it costs.

**Representative compensation typically comes from proceeds, not from you.** Understand the full distribution math.

**Hybrid approaches — representative plus heir confirmations — satisfy conservative underwriters.**

**Representative problems require specific responses.** Uncooperative, incompetent, self-dealing, or absent representatives each have different handling paths.

**Title companies have specific requirements for estate sales.** Anticipate and meet them.

**Protect the representative from fiduciary liability concerns.** Fair value documentation, court approval where applicable, professional advisor involvement.

**Representative-who-is-also-heir is most common.** Manage the dual role carefully.

**Multi-property packages under single-signature are the most efficient large deals possible.**
