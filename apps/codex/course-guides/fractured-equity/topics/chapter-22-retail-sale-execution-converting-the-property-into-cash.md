## 22.1 The Final Phase

You've acquired the property. You've secured it. You've handled the occupants, managed the utilities, paid the taxes, and done whatever rehab you planned to do. Now comes the phase that determines your actual profit: selling it.

This chapter covers **retail sale execution** — selling to an end buyer (typically a homeowner or small investor) for the highest price the market will bear. This is different from wholesaling (selling as-is to another investor, usually below market) or holding as rental (keeping for cash flow).

Retail sale is where most fractured equity operators make their largest profits. A property acquired for $140,000 with $20,000 in rehab sells retail for $235,000. Net profit after selling costs: $55,000-$65,000. Same property wholesaled as-is might have netted $15,000.

But retail sale of a fractured-equity-acquired property carries unique challenges:

1. Your buyer's title company will scrutinize your acquisition chain
2. Schedule C issues from your purchase may surface again
3. Disclosure obligations apply to defects you discovered
4. Standard forms assume normal ownership history you don't have
5. Buyers' lenders have their own title requirements

This chapter teaches you how to navigate these challenges. Listing strategy. Market preparation. Showing logistics. Contract negotiation. Title issues specific to fractured equity resales. Closing as seller. Handling the surprises that come up when you're the one trying to convey clean title.

An operator who closes retail cleanly converts maximum equity to cash. An operator who stumbles at retail can lose deals at the finish line.

## 22.2 Pre-Listing Preparation

Before the property hits the market, extensive preparation makes the difference between sitting and selling.

### Basic Clean and Prep

1. Professional deep clean ($300-$800)
2. Yard/landscaping cleanup ($200-$500)
3. Remove all personal property and debris
4. Interior paint touch-ups or full paint
5. Fresh caulk in bathrooms and kitchen
6. Replace burned-out bulbs
7. Ensure all appliances functional

### Rehab Items (Depending on Strategy)

1. Flooring refresh or replacement
2. Kitchen update (paint cabinets minimum, new countertops if budget allows)
3. Bathroom update (paint, new hardware, regrout)
4. HVAC serviced
5. Plumbing leaks fixed
6. Electrical issues addressed
7. Exterior paint or pressure wash

### Staging

For higher-end properties or slow markets, staging can accelerate sale:

1. Furniture rental for key rooms ($2,000-$5,000 for 3 months)
2. Professional staging ($3,000-$7,000 for multi-room)
3. Virtual staging ($100-$300 per room for listing photos)

For most fractured equity flips in middle markets, minimal staging is sufficient. Empty and clean often sells fine.

### Photography

Professional listing photos pay for themselves:

1. Standard real estate photography ($150-$300)
2. Drone/aerial for properties with land ($200-$400)
3. Twilight shots for specific properties ($300-$500)

Bad listing photos kill sales. Good photos drive showings.

### Pre-Listing Inspection (Optional)

Some operators get pre-listing inspections to identify issues before buyer inspections:

1. Typical cost: $400-$600
2. Reveals problems you didn't know about
3. Allows proactive repair or disclosure
4. Sometimes provided to buyers as reassurance

Worth it for mid-to-high-end properties. Overkill for quick flips.

## 22.3 Pricing Strategy

Pricing the property correctly drives speed and profit.

### Market Analysis — Comparable Sales (Comps)

1. Properties sold in last 90 days
2. Within 1/2 mile when possible
3. Similar size (within 15%)
4. Similar condition
5. Similar features
6. Adjust for differences

### Active Listings

1. Your competition
2. Days on market tells you pricing accuracy
3. Price reductions indicate overpricing
4. Withdrawn listings indicate market rejection

### Pending Sales

1. Most current market signal
2. Contracts signed but not yet closed
3. Shows what buyers are actually paying now

### Pricing Approaches

**Price at Market.** List at fair market value based on comps. Expected result: 30-60 days on market (average), multiple showings, 1-3 offers, close at or near list price. Best for: stable markets, when you need predictable timeline.

**Price Below Market.** List 3-5% below market value. Expected result: faster showings, multiple offers (often), potential bidding war, close at or above list price. Best for: hot markets, quick exit needed, attention-getting strategy.

**Price Above Market.** List 3-5% above market value. Expected result: slower initial activity, fewer showings, price reduction after 30-45 days, close below original list but possibly near market. Best for: unique properties, patient capital, test of market.

**Price Strategically.** Price at psychological breakpoints: $199,900 instead of $205,000 (more search hits); $249,000 instead of $252,000 (cleaner number); $299,000 instead of $305,000 (separates from $300K+ search). Online search breakpoints affect buyer visibility significantly.

### Calculating Your Floor

Know your walk-away price:

1. All-in cost (purchase + rehab + holding + selling costs)
2. Minimum acceptable profit
3. Floor = all-in + minimum profit

Don't accept offers below your floor without careful consideration of carrying costs.

## 22.4 Listing Agent vs. FSBO

You can list with an agent or sell yourself.

### Listing With an Agent — Advantages

1. MLS exposure (largest buyer pool)
2. Professional marketing
3. Handles showings
4. Manages offers and negotiations
5. Disclosure guidance
6. Closing coordination

### Listing With an Agent — Disadvantages

1. Commission costs (typically 5-6% total, split between listing and buyer's agents)
2. On $240,000 sale: $12,000-$14,400 in commissions
3. Less control over process
4. Agent's interests not always aligned with yours

### Finding the Right Agent

Look for an agent who is investor-friendly (works with flippers regularly), familiar with fractured equity acquisitions, has a track record in your market, is willing to handle unusual title situations, and communicates well. Interview multiple agents before committing.

### For Sale By Owner (FSBO) — Advantages

1. No listing commission (save 2.5-3%)
2. Complete control
3. Direct buyer interaction
4. Can still offer buyer's agent commission

### FSBO — Disadvantages

1. You handle marketing
2. You handle showings
3. You handle negotiations
4. Smaller buyer pool (MLS restricted)
5. Legal/closing complexity you manage

### FSBO With MLS Entry Service

Hybrid approach: flat-fee MLS service ($300-$800) gets the property on MLS without a full agent. You offer buyer's agent commission (3%) and save 2.5-3% listing commission. Best for operators comfortable with the sales process who want to cut costs.

For fractured equity sales, most operators use agents for the MLS exposure. The deal complexity requires someone who knows the market well. Save FSBO for straightforward properties in hot markets where you can handle the process.

## 22.5 Marketing the Property

Beyond MLS listing, multiple marketing channels drive buyers.

**MLS** is the primary tool. Every listing agent will put the property on MLS. It syndicates to Zillow, Realtor.com, Redfin, Trulia, Homes.com, and hundreds of aggregator sites. Good listing description plus good photos is essential.

**Social Media** includes Facebook Marketplace (free, local buyers), Facebook neighborhood groups, Instagram for visual properties, and NextDoor for neighborhood buyers.

**Signage** — "For Sale" sign with agent info or your number, open house signs when applicable, directional signs to property. Physical signage catches drive-by buyers, especially in neighborhood-desired areas.

**Open Houses** — First weekend on market generates most interest. Subsequent open houses have diminishing returns. Agent can host or you can (if FSBO).

**Targeted Marketing** — Investor lists if selling to investors, cash buyer networks for quick sales, neighborhood flyers for specific areas, email lists of past buyers/prospects.

**Print and Traditional** — Usually not cost-effective for individual properties. Skip unless local market specifically requires it.

## 22.6 Showings and Buyer Qualification

### Showing Logistics

**Lockbox** means MLS agents can show without owner present (most common). **Appointment-only** means agent or owner present for each showing (more control). Lockbox is standard for vacant flip properties. Appointment-only for occupied or higher-value properties.

**Preparation for showings**: property cleaned and staged, lights on (leave on during heavy showing periods), temperature comfortable (not too hot/cold), pet-free if possible, fresh smell (not overwhelming scent products).

**Feedback collection** is how you calibrate strategy. Track common concerns (price, condition, location) and adjust based on feedback patterns.

### Showing Volume as Market Signal

| Signal | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Many showings, no offers | Pricing issue or condition issue |
| Few showings, no offers | Marketing or pricing issue |
| Many showings, multiple offers | Priced right — sell |
| Few showings, multiple offers | Priced below market |

### Buyer Qualification

**Cash buyers**: verify proof of funds. Usually fastest closings. No financing contingency. Usually investors or wealthy end-buyers.

**Financed buyers**: require pre-approval letter. Subject to lender requirements. Longer closing (30-45 days typical). Most common buyer type.

**Buyer financing types:**
- **Conventional**: standard loans, 10-20% down, good credit
- **FHA**: low down payment, property condition requirements
- **VA**: veterans, no down payment, specific requirements
- **USDA**: rural properties, income limits
- **Cash**: no financing, fastest close

Different buyer types have different implications for your closing.

## 22.7 The FHA/VA Challenge

Fractured equity properties sometimes struggle with FHA and VA financing.

FHA and VA loans have strict property condition requirements: safe and sanitary, no major defects, all systems functional, lead-based paint (pre-1978 homes) disclosure, specific safety features.

**Common fractured equity issues**: homes acquired "as is" may have code issues; deferred maintenance is common in inherited properties; older homes (pre-1978) require lead paint disclosures; HUD inspections catch issues purchasers' inspections might miss.

**Your options:**

**Option 1: Cure issues before listing.** Address all likely FHA/VA concerns in your rehab. Higher upfront cost, broader buyer pool.

**Option 2: Cure during contract period.** Accept FHA/VA offer, cure identified issues before closing. Risk: additional costs reduce profit.

**Option 3: Limit to cash/conventional.** List as cash-only or conventional financing only. Smaller buyer pool, potentially lower price.

**Option 4: Cure as appraisal requires.** Accept offer, let appraiser identify specific issues, cure those. Most efficient for unknown-condition properties.

If you know about issues, you may need to disclose. Hiding FHA/VA issues creates legal exposure.

## 22.8 The Title Commitment Challenge

Your buyer's title company will order a title commitment. What they find creates your Schedule C issues — again.

Your acquisition chain looks unusual to title examiners:

1. Affidavit of Heirship (less common on their review)
2. Multiple Releases of Interest from heirs
3. Recent probate filings
4. Administrator's deed from recently-opened estate
5. Any curative documents you recorded

Even if your purchase was insured, the new title company may raise issues.

### Common Title Company Responses

**Response 1: Accept your title without issues.** If your curative work was thorough and the records clean, the new title company may issue without problems.

**Response 2: Raise specific issues for cure.** Title company may require additional affidavits, specific heir acknowledgments, age-of-AOH verification (4+ years preferred), curative deeds for errors, releases of interests that weren't properly addressed.

**Response 3: Decline to insure.** Some title companies won't insure unusual acquisition chains. You'll have to find a different title company.

**Response 4: Insure with exceptions.** Title policy excludes specific issues from coverage. Buyer may accept or reject.

### Your Strategies

**Work with same title company.** Use the same title company for purchase and resale. They know the chain, already insured it, and will usually insure again.

**Document thoroughly.** Maintain a comprehensive file of all heir research, releases, probate documents. Share with buyer's title company as needed.

**Build title buffer.** If possible, hold property 4+ years from AOH recording before retail sale. Statutory challenge periods run, title ages cleanly.

**Use title clearing.** If title issues exist, address them before listing — quiet title actions, corrective deeds, missing heir resolution, additional AOHs if needed.

**Budget for title issues.** Expect $500-$3,000 in additional title work on retail sales from fractured equity. Budget accordingly.

## 22.9 Disclosure Obligations

As seller, you have disclosure obligations that affect what you must tell buyers.

**Texas Property Code § 5.008** requires seller to provide a Seller's Disclosure Notice for most residential sales. Includes questions about known structural issues, known system problems, known environmental issues, known legal issues, and known property history.

**What you must disclose**: known material defects, environmental hazards you know about, flood history if relevant, legal issues affecting property, deaths on property (varies by circumstance).

**What you don't have to disclose**: issues you don't know about, prior owner history unrelated to property, neighborhood perceptions, minor defects.

### The Fractured Equity Disclosure Challenge

You may know less about the property than a typical seller. You bought from heirs who may not have known details. Your disclosure can be:

*"Seller acquired property through fractured equity acquisition from heirs of deceased owner. Seller's knowledge of property history, defects, and systems is limited to conditions observed during ownership period."*

This transparent disclosure protects you while accurately conveying information.

### Specific Issues to Address

**If you know about past problems**: roof leaks (even if repaired), foundation issues (even if repaired), water intrusion, pest issues, major system problems.

**If you did renovations**: permits pulled or not pulled, contractors used, warranties on work, materials used for specific repairs.

**Deceased previous owner**: in Texas, death of former owner in the property is generally not required disclosure unless the death was violent and relates to property condition, the buyer specifically asks, or state or local rules require. When in doubt: disclose. Failure-to-disclose claims are common and expensive.

## 22.10 Negotiating Offers

Offers come in. How you handle them drives final price.

**Review initial offers carefully**: offer price, earnest money amount, closing timeline, financing type, contingencies, inclusions/exclusions, and special requests.

**Decision framework:**
- **Accept as-is**: full price or acceptable terms, clean contingencies, good buyer — accept.
- **Counter**: price off or terms need adjustment — counter with specific changes.
- **Reject**: price too low or terms unacceptable — reject and wait for better offer.

**Multiple offer situation**: notify all buyers of multiple offer situation, request best and final offers, compare total packages (not just price), select best combination of price, certainty, and terms.

**Price negotiation**: meeting in the middle is the starting point. Justify your counter with market data. Consider buyer's lender requirements. Net position matters more than list price.

**Closing date**: your preference is based on holding costs. Trade price for timing when useful.

**Contingencies:**
- Inspection contingency: expected
- Financing contingency: expected for financed buyers
- Appraisal contingency: expected
- Unusual contingencies (sale of other property, specific conditions): negotiate

**Earnest money**: higher equals more committed buyer. Standard is 1-2% of price. Can negotiate for larger earnest on a lower offer.

**Inclusions**: appliances typically included. Fixtures vs. personal property distinctions matter. Specific items (safes, chandeliers, etc.) should be specified in the contract.

## 22.11 The Option Period and Inspection

Texas real estate contracts typically include a termination option period.

Under TREC contracts, buyer pays for a "termination option" — a period during which buyer can terminate contract for any reason and get earnest money refund.

**Standard terms:**
1. Option fee: $100-$500 typically
2. Option period: 5-10 days
3. Termination right: buyer can terminate for any reason during period
4. Inspection typically occurs during this period

You want a short option period. Buyer wants a long period. Negotiate to reasonable middle ground (7 days is standard).

**During option period, buyer activities include**: property inspection, lender appraisal (sometimes starts here), additional due diligence, and negotiation of repairs or price changes. Your activities: allow reasonable access, maintain property condition, continue marketing (sometimes, depending on contract), and prepare for next phase.

### Inspection Response

After inspection, buyer typically:

**Accepts property as-is.** No further action. Proceed to closing.

**Terminates.** Contract ends, earnest money refunded.

**Requests repairs.** Common. Buyer provides list of issues, requests repairs or credit.

### Negotiation After Inspection

Your options when buyer requests repairs:

**Complete all requested repairs.** Most deals end here. Resolve common issues (plumbing leaks, electrical, HVAC).

**Refuse all repairs.** Risk termination. Only if requests are unreasonable or property is sold as-is.

**Counter with partial.** Accept critical repairs, reject cosmetic. Most common approach.

**Credit instead of repairs.** Give buyer money at closing instead of doing the work yourself.

**Negotiate price reduction.** Adjust purchase price to reflect needed repairs.

## 22.12 Appraisal Issues

For financed buyers, appraisal is often the next hurdle. The lender orders an appraisal. The appraiser inspects the property, reviews comps, and assigns value.

**Possible outcomes:**

**Appraisal at or above contract price.** No issue. Proceed to closing.

**Appraisal below contract price.** Lender won't lend more than appraised value. Your options:

- **Option 1: Buyer pays difference.** Buyer brings extra cash to cover the gap. Common in hot markets.
- **Option 2: Reduce price to appraisal.** You lower price to appraisal amount. Preserves deal, reduces profit.
- **Option 3: Split the difference.** You and buyer each absorb part. Negotiated solution.
- **Option 4: Challenge the appraisal.** Provide additional comps or information. Sometimes successful, rarely a substantial change.
- **Option 5: Walk away.** If no agreement, buyer may terminate (depending on contract terms).

**Preventing appraisal issues**: accurate pricing to market, property conditions that support value, recent comps that support price, and documented renovations.

**FHA/VA appraisals** include condition requirements. The appraisal may require specific repairs as a condition of loan approval: chipping/peeling paint (lead paint concern pre-1978), safety handrails, smoke detectors, water heater safety, electrical issues. Complete required repairs to preserve the deal.

## 22.13 Closing as Seller

Your closing as seller mirrors your closing as buyer but in reverse.

**Pre-closing tasks for you:**
1. Confirm Schedule C items from buyer's title commitment are addressed
2. Prepare final property condition
3. Gather documents needed at closing
4. Forward utility transfer information
5. Turn over keys

**Closing day — in-person closings:**
1. Meet at title company
2. Review closing statement line by line
3. Sign deed conveying property to buyer
4. Receive funds via wire or check
5. Turn over keys and garage door openers
6. Complete any final paperwork

**Remote/mail-away closings**: documents sent to you, sign with notary, return originals, funds wired when all complete.

**What you receive**: sale proceeds (net of closing costs, liens paid off, etc.), copy of deed, copy of closing statement, title company insurance (for your prior ownership).

**Closing costs as seller** in Texas:
1. Title insurance (seller typically pays in Texas)
2. Escrow fees
3. Attorney fees
4. Recording fees (some)
5. Prorated property taxes
6. HOA transfer fees if applicable
7. Commission to listing and buyer's agents

Typical seller closing costs in Texas: **6-8% of sale price including commissions**.

## 22.14 Calculating Your Actual Profit

After closing, calculate actual profit accurately.

**Revenue**: sale price.

**Acquisition costs**: purchase price paid to heirs/estate, legal fees (attorney, court costs), title work for acquisition, research and verification costs.

**Holding costs**: property taxes during hold, insurance, utilities, maintenance, security, interest on any debt.

**Rehab costs**: all materials and labor, permits and inspections, design work if any.

**Selling costs**: real estate commissions, title insurance (seller portion), escrow and closing fees, attorney fees, recording fees, prorated taxes, staging and photography.

**Your net profit**: Sale Price − All Costs = Net Profit.

**Example Calculation:**

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Sale price | $235,000 |
| Acquisition (purchase + legal + title) | $145,000 |
| Holding (6 months of taxes, insurance, utilities) | $3,500 |
| Rehab | $18,000 |
| Selling costs (6.5% total) | $15,275 |
| **Net profit** | **$53,225** |

This is the real number. Factor in your time and the number becomes your hourly return on the deal.

**Tax considerations:**
1. Short-term capital gains (property held < 1 year): ordinary income tax rates
2. Long-term capital gains (property held > 1 year): reduced rates
3. Section 1031 exchanges (if applicable): defer taxes
4. Depreciation recapture (if held as rental): specific calculations

Consult your tax advisor. Tax strategy can significantly affect net profit.

## 22.15 The Walk-Through Challenge

Many deals hit turbulence at final walk-through.

**What buyer checks:**
1. Property condition matches contract
2. Repairs (if any) completed
3. Appliances and fixtures present as agreed
4. No unexpected issues
5. Property clean and ready

### Common Walk-Through Issues

**Missing items**: appliances that should be included, fixtures that should be included, specific items mentioned in contract.

**Damage since contract**: new damage to property, missing or broken components, weather damage.

**Incomplete repairs**: repairs promised but not done, shoddy repair work, additional issues discovered.

**Cleanliness**: property not clean, debris left behind, odors.

### Your Response

**Minor issues**: fix immediately if possible. Agree to credit at closing. Address before next walk-through.

**Major issues**: honest assessment of impact. Negotiate resolution. May require additional escrow or credit.

**Unreasonable demands**: push back with documentation. Refer to contract terms. Don't cave to every request.

## 22.16 Handling Post-Closing Issues

Sometimes issues emerge after closing.

**Common post-closing issues:**
- System failures (HVAC stops working, plumbing leak discovered, electrical problem surfaces)
- Undisclosed defects (buyer discovers issue you didn't disclose, claims failure-to-disclose, demands compensation or repair)
- Title issues (new claim against title, previously unknown heir surfaces, recording errors)

**Your protections:**

**Title insurance.** Your buyer's title insurance covers most title issues post-closing. Point the buyer to their title company.

**As-is language.** Well-documented as-is sales with proper disclosure protect you.

**Time limits.** Texas has statutes of limitations for property-related claims. Most claims must be filed within specific periods.

**Response strategy:**

**Legitimate issues.** Address honestly. Negotiate fair resolution. Document your response.

**Frivolous claims.** Refer to attorney. Don't engage directly with an aggressive buyer. Document everything.

**Legal exposure.** Consult attorney for significant claims. Insurance may help (title, liability). Early resolution is often cheaper than litigation.

## 22.17 The Complete Deal Economics — Worked Example

Here is a complete deal walked from acquisition to sale.

**The Property:** 3-bedroom, 2-bathroom, 1,450 sqft home in Northeast San Antonio. ARV: $235,000. Acquisition opportunity: fractured equity, 4 heirs, decedent died 2020, AOH approach viable.

**Acquisition Phase:**
1. 4 heirs at 25% each
2. Negotiated $35,000 per heir ($140,000 total)
3. AOH recorded (4 heirs cooperative)
4. Legal fees: $3,500
5. Title fees for acquisition: $1,800
6. Research and misc: $700
7. **Total acquisition: $146,000**

**Holding Phase (6 Months):**
1. Property taxes: $2,400 (prorated)
2. Insurance: $900
3. Utilities: $1,200
4. Security and maintenance: $500
5. **Total holding: $5,000**

**Rehab Phase:**
1. Interior paint (full): $3,500
2. Flooring (LVP throughout): $5,500
3. Kitchen refresh (paint cabinets, new countertops, hardware): $4,200
4. Bathroom refresh (paint, hardware, regrout): $1,800
5. HVAC service and minor repair: $700
6. Plumbing minor repairs: $400
7. Exterior paint touch-up: $800
8. Landscaping: $600
9. Minor electrical: $500
10. **Total rehab: $18,000**

**Selling Phase:**
1. Listed at $239,900
2. 2 offers within 14 days
3. Accepted offer: $235,000 (full price after negotiation)
4. Conventional financing
5. 30-day close
6. Closing costs (seller): $15,275 (6.5% including commissions)

**Final Numbers:**

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Revenue | $235,000 |
| Acquisition | $146,000 |
| Holding | $5,000 |
| Rehab | $18,000 |
| Selling | $15,275 |
| **Total costs** | **$184,275** |
| **Net profit** | **$50,725** |

**Deal Timeline:**
1. Initial contact with heirs: Month 1
2. Heir signings complete: Month 4
3. Closing on acquisition: Month 5
4. Post-acquisition cleanup and rehab: Months 5-7
5. Listed for sale: Month 8
6. Under contract: Month 9
7. Retail sale closing: Month 10

Total deal cycle: 10 months. Capital deployed: approximately $169,000 ($146,000 acquisition + $5,000 holding + $18,000 rehab before resale). **Annualized return on capital: approximately 36%.**

This is a solid mid-range deal. Not the biggest winner, but representative of typical outcomes. Operators running 3-5 of these per year generate $150K-$250K annual net profit.

## 22.18 Key Takeaways for Operators

To close out the acquisition-to-sale playbook, internalize these principles for retail sale execution:

**Retail sale is where maximum profit comes from.** Done right, it doubles or triples wholesale profits.

**Pre-listing preparation drives sale velocity.** Clean, rehab, photos, staging (when appropriate).

**Price strategically.** Understand market. Know your floor. Use psychological breakpoints.

**Listing agent vs. FSBO is a real choice.** Most fractured equity sales benefit from an agent.

**Multiple marketing channels multiply buyers.** MLS is primary; social, signage, open houses supplement.

**Qualify buyers before accepting offers.** Cash vs. financed affects timeline and risk.

**FHA/VA financing adds complications.** Prepare property or limit buyer types.

**Your buyer's title company will scrutinize your acquisition chain.** Budget for additional title work.

**Disclosure obligations are real.** Provide accurate disclosures with fractured equity context.
