## 29.1 How to Read This Chapter

This chapter presents six case studies demonstrating leverage tools in action. Each case is based on real patterns operators encounter, with names, locations, and specific details altered to protect privacy.

Read these cases with three questions in mind:

1. What tool or combination of tools was used, and why?
2. What would have happened without intervention?
3. Was the ethical framework from Chapter 28 applied appropriately?

Not every case ends well. Not every decision is defensible. Cautionary examples are included because pretending every leverage deal works perfectly would teach you nothing useful. Real businesses include mistakes, miscalculations, and outcomes that weren't ideal for everyone involved.

The goal is developing **judgment** — the ability to recognize situations, choose tools, anticipate complications, and make decisions that sustain both your business and your character.

## 29.2 Case Study 1 — The Stuck Estate

*A textbook partition case where leverage unlocked a deal nobody else could close.*

### Background

- Decedent: James Walker, died 2015 intestate in Bexar County
- Surviving heirs: 4 children (ages 48–62 at time of deal)
- Property: 2-bedroom home in Eastside San Antonio, ARV $175,000
- Condition: Fair; occupied by Walker's longtime girlfriend who was not an heir
- Time elapsed since death when operator engaged: 7 years

### The Impasse

For 7 years after James's death, the property sat. The four children couldn't agree on anything:

1. Oldest son wanted to sell immediately and split proceeds
2. Second daughter wanted to keep it as rental in the family
3. Third son wanted to let the girlfriend continue living there
4. Youngest daughter wanted to sell to herself specifically at below-market price

Meanwhile, taxes accumulated ($8,400 over 7 years), the girlfriend lived rent-free, and the property slowly deteriorated.

### Operator Engagement

Operator identified the property through delinquent tax research. Contacted all four heirs over 3 months:

- Oldest son: Ready to sell, wanted $40,000 for his 1/4 interest
- Second daughter: Refused to engage; wanted property kept
- Third son: Engaged but wouldn't sell while girlfriend occupied
- Youngest daughter: Wanted $60,000 for her 1/4 interest (above market)

### Voluntary Attempt

Operator made offers to all heirs based on $175,000 × 25% = $43,750 gross per interest, discounted to $30,000 net per interest accounting for complications.

Oldest son accepted $30,000. Third son conditionally accepted if girlfriend could stay 6 months after closing. Others refused.

### Decision Point

Operator now owned 1/4 interest (from oldest son) with potential for 2/4 (pending third son's girlfriend condition). Options:

1. Walk away with single interest, hope for future negotiation
2. Continue voluntary efforts
3. File partition action to force resolution

Deal economics: Property worth $175,000. Operator had $30,000 invested plus $4,500 legal fees. Partition costs estimated $18,000–$22,000. Total potential acquisition cost if successful: $130,000–$145,000. Net profit potential: $25,000–$40,000 after rehab and sale.

**Decision: File partition.**

### Partition Action

- Filed in Bexar County District Court
- Named second daughter, third son, youngest daughter as defendants
- Requested partition by sale
- Months 1–3: Service and initial responses. All three filed answers through attorneys.
- Months 4–8: Discovery phase. Depositions revealed family conflict that prevented voluntary resolution.
- Month 9: Court-ordered mediation

### The Mediation

Mediation lasted 6 hours. Each heir's attorney presented their client's position. Eventually a settlement framework emerged:

- Second daughter: Accept $35,000 for interest
- Third son: Accept $32,000 if girlfriend could stay 12 months
- Youngest daughter: Accept $38,000
- Total acquisition of remaining 3 interests: $105,000
- Plus original $30,000 to oldest son: $135,000 total
- Plus legal costs: approximately $16,000
- Plus girlfriend's 12-month occupancy (no rent income)

### Resolution

Settlement executed. All four heirs received payments. Operator became 100% owner.

- Girlfriend continued living there for agreed 12 months
- Operator paid taxes during occupancy period
- After her departure, property rehabbed ($28,000)
- Sold at $182,000 in month 18

### Final Numbers

| Line Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Revenue | $182,000 |
| Acquisition (all four heirs) | $135,000 |
| Legal (voluntary + partition) | $20,500 |
| Rehab | $28,000 |
| Holding (18 months: taxes, insurance, etc.) | $9,500 |
| Selling costs | $11,800 |
| **Total costs** | **$204,800** |
| **NET LOSS** | **$(22,800)** |

### Lessons from Case 1

1. Even well-executed partition cases can lose money when conditions are wrong.
2. The 12-month girlfriend occupancy was a significant concession that ate into margins.
3. Post-mediation total cost was too high for this particular property's economics.
4. ARV was modest relative to the complexity of the situation.
5. Partition tools worked as designed — the deal closed — but economics were marginal from the start.

### Ethical Assessment

Each heir received fair compensation. Family dynamics that had paralyzed them for 7 years were resolved. Property returned to productive use. Girlfriend received 12 additional months of housing. Operator lost money but operated ethically throughout.

The lesson isn't that the deal was unethical — it wasn't. The lesson is that partition economics require careful underwriting. The same deal with $240,000 ARV instead of $175,000 would have been strongly profitable. The same deal with better family dynamics would have settled earlier and cheaper. Run the numbers before you file. Model the concessions you're likely to make in mediation before you sit down at the table.

## 29.3 Case Study 2 — Tax Sale Intervention at the Last Minute

*A pre-sale intervention with 19 days to closing.*

### Background

- Property: 3-bedroom home in South Houston, ARV $145,000
- Owner of record: Deceased 2017, 3 children as heirs
- Tax delinquency: 5 years, $18,600 total with penalties and fees
- Scheduled tax sale: 19 days from operator's identification of opportunity

### The Family

Three children: one in Houston, one in Dallas, one in California. Had essentially forgotten about the property in the years since their father's death. Tax notices went to an old address.

### Rapid Outreach

Operator had 19 days. Normal pacing wasn't possible.

- Day 1–2: Identified all three heirs through skip tracing
- Day 3: Contacted Houston daughter
- Day 4: Conference call with all three
- Day 5: Family meeting in Houston (daughter hosted)

### The Conversation

Operator explained the situation honestly:

- Tax sale in 14 days
- Without intervention, property lost entirely
- Tax sale proceeds would go to taxing authority first; minimal return to family if any
- Alternative: Operator acquires property, pays taxes, pays family $15,000 each
- Family would need to sign quickly to beat sale date

### Family Response

Initial resistance from California sister, who wanted to consult an attorney. Operator:

1. Encouraged her to do so
2. Provided reasonable names of Houston attorneys
3. Offered to extend timeline if she could find an attorney quickly
4. Said explicitly: *"If I can't make this work with your attorney's involvement in 10 days, the sale proceeds without us and your family loses the property entirely."*

California sister consulted attorney. Attorney confirmed operator's framing was accurate, proposed they negotiate for a slightly higher amount. Final deal: $17,500 each ($52,500 total).

### Execution

- Day 8–10: Release of Interest agreements drafted and sent
- Day 11–12: All three heirs signed with local notaries
- Day 13: AOH filed and recorded
- Day 14–15: Title work, closing preparation
- Day 16: Closing, tax payment to authority
- Day 17: Sale cancellation confirmed
- Day 19 (original sale date): Would have been tax sale

### Post-Acquisition

- Property assessed: Needed $15,000 rehab
- Operator completed rehab in 10 weeks
- Listed at $149,000
- Sold at $142,500 after 30 days on market

### Final Numbers

| Line Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Revenue | $142,500 |
| Acquisition (heir payments + taxes) | $71,100 |
| Rehab | $15,000 |
| Legal (rushed timeline) | $5,500 |
| Holding | $2,800 |
| Selling costs | $9,300 |
| **Total costs** | **$103,700** |
| **Net profit** | **$38,800** |

### Ethical Assessment

Each heir received $17,500 ($52,500 family total) when the alternative was losing everything. The family was fully informed, encouraged to get attorney review, and given an accurate timeline. Pressure existed but was real — the tax sale was genuinely scheduled. California sister had attorney review before signing.

Strong ethical outcome. Family benefited significantly versus the alternative. Operator earned a fair return for risk and speed of execution.

### Lessons from Case 2

1. Tax sale intervention can work with tight timelines when execution is sharp.
2. Encouraging attorney consultation strengthens both ethics and deal certainty.
3. Honest framing of alternatives builds trust and closes deals.
4. Fair prices paid even under time pressure produce sustainable business.
5. Modest property (ARV below $150K) can still produce good returns through the tax sale pipeline.

## 29.4 Case Study 3 — The Creditor-Suit Case That Shouldn't Have Happened

*A cautionary example where aggressive tactics produced short-term profit and long-term destruction.*

### Background

- Property: 4-bedroom home in Austin, ARV $385,000
- Decedent: Robert Mitchell, died 2019
- Heirs: Wife Susan (elderly, 79), son David, daughter Linda
- Susan living in property since Robert's death
- Property was Susan's homestead

### The Setup

Operator (not the author of this Codex) identified the property as an attractive acquisition target. Initial approach to Susan was received with confusion — she didn't understand why someone was calling about her home. She referred them to son David.

### David's Situation

David had a history of financial problems:

- Small credit card judgment from 2016: $4,200
- Divorce-related obligations
- Ongoing child support issues
- Generally uncooperative with family

### Operator's Strategy

Operator saw opportunity through the creditor-suit pipeline:

1. Purchased David's old credit card judgment for $1,200
2. Abstracted judgment in Travis County
3. Filed execution against David's 1/3 interest in the property

Note: Operator knew Susan was elderly and in the property as homestead. Operator knew homestead protections applied against ordinary creditor claims against Susan's interest, but not necessarily against David's separate interest.

### The Sheriff's Sale

- Operator bid at sheriff's sale
- No other bidders appeared
- Operator acquired David's 1/3 interest for $4,500 (the judgment amount plus costs)
- Actual market value of 1/3 interest: approximately $128,000

### The Aftermath

Operator now owned 1/3 interest and immediately filed a partition action against Susan and Linda.

- Susan, 79, learned her home was subject to forced sale
- Linda, living in another state, received service of process
- Family was blindsided and devastated
- Local attorney took case on Susan's behalf and publicized the situation

### The Response

Local media picked up the story:

- Elderly widow losing home to aggressive investor
- Son's small debt used as leverage tool
- Community outrage
- Attorney General inquiry

Operator settled quickly — agreeing to sell the 1/3 interest back to Susan's family at cost, essentially walking away with zero profit. But:

- Reputation in Austin real estate community permanently damaged
- Title companies stopped working with operator
- Attorneys refused to take operator's cases
- Operator left the market within 18 months

### Ethical Assessment

This case represents clear ethical failure on multiple dimensions:

**Target vulnerability:** Elderly widow was clearly the ultimate victim, regardless of the technical legal ownership structure.

**Manufactured leverage:** A stale $4,200 judgment was exploited aggressively to acquire a $128,000 interest.

**Disproportionate response:** David's small debt was completely dwarfed by consequences to his mother.

**Family impact:** Elderly widow's peace and home threatened over a tactical leverage maneuver.

**"Would you tell" test failure:** Operator couldn't have explained this approach to neutral parties without shame.

### Lessons from Case 3

1. Legal techniques can be used in legally permissible but ethically wrong ways.
2. Targeting elderly widows and their homes crosses clear ethical lines.
3. Small debts should not be used as leverage for disproportionate property acquisition.
4. Community backlash can destroy operators permanently — even when the deal settles.
5. Settlement paid at cost still ended the operator's career.
6. The "would you tell" test should have prevented this deal before it started.

*This case happened. The operator is no longer in the business. Their name is remembered in Austin for exactly the wrong reasons.*

## 29.5 Case Study 4 — The Administrator Shortcut

*A clean single-signature acquisition — no leverage tools required.*

### Background

- Property: Ranch-style home in Corpus Christi, ARV $195,000
- Decedent: Margaret Johnson, died 2022 with valid will
- Will designated daughter Patricia as Independent Executor
- Estate had 3 beneficiaries (Patricia and two siblings)
- Estate had $18,000 in other assets plus the property

### Why Single-Signature Worked Here

This case demonstrates when leverage tools aren't needed because the legal structure already supports efficient resolution:

1. Valid will with independent executor provision
2. Executor actively administering estate
3. Beneficiaries in agreement about selling property
4. Estate needed cash to pay debts and distribute
5. Administrator authority under Texas Estates Code § 402.051 sufficient for the transaction

### Operator Engagement

Operator identified property through probate court records. Contacted Patricia directly.

Patricia was cooperative:

- Had been administering estate for 8 months
- Selling property was part of her plan
- Had received two below-market offers from other investors
- Open to hearing operator's proposal

### The Deal

- Purchase price: $128,000 (66% of ARV, reflecting condition and needs)
- Single contract with Estate as seller
- Patricia's signature alone sufficient under her executor authority
- Siblings signed acknowledgments (belt and suspenders approach)
- Closing in 45 days

### The Math from Patricia's Perspective

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Estate cash from sale | $128,000 |
| Less: Debts and administrative costs | $(14,000) |
| Less: Executor compensation | $(6,000) |
| Plus: Other estate assets | $18,000 |
| **Net for distribution** | **$126,000** |
| **Per beneficiary (3 equal shares)** | **$42,000 each** |

Patricia was satisfied — higher net to beneficiaries than competing offers would have produced after protracted negotiations.

### Execution

- Weeks 1–2: Contract execution
- Weeks 3–4: Title work (clean because single-signature under administrator authority)
- Weeks 5–6: Closing preparation
- Week 6: Closing completed

### Post-Acquisition

- Property in decent condition
- $8,000 rehab (paint, flooring refresh, cleanup)
- Listed at $196,000
- Sold at $192,000 in 22 days

### Final Numbers

| Line Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Revenue | $192,000 |
| Acquisition | $128,000 |
| Rehab | $8,000 |
| Legal | $2,200 |
| Holding | $1,800 |
| Selling costs | $12,500 |
| **Total costs** | **$152,500** |
| **Net profit** | **$39,500** |

### Total Timeline

- Initial contact to closing: 8 weeks
- Closing to resale: 11 weeks
- Total deal cycle: 19 weeks

### Lessons from Case 4

1. Single-signature deals are the operator's dream scenario: fast, cheap, clean, cooperative.
2. No leverage tools were needed because the legal structure already supported voluntary resolution.
3. Patricia's cooperation was rewarded with higher net proceeds to family than competing offers would have produced.
4. Not every deal needs Part V tools — sometimes Part IV is all you need.
5. Legal costs of $2,200 versus $16,000–$20,000 in partition cases illustrate the economic value of deal selection.

### Ethical Assessment

Clean transaction on all dimensions. Arms-length negotiation. Administrator had full authority and understood the market. Beneficiaries received meaningful distribution. Fast, efficient execution. Operator earned a fair return. Everyone satisfied.

This is what fractured equity work looks like when it goes right.

## 29.6 Case Study 5 — The Partition That Became a Voluntary Sale

*How threatening partition sometimes produces voluntary resolution without actually executing partition.*

### Background

- Property: Duplex in Fort Worth, ARV $225,000 (both units)
- Decedent: Robert Chen, died 2018 intestate
- Heirs: 5 children, all adults, various geographic locations
- Property had been rented as duplex generating $2,400/month total
- Oldest son Michael had been collecting rents since father's death

### The Complication

Michael had been managing the property but:

- Not sharing rent income with siblings
- Had deposited approximately $120,000 in rents over 4 years into his personal account
- Had been uncommunicative when siblings asked for accounting
- Was using the rents for his own expenses

### Operator Engagement

Sibling Jennifer contacted operator for advice. Situation assessed:

- 5 heirs with roughly equal 1/5 interests
- Significant accounting claim against Michael for withheld rents
- Property itself valuable
- Family unable to resolve voluntarily

### Strategic Approach

Operator acquired interests from 4 of 5 heirs (all but Michael):

- Jennifer: $22,000 for her 1/5 interest
- 3 other siblings: $22,000 each for their 1/5 interests
- Total paid: $88,000 for 4/5 interest
- Plus assignment of each sibling's accounting claim against Michael for unpaid rent shares

Operator now owned 4/5 interest and held $96,000 in accounting claims ($120,000 × 4/5).

### The Approach to Michael

Operator and attorney met with Michael directly. Options presented clearly:

**Option A:** Michael sells his 1/5 interest for $22,000. Accounting claims released. Clean break.

**Option B:** Operator files partition action AND pursues accounting claims separately. Michael faces both forced sale and a judgment for $96,000+.

Michael's attorney reviewed the situation and advised him that:

1. Partition would succeed — operator held 4/5 interest
2. Accounting claims were well-documented and likely to result in a substantial judgment
3. Combined legal costs would exceed $40,000
4. Best outcome: accept buyout, avoid litigation

### The Settlement

- Michael accepted $28,000 for his 1/5 interest (negotiated up from $22,000)
- Accounting claims released as part of settlement
- Operator now owned 100%
- No partition action actually filed

### Execution Timeline

- Months 1–4: Initial engagement and 4-heir acquisitions
- Months 4–5: Negotiation with Michael
- Month 5: Settlement and closing
- Month 6: Minor rehab
- Month 7: Refinanced as investment property (kept as rental)

### Final Position

| Item | Detail |
|---|---|
| Total acquisition cost | $116,000 for 100% of duplex |
| Appraised value | $225,000 |
| Refinance at 70% LTV | $157,500 cash out |
| Monthly rental income | $2,400 |
| Monthly cash flow (after mortgage and expenses) | $950 |
| 10-year rental cash flow projection | $114,000+ while appreciation builds |

### Why This Approach Worked

1. Multiple tools were leveraged without actually filing any of them.
2. Accounting claims (Chapter 24) created real legal exposure for Michael.
3. Partition action readiness (Chapter 25) provided additional pressure.
4. Michael's attorney recognized the legal reality and advised settlement.
5. Settlement was fair — $28,000 was within the range of what partition would have produced.
6. Nobody went to court; all parties benefited from efficient resolution.

### Ethical Assessment

Michael received fair value for his interest. Accounting claims were legitimate — he had actually collected rents without sharing. Other siblings recovered their shares of both property value and uncollected rents through the operator's acquisition of their claims. Voluntary resolution avoided the family trauma of extended litigation.

This is how leverage tools work best: the threat of them creates voluntary resolution at fair values. Nobody actually had to go through partition, but the tool's availability changed what was negotiable.

### Lessons from Case 5

1. Combining tools (contribution claim + partition readiness) creates stronger leverage than either alone.
2. Settlement is usually the outcome even when tools are formally deployed — be prepared to negotiate throughout.
3. Attorney-to-attorney communication often produces resolution faster than operator-to-heir direct negotiation.
4. Deal economics support both wholesale (flip) and hold (rental/BRRRR) exit strategies.
5. The BRRRR approach (Buy, Rehab, Rent, Refinance, Repeat) works well when the property supports it.

## 29.7 Case Study 6 — When to Walk Away

*A cautionary example of a deal that should never have been pursued.*

### Background

- Property: 2-bedroom home in small East Texas town, ARV $85,000
- Decedent: Died 1987 (35+ years before operator engagement)
- Current situation: Property had passed through multiple generations of heirs
- Estimated heirs: 17 different descendants across 3 generations
- Title had never been cleared; no probate in decades

### Initial Attraction

Operator identified opportunity:

- Property had been on delinquent tax list for years
- Small investment potential through partial interests
- Partition would theoretically force sale
- Profit potential of $20,000–$30,000 if successful

### Due Diligence Reality

Extensive research revealed the real challenges:

- 17 heirs spread across 12 states and 3 countries
- 3 heirs had died since the original decedent, creating sub-heirs
- Several heirs had married multiple times, creating complex heirship chains
- 4 heirs could not be located despite extensive skip tracing
- Total estimated legal costs to clear title: $35,000–$55,000
- Timeline to resolution: 24–36 months minimum
- Ongoing risk of new heirs discovered during process

### The Math

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| ARV | $85,000 |
| Realistic acquisition cost (17 heirs at $2,500–$3,500 each) | $42,500–$59,500 |
| Legal to clear title and complete acquisition | $35,000–$55,000 |
| Rehab (property in poor condition) | $15,000–$25,000 |
| Selling costs | $5,500 |
| **Total potential cost** | **$98,000–$145,000** |
| Revenue from sale (rural market) | $75,000–$85,000 |
| **Potential loss** | **$13,000–$70,000** |

### Additional Risk Factors

1. Small-town market — limited buyer pool and extended days on market
2. Property deterioration during 2–3 year title clearance process
3. Uncertain outcome on missing heir procedures
4. Time and attention cost to manage a 17-heir case over 2–3 years
5. Capital tied up in a marginal deal that crowds out better opportunities

### The Decision

**Operator walked away.**

Even with leverage tools available, some deals don't make economic sense:

1. Too many heirs to coordinate
2. Title too clouded to resolve efficiently
3. Market too weak to support aggressive acquisition costs
4. Time commitment too large relative to profit potential
5. Risk of total loss too high

### The Operator's Reasoning

*"I have finite time, finite capital, and finite attention. This deal would consume significant amounts of all three for marginal profit potential and substantial loss potential. Even if I had the tools to force resolution, the economics don't work. I'm passing."*

### Lessons from Case 6

1. Having the tools to force resolution doesn't mean every deal is worth pursuing.
2. Due diligence should precede any substantial commitment of time or capital.
3. Extended title clouds (30+ years) often aren't worth the clearance effort relative to property value.
4. Many heirs scattered geographically multiplies complexity disproportionately.
5. Small markets limit exit options regardless of acquisition strategy.
6. Walking away is a legitimate strategy — not a failure.
7. Sometimes the best deal is the one you don't do.

### Ethical Assessment

Walking away is ethically neutral — neither positive nor negative. The family's situation continues unchanged. Someone else might eventually address it, or the property might be lost to tax sale. Operator chose to allocate resources elsewhere.

Walking away is specifically *not* unethical because operators have no obligation to help every potentially-acquirable property. Operators have obligations when they engage. They have no obligation to engage in the first place.

## 29.8 Patterns Across All Cases

Six cases. Six different outcomes. What patterns emerge?

### Pattern 1: Deal Economics Matter More Than Tool Availability

Cases 1 and 6 demonstrate that having the tools doesn't guarantee profitable deals. Underwriting before commitment is essential. Some deals are genuinely marginal even with best execution — the math doesn't improve just because you know partition procedure.

### Pattern 2: Fair Dealing Produces Sustainable Outcomes

Cases 2 and 4 show ethical dealings producing good economic results. Case 3 shows an extractive approach destroying an operator even when technically legal. The correlation between ethical practice and business longevity is not coincidental.

### Pattern 3: Single-Signature Deals Are the Operator's Dream

Case 4 shows why operators should prioritize finding properties where the legal structure already supports single-signature acquisition. Faster timelines, lower legal costs, cleaner title, and cooperative counterparties. Leverage-driven deals are the fallback — not the target.

### Pattern 4: Tools Combine Strategically

Case 5 demonstrates the multi-tool approach — accounting claims combined with partition readiness produced voluntary settlement without either tool being fully executed. The threat of tools often produces better outcomes than their full use.

### Pattern 5: Judgment About When to Walk Away Distinguishes Operators

Case 6 shows that the operator who walks away from a bad deal can be as important as the operator who closes a good one. Finite resources require allocation discipline. Every deal you take depletes capacity for the next one.

### Pattern 6: Ethical Choices Shape Long-Term Careers

Case 3 shows the dramatic consequences of ethical failure even when technically legal. The operator's career ended. Fair operators build decades-long practices. Ethics aren't just about individual transactions — they determine whether you have a sustainable business at all.

## 29.10 Key Takeaways Before Moving to Part VI

From Part V as a whole:

**Leverage is never first choice.** Voluntary resolution is always preferred. Leverage tools exist for situations where voluntary resolution has genuinely failed.

**Six primary tools exist** — each appropriate for different situations. Match tool to situation thoughtfully.

**Tools combine strategically.** Multi-tool approaches often produce voluntary resolution without full execution of any single tool.

**Ethical framework must govern tool selection.** Legal techniques can be used ethically or extractively. Choose consciously.

**Economics must justify leverage costs.** Legal fees of $4K–$75K+ require deal profit potential that absorbs them and then some.

**Reputation compounds over time.** Fair operators build sustainable businesses. Extractive operators eventually face consequences — often permanent ones.

**Walking away is a legitimate strategy.** Not every deal should be pursued, even with tools available.

**Settlement is usually the outcome.** Most cases settle before full trial. Be prepared to negotiate throughout the entire process.

**Case studies show patterns.** Pattern recognition develops through experience. Start with these six, add your own as you close deals.

**Judgment matters more than tools.** Knowing when to use which tool, when to walk away, when to settle — this judgment separates successful operators from struggling ones.
