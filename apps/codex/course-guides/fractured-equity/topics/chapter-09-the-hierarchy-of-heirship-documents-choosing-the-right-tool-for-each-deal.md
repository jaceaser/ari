## 9.1 Why This Chapter Exists

In every deal where someone died, you need a document that establishes who the legal heirs are. Without it, you can't acquire clean title, you can't satisfy the title company's Schedule C, and the property can't be resold. This chapter teaches you the full set of heirship documents, what each one does, when to use each, and what each costs in money and time.

Picking the wrong document is expensive. An operator who files an Affidavit of Heirship when the fact pattern demanded a Judgment Declaring Heirship wastes four months discovering the title company won't accept the AOH, then has to restart the process through the courts. Conversely, an operator who files for a Judgment Declaring Heirship in a simple case where a Small Estate Affidavit would work wastes $4,000 in legal fees on a $75 filing.

The operators who master this chapter choose the right document every time. They move faster, spend less, and close more deals.

The teaching framework here is a decision hierarchy: start with the cheapest, fastest tool that will work; escalate only when necessary. Underwriter requirements, fact-pattern complexity, and timeline constraints all affect the choice.

## 9.2 The Full Set of Heirship Documents

Here are the tools available, from lightest to heaviest:

**1. Affidavit of Heirship (AOH).** A sworn statement by two disinterested witnesses identifying the decedent's heirs. Recorded in the real property records. Texas Estates Code § 203.001. Cheapest and fastest; subject to the four-year maturity rule for title insurance purposes.

**2. Small Estate Affidavit.** A court-filed affidavit for small estates (under $75,000 in Texas, excluding homestead) with no will. Allows heirs to collect estate property without full probate. Texas Estates Code § 205.001 et seq.

**3. Muniment of Title.** A streamlined probate for testate estates (decedent had a will) where no administration is needed. Filed in probate court, results in a court order accepting the will. Texas Estates Code § 257.001 et seq.

**4. Judgment Declaring Heirship.** A full court proceeding that results in a judgment formally identifying the decedent's heirs. Most authoritative. Required by some title underwriters in complex cases. Texas Estates Code § 202.001 et seq.

**5. Formal Probate (Independent Administration).** Full probate proceeding for testate estates, resulting in appointment of an executor who has authority to administer and convey estate property. Texas Estates Code § 401.001 et seq.

**6. Formal Probate (Dependent Administration).** Full probate proceeding for intestate estates (or testate where independent administration isn't available), with ongoing court supervision of the administrator's actions.

**7. Determination of Heirship Combined with Administration.** A combined proceeding that establishes heirship and appoints an administrator in the same case. Texas Estates Code § 202.005.

**8. Executor or Administrator Deed.** Not technically a heirship-establishing document, but a deed executed by a previously-appointed personal representative under their existing authority. Covered briefly here and in depth in Chapter 18.

Each of these serves a different purpose. Understanding the differences lets you choose correctly.

## 9.3 Affidavit of Heirship (AOH) — Your Default Tool

The AOH is the workhorse of this business. The majority of deals you'll do will use an AOH as the primary heirship-establishing document.

### What It Is

A sworn affidavit signed by two witnesses who knew the decedent (but who are not themselves heirs) stating:

1. When and where the decedent died
2. The decedent's family structure (spouse, children, parents, siblings, etc.)
3. Who the decedent's legal heirs are under intestate succession (or under the will, if any)
4. Other relevant facts about the estate

The AOH is signed, notarized, and recorded in the real property records of the county where the property is located.

### What Makes It Useful

**Cheap.** Attorney drafting fees typically $300–$800. Recording fees $35–$75. Total cost: under $1,000 in most cases.

**Fast.** Can be prepared, signed, and recorded within a week if witnesses are available.

**No court involvement.** No filing, no hearing, no judge.

**Widely accepted** for many deal types once it has matured.

### The Four-Year Maturity Rule

Under Texas Estates Code § 203.001, a recorded AOH becomes prima facie evidence of the facts it recites when it has been on record for five years (amended from four years in 2023). In practice, most title underwriters apply a four-year rule based on older statute language, though some have updated to five years.

**Before four (or five) years:** The AOH is still useful and legally valid, but it doesn't carry the strong presumption of accuracy. Underwriters are more skeptical.

**After maturity:** The AOH is treated as prima facie evidence and is widely accepted by underwriters for establishing heirship in standard cases.

### When to Use an AOH

1. The decedent died at least 4–5 years ago (AOH will be mature quickly or immediately after filing)
2. The fact pattern is clear: no contested heirship, no potential outside children, no missing heirs
3. The property is not extremely high-value (under $500,000 typically — at higher values, underwriters want more formal heirship establishment)
4. Two disinterested witnesses are available who knew the decedent and the family
5. Your title company is willing to accept an AOH for the specific fact pattern

### When NOT to Use an AOH

1. Death was recent (AOH won't be mature for years)
2. Contested heirship exists (disputing parties among the heirs)
3. Missing heirs (people who should be listed as heirs can't be located)
4. Potential for outside children that haven't been conclusively ruled out
5. High-value property where underwriter wants more formal proof
6. Title underwriter specifically requires a Judgment Declaring Heirship

## 9.4 The AOH in Practice — Drafting, Signing, Recording

### Finding Witnesses

You need two witnesses who:

1. Knew the decedent personally
2. Have first-hand knowledge of the decedent's family structure
3. Are not themselves heirs or beneficiaries of the estate (no financial interest)
4. Are willing to sign under oath about the family facts

Good AOH witnesses are often:

1. Friends of the decedent (longtime neighbors, church members, work colleagues)
2. Extended family members who aren't in the line of inheritance (aunts/uncles, cousins-by-marriage, in-laws of the decedent's children)
3. Step-relatives who aren't legal heirs
4. Long-term employees, care providers, or professional associates

Be careful about witnesses who have a financial interest. A witness who will benefit from the deal (even indirectly) is not "disinterested" and title underwriters may challenge the AOH.

### Drafting the AOH

Your attorney will draft the AOH based on information you've gathered through genealogical research and interviews. The AOH should include:

1. Full identification of the decedent (name, dates of birth and death, county of residence, marital history)
2. Full family tree (spouse(s), children — including outside children if any, parents, siblings, etc.)
3. Whether the decedent left a will
4. Identification of current legal heirs under applicable law
5. Description of the real estate affected
6. Specific factual statements that support the heirship conclusions

### Execution

Both witnesses sign before a notary. The notary certifies the signatures and verifies identity. The AOH is then ready to be recorded.

### Recording

File the AOH with the county clerk in the county where the property is located. Pay recording fees (typically $35–$75 depending on page count). The AOH becomes part of the public real property records.

### After Recording

Save copies for your deal file. The recorded AOH will be referenced in any title commitment on the property going forward. Title examiners will pull it as part of their research.

## 9.5 Small Estate Affidavit

For very small estates with no will, the Small Estate Affidavit is a faster and cheaper alternative to probate.

### What It Is

A court-filed affidavit that allows heirs to collect property of the estate without a formal administration. Filed in probate court, it requires the court's approval before it can be relied on for conveyance.

### Requirements (Texas Estates Code § 205.001)

1. Total value of the decedent's probate estate (excluding homestead) is $75,000 or less
2. No application for appointment of a personal representative is pending
3. 30 days have passed since the decedent's death
4. Decedent died intestate (no will) — a Small Estate Affidavit generally can't be used for testate estates

### What the Small Estate Affidavit Contains

1. Statement of the decedent's death and residence
2. List of all heirs and their relationship to the decedent
3. Statement that no administration is pending
4. Identification of all estate property, including specific real estate
5. Two disinterested witnesses' confirmation of the facts

### Process

1. Heirs and two disinterested witnesses sign the affidavit under oath
2. Heirs file the affidavit with the probate court
3. Court reviews and, if appropriate, issues an order approving the affidavit
4. Approved Small Estate Affidavit can be recorded in real property records and used to support conveyances

### Cost and Timeline

1. Attorney fees: $1,500–$3,000
2. Court filing fees: $250–$450
3. Total time: 30–90 days

### When to Use

1. Estate is small and within the statutory limit
2. Decedent died intestate
3. Limited estate property (typically one house, a vehicle, and modest personal property)
4. All heirs are identified and cooperative
5. Speed matters (faster than full probate)

### When NOT to Use

1. Estate exceeds statutory limits
2. Will exists (use muniment of title instead)
3. Contested heirship
4. Missing heirs
5. Non-Texas property

## 9.6 Muniment of Title

For testate estates where the only need is to admit the will to probate (without full administration), Texas offers the muniment of title procedure.

### What It Is

A streamlined probate proceeding. The will is admitted to probate, the court issues an order, and title passes according to the will — all without appointing an executor or conducting administration.

### Requirements (Texas Estates Code § 257.001)

1. The decedent left a valid will
2. There are no unpaid debts owed by the estate (other than liens secured by real estate)
3. Administration is not otherwise necessary
4. All interested parties (beneficiaries under the will) consent or are given proper notice

### What It Does

1. Admits the will to probate
2. Establishes the will's validity
3. Creates a court record that title passes according to the will's terms
4. No executor is appointed; no administration occurs

### Cost and Timeline

1. Attorney fees: $1,500–$3,500
2. Court filing fees: $250–$500
3. Total time: 60–120 days

### When to Use

1. Testate estate with a clear, uncontested will
2. Limited or no unsecured debts
3. Primary assets are real estate passing under the will
4. Faster alternative to full independent administration

### When NOT to Use

1. Significant debts or complex administration needed
2. Contested will
3. Need for an executor to take specific actions beyond conveyance
4. Intestate estate (no will — use AOH, SEA, or JDH instead)

### Practical Note

Muniment of title is elegant when it fits. You end up with a court order that the title insurance industry respects, without the complexity and cost of full probate. Know when it applies.

## 9.7 Judgment Declaring Heirship (JDH)

When heirship is contested, complex, or involves missing heirs — or when the title underwriter simply requires a higher-authority document — you'll need a Judgment Declaring Heirship.

### What It Is

A formal court proceeding where interested parties (or a creditor) petition the probate court to declare who the decedent's legal heirs are. The court holds a hearing, considers evidence, and issues a judgment formally identifying the heirs and their respective shares. Governed by Texas Estates Code § 202.001 et seq.

### Process

1. Petition filed in probate court by an interested party (heir, creditor, or other person with legal interest)
2. Court appoints an attorney ad litem to represent unknown or missing heirs
3. All known heirs are served with notice
4. Diligent search is conducted for unknown heirs, with service by publication if necessary
5. Evidentiary hearing is held (may be through affidavits and testimony)
6. Court issues Judgment Declaring Heirship

### Cost and Timeline

1. Attorney fees: $3,500–$8,500
2. Court filing fees: $500–$1,000
3. Attorney ad litem fees: $500–$2,500
4. Publication costs (for service by publication): $100–$500
5. Total time: 4–8 months

### When to Use

1. Contested heirship
2. Missing heirs despite diligent search
3. Potential equitable adoption or other unusual relationships
4. High-value properties where underwriter requires JDH
5. Complex multi-generational fact patterns
6. When an AOH's four-year wait is too long
7. When multiple title underwriters have rejected AOH-based approaches

### When NOT to Use

1. Simple, clear heirship that an AOH can cover (JDH is overkill)
2. Small-value deals where JDH cost erodes the profit
3. Emergency-speed deals (JDH takes months)

### The Attorney Ad Litem

In a JDH proceeding, the court appoints an attorney ad litem to represent unknown heirs and protect their interests. The attorney ad litem investigates the claimed heirship, searches for missing heirs, and ensures the court has accurate information before issuing the judgment.

The attorney ad litem costs money and adds time, but they also provide validation. A judgment issued after the ad litem's investigation carries more weight than an AOH because a neutral attorney has verified the facts.

### Why Underwriters Love JDHs

A JDH is a court order. It's been litigated (even if uncontested), a neutral attorney has verified the facts, and the court has formally declared who the heirs are. If a problem surfaces later — a missing heir shows up, a disputed relationship emerges — the JDH provides strong protection.

For complex fact patterns, the JDH is often worth the cost and time even when simpler alternatives exist, because the additional certainty lets you close deals that underwriters otherwise wouldn't touch.

## 9.8 Formal Probate — Independent Administration

For larger or more complex estates, formal probate with an appointed executor (testate) or administrator (intestate) may be required or strategically valuable.

### What Independent Administration Is

Texas Estates Code § 401.001 et seq. allows most Texas probates to proceed as "independent administrations" — meaning the personal representative (executor or administrator) has broad authority to act without ongoing court supervision.

### How to Obtain Independent Administration

1. Testate: Will names an independent executor, OR beneficiaries consent to independent administration
2. Intestate: All heirs consent to independent administration, AND the court approves

### What Independent Administration Allows

Once appointed, the independent executor or administrator can:

1. Collect estate assets
2. Pay debts
3. Sell real estate (with a single signature)
4. Distribute property to heirs/beneficiaries
5. Close the estate

All without ongoing court involvement. The court appoints the representative, and the representative then operates with substantial authority.

### Cost and Timeline

1. Attorney fees: $3,000–$10,000+ depending on complexity
2. Court filing fees: $400–$800
3. Bond (if required for administrator): varies
4. Total time: 6 months to 2+ years

### When to Use

1. Significant estate with multiple assets
2. Testate estate with will directing independent administration
3. Intestate estate where all heirs will cooperate
4. Need for an executor to actually administer (collect assets, pay debts, etc.)
5. Administrator's single-signature authority is valuable for the acquisition strategy (see Chapter 18)

---

## 9.9 Formal Probate — Dependent Administration

When independent administration isn't available, dependent administration involves ongoing court supervision.

### What It Is

Texas probate with the administrator operating under court supervision. Every significant action (sale of property, payment of debts, distribution) requires court approval.

### When It's Required

1. Contested estates
2. Cases where beneficiaries don't consent to independent administration
3. Minor beneficiaries or heirs who cannot sign for themselves where court supervision is protective
4. Creditor disputes requiring court oversight

### Why Operators Usually Avoid This

Dependent administration is slow, expensive, and procedurally complex. Every sale requires a court hearing and approval. Timelines extend to years. Costs accumulate. For fractured equity purposes, dependent administration is rarely the right tool.

### When It's the Right Tool

Occasionally, a creditor-initiated proceeding to force administration on an uncooperative estate might use dependent administration. This is an advanced technique covered in later chapters.

## 9.10 Determination of Heirship Combined with Administration

Texas Estates Code § 202.005 allows a petition to combine a determination of heirship with the appointment of an administrator.

### When This Is Useful

1. The estate needs administration (property needs to be sold, debts paid, etc.)
2. AND heirship is uncertain or contested
3. Combining the two proceedings is more efficient than sequential filings

### Process

1. File a combined petition requesting both heirship determination and appointment of an administrator
2. Proceed through normal heirship proceeding (attorney ad litem, service, hearing)
3. Court simultaneously issues JDH and appoints administrator
4. Administrator then has authority to act as determined heirs' representative

### Cost and Timeline

Similar to JDH plus administration — $5,000–$12,000 total, 6–12 months timeline.

### When Operators Use This

Primarily when they want to leverage the administrator's single-signature authority (Chapter 18 technique) but heirship is contested or uncertain. The combined petition gets you both in one proceeding.

## 9.11 Decision Framework — Which Document When

| Fact Pattern | Document | Cost | Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|
| Simple intestate death, more than 5 years ago, clean family, modest value | Affidavit of Heirship | Under $1,000 | 1 week |
| Simple intestate death, within last 5 years, clean family — timeline flexible | AOH (file now, close when mature) | Under $1,000 | Years (maturity wait) |
| Simple intestate death, within last 5 years, clean family — timeline matters | Judgment Declaring Heirship | $3,500–$8,500 | 4–6 months |
| Simple estate, testate, clean will, limited debts | Muniment of Title | $1,500–$3,500 | 60–120 days |
| Small intestate estate under $75K, no homestead concerns | Small Estate Affidavit | $1,500–$3,000 | 30–90 days |
| Complex fact pattern, multiple potential outside children, missing heirs | Judgment Declaring Heirship | $3,500–$8,500+ (including ad litem) | 4–8 months |
| High-value property ($500K+), any complexity in heirship | Judgment Declaring Heirship | $3,500–$8,500+ | 4–8 months |
| Testate estate with significant administration needs | Formal Probate — Independent Administration | $3,000–$10,000 | 6 months to 2 years |
| Intestate estate where you want administrator authority for Chapter 18 technique | Determination of Heirship Combined with Administration | $5,000–$12,000 | 6–12 months |

## 9.12 The Cost-Benefit Decision

The hierarchy of heirship documents lets you match the tool to the fact pattern. The question isn't "which document is best?" but "which document is cheapest and fastest while still satisfying the title underwriter?"

### The General Principle

Start light, escalate only as required. If an AOH will work, use an AOH. If the title company accepts it and the fact pattern supports it, don't spend $8,000 on a JDH you don't need.

### When to Escalate

1. Title underwriter explicitly requires higher-authority document
2. Fact pattern has complexities an AOH can't cover
3. Timeline pressure makes AOH maturity unworkable
4. Value of the property warrants the additional cost
5. Multiple title companies have rejected the lower-level approach

### When to Start Heavy

For some fact patterns, starting with the heavier document is strategically correct. A contested heirship with missing heirs and potential outside children will almost certainly need a JDH. Starting there saves the wasted time and money of trying a lower-level approach first.

### The Economic Math

On a **$75,000 spread deal:**

1. AOH: $1,000 cost, negligible impact on net
2. Muniment: $3,000, small impact
3. JDH: $6,500, meaningful but not deal-breaking
4. Combined probate: $10,000, significant

On a **$200,000 spread deal**, all of these are small relative to the deal size and you should use whichever works best for the fact pattern without over-optimizing on cost.

On a **$40,000 spread deal** (if you're working sub-minimum deals early in your career), even AOH at $1,000 is material, and a JDH would eat a quarter of your profit. Pick accordingly.

## 9.13 The Judgment Declaring Heirship in Depth

Because the JDH is so important for complex deals, here is additional detail on how to work with one effectively.

### Who Can File a JDH Petition

1. Any heir of the decedent
2. Any creditor of the estate
3. Any person whose interest in the decedent's estate would be affected by the determination

As an operator, you may not qualify to file directly — but an heir you've acquired from can file, or a creditor (which you might become, through certain strategies covered in Part V) can file.

### Information Required in the Petition

1. Identification of the decedent and the petitioner's relationship
2. Statement of all known heirs and their addresses
3. Description of estate property requiring heirship determination
4. Statement of diligence performed in identifying heirs
5. Request for determination of heirship

### The Diligent Search Requirement

The court expects you to have made reasonable efforts to identify all potential heirs before filing. Reasonable effort typically includes:

1. Genealogical research
2. Obituary searches
3. Birth and marriage record searches
4. Skip tracing for known relatives
5. Inquiries with extended family members
6. Verification of any claimed outside children

Document this work. The attorney ad litem will want to see it, and the judge may ask questions about the diligence performed.

### The Attorney Ad Litem's Role

The ad litem is appointed to protect unknown and missing heirs. They:

1. Review the petition and supporting materials
2. Conduct independent investigation
3. Interview witnesses if appropriate
4. Search for missing heirs the petitioner didn't find
5. Recommend to the court whether the claimed heirship is correct

A good ad litem adds real value — they catch heirs that operators and attorneys missed. A lazy ad litem just rubber-stamps the filing. Quality varies.

### Service on Known and Unknown Heirs

1. **Known heirs:** served by personal service, certified mail, or publication if addresses are unknown
2. **Unknown heirs:** served by publication (notice in a newspaper for a specified period)

The court won't issue a JDH until all required service has been completed.

### The Hearing

The hearing may be in person or by affidavit, depending on the court and the complexity. Witnesses may testify. The attorney ad litem presents their findings. The judge reviews evidence and issues the judgment.

### The Judgment Itself

The JDH specifies:

1. Identity of the decedent and date of death
2. Names of all legal heirs
3. Each heir's relationship to the decedent
4. Each heir's fractional share of the estate

Once recorded, the JDH is authoritative for all future title purposes.

## 9.14 Practical Workflow — Getting Heirship Documents Into a Deal

Here's how heirship document timing typically works in a deal:

### Early in the Deal (Week 1–4)

1. Identify the decedent and the need for heirship establishment
2. Begin genealogical research
3. Identify heirs and witnesses
4. Evaluate fact-pattern complexity
5. Decide which heirship document to pursue

### Middle of the Deal (Week 4–12)

1. If AOH: Draft, sign, record
2. If Small Estate Affidavit: File with court, obtain order
3. If Muniment of Title: File, attend hearing, obtain order
4. If JDH: File petition, manage service, work with ad litem, attend hearing
5. Continue acquiring interests from heirs in parallel

### Late in the Deal (Week 12–20)

1. Obtain final heirship document
2. Provide to title company
3. Title company updates Schedule C
4. Resolve any remaining title requirements
5. Close the deal

### For JDH-Dependent Deals, Timeline Is Longer

1. JDH petition filed at Week 6
2. Service completed at Week 10
3. Hearing scheduled at Week 16
4. Judgment entered at Week 18–20
5. Closing at Week 22–26

The longer timeline means you need to manage cash flow and pipeline accordingly.

## 9.15 Key Takeaways for Operators

**The hierarchy ranges from cheap/fast to expensive/slow.** AOH → Small Estate Affidavit → Muniment of Title → JDH → Formal Probate. Match the tool to the fact pattern.

**AOH is your default for simple cases.** It's cheap, fast, and works when the fact pattern is clean and the AOH is mature.

**JDH is your tool for complex or contested cases.** The court's involvement and the attorney ad litem's verification produce the strongest heirship documentation.

**Muniment of title is elegant for uncontested testate estates.** When a will exists and no administration is needed, muniment is often the right call.

**Know your title underwriter's preferences.** Different underwriters accept different documents in different situations. Build this knowledge into your deal selection.

**Document your due diligence.** Regardless of which tool you choose, maintain the paper trail supporting your heirship conclusions. Title examiners and attorneys ad litem will ask for it.

**Timeline matters.** AOH in a simple case can be completed in a week; JDH in a complex case takes 4–8 months. Factor timeline into your pipeline and underwriting.

**Combined proceedings can be efficient.** Determination of Heirship Combined with Administration gets you both in a single case when both are needed.
