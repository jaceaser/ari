## The Uniform Partition of Heirs Property Act — What Operators Need to Know

The Uniform Partition of Heirs Property Act (UPHPA) was promulgated by the Uniform Law Commission in 2010 to address the vulnerability of heirs property — real estate inherited by multiple family members who never formally probated the estate or formalized their ownership — to forced partition sales that stripped families of property at below-market prices.

The act creates three key protections that materially affect fractured-equity deal structure in adopting states:

1. **Right of first refusal.** When a non-family co-tenant seeks partition by sale, the other co-tenants have a right to purchase the petitioning co-tenant's interest at a fair value determined by the court, preventing forced sale to outside parties.

2. **Mandatory independent appraisal.** Before the court can order a partition by sale, it must appoint an independent appraiser to determine the fair market value of the property.

3. **Preference for partition in kind.** Courts must consider whether partition in kind (physical division) is feasible and must weigh non-economic factors — including the longstanding family use of the property, sentimental attachment, and the practicality of dividing the land — before ordering a sale.

These protections do not prevent partition. They change the timeline, cost, and structure of partition in states that have adopted the act. Operators working in UPHPA states must account for these elements in their underwriting and deal structure.

## UPHPA Adoption Status — Fifty-State Survey

*Status date: April 20, 2026*

The UPHPA has been adopted in a growing number of states. Adoption is ongoing — new states continue to consider and pass the act. The following reflects known adoption status as of the status date above. **Always verify current status with local counsel before relying on this survey in any specific transaction.**

### States That Have Adopted the UPHPA

The following states have enacted the UPHPA or a substantially similar heirs property partition reform statute:

Alabama, Alaska, Arkansas, California, Colorado, Connecticut, Florida, Georgia, Hawaii, Illinois, Iowa, Kentucky, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, Mississippi, Montana, Nebraska, Nevada, New Mexico, New York, North Carolina, North Dakota, Ohio, Oklahoma, Oregon, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, South Carolina, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Utah, Vermont, Virginia, Washington, West Virginia, Wisconsin

*Note: Texas adopted the UPHPA effective September 1, 2021. Partition actions filed on or after that date in Texas involving heirs property are subject to UPHPA protections.*

### States That Have Not Yet Adopted the UPHPA

In states without UPHPA adoption, traditional partition law applies. A co-tenant seeking partition by sale is generally entitled to it as a matter of right, without a mandatory appraisal or right of first refusal for other co-tenants.

Operators should verify current legislative status in any state not listed above — adoption is ongoing, and this survey may not reflect the most recent legislative sessions.

### States With Material Modifications

Some states adopted the UPHPA with modifications to the uniform text. These modifications may affect:

- The definition of "heirs property" (some states use narrower or broader definitions)
- The right-of-first-refusal mechanics (some states altered the price-matching procedure)
- The appraisal process (some states modified who appoints the appraiser or the timing)
- The factors courts must consider in partition-in-kind analysis

When working in a state that has adopted the UPHPA, always confirm whether the state adopted the uniform act verbatim or with modifications. Local counsel will know.

## Practical Implications for Fractured-Equity Operators

### In UPHPA States

**Partition by sale is not automatic.** The court must go through the appraisal process and consider partition in kind before ordering a sale. Plan for additional time — typically 60 to 120 additional days compared to non-UPHPA partition timelines, depending on the court and appraiser availability.

**The right of first refusal changes acquisition structure.** If you acquire a fractional interest and then file for partition, the other co-tenants have the right to buy your interest at the appraised value before the property is sold to a third party. This is not necessarily a problem — it means the other heirs must pay you a fair price or accept the sale — but it affects how you structure the deal and communicate with the family.

**Partition in kind is a real possibility.** In UPHPA states, courts take the partition-in-kind analysis seriously. If the property is divisible, the court may order a physical split rather than a sale. Underwrite accordingly — if partition in kind would produce a parcel of land rather than a cash distribution, that is the exit.

**Heirs property definition matters.** UPHPA protections apply only to property that qualifies as "heirs property" — real property co-owned by persons who are each relatives of the other co-owners, where the ownership passed to them through inheritance. If co-ownership arose through purchase or other means, UPHPA protections may not apply.

### In Non-UPHPA States

**Traditional partition rules apply.** A co-tenant can petition for partition as a matter of right. If partition in kind is not practicable, the court will order a partition by sale. There is no mandatory appraisal and no right of first refusal.

**The partition threat is more immediate.** In non-UPHPA states, the operator who holds a fractional interest can move more quickly to a forced sale outcome. This can accelerate negotiations with other heirs who prefer to sell voluntarily rather than face a court-ordered sale.

**Watch for UPHPA adoption.** If a state you work in is considering UPHPA adoption, pending legislation could change the rules for transactions you are currently structuring. Monitoring legislative activity in target states is part of the operator's market intelligence.
