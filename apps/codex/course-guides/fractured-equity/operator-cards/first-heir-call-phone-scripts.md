## Full Script Library

*These scripts are starting points. Adapt the wording to your voice, your company name, and the specific circumstances of each contact. A script delivered flat and robotically produces worse results than a conversation that follows the script's structure. Use these as a framework, then make them your own.*

---

### D.1 The Opening Phone Call

The first fifteen seconds determine whether the person engages or hangs up.

#### D.1.1 Contacting a Known Heir (Reaching a Relative First)

> "Hello, is this [name]? My name is [your name], and I'm calling from [your company] in [city]. I'm trying to reach your [relationship — cousin, brother, aunt, grandfather]. There is some family property business involving his name, and I'd like to speak with him directly if I can. This isn't collections, he isn't in trouble, and he isn't being sued. It's about a property matter that may **actually benefit** him. Would you happen to know how to reach him, or know someone who might?"

**Why this works:**
- Identifies you by name, company, and city — builds credibility immediately
- Names the relationship you're aware of — shows you've done homework
- Pre-empts the three fears: debt collection, legal trouble, lawsuits
- Frames the matter as potentially beneficial to the person
- Ends with an open-ended question that invites cooperation rather than demanding information

#### D.1.2 Contacting the Target Directly

> "Hello, is this [name]? My name is [your name], and I'm calling from [your company] in [city]. I believe you may be an heir to a property in [county, state] that belonged to [deceased person's name]. Is that a name that means something to you? If it does, I'd like to explain the situation — this is about **a property** matter, it isn't collections or legal trouble, and it's something that may **actually benefit** you. Do you have a few minutes?"

**Key variations:**
- If the person confirms the name: proceed to explain the property situation, the operator's role, and the proposed path forward
- If the person hesitates: offer to call back at a better time, or to send written information they can review
- If the person is suspicious: acknowledge the suspicion, offer to provide verification information (company address, attorney contact, references), invite them to call you back after verifying
- If the person denies the connection: thank them for their time, verify once more that you have the right person, and close politely

#### D.1.3 Contacting a Defunct-LLC Member

> "Hello, this is [your name] with [your company]. I work with property owners whose LLCs have gone into forfeiture. I see that [LLC name] is currently in forfeiture at the Texas Comptroller and that it owns a property at [address]. I wanted to reach out because this situation means the LLC cannot currently sell that property without going through a cleanup process. I'm not with the state, I'm not **collections**, and you're not in trouble. I buy properties in this kind of **situation** and I handle the cleanup as part of the transaction. Would you be open to a brief conversation about your specific situation? Whatever we discuss, you would want to run it by your own attorney before **committing** to anything."

#### D.1.4 Contacting a Dormant-Trust Trustee

> "Hello, this is [your name] with [your company]. I believe you are named as trustee of the [trust name], which holds a property at [address]. I work with trustees whose trusts have become inactive — where the trust still holds **property** but the administration has not been updated in a while. I'm not with the state or any enforcement agency. I buy properties in this kind of **situation** and I handle the work that goes into bringing the trust administration current so the sale can happen cleanly. Would you be open to a brief conversation about your specific situation? Whatever we discuss, you would want to run it by your own attorney before **committing** to anything."

#### D.1.5 Contacting a Trust Beneficiary

> "Hello, this is [your name] with [your company]. I believe you are a beneficiary of the [trust name] created by [grantor's name]. The trust holds a property at [address], and the administration appears to be inactive. I work with beneficiaries in this kind of situation. I buy properties held by inactive trusts and handle the work required to bring the trust into a position to sell. In many cases beneficiaries are not aware of their interest until something brings it to their attention. Would you be open to a brief conversation about your situation? You would want to verify anything I tell you with your own attorney before acting on it."

#### D.1.6 Contacting Heirs of a Deceased Member or Beneficiary

> "Hello, this is [your name] with [your company]. First let me say I'm sorry for your loss — I understand that [decedent's name] passed away in [year]. I'm reaching out because [decedent's name] was [describe role — e.g., 'a member of an LLC that owned real property in (county, state)' or 'a beneficiary of a trust that owns property in (county, state)']. When someone passes away, their interest in that kind of asset passes to their heirs, which may include you. The situation may require some work to resolve — there are some administrative and legal steps involved — and I specialize in handling that kind of work and buying properties in this kind of situation. I wanted to reach out to explain the situation and see whether you'd be open to a brief conversation. This isn't urgent, there's no pressure, and anything we discuss you would want to verify with your own attorney."

### D.2 Follow-Up Calls

#### D.2.1 Follow-Up After Initial Contact

> "Hi [name], this is [your name] with [your company]. We spoke briefly last [day/week] about the family property matter. I wanted to check in and see if you've had a chance to think about it, discuss it with anyone, or consult with an attorney. I've also done some additional research on my end and have some information I could share with you when you're ready. Is this a good time, or would another time work better?"

#### D.2.2 Scheduling an In-Person or Video Meeting

> "I think this might be easier to discuss in more detail. Would you be open to meeting — either in person if you're close to [city], or by video call if that's easier? I can bring [materials — maps of the property, title information, a summary of the legal situation] and walk through everything carefully. This gives us more time than a phone call and lets you ask every question you have. There's still no commitment — just a chance to understand the full picture. What does your schedule look like this week or next?"

### D.3 Introducing Deal Terms

#### D.3.1 Presenting Initial Terms

> "Here's what I can offer for your interest. Based on the property's approximate current market value of [amount] and your fractional ownership of [fraction], and accounting for the work that will go into resolving the situation — [briefly describe: legal costs, skip-trace work, title clearing, closing costs] — I can offer [amount] for your interest. I'd pay that at closing, and I would handle **all of** the cleanup work at my cost. You wouldn't need to write any checks, hire any lawyers yourself, or spend any time on the administrative side of this. If you agree in principle, I'd send you a written agreement that your attorney could review before you sign anything. How does that sound as a starting point?"

#### D.3.2 Handling a Counter-Offer

> "I appreciate you thinking about that. Let me explain where the numbers come from on my side. [Walk through the cost structure — property value, cleanup costs, risk, reasonable margin for the work involved.] Given those costs, if I went higher than [your maximum], the deal wouldn't work for me. What I can do is [propose any specific movement — a slightly higher price, faster closing, covering a specific expense, flexibility on timing]. Is there anything on your side that matters more than the headline price — timing, a specific use of the funds, protection for other family members? Sometimes there's room to restructure that helps both of us."

#### D.3.3 Handling a Refusal

> "I understand. Thank you for considering it. I want to respect your decision. If you change your mind down the road — whether it's three months from now or three years — my number is the same. If circumstances change or you want to revisit, I'm happy to talk again. I'd also ask: if you don't want to sell, would it be helpful for me to share any information I've gathered about the property and the situation? Sometimes the research is useful to the family even if the deal doesn't happen. Is there anything like that I could share with you?"

### D.4 Difficult Conversations

#### D.4.1 When the Person Is Suspicious

> "I understand — a stranger calling about family property that you may not have known about is going to sound suspicious, and it should. I'd feel the same way. Let me give you some information you can use to verify who I am. My company is [name]. Our office address is [address]. Our attorney who works on these matters is [attorney name] at [firm]. You can look us up, check the public records I'm describing, call my attorney directly. I'd rather you take the time to verify than make any decision without doing that. Please take a week, do whatever checking you need to do, and call me back when you're comfortable. Here's my number again: [number]. There's no time pressure on this."

#### D.4.2 When Family Members Disagree

> "It sounds like there's some disagreement in the family about what to do. That's common — most family property situations have some of that. Here's what I'd suggest. Before any decision gets made, have everyone who's involved get the same information and have a chance to talk it through together. I'm happy to speak with everyone — separately or together — and answer questions. If there are family members who want their own attorneys to review anything, that's fine. I would rather take more time and have the family comfortable with the decision than push something through that leaves resentment. What's the best way to involve the others?"

#### D.4.3 When the Person Is Grieving or Stressed

> "I can hear that this is a hard time. I don't want to add to that. Let's pause this conversation. I'll step back and give you time. When you're ready to continue — whether that's next week or three months from now — please call me. My number is [number]. And if in the meantime you have any questions or want any information without any commitment on your end, call me and I'll answer what I can. Take care of yourself first. The property isn't going anywhere."

#### D.4.4 When You Must Deliver Bad News

> "[Name], I need to let you know about something that's come up. [Explain the issue clearly and briefly — what changed, why, and what it means.] I wanted to tell you directly rather than let you hear about it from someone else or find out when you're expecting one thing and I deliver another. Here's what I'm proposing we do about it: [specific adjustment]. I know this isn't what we originally discussed, and I understand if you need time to think about it or talk to your attorney. What are your thoughts?"

I am writing because I believe you may be an heir to real property located in [county, state] that belonged to [decedent's full name]. Our research indicates that [decedent's name] was your [relationship], and that upon his or her passing, you may have inherited an interest in real property that has not yet been addressed.

>
> Before deciding anything, you should feel free to consult with your own attorney about the matter. I am happy to cooperate with your attorney and to provide any verification information you need.
>
> Thank you for your time.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Your Name]
> [Title]
> [Company]
> [Phone]
> [Email]

> [Date]
> [Name]
> [Address]
>
> Dear [Name],
>

>

>
> I would like to discuss this with you. My phone number is [number] and my email is [email]. Before making any decision, you would want to consult with your own attorney. I am happy to coordinate with your attorney and provide whatever verification information would be helpful.
>
> Thank you for your time.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Your Name]

> [Date]
> [Name]

In the cases I handle, I buy the property and coordinate this preliminary work at my cost.
>
> I would like to discuss this with you briefly. My phone number is [number] and my email is [email]. Please consult with your own attorney before making any decision. I am happy to coordinate with your attorney and provide verification information.
>
> Thank you for your time.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Your Name]

>
> Thank you again.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Your Name]

### D.9 General Principles for All Outreach

**Identify yourself clearly.** Full name, company, city. Never anonymous; never using a persona or fake name. Your credibility starts with transparency about who you are.

**Pre-empt common fears.** Debt collection, legal trouble, fraud, scams. Saying explicitly that the call is none of these lowers defensiveness.

**Keep the first communication short.** Under ninety seconds on a first phone call; under one page for a first letter. The point is to open the door, not to close the deal.

**Invite verification.** Always give the person the information they need to verify your legitimacy. Never pressure them to commit without verifying.

**Respect no.** When the person declines, accept gracefully. Leave the door open for future contact. Do not escalate, argue, or pressure.

**Document every contact.** Log every call, every email, every letter with dates, content summary, and outcome. The log becomes part of your diligent search documentation.

**Honor commitments.** If you say you will call back on a specific day, call back that day. Dropped follow-ups are the single most common way deals die.

**Match your tone to the situation.** A grieving family member needs gentleness. A sophisticated investor needs crispness. A skeptical relative needs patience and documentation.

**Never misrepresent.** Do not claim to be anyone you are not. Do not overstate what you can offer. Do not hide relevant facts.

**Always recommend independent legal review.** Every written and verbal communication should include some form of "before you decide anything, you should consult with your own attorney." This protects the counterparty and protects the operator from later claims of pressure or misrepresentation.

**Adapting for Spanish-speaking counterparties.** When working with Spanish-speaking counterparties — particularly common in Texas, California, Florida, Arizona, and New Mexico — have the scripts translated and conduct the conversations in Spanish. If you do not speak Spanish fluently, engage a trusted bilingual colleague or translator rather than trying to conduct sensitive conversations in a language you don't command. Half-fluent Spanish delivered in a family property conversation often produces misunderstandings that damage the deal.
