## How to Use This Appendix

Appendix D covered the standard phone scripts and correspondence templates for most fractured-equity outreach. This appendix covers specialized situations — when the target is incarcerated, in a care facility, speaks a different language, is abroad, requires attorney-to-attorney communication, or presents other circumstances that demand a different kind of letter. Each situation has its own rhythm and its own etiquette. The wrong tone can shut down a contact that the right tone would have opened.

As with all templates in the Appendices, these letters are starting points. Adapt them to the specific circumstances, the specific individual, and the specific case. Work with counsel on any letter that has legal implications. For letters in languages other than English, engage a qualified translator who can produce a natural, culturally appropriate version — machine translation rarely produces the tone and register needed for sensitive communications.

## E.1 Letter to an Incarcerated Heir

Incarcerated heirs are frequently easy to contact because their location is known and they receive mail regularly. A letter to a correctional facility typically reaches the recipient within a week. The tone should be respectful, clear, and attentive to the fact that the recipient's circumstances limit how they can respond.

### E.1.1 Initial Letter to an Incarcerated Heir

---

[Date]

[Incarcerated Person's Full Name]
[Inmate Number]
[Facility Name]
[Facility Address]

Dear [Name],

My name is [your name], and I work with [your company] in [city, state]. I am writing to you because I believe you are an heir to real property located in [county, state] that belonged to [decedent's full name], who was your [relationship]. [Decedent's name] passed away on [date], and under [state] law, his or her interest in real property passed to the surviving heirs, which may include you.

I am not with any government agency, I am not a debt collector, and I am not representing anyone in a lawsuit against you. I buy interests in real property in situations where the title needs to be cleared, and I handle the legal and administrative work as part of the transaction. If you have an interest in this property, I may be able to offer you a cash payment in exchange for your interest.

I understand that your circumstances make it difficult to handle correspondence easily. I have enclosed a stamped envelope addressed to me so you can respond without needing to arrange postage on your own.

If you have an attorney representing you for other matters, or if you have a family member you would prefer I coordinate with, please write their name and contact information in your reply and I will reach out to them.

I would appreciate your response. Take the time you need — there is no deadline. If you want more information before deciding anything, write to me and I will send additional materials. Before signing any agreement, you should review it with an attorney, and I will cooperate with whatever attorney you choose.

Thank you for your time.

Sincerely,

[Your Full Name]
[Title]
[Company Name]
[Company Address]
[Phone]
[Email]

---

**Notes for Letters to Incarcerated Recipients**

1. Always include a stamped, self-addressed envelope. Incarcerated people often lack easy access to postage.
2. Print the letter on standard paper — some facilities reject mail with unusual materials, staples, glitter, adhesives, or foreign stickers.
3. Keep content straightforward — facilities may screen mail, and overly legal or complex language can delay delivery.
4. Do not include photographs, cards, or other items that might trigger additional screening.
5. Address the envelope exactly as instructed by the facility — include the inmate number prominently.
6. Allow at least two to four weeks for a response; facility mail is slower than regular mail.
7. Follow up with a second letter if no response within 60 days.

### E.1.2 Follow-Up Letter When Incarcerated Heir Has Responded

---

[Date]

[Incarcerated Person's Full Name]
[Inmate Number]
[Facility Name]
[Facility Address]

Dear [Name],

Thank you for your response dated [date]. I appreciate you taking the time to write back.

I understand that you are interested in learning more / you have questions / you would like me to work with [person you named]. To respond to what you asked:

[Address their specific questions and requests. Keep the response organized and easy to follow. If they requested specific information, provide it clearly.]

Here is what I am proposing as a next step: [explain clearly — whether you will be sending a more detailed proposal, arranging for them to speak with an attorney they can trust, coordinating with a family member they named, or other specific action].

I have again enclosed a stamped envelope addressed to me. Please write back when you are ready, and include any additional questions or concerns.

Thank you.

Sincerely,

[Your Full Name]

## E.2 Letters to Care Facility Staff

When the target is in a nursing home, assisted living, rehabilitation center, or psychiatric facility, direct outreach to the target is often impractical. The letter goes to facility social work staff or administration. Facility letters must respect HIPAA and the facility's obligations — you are not asking them to confirm or deny that someone is a resident, you are asking them to forward a letter if the person is a resident, or to pass your contact information to the person's family or legal representative.

### E.2.1 Letter to Facility Social Work Department

---

[Date]

Social Work Department
[Facility Name]
[Facility Address]

Re: Request to Forward Correspondence to a Possible Resident

Dear Social Work Department,

I am writing to request your assistance with a family property matter that involves someone I believe may be a resident at your facility. I am not requesting any confidential information, and I understand you cannot confirm or deny whether a specific person is a resident.

My request is simply this: if [Resident's Full Name] is currently a resident at your facility, I would appreciate your department forwarding the enclosed letter to them or, if appropriate, to the family contact or legal representative of record.

The letter pertains to real property located at [address] in [county, state]. [Resident's name] appears to be an heir to that property based on my research into the chain of title. Their participation in a property transaction would be financially beneficial to them, but without contact I cannot discuss the matter with them or their representatives.

I am enclosing:

- The letter to be forwarded to [Resident's name] if he or she is a resident
- A stamped, self-addressed envelope for any response your department may wish to send me
- My full contact information below for any questions

If [Resident's name] is not a resident, please feel free to disregard this request or return the enclosed letter unopened.

I understand that you have professional obligations to your residents and to HIPAA requirements. I am not asking you to violate any of those obligations. I am only asking for your help in facilitating contact if it is appropriate to do so.

Thank you for your time and assistance.

Sincerely,

[Your Full Name]
[Title]
[Company]
[Address]
[Phone]
[Email]

### E.2.2 Letter to Facility Administrator (Follow-Up)

When an initial letter to the social work department has produced no response after a reasonable period, a follow-up letter to the facility administrator — often on attorney letterhead — sometimes produces results the social work department letter alone did not.

---

[Date]

Administrator
[Facility Name]
[Facility Address]

Re: Real Property Matter Involving Possible Resident

Dear Administrator,

This firm represents [your name or company] in a real property matter in [county, state]. We have reason to believe that [Resident's Full Name] may be a resident at your facility and has an inherited interest in real property that cannot be resolved without their participation or the participation of their legal representative.

Approximately [number] weeks ago, we sent correspondence to your Social Work Department requesting that, if [Resident's name] is a resident, our letter to them be forwarded or that their family or legal representative be given our contact information. We have not received a response.

We are not requesting any confidential information about whether [Resident's name] is a resident. We are requesting only that, if [Resident's name] is in fact a resident, your facility facilitate one of the following:

1. Forwarding of our correspondence to [Resident's name] for their review;
2. Provision of our contact information to [Resident's name]'s legal representative, guardian, or designated family contact; or
3. Direct communication from that representative to this office to discuss the matter.

We recognize that your facility has HIPAA obligations and resident privacy obligations that must be respected. Our request is limited to facilitating contact, not to disclosure of any protected information.

Enclosed is a stamped, self-addressed envelope for any response. If [Resident's name] is not a resident, please disregard this letter.

Thank you for your attention to this matter.

Sincerely,

[Attorney Name]
[Firm Name]
[Address]
[Phone]
[Email]

---

**Notes for Care Facility Correspondence**

1. Always emphasize that you are not asking for protected information.
2. Acknowledge the facility's HIPAA and resident privacy obligations explicitly.
3. Provide a stamped self-addressed envelope for easy response.
4. Include the enclosed letter to the resident in a sealed envelope addressed to the resident, so it can be forwarded unopened if the facility chooses.
5. Allow at least 30 days for an initial response; 60 days before following up.
6. Follow up to the administrator (often through counsel) rather than escalating to the social work department.
7. Never characterize the matter as urgent or threatening — facility staff have many demands on their time and respond better to respectful, low-pressure requests.

## E.3 Spanish-Language Letters

Spanish-language correspondence is common in Texas, California, Florida, Arizona, New Mexico, and increasingly in other states with growing Hispanic populations. Have letters professionally translated — do not rely on machine translation for sensitive communications. A natural, culturally appropriate Spanish letter conveys respect that a literal translation does not.

### E.3.1 Initial Letter to a Spanish-Speaking Heir (Spanish)

---

[Fecha]

[Nombre Completo]
[Dirección]

Estimado/a [Nombre],

Mi nombre es [su nombre], y trabajo con [compañía] en [ciudad, estado]. Le escribo porque creo que usted puede ser heredero/a de una propiedad inmueble en [condado, estado] que perteneció a [nombre del difunto], quien fue su [relación]. [Nombre del difunto] falleció el [fecha], y según la ley de [estado], su interés en propiedades inmuebles pasó a sus herederos, entre los cuales podría encontrarse usted.

No soy parte de ninguna agencia gubernamental, no soy cobrador de deudas, y no represento a nadie que esté demandándolo. Yo compro intereses en propiedades inmuebles en situaciones donde el título necesita ser aclarado, y me encargo del trabajo legal y administrativo como parte de la transacción. Si usted tiene un interés en esta propiedad, puede que yo le ofrezca un pago en efectivo a cambio de ese interés.

Me gustaría conversar brevemente con usted sobre esto. Mi número de teléfono es [número] y mi correo electrónico es [correo]. Si prefiere, puedo enviarle información adicional por escrito antes de hablar.

Antes de tomar cualquier decisión, le recomiendo que consulte con su propio abogado. Estoy dispuesto a cooperar con su abogado y proporcionar toda la información de verificación que necesiten.

Gracias por su tiempo.

Atentamente,

[Su Nombre Completo]
[Título]
[Compañía]
[Teléfono]
[Correo Electrónico]

### E.3.2 Letter to a Mexican Parish (Spanish)

Parish letters to churches in Mexico and other Latin American countries require specific cultural conventions — a respectful salutation to the priest, an explicit request framed with humility, acknowledgment of the parish's resources, and a small offering to support the parish.

---

[Fecha]

Muy Reverendo Padre
[Parroquia]
[Dirección]
[Ciudad, Estado, País]

Muy estimado Padre,

Le escribo para solicitar respetuosamente su ayuda en la búsqueda de registros sacramentales de un familiar mío.

Nombre completo: [nombre del familiar]
Fecha aproximada de nacimiento o bautismo: [fecha]
Nombres de los padres: [padre] y [madre]
Lugar de nacimiento (aproximado): [pueblo o ciudad]

Estoy buscando los siguientes registros, si existen en los archivos de su parroquia:

- Acta de bautismo de [nombre del familiar]
- Acta de matrimonio si se casó en esa parroquia
- Acta de defunción si falleció en esa parroquia
- Cualquier otra información sobre la familia que pueda estar disponible

El motivo de esta búsqueda es una cuestión de herencia familiar en los Estados Unidos. Mi familia ha intentado localizar a familiares que pueden haber permanecido en [pueblo/región], y los registros parroquiales son nuestra mejor esperanza para establecer con certeza los detalles de la familia.

Adjunto una pequeña ofrenda para la parroquia como señal de mi gratitud, así como un sobre con estampilla y dirección para su respuesta. Si los registros requieren un trabajo más extenso para obtener copias certificadas, por favor indíqueme cuál sería el costo y con gusto lo cubriré.

Cualquier información que pueda proporcionar me sería de gran ayuda, y estaré profundamente agradecido. Que Dios le bendiga a usted y a la parroquia.

Atentamente,

[Su Nombre Completo]
[Dirección]
[Teléfono]
[Correo Electrónico]

---

**Notes for Parish Correspondence**

1. Write in Spanish when writing to parishes in Mexico, Central America, or other Spanish-speaking regions — have the letter professionally translated and reviewed by a native Spanish speaker familiar with religious conventions.
2. Address the priest with appropriate deference — "Muy Reverendo Padre" or "Muy estimado Padre."
3. Include a small monetary offering to the parish — even $10 to $25 demonstrates respect and gratitude. For international parishes, check on the most effective way to send this (sometimes money orders in pesos, sometimes a designated charity contribution, sometimes wire transfer).
4. Include a stamped self-addressed envelope or international reply coupons for the response.
5. Provide full biographical details to help the parish search their records efficiently.
6. Offer to cover any costs for certified copies.
7. Allow four to eight weeks for an international response.
8. Follow up with gratitude if the parish responds helpfully — respect and appreciation build relationships that may help with future searches.

## E.4 Attorney-to-Attorney Letters

When a counterparty has legal representation, communications flow attorney-to-attorney. These letters have a different register — professional, precise, focused on substance, and directed at resolving the matter efficiently.

### E.4.1 Initial Attorney-to-Attorney Letter

---

[Date]

[Counterparty's Attorney Name]
[Firm Name]
[Address]

Re: [Case identifier or property description] — Proposed Transaction

Dear [Attorney Name],

This firm represents [client name], who has an interest in acquiring real property located at [address] in [county, state]. Our review of the chain of title indicates that your client, [counterparty name], holds [describe interest — fractional interest as heir, membership interest in defunct LLC, beneficial interest in dormant trust, etc.].

We write to propose a transaction in which [client name] would acquire [describe what is being acquired] for a purchase price of [amount]. The transaction structure would include [brief description of structure — pathway being used, any cleanup work being funded by the acquirer, closing mechanics].

Enclosed are the following items for your review:

- A term sheet outlining the proposed transaction
- Supporting documentation regarding the chain of title
- [Any other relevant materials]

We would appreciate the opportunity to discuss this matter with you at your convenience. Please contact me at your earliest convenience to arrange a call.

We look forward to working cooperatively with you on this matter.

Very truly yours,

[Attorney Name]
[Firm Name]

---

### E.4.2 Letter to a Drafting Attorney Seeking Trust Instrument

When researching a dormant trust, the attorney who drafted the trust instrument is often the most likely source of a copy. A respectful attorney-to-attorney letter can produce cooperation that a cold call to the firm does not.

---

[Date]

[Attorney or Firm Name]
[Firm Address]

Re: Request for Information — [Trust Name] dated [Trust Date]

Dear [Attorney Name]:

This firm represents [client name] in connection with a real property matter that involves [Trust Name] dated [Trust Date]. Our research indicates that your firm may have drafted the trust instrument for [Grantor's Name], and we write to inquire about the possibility of obtaining information from your files.

The trust holds title to real property located at [address] in [county, state]. The trust administration appears to have become inactive, and our client is working with the [trustee and/or beneficiaries] to explore options for the property. A copy of the trust instrument would significantly aid our analysis and our ability to propose a constructive path forward.

We understand that your files may no longer be easily accessible, that fees may apply for retrieving archived documents, or that the file may have been transferred to another firm upon retirement or reorganization. We are prepared to cover reasonable costs for retrieval or certification of documents.

We have obtained authorization from [describe whoever has authorized you — the current trustee if identified, the beneficiaries, the grantor's estate, etc.] to request this information. A copy of that authorization is enclosed.

If you can assist, please contact me at your earliest convenience. If the file is no longer available, any information you might have about where it was transferred or how it might be located would be equally appreciated.

Thank you for your time.

Very truly yours,

[Attorney Name]

## E.5 Letter Requesting Trustee Resignation

Sometimes a trust's dormancy is caused by a trustee who is unable or unwilling to serve but who has not formally resigned. A respectful letter requesting formal resignation can unblock the situation.

### E.5.1 Letter to a Dormant Trustee

---

[Date]

[Trustee's Full Name]
[Address]

Re: [Trust Name] — Trustee Role

Dear [Name],

I am writing regarding your role as trustee of the [Trust Name] dated [date]. The trust holds title to real property located at [address] in [county, state], and based on a review of the records, it appears that the trust's administration has been inactive for some time.

I work with trust beneficiaries and property owners in situations where trust administration has become dormant. The beneficiaries of the [Trust Name] — [list beneficiaries if known] — have expressed interest in moving forward with a resolution of the trust property.

To proceed, we need clarity on your intentions with respect to the trusteeship. If you are willing and able to continue serving as trustee and to coordinate the resolution, we would be glad to work with you. If, on the other hand, you would prefer to resign so that a successor trustee can be appointed — either a person named in the trust instrument as a successor, a family member willing to serve, or a professional trustee — that would also allow the matter to move forward.

Enclosed are two documents for your consideration:

1. A proposed resignation document that would allow you to formally step down from the trusteeship if that is your preference. Your signature before a notary would complete the resignation. You should have your own attorney review this document before signing.
2. Information about the trust and the property for your reference.

I am not asking you to make an immediate decision. Take the time you need. If you want to discuss the matter first, please call me at [number] or have your attorney call me.

Thank you for your time.

Sincerely,

[Your Name]

## E.6 Defunct-LLC Specialized Letters

### E.6.1 Letter to a Registered Agent of a Defunct LLC

When the registered agent is a commercial service, a letter may produce information about who to contact — even if the agent cannot share client information directly, they can sometimes forward correspondence.

---

[Date]

[Registered Agent Name]
[Agent Address]

Re: [LLC Name] — Request to Forward Correspondence

Dear [Registered Agent]:

Your company serves as registered agent for [LLC Name], a Texas limited liability company currently in franchise tax forfeiture. The LLC holds title to real property located at [address] in [county, state].

I am attempting to contact the members or authorized representatives of [LLC Name] regarding a potential transaction involving the real property. I understand that your role as registered agent does not include disclosing client information to third parties, and I am not requesting any confidential information.

I am requesting only that, if you maintain current contact information for [LLC Name] or its members, you forward the enclosed letter to them or provide them with my contact information so they may reach out directly.

Enclosed:

- A letter to be forwarded to [LLC Name] or its members
- A stamped, self-addressed envelope for any response
- My full contact information

If the registered agent relationship has terminated or if you have no current forwarding information, please disregard this request or return the enclosed letter.

Thank you for your cooperation.

Sincerely,

[Your Name]

---

### E.6.2 Letter to Comptroller for Franchise Tax Payoff Information

This is ordinarily handled by your CPA, but operators should understand what goes into such a request. Once the LLC members have authorized inquiry, the CPA or attorney submits a request for the precise franchise tax balance.

---

[Date]

Texas Comptroller of Public Accounts
[Address for Franchise Tax Division]

Re: [LLC Name] — Taxpayer Number [if known] — Request for Account Status and Payoff Information

Dear Comptroller:

This office represents [LLC Name] in preparing for reinstatement after franchise tax forfeiture. In connection with that work, we request an account status statement and a precise payoff amount for all outstanding franchise tax obligations, penalties, interest, and fees.

Please provide:

- A current account status statement showing all years for which franchise tax reports remain unfiled;
- The balance of tax owed for each past-due year, if any;
- Penalties accrued for each past-due year;
- Interest accrued through the response date;
- Any filing fees or additional amounts necessary to bring the entity current;
- A total payoff amount valid for a reasonable period to permit reinstatement to proceed.

Enclosed is written authorization from [name of authorizing member or manager], in their capacity as [describe role], to receive this information on behalf of [LLC Name].

Please respond by mail, email, or fax using the contact information below. Thank you for your assistance.

Sincerely,

[CPA or Attorney Name]
[Firm]
[Contact Information]

## E.8 Respectful Decline and Closure Letters

### E.8.1 When You Are Declining to Pursue a Deal

Sometimes after diligence, the operator decides not to pursue a deal that looked promising initially. A clean decline preserves the relationship for possible future deals or referrals.

---

[Date]

[Counterparty Name]
[Address]

Dear [Name],

Thank you for the time you spent with me over the past [weeks/months] discussing the potential transaction involving the property at [address].

After completing my full review, I have determined that the transaction is not one I am able to move forward with at this time. The specific reasons include [briefly, honestly — e.g., "the franchise tax balance came in higher than my underwriting could support at the purchase price we had been discussing," or "the title issues turned out to be more complex than I can address within the timeframe available," or "the economics of the deal no longer work at the numbers we had been considering given the additional costs that emerged in diligence"].

I want to thank you for your openness and cooperation throughout our discussions. You were straightforward and professional in every interaction, and I regret that the deal did not come together.

If your situation changes, or if you would like recommendations for other operators who might be able to work with you on this matter, please feel free to contact me.

Thank you again.

Sincerely,

[Your Name]

---

### E.8.2 When Counterparty Has Declined

---

[Date]

[Counterparty Name]
[Address]

Dear [Name],

Thank you for letting me know that you have decided not to move forward with the transaction we had been discussing. I respect your decision and appreciate the time and consideration you gave to the matter.

I want you to know that the door remains open. If your circumstances change at any point in the future — whether that's six months from now or several years from now — please feel free to reach out. My contact information is below.

In the meantime, I want to thank you for the information and conversations we shared. If there is anything further I can do to be helpful — even absent a transaction — please let me know.

Best wishes.

Sincerely,

[Your Name]

## E.9 Principles for Specialized Correspondence

**Match the format to the recipient.** Incarcerated people need simple, clearly-formatted correspondence with self-addressed envelopes. Professional counterparties need letters that meet professional standards. Parishes need respectful salutations and cultural awareness.

**Always enclose a response mechanism.** Stamped self-addressed envelope, email address, phone number. Make responding as easy as possible.

**Respect the recipient's situation.** The person in a care facility, the incarcerated person, the grieving family member — each deserves care proportional to their circumstances.

**Avoid legal threats or pressure.** Specialized letters are relationship-builders. Pressure tactics destroy the relationship and usually the deal.

**Invite verification and counsel.** Every letter should include some form of "please have your own attorney review this" or "feel free to verify my company through public records."

**Keep letters brief.** One page, or two at most, for initial contact. Longer letters get set aside. Short letters get read.

**Use appropriate letterhead.** Attorney letters on attorney letterhead. Company letters on company letterhead. Personal letters on professional stationery.

**Translate with professionals, not with machines.** Letters in Spanish, French, Portuguese, or other languages should be professionally translated or reviewed by a native speaker familiar with the register needed.

**Document everything.** Every letter sent, every response received — all filed with the diligent search documentation. These records become evidence if the matter ever requires proof of outreach.

**Follow up only when appropriate.** One follow-up after reasonable time is usually appropriate. More than one follow-up on the same matter to the same person can become harassment. Know when to stop and move on.
