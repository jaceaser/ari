## How to Use This Template

This appendix provides a sample affidavit of heirship — a document used throughout Texas fractured-equity work to establish, on a non-judicial basis, the identity of the heirs of a deceased person. The affidavit is one of the most important documents in the operator's toolkit.

Affidavits of heirship are governed in Texas by Tex. Est. Code Chapter 203. Under § 203.001, an affidavit of heirship becomes prima facie evidence of the matters stated in it after being on file in the real property records for five years. Before five years, the affidavit has evidentiary weight but is more vulnerable to challenge. The quality of the affidavit — accuracy, completeness, specificity, and the credibility of the witnesses — determines how much it helps your title.

This template is a starting point, not a final document. Every affidavit must be adapted to the specific family, the specific decedent, and the specific property. Work with a qualified Texas attorney on the final form.

For operators working outside Texas: every state has an equivalent framework for affidavits of heirship or similar declarations. The names differ (heirship affidavit, affidavit of descent, declaration of heirs), the evidentiary weight differs, and the statutory requirements differ. Have counsel in your state provide an analogous template adapted to that state's framework.

## B.1 When to Use an Affidavit of Heirship

Affidavits of heirship are appropriate when:

1. A person has died without a will (intestate) and their heirs need to be identified for real property transactions
2. A person died with a will that was never probated, and more than four years have passed (the Texas deadline for formal probate), leaving affidavit of heirship as the practical path forward
3. Real property was owned by a decedent and the chain of title needs to show how ownership passed to the heirs
4. A prior property transaction in the chain of title involved an incomplete treatment of heirship that needs to be cured
5. Title insurance underwriting requires documentation of heirship as a condition of issuing a policy

Affidavits of heirship are **not** appropriate when:

1. The decedent left a will that should be probated
2. Heirs are disputed and a judicial determination of heirship is needed to resolve the dispute
3. The chain of title has other problems (forged deeds, fraudulent conveyances, competing claims) that an affidavit alone cannot cure
4. The value of the property and complexity of the family justify a formal Chapter 202 determination of heirship despite the additional cost

## B.2 Who Signs the Affidavit

Under Texas practice, an affidavit of heirship is typically signed by two disinterested witnesses — people who knew the decedent and the family but who have no financial stake in the outcome. "Disinterested" generally excludes:

1. The heirs themselves
2. Spouses of heirs
3. Business partners of heirs with respect to the property
4. Anyone else who benefits directly from the heirship being declared as stated

Appropriate witnesses are typically:

1. Longtime friends of the decedent or of the family
2. Neighbors who knew the family over an extended period
3. Former coworkers, former employers, clergy, or other community members
4. Family friends from the decedent's generation who knew the full family composition

The witnesses must have actual knowledge of the facts they are attesting to. They must have known the decedent personally, known the family, and known about the major events — marriages, births, deaths, divorces — that determine the heirship.

---

## B.3 Key Contents of a Thorough Affidavit

A well-prepared affidavit of heirship addresses:

1. Identity of the decedent — full name, residence, date and place of death
2. Marriages — each marriage of the decedent, with dates, names of spouses (including maiden names), and how each marriage ended (death of spouse, divorce, annulment)
3. Children — every child of the decedent, including children from every marriage and children born outside of marriage, with names, dates of birth, and current status (living, deceased, addresses if known)
4. Predeceased children — dates of their deaths and the identity of their children (grandchildren of the decedent who inherit by representation)
5. Parents — whether the decedent's parents predeceased him or her
6. Siblings — when the decedent had no surviving spouse, children, or parents, siblings and their descendants become relevant
7. Known adoptions — legally adopted children and children legally adopted out of the family
8. Statement confirming the witnesses knew the decedent and the family over a substantial period
9. Statement confirming the witnesses have no financial interest in the outcome
10. Property description — the real property to which the affidavit relates
11. Sworn oath and notarization

## B.5 Notes for Preparing the Affidavit

**Attach the Death Certificate**

A certified copy of the decedent's death certificate should be attached as an exhibit. This provides primary documentary evidence of the death and authenticates the date, place, and manner of death stated in the affidavit.

**Attach Additional Supporting Documents When Available**

1. Marriage certificates for the decedent's marriages
2. Divorce decrees for prior marriages that ended in divorce
3. Birth certificates of the decedent's children
4. Death certificates of any predeceased children or other deceased heirs
5. Adoption records when applicable

These documents are not required in every case, but attaching them strengthens the affidavit's credibility and reduces the likelihood of future challenge.

**Specificity Matters**

The most common weakness in affidavits of heirship is vagueness. Affidavits that say "the decedent had several children" or "the decedent may have had a previous marriage" invite challenge. Affidavits that say "the decedent had exactly three children, as follows: [list], and no other children" produce stronger evidence and are less vulnerable to later questions.

**Handle the Hard Cases Directly**

When the family facts include complexities — children born outside marriage, adoptions, prior marriages the family prefers to minimize, heirs who are estranged — address those facts directly and truthfully. An affidavit that glosses over or omits uncomfortable facts is the affidavit most likely to be unwound when the omitted heir surfaces. Texas law protects the rights of non-marital and adopted children; the affidavit must reflect actual facts, not the family's preferred narrative.

**Witness Selection**

Strong witnesses are the foundation of a strong affidavit. Select witnesses who:

1. Knew the decedent personally over an extended period — typically ten or more years
2. Are still alive and reachable for follow-up questions
3. Have reputations in the community that support their credibility
4. Have no financial stake in the outcome
5. Can articulate their knowledge clearly in conversation with an attorney or title examiner

Witnesses who are themselves elderly or in declining health are not ideal because their availability to answer later questions is limited. When possible, include at least one witness whose life expectancy extends several years.

**Execution**

The affidavit is executed before a notary public. Both witnesses must be physically present at the time of signing and must swear or affirm the truthfulness of the statements. The notary verifies the identity of the witnesses and completes the acknowledgment.

**Recording**

Once executed, the affidavit is filed in the real property records of the county where the property is located. Filing fees are typically modest ($25 to $75 depending on the county). The county clerk stamps the affidavit with the filing date and book-and-page or instrument number, which becomes part of the permanent real property record. This starts the five-year clock under Tex. Est. Code § 203.001 after which the affidavit becomes prima facie evidence.

## B.6 Adapting for Other States

Every state has an equivalent to the affidavit of heirship. Names and specifics vary:

1. **California** — Affidavit of Death of Joint Tenant; Petition for Succession of Real Property (small estate)
2. **Florida** — Affidavit of Heirs used in summary administration
3. **Georgia** — Affidavit of Descent
4. **Arizona** — Affidavit of Heirs under A.R.S. § 14-3971
5. **North Carolina** — Affidavit of Descent
6. **Tennessee** — Affidavit of Heirship
7. **Other states** — generally some form of non-judicial heirship declaration

The statutory evidentiary weight varies by state. Some states follow frameworks similar to Texas (gaining prima facie effect after a period of recording). Others require the affidavit to be filed in probate court rather than in the real property records. Some states have stricter requirements about who may serve as witness. Your state-licensed attorney will provide the correct form and procedure for your jurisdiction.

## B.7 When an Affidavit Alone Is Not Enough

Affidavits of heirship are powerful but have limits. In the following situations, an affidavit alone will not produce insurable title or will not be accepted by all title companies:

1. **Disputed heirship** — when heirs disagree about who should inherit or what shares apply
2. **Unknown heirs** — when there is genuine uncertainty about whether additional heirs exist
3. **Complex family situations** — multiple prior marriages, adoption chains, non-marital children whose paternity was never established, international family members whose status is unclear
4. **Large property values** — title insurers sometimes require judicial heirship for higher-value transactions even when affidavits could technically support the chain
5. **Prior defects in title** — when the chain of title has other problems beyond the heirship question, a judicial proceeding may be the cleaner resolution
6. **Charitable or institutional beneficiaries** — some situations involving charitable heirs require additional process beyond an affidavit

In these situations, the judicial determination of heirship under Tex. Est. Code Chapter 202 — or its equivalent in other states — is the appropriate tool.
