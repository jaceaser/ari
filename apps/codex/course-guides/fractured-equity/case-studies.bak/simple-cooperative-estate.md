## The Scenario

A single-family residential property, estimated market value $95,000, with $6,400 in accumulated tax delinquency. The original owner had died, but unlike many fractured equity situations, the estate had an independent executor — a family member who had been appointed and was actively trying to resolve the property. No probate court gridlock. One person with legal authority to sign on behalf of the estate.

## The Play

One conversation. The executor understood the situation, was motivated to close, and simply needed a credible buyer who could move quickly and pay off the tax delinquency. The operator paid $8,500 — covering the taxes and providing modest consideration to the estate.

One deed. Properly executed, notarized, and recorded within a week.

From there, the property needed minor cosmetic repairs — paint, cleaning, and landscaping. Nothing structural. The work took about three weeks.

## The Outcome

- Acquisition cost: $8,500
- Repair and holding costs: approximately $8,600
- Total investment: approximately $17,100
- Sale price: $92,000
- Net profit: approximately $74,900
- Timeline: 75 days from acquisition to close of sale

## The Takeaway

Not every fractured equity deal is complex. The system teaches the hard cases — hostile heirs, missing heirs, multi-county complications — because those are the deals where inexperienced operators fail. But many deals, once you know how to identify them, are straightforward transactions with a motivated seller who just needs someone to show up and move.

The cooperative estate is the gateway drug. It's how you build the confidence, the title company relationships, and the local attorney network that you'll need when the harder deals come along. Simple deals done right pay well, close fast, and fund the infrastructure for more complex acquisitions.

The discipline is in the identification: knowing how to find these properties, recognizing the cooperative executor when you find one, and moving quickly enough that someone else doesn't get there first.
