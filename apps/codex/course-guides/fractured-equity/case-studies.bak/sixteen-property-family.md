## The Scenario

A single tax-delinquent property in a small Texas county caught the operator's attention. The county records showed the owner had died in 2001 — twenty years earlier — and the property had been sitting in limbo ever since. No probate. No deed transfers. Just an abandoned house, $4,200 in back taxes, and six potential heirs scattered across three states.

## The Play

The first phase was straightforward fractured equity work: track down all six heirs, negotiate the purchase, and get the deeds signed. Four of the six heirs were cooperative and eager to sell. Two required more work — one was simply unresponsive, and one had reservations about selling the family home. Contribution pressure from accumulated tax liability helped bring both around. Total acquisition cost for the first property: $4,200 in back taxes plus modest heir payments.

But while doing the genealogical research on the decedent's family tree, the operator noticed something. The research kept turning up other property records — deed instruments from other counties, tax records in adjacent jurisdictions, land patents in rural areas. The same decedent who owned the first property had never consolidated his holdings through an estate. He owned fifteen additional properties across three counties, all in the same legal limbo as the first one.

The operator went back to the same six heirs. The conversation was simple: the same decedent who owned the property you just sold me also owned fifteen other properties. I'd like to buy all of them in a single transaction. One set of deeds. One check.

The heirs agreed. The additional payment for the fifteen properties was $22,000.

## The Outcome

- First property acquisition cost: $4,200
- Additional fifteen properties: $22,000
- Total acquisition cost: approximately $27,000
- Combined market value: approximately $950,000

## The Takeaway

Package-property research on the first deal multiplied the outcome by sixteen times. When you're deep in the genealogical research on a fractured equity deal — and you will be — don't stop at the target property. Look at every property record connected to the decedent. The same fractured ownership that made your target invisible to other buyers is likely making fifteen other properties equally invisible. One conversation, one round of paperwork, and one check can cover all of them.

The heir research you did to close the first deal is the same research you need to identify the additional properties. The cost of that research is already sunk. The marginal cost of adding fifteen more properties to the conversation is almost zero. This is why package-property awareness is standard operating procedure in sophisticated fractured equity practice.
