## The Scenario

A residential property in a Texas suburb, estimated market value $220,000. The original owner died without a will, leaving eight heirs. The property had $11,800 in accumulated tax delinquency. Three of the eight heirs flatly refused to sell — one had emotional attachment to the property, one was simply unreachable, and one was hostile to any outside buyer getting involved.

## The Play

**Phase 1 — Partial interest acquisition.** Five of the eight heirs were willing to sell at prices that reflected the distressed ownership situation. Total cost for the five interests: $18,400. The operator now held approximately 62.5% of the property.

**Phase 2 — Contribution demands.** The operator began paying the property taxes, insurance, and necessary maintenance costs — and documenting every payment meticulously. After six months, formal contribution demand letters went to all three holdouts, calculating exactly what each owed. Total contribution demand: approximately $9,200 across the three holdouts.

**Phase 3 — Legal filings.** Two of the three holdouts settled within 60 days of receiving the demand letters. One settled by selling their interest for a small payment. The other settled by accepting a contribution offset — their share was acquired for effectively zero consideration, credited against what they owed.

The third holdout defaulted on the contribution demand entirely. The operator filed a contribution suit, obtained a judgment, and the court ordered a sheriff's sale of that heir's interest. The operator bid the judgment amount at the sheriff's sale and acquired the final interest.

**Total timeline:** 14 months from first partial interest purchase to full ownership.

## The Outcome

- Partial interest acquisitions: $18,400
- Contribution payments and attorney fees: ~$8,500
- Total acquisition cost: approximately $35,000
- Sale price after minor repairs: $205,000
- Net profit: approximately $157,000

## The Takeaway

The contribution/partition toolkit converts hostile partial interests into full acquisitions. You don't need every heir to say yes. You need some heirs to say yes, and then you use the legal framework to resolve the holdouts. The demand letter alone resolved two of the three hostile heirs. Only one required actual litigation — and even that followed a predictable legal pathway with a clear resolution.

The key discipline is documentation from day one. Every tax payment, every insurance bill, every maintenance expenditure needs to be logged, receipted, and preserved. That documentation is what makes the contribution claim airtight and the demand letter credible.
