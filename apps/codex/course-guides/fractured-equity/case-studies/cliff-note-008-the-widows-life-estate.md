## The Situation

Delmar Ortega inherited 40 rural acres from his father in 1988. He kept it in his sole name throughout his marriage to Consuelo. They never lived on it together as their primary residence. He died intestate in 2019 at an undisclosed age. Consuelo was 74 and still living on the property. They had three adult children.

Because the property was inherited by Delmar alone before any relevant commingling, it remained his separate property. The distribution rules for intestate separate property with a surviving spouse and descendants are different from the community property rules — and produce a different ownership structure.

## What the Operator Did

Texas Estates Code § 201.002 applies when a decedent dies with separate property, a surviving spouse, and descendants. The surviving spouse receives a one-third life estate in the separate property. The descendants take two-thirds of the remainder immediately, plus the remaining one-third after the life tenant's death.

This created two distinct ownership interests:
- Consuelo: one-third life estate (meaning she holds the right to possess and use one-third of the property for her lifetime)
- Three children: two-thirds remainder interest immediately, plus remainder of the one-third life estate upon Consuelo's death

The operator addressed both interests separately.

First, the operator acquired the three children's 66.67% combined remainder interest for $44,000 total — a negotiated price across three separate transactions.

Second, the operator negotiated Consuelo's life estate release. At 74 years old, the actuarial value of her life estate on a $285,000 property was approximately $35,000. The operator paid her $22,000 cash plus relocation assistance: prepaid first month's rent and security deposit at a nearby supportive-living apartment. The total economic package was comparable to the actuarial floor while solving her practical relocation problem. A Life Estate Release Deed was executed.

Independent legal counsel for Consuelo was paid for by the investor. This is not optional — when you are asking an elderly person to release a statutory right, independent counsel is ethically required and legally protective.

## The Outcome

Full consolidation for $75,500 total against a $285,000 property. Children's 66.67%: $44,000. Consuelo's life estate: $22,000 plus relocation. Consuelo moved to a supportive-living apartment with first month prepaid. Independent legal counsel provided at investor's expense.

## The Lesson

Life estates held by elderly surviving spouses are negotiable but are not cheap. The actuarial value is the floor, not the ceiling. Budget the actuarial value plus a relocation premium — the practical cost of helping someone move is often the thing that unlocks cooperation. Always pay for the life tenant's independent counsel. It protects the transaction, creates a paper trail of informed consent, and is the ethical standard for this kind of acquisition.
