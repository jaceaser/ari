## The Situation

The family knew the decedent had executed a will — they had discussed it, some had seen it signed, and one family member had a photocopy. But the original was gone. It wasn't in the decedent's files, not in a safe-deposit box, not with any attorney. The original simply could not be produced.

The photocopy showed a will that favored one child meaningfully over the others. If the family defaulted to intestate succession, the distribution would be equal — and the favored child would receive significantly less than the decedent intended.

The legal challenge: when an original will cannot be found after a testator's death, Texas law presumes the testator destroyed it with intent to revoke. That presumption must be overcome before a lost will can be admitted.

## What the Operator Did

Counsel filed an application to probate a lost will under Texas Estates Code § 256.153. The statute requires three things: first, the terms of the will must be substantially proven (photocopy satisfies this); second, the original must be established to have existed and simply cannot be produced (as opposed to having been destroyed by the testator); third, the revocation presumption must be defeated.

The revocation argument was the critical battle. Counsel assembled the evidence: witness testimony from individuals who had been present at the will signing, establishing that the decedent had executed the will with full intent; evidence that the decedent had not been observed destroying any documents; and testimony about the decedent's expressed wishes, consistent with the photocopy's provisions.

No witnesses testified that the decedent had changed her mind or expressed intent to revoke. The court found the evidence sufficient to overcome the revocation presumption and admitted the lost will after a hearing.

## The Outcome

With the will admitted, the favored child's inheritance was established. She acquired clear title to the property through the probate process, then sold to the investor at $48,000. The other siblings, recognizing the court-admitted will as controlling, released their claims without further contest.

## The Lesson

Lost wills can be probated with the right evidence package. The revocation presumption is rebuttable — it's not a bar, it's a hurdle. If witnesses can attest to the execution, if the photocopy accurately reflects what was signed, and if no credible evidence of revocation exists, the court has sufficient basis to admit the will.

The two practical pieces to build:
1. Witness testimony establishing execution and the decedent's intent
2. Evidence (or the absence of contrary evidence) on revocation

A photocopy alone, without witnesses, is a weaker case. A photocopy with witnesses who saw the original signed is often sufficient. If the decedent discussed the will's contents in the years after signing — consistent with the photocopy — that's additional evidence of no revocation.

Don't assume a missing original means the will is gone. Work the probate path.
