## The Situation

The decedent had left a will that favored one child — call him the favored son — over the others. The will was valid, the probate was open, and technically the favored son was going to receive a meaningfully larger share. But the other beneficiaries were not reconciled to that outcome. They felt the will was unfair, and the family conflict around the estate had been simmering for months.

The investor encountered a property that couldn't close cleanly — not because of a title defect, but because the family wasn't done fighting. Even if the favored son could legally sell his interest, any buyer would be stepping into an actively hostile family dynamic with litigation risk hovering over the transaction.

## What the Operator Did

The Family Settlement Agreement is a tool established under Texas Estates Code § 122.107: the beneficiaries and heirs of an estate may unanimously modify the distribution established by a will through a written agreement. The court typically approves such an agreement when all parties have signed and no creditor rights are adversely affected.

The investor's involvement was catalytic. Rather than waiting for the family to exhaust itself in litigation, the investor brought a structured proposal: what if the distribution were modified to a formula that all parties could accept, the investor would fund the acquisition at a price that reflected that agreed distribution, and the estate could close?

The Family Settlement Agreement negotiation went to the substance of what each heir believed was fair — not to the legal merits of the will, which was valid. The favored son agreed to take a reduced share in exchange for peace, certainty, and a meaningful cash payment. The other beneficiaries accepted less than equal shares but more than they'd have gotten under the will's alternative outcomes if the dispute went to litigation.

All beneficiaries signed the FSA. The court reviewed and approved it. With the FSA on record and court-approved, each beneficiary's share was legally established — and the investor could acquire based on those FSA-modified ownership percentages.

## The Outcome

The transaction closed smoothly. Every FSA-designated owner participated in the sale. No holdouts. No litigation threats. The family dynamic had been reset by the negotiation process itself — reaching agreement, even through difficulty, resolved the emotional dimension that had been poisoning the estate.

Acquisition pricing reflected fair value at the FSA-designated shares — not a deep Fractured Equity discount, but a clean transaction that worked because all parties were aligned.

## The Lesson

The Family Settlement Agreement is a powerful tool for contested estates where the conflict isn't about title defects or legal invalidity — it's about disagreement over a distribution that all parties have the right to modify by unanimous consent.

Two things to understand about FSAs:

First, unanimous consent is required. Every beneficiary who has an interest under the will must sign. One holdout kills the agreement. The negotiation process must bring everyone to the table.

Second, the investor can be the economic engine that makes the FSA work. The reason heirs in a contested estate often can't settle among themselves is that settlement feels like a loss — someone is giving something up and getting nothing in return except the end of the fight. When the investor is funding the acquisition, every party is receiving actual cash consideration. That changes the psychology of negotiation from zero-sum to a transaction where everyone is being paid.

Structure the FSA as part of the acquisition, fund it as part of the closing, and let the certainty of a funded transaction be what brings the last holdout across the line.
