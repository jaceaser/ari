## The Situation

The decedent died in 2017 and left a will that gave one child a larger share of the estate than the other children would receive under the default intestate distribution. It was a deliberate choice — the favored child had stayed close, helped with care, and the decedent had wanted to reward that.

But no one probated the will. Seven years passed. The property sat, taxes accumulated, and the family avoided dealing with it. When the favored child finally decided to act — motivated by the investor's outreach and the prospect of resolution — it was too late for the will.

Texas Estates Code § 256.003 imposes a four-year statute of limitations on probating wills. After four years, a will can only be admitted to probate through muniment of title, and even then only with a showing that the delay wasn't caused by the applicant's default — a difficult showing at seven years of sitting on the document.

## What the Operator Did

Counsel analyzed the situation and concluded the will was effectively unprobatable. The seven-year gap was simply too long to overcome with a non-default showing. The favored child had known about the will and had failed to act; that's textbook default.

With the will off the table, the investor shifted to intestate analysis. Under Texas intestate succession, the property divided equally among all children — a meaningfully different distribution than the will specified. The favored child would receive the same share as everyone else.

The investor approached each child with their intestate share calculation and a buyout offer. Most accepted without significant resistance. The equal split was defensible, the numbers were straightforward, and the children who didn't benefit from the will had little reason to object to the intestate distribution.

One child refused. Partition action followed.

## The Outcome

Full consolidation achieved after six months. The partition proceeding resolved the holdout's interest on terms that reflected the partition floor — a familiar dynamic once the partition math was shown.

The favored child was frustrated. She had expected the will to control, had perhaps assumed that because she knew about it, it still mattered. It didn't. Seven years of inaction had cost her the favorable distribution her parent had intended.

## The Lesson

Unprobated wills past the four-year mark typically fail. This is not a technicality — it's a deliberate policy. Texas courts require timely probate precisely because stale estates create exactly the kind of uncertainty that clouds title and discourages transactions.

When you encounter an estate with a known but unprobated will past the four-year window, run the intestate analysis immediately. That's the actual legal landscape. Educate the family about what the statute says, negotiate on intestate terms, and don't waste time arguing about what the will said. The will doesn't control anymore.

The harder message to deliver: the child who sat on the will is the one who lost the most. Inaction has consequences.
