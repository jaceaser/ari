## The Situation

Wilfred Abernathy died with three biological children. His long-time companion Desmond had come into Wilfred's life at age 12 and had been raised by Wilfred as his son for decades. No formal adoption papers were ever executed. Desmond had no legal documentation of the relationship.

Desmond claimed to be an heir. The three biological children had varying opinions about his claim. Two neighbors who had known the family for decades and Wilfred's own sister were willing to swear to what they had witnessed: Wilfred treating Desmond as his son, introducing him as his son, and acting as a parent to him throughout Desmond's upbringing.

The question for the operator was whether Desmond's claim could be recognized — and if so, how to structure that recognition in a way title insurance would accept.

## What the Operator Did

Texas Estates Code § 201.054 establishes that adopted children inherit equally with biological children. Formal adoption through court order is the clearest proof. But Texas also recognizes equitable or informal adoption through the facts of the relationship — when the circumstances clearly demonstrate that the decedent treated, represented, and functioned as a parent to the claimed adoptee.

The AOH was drafted to include testimony from two disinterested witnesses: the neighbor and Wilfred's sister. Their recitals documented the specific elements of the equitable adoption: consistent treatment of Desmond as a son throughout his childhood and adult life, Wilfred's public representation of Desmond as his son to neighbors, friends, and family, and Wilfred's acceptance and performance of parental obligations over Desmond from the time he arrived at age 12.

The AOH was presented to the title company. The title company accepted the witness recitals. Desmond was treated as a fourth heir at 25%.

With four heirs each holding 25%, the operator acquired each interest separately. Desmond was grateful to be formally recognized and compensated — he received $8,500 for his 25%. The three biological children were acquired in separate transactions. Total consolidation: $34,000.

## The Outcome

Desmond's 25% was acquired for $8,500. The three biological children were acquired separately. Full consolidation at $34,000.

## The Lesson

Informally adopted children can be recognized through AOH witness testimony when they satisfy the equitable adoption elements. Do not dismiss a claimed heir simply because no formal paperwork exists. The elements — consistent treatment as a child, public representation as a child, acceptance of parental obligations — can be established through witness recitals in the AOH. The business-model benefit is real: expanding the heir pool creates more acquisition opportunities. An informally adopted child who has waited years to be acknowledged is typically the most motivated and the most grateful seller in the deal.
