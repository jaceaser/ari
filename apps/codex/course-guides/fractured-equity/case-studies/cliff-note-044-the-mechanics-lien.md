## The Situation

A contractor had filed a $38,000 mechanic's lien against the property for roof work. The property owner had hired the contractor, the work had begun, and then the owner died before the work was finished and before any payment was made. The heirs inherited the property subject to the lien.

Two threshold questions required immediate investigation: was the lien timely filed under the statutory requirements of Texas Property Code Chapter 53, and what was the quality and extent of the actual work performed?

Texas Property Code Chapter 53 governs mechanic's and materialman's liens. These liens must be filed within specific statutory windows after the work is completed or abandoned. A lien filed outside those windows is invalid. Additionally, the statutory framework requires specific notice procedures — failure to comply with notice requirements can also defeat the lien.

A second dimension: mechanic's liens occupy a constitutionally recognized exception to Texas homestead protection. Unlike most judgment liens that cannot reach a homestead, a mechanic's lien for improvements to the property itself can attach to a homestead under Texas Constitution Article XVI § 50. The lien had real bite.

## What the Operator Did

Investigation proceeded on two tracks simultaneously.

Track one: lien validity. Counsel reviewed the filing dates against the statutory windows under Chapter 53. The lien had been filed within the required timeframe. Notice procedures were also checked — compliant. The lien was facially valid.

Track two: work quality. The operator had the roof inspected by an independent contractor. The inspector's report documented what had actually been done: the contractor had performed a substantial portion of the agreed scope, but the work was shoddy — improper installation, flashing problems, code issues that would need correction. The contractor hadn't finished the work and what was finished wasn't done right.

This created negotiating leverage. A contractor who files a $38,000 lien for work that was substandard and incomplete is exposed to counterclaims — breach of contract, negligent workmanship, warranty claims. Pursuing the full $38,000 face value through litigation meant the contractor faced counterclaims on the workmanship, attorney's fees, and a contested hearing. Contractors in this position generally prefer to settle.

The negotiation: $12,000 to release the lien in full — 32% of face value, reflecting immediate cash and finality versus the litigation alternative. Contractor accepted.

The investor also completed the remaining roof work through a separate contractor. That completion cost: $4,500.

Heir buyouts for the full property: $18,000 combined.

## The Outcome

- Heir buyouts (combined): $18,000
- Mechanic's lien settlement: $12,000 (released from $38,000)
- Roof completion cost: $4,500
- Total acquisition and cure: $34,500
- Sale price: $155,000
- Net profit: $67,000

Release recorded. Full title insurance issued.

## The Lesson

Mechanic's liens are among the few encumbrances that can reach a homestead property — they're constitutionally recognized exceptions to Texas homestead protection. This makes them more dangerous than a typical judgment, and it also makes them more common as a deal obstacle in fractured equity situations where the owner was in the middle of improvements when they died.

The investigation path is two-track: validity first, then workmanship. If the lien isn't validly filed — wrong timing, defective notice — it's defensible or void and can be challenged. If the lien is valid, the next question is whether the underlying work supports the claimed amount. Contractors overstate lien values routinely, and substandard or incomplete work is frequently the basis for negotiating a steep reduction.

Contractors accept 30 to 40 percent of face value for immediate cash and closure. The litigation alternative is expensive, time-consuming, and uncertain. A contractor who filed a $38,000 lien knows that getting a judgment and collecting it against an estate with multiple heirs, no probate, and a disputed property is a multi-year project. Cash today — even at a steep discount — is the rational choice for most contractors.

The completion cost discipline matters: budget for finishing whatever the contractor left incomplete. The inspector who documents the workmanship problems is also telling you what's left to be done and what it will cost. That number is part of your deal underwriting, not a surprise that shows up after closing.
