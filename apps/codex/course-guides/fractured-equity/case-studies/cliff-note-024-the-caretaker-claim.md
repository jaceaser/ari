## The Situation

Gladys lived at the decedent's home for four years, providing personal care — bathing, cooking, transportation, daily support — in exchange for free room and board. The arrangement was informal and unwritten. Nothing was ever reduced to a lease, a contract, or a written agreement of any kind. When the decedent died, Gladys remained in the property. The heirs want her out. Gladys's position: she earned the right to stay through her years of care.

Two separate legal questions arise. First, does Gladys have any interest in the real property? Second, does she have any claim for the value of her services? The answers are different.

On the real property question: informal arrangements during the decedent's life do not create enforceable post-death tenancy rights. Gladys was a permissive occupant — the decedent permitted her to be there. That permission ended at death. Texas Business and Commerce Code § 26.01, the Statute of Frauds, bars oral grants of real property interests exceeding one year. An alleged informal agreement to let Gladys live there indefinitely — assuming it even rose to that level — could not create a property interest because it was never in writing.

On the services question: Gladys may have a creditor claim against the estate for the value of unpaid services rendered over four years. That is a legitimate possibility — but it is a claim against the estate's assets, processed through probate, not a lien on the real property and not a right to occupy.

## What the Operator Did

The operator served a 30-day notice to vacate on Gladys as a former permissive occupant whose permission had expired at the decedent's death. Gladys consulted an attorney. That attorney reviewed the facts and the law and correctly advised her: she had no real property claim. The oral arrangement, even if it existed exactly as she described it, could not create the property interest she was claiming.

With that legal reality established, the operator negotiated directly. The offer was $2,500 as a goodwill payment plus a 45-day vacate timeline. The $2,500 was not compensation for any legal right Gladys held — she had none — but an acknowledgment that she had provided genuine service and that cooperative behavior had value to both parties. Gladys accepted and moved out on schedule.

## The Outcome

Vacant possession was obtained cooperatively within the 45-day window. The transaction was clean and did not require JP Court proceedings. Gladys retained her ability to pursue whatever creditor claim she might have against the estate for the value of her four years of personal care services — that is a separate matter that does not affect the real property.

## The Lesson

Caretaker occupants have no real property claim from informal arrangements. The care they provided — however genuine, however extensive — does not translate into a property interest in the absence of a written agreement. Any legal recourse Gladys had was as a creditor of the estate, not as an owner or tenant of the property. Once her attorney communicated that reality, the negotiation was simple.

Pay modest goodwill for cooperation. The $2,500 was not paid because Gladys had leverage — she did not. It was paid because it was cheaper than the time, legal fees, and friction of a contested eviction proceeding. Distinguish between what is legally owed and what is operationally efficient. Here they pointed in the same direction.
