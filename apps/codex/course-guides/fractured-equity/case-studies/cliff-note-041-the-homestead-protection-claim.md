## The Situation

The decedent's home was her homestead — a property she had designated and occupied as her primary residence under Texas law. A creditor held a $145,000 judgment against her estate and was attempting to execute that judgment against the property. The surviving spouse continued to occupy the home.

The creditor's execution attempt created a cloud on the title that had to be resolved before the heirs could sell.

Texas homestead law is among the strongest in American jurisprudence. Texas Constitution Article XVI § 50 and § 51 establish that a homestead is protected from forced sale for the payment of debts — with a short list of explicit exceptions: property taxes, the purchase-money mortgage, mechanic's liens, and a few others. A general unsecured judgment creditor is not on that list.

The creditor's judgment, regardless of its size, could not be enforced by forcing the sale of this property while it retained its homestead character. Texas Property Code § 41.001 et seq. provides the statutory framework that operationalizes this constitutional protection.

## What the Operator Did

The operator identified the homestead issue during due diligence and brought it to counsel before making an offer. The legal analysis was clean: the property was the decedent's homestead, the surviving spouse continued to occupy it as her homestead, and the creditor's judgment was a general unsecured debt — the exact type of obligation that homestead protection is designed to defeat.

Counsel sent a letter to the creditor's counsel making the position explicit. The letter demonstrated: the homestead status of the property at time of death, the surviving spouse's continued occupancy, the constitutional and statutory basis for protection, and the legal conclusion that execution against this specific property was unavailable regardless of the judgment's validity.

The creditor's options for collecting remained open in other directions. Wages and non-exempt assets could still be pursued. Bank accounts, vehicles, and other property outside the homestead exemption remained available to satisfy the judgment. But the home itself — as long as it retained homestead character — was untouchable.

The creditor abandoned the execution attempt against the home.

## The Outcome

- Judgment amount: $145,000
- Judgment enforceability against this property: zero — defeated by homestead protection
- Creditor's response: abandoned execution against the home
- Heirs' sale to investor: $165,000, unencumbered
- Title closed clean

## The Lesson

Texas homestead is one of the strongest protections in American law. Most practitioners who encounter it understand the basics, but the practical implications for fractured equity operators are specific and important.

First: verify homestead status before assuming a judgment attaches to the home. The creditor in this case had a $145,000 judgment and apparently believed that entitled them to pursue the home. It didn't, because the property was homestead. The operator who understands this before making an offer can accurately price the deal — the $145,000 judgment was not a real liability against the acquisition.

Second: the protection survives the decedent's death and extends to the surviving spouse's continued occupancy. A homestead doesn't lose its character because the original owner died, as long as the surviving spouse or minor children continue to occupy it as their primary residence.

Third: the exceptions are narrow and specific. Property taxes, purchase-money mortgages, mechanic's liens on improvements to the property itself, and home equity lines governed by Article XVI § 50(a)(6). General unsecured judgments — credit card debt, personal loans, civil judgments from accidents or disputes — cannot reach the homestead by forced sale. That's the constitutional protection, and it's not subject to negotiation or creative interpretation.

The value of this knowledge is in underwriting accuracy. A property facing a six-figure judgment that turns out to be homestead-protected is not encumbered by that judgment at all. Price accordingly.
