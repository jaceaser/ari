## The Situation

This case builds directly on the structure of Cliff Note 76. A minor heir held a fractional interest in an inherited property. Court-supervised sale at market value was required under Texas Property Code § 29.002. The minor's proceeds were determined to be $24,000.

The difference between this case and the standard minor heir situation was the parents' preference for where those proceeds went. The default outcome in a court-supervised minor's property sale is a blocked savings account — money deposited in an interest-bearing account that the minor cannot access until reaching the age of majority. Safe, conservative, and bureaucratically straightforward.

The parents had a different goal. They wanted the $24,000 deposited in a 529 college savings plan — a federally recognized education savings vehicle where contributions grow tax-free when used for qualified education expenses. The 529 provides better long-term benefit for the minor than a savings account: tax-advantaged growth, flexibility across educational expenses, and a clear purpose aligned with the family's actual goals for the child.

The question was whether Texas courts would approve a 529 plan as an alternative to the standard blocked savings account in a guardianship proceeding.

## What the Operator Did

The operator's counsel drafted the guardianship petition to specifically request 529 disposition as the proposed use of the minor's proceeds. This is not an afterthought — the petition must affirmatively identify where the proceeds will go and explain why that disposition serves the minor's best interests. A petition that simply requests a sale and leaves the disposition vague will default to the court's standard supervised account procedure.

The legal analysis supporting the 529 request was straightforward. Section 529 plans are established under the Internal Revenue Code as recognized vehicles for holding and growing funds for a minor's educational benefit. They are not speculative investment accounts — they are tax-advantaged savings instruments with federal recognition and broad familiarity in family law and probate courts. A parent-owned 529 with the minor as beneficiary means the assets are administered by the parents (as account owners) under the plan's terms and federal rules, for the exclusive benefit of the minor beneficiary.

Texas courts have consistently recognized that a 529 plan disposition serves a minor's best interests when the parents are appropriate account owners and the amounts involved are reasonable. The court reviewing the petition approved the 529 disposition, finding it served the minor's educational and financial interests at least as effectively as a blocked savings account — and with meaningful additional benefits in the form of tax-advantaged growth.

The $24,000 in proceeds from the minor's property interest was deposited directly into the 529 plan upon closing. The account was parent-owned with the minor as named beneficiary.

## The Outcome

Minor's $24,000 deposited in a 529 plan. Tax-advantaged growth for future educational expenses. Full acquisition of all interests completed. The family received a better financial outcome for the minor than the standard supervised savings account would have produced — and the operator completed the transaction with court-approved, insurable title.

## The Lesson

The 529 plan is a legitimate and court-recognized disposition vehicle in minor heir proceedings. The default option — the blocked savings account — is not the only option, and it is frequently not the best option for the family. Parents who understand the 529's advantages naturally prefer it. Courts that understand the 529's legal standing routinely approve it.

The operator's role is to ask the question. If the minor's parents express any interest in how the proceeds will be invested, raise the 529 as an option and have counsel draft the petition to request it specifically. A petition that doesn't ask for the 529 won't get it — the court won't spontaneously order a 529 disposition when the standard blocked account is the default. The request must be affirmative, the legal basis must be articulated, and the specific plan and account structure must be identified in the petition.

The practical outcome for the operator is the same either way — the minor's share sells at market value, the court approves the transaction, and closing proceeds normally. But for the family, the difference between a blocked savings account earning minimal interest for 8 years and a 529 plan growing tax-free toward college tuition is substantial. The operator who raises this option earns goodwill and often removes friction from the transaction — families who feel their interests are being looked after are more cooperative throughout the process.
