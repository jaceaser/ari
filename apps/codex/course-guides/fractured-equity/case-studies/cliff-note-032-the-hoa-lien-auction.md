## The Situation

The decedent owned a property in a homeowners association and fell $18,000 behind on assessments over eight years. The HOA, under Texas Property Code § 209.009, has the authority to file an assessment lien and foreclose it — subject to significant restrictions. The most important restriction is constitutional: Article XVI, Section 50 of the Texas Constitution protects a homestead from forced sale for any debt other than purchase money, home equity loans, mechanic's liens, and property taxes. HOA assessments are not on that list. A living owner occupying the property as a homestead is protected from HOA assessment foreclosure.

But the decedent is dead. The heir who inherited the property has moved out and does not occupy it as their primary residence. When a property is no longer occupied as a homestead, the constitutional homestead protection is extinguished. The HOA can now foreclose. The foreclosure has been properly noticed and is scheduled.

A second legal reality: HOA foreclosure conveys title subject to senior liens. A first-lien mortgage on the property is senior to the HOA assessment lien. The HOA foreclosure sale clears the HOA lien but does not extinguish the mortgage. The buyer at the HOA auction takes the property subject to the outstanding mortgage and must either pay it off or refinance it.

## What the Operator Did

The investor researched the auction through publicly available records — the HOA's foreclosure notices, the property's mortgage balance, and the property's market value. The math supported attending: the property was worth approximately $135,000, the auction was likely to clear at well below market value given the thin pool of buyers who understand HOA auction mechanics, and the underlying mortgage was a refinanceable amount.

The investor attended the auction and bid $27,000 — the winning bid. The HOA foreclosure deed conveyed the property to the investor, and the $18,000 HOA lien was extinguished by the sale. The underlying mortgage remained as a senior obligation.

The investor then arranged refinancing of the existing mortgage into the investor's own name. This step accomplished two things: it cleared the subject-to structure of the original mortgage, and it gave the investor a clean financing arrangement they controlled. With the refinancing complete, the investor held the property in fee simple subject only to the new mortgage.

The property was then listed and sold.

## The Outcome

Sale price: $135,000. Net profit after all costs — the $27,000 auction purchase, the underlying mortgage refinancing and any payoff at closing, transaction costs, and carrying costs — was approximately $82,000.

## The Lesson

HOA foreclosures are a third distinct category in the real estate distress landscape, separate from tax sales and separate from mortgage foreclosures. They have their own mechanics, their own statutory authority under Texas Property Code § 209.009, and their own specific limitation: the constitutional homestead protection ends when homestead character ends. When an heir vacates the inherited property, the protection does not follow them.

The critical budget item is the senior mortgage. HOA foreclosure clears the HOA lien — that is its function — but it does not clear anything senior to it. Know the mortgage balance before you bid. Know whether you can refinance it or must pay it off at auction. Include that number in your maximum bid calculation. The acquisition math must account for all costs to achieve clean title, not just the auction price.
