## The Situation

An abstract of judgment originally filed in 2011 was re-filed — re-recorded in the real property records — in 2021. The amount claimed was $47,000. Heir Cornelius had inherited a 25% interest in the property in 2023, and the title commitment flagged the abstract on Schedule C.

The title company saw a judgment with a recent 2021 filing date and treated it as a current lien. The investor needed to determine whether that 2021 filing actually renewed the abstract or was simply a re-recording of the original 2011 instrument.

Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code § 34.001 governs the lifespan of abstracts of judgment. A judgment lien created by filing an abstract has a 10-year life. To extend that life beyond 10 years, the creditor must conduct writ-of-execution activity — actually attempting to collect — within the 10-year window. Re-recording the abstract alone, without execution activity in the intervening period, does not renew the lien. This principle is well-established in the Texas case law, including the line of cases following In re Henderson.

The critical question: had there been any writ-of-execution activity between 2011 and 2021?

## What the Operator Did

Counsel pulled the full docket history for the underlying lawsuit — the court file that gave rise to the 2011 judgment. Docket review covered the period 2011 through 2021.

The findings were decisive: no writ of execution had been issued. No levy attempted. No sheriff's return filed. The creditor had obtained the judgment in 2011 and then done nothing to collect it for ten years. The 2021 filing was a mere re-recording of the original abstract — a document that had already expired under § 34.001.

An expired abstract cannot be renewed by re-recording. The 2011-to-2021 period was a decade of inaction, and inaction is precisely what the statute punishes with expiration.

Counsel prepared and sent a release demand letter to the judgment creditor's counsel. The letter cited the § 34.001 expiration, documented the absence of writ-of-execution activity in the docket, and demanded a release instrument or a written acknowledgment of the lien's expiration.

The title company reviewed the documentation and accepted the position. The abstract was removed from Schedule C.

## The Outcome

- Abstract of judgment: $47,000 (claimed)
- Actual enforceability: zero — expired under § 34.001
- Release demand result: title company accepted and removed from Schedule C
- Closing on Cornelius's 25% interest: $5,000
- Closing proceeded without paying the $47,000

## The Lesson

Re-recording is not renewal. This is a fundamental distinction that inexperienced operators — and sometimes inexperienced title officers — get wrong. When a creditor re-files or re-records an abstract of judgment, it looks current in the public records because the filing date is recent. But the legal life of the abstract is measured from the original judgment date, and it can only be extended by actual collection activity — a writ of execution issued and pursued within the 10-year window.

The investigation is a docket review. Pull the court file. Look for any writ of execution, any levy, any sheriff's return, any order of sale. If the docket shows a decade of quiet — judgment entered, nothing else — then the abstract expired regardless of when the re-recording happened.

The demand letter is usually sufficient. Creditors whose abstracts have expired typically don't fight the expiration argument hard. They already knew the lien was stale — that's often why they re-recorded it, hoping someone wouldn't look closely. When someone does look closely and sends a documented demand letter, they usually concede.

The lesson for deal underwriting: when you see an abstract with a recent filing date, don't assume it's current. Pull the original judgment date. Calculate the 10-year window. If the original is more than 10 years old, go straight to the court docket and look for execution activity. You may be looking at a paper obstacle that dissolves under scrutiny.
