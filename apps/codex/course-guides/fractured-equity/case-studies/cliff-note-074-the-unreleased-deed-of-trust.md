## The Situation

The title search came back with a deed of trust from 1987 in the open-lien position. The lender had been a savings institution that no longer existed under that name. The mortgage had been paid off in 1995 — the family had a faded old payoff letter in a box — but the release had never been filed with the county. Nearly 30 years of payoff history, and nothing in the official record to show the lien was gone.

The title commitment flagged the instrument as an open lien. Until that lien was released, no title company was going to commit to the property, and no buyer's lender was going to loan against it. The 37-year-old deed of trust was blocking the transaction.

This situation is more common than most operators expect. Lenders in the 1980s and 1990s had inconsistent release procedures. Payoffs happened, checks were cashed, and the internal mortgage department often failed to follow through on the recording obligations. The result is a generation of properties carrying open-lien flags from mortgages that were paid off decades ago.

## What the Operator Did

The path to resolution ran through the lender's corporate successor. A savings institution that originated a 1987 mortgage and processed a 1995 payoff went through multiple institutional cycles over the following three decades — acquisitions, mergers, FDIC receiverships, portfolio sales. Tracing the successor required working through the institutional history: who acquired whom, when, and whether the mortgage servicing portfolio transferred with each acquisition.

The operator conducted the successor research through FDIC records, state banking department records, and publicly available M&A filings to trace the chain from the 1987 originator to the current institutional successor that held or administered the old portfolio. After several calls and a formal written request with the payoff letter attached, the successor institution confirmed the payoff was in their records and issued a formal release instrument.

The release — a properly executed release of deed of trust signed by an authorized officer of the successor institution — was recorded in the county deed records. The open-lien flag cleared. Title commitment issued clean.

The operator also researched the alternative path as a contingency. Texas Property Code § 43.002 provides a curative mechanism called the Release of Ancient Lien. When a lender is defunct and no successor institution can be identified, a property owner can execute a sworn affidavit establishing the facts: the loan was made, the loan was paid, the payoff occurred more than four years ago, no foreclosure has been initiated, and the lien holder cannot be located. Under § 43.002, a properly executed and recorded Release of Ancient Lien affidavit extinguishes the stale lien from the public record without requiring a court proceeding. It is available when, and only when, the lender has genuinely ceased to exist and no successor can be found.

In this case, the successor route worked — a proper institutional release was obtained. The § 43.002 path was not needed but was ready as a fallback.

## The Outcome

Release recorded. Title cleared. Closed normally. The 37-year-old deed of trust that had blocked the transaction was eliminated from the title chain by a recorded institutional release.

## The Lesson

Unreleased mortgages are one of the most predictable title problems in fractured equity work. Properties that have been in family hands for decades often carry a generation's worth of mortgage history in which nobody followed up on the release paperwork. Every payoff that was processed but not released becomes an open-lien flag in the next buyer's title commitment.

The resolution path runs in order of preference: first, pursue the institutional successor for a proper release. This is the cleanest outcome — an authorized release signed by the lender or its successor carries full authority to discharge the lien. Second, if the lender is genuinely defunct with no traceable successor, use the Release of Ancient Lien affidavit under § 43.002. This is the statutory cure designed for exactly that scenario.

What you don't do is walk away from the deal because a 37-year-old mortgage appears in the chain. An unpaid lien from the 1980s is not an obstacle; it's a paperwork problem. Know the two resolution paths, execute the right one for the specific lender situation, and record the result. The title comes back clean.
