## The Situation

The investor purchased a property at a Harris County tax auction for $28,500. The property's market value is $165,000 — a substantial spread. But the investor cannot simply flip the property the next week. Texas Tax Code § 34.21 grants redemption rights to former owners after a tax sale, and the rules depend on property classification.

For a homestead property — the former owner's primary residence — the redemption period is two years. The former owner may redeem the property during that window by paying the auction price, any subsequent taxes paid by the purchaser, and a premium: 25% of the redemption amount if redeemed in the first year, 50% if redeemed in the second year. If they redeem, the investor gets their money back with the premium — not a bad return — but they do not keep the property.

For non-homestead property, the redemption period is only six months, with a 25% premium.

The investor acquired what appears to be a homestead property. The two-year clock starts running at the auction date. Marketing the property for retail sale during the redemption window is problematic — most buyers cannot secure traditional financing on a property with an active redemption right outstanding, and title companies will flag the cloud.

## What the Operator Did

The investor monitored the redemption window systematically. At the 20-month mark post-sale, the former owner had made inquiries about financing to redeem but had not been able to secure the funds — the redemption amount, including the escalating premium, was too large. The 24-month deadline passed without a redemption filing.

Once the redemption window closed, the investor filed a quiet title action. A quiet title suit names all parties who might claim an interest in the property and asks the court to declare the investor's title superior to all other claims. In the context of a tax auction purchase, the quiet title addresses any procedural irregularities in the tax suit that might give a future title company pause, and formally establishes that the redemption period has expired without exercise.

The quiet title was granted by the court. The investor's title was now fee simple, with the cloud cleared.

## The Outcome

The property sold for $158,000 — slightly below the estimated $165,000 value, but close. Net profit after all costs — the $28,500 auction purchase price, subsequent taxes paid during the hold period, quiet title legal fees, and carrying costs over approximately 24 months — was approximately $120,000.

## The Lesson

Tax auction buying is a distinct strategy that requires understanding the redemption rules before you bid. Bid on a homestead property knowing you will hold it for at minimum two years before you can market it cleanly. Bid on a non-homestead property knowing your window is six months. Model the carry costs over that period into your maximum bid calculation.

Do not try to sell during the redemption window. You will encounter title insurance problems and financing obstacles that make the transaction impractical for most buyers. Wait out the window, file quiet title as soon as the window closes, and then market the property with a clean title. The patience required is the price of the spread between the $28,500 auction price and the $158,000 sale price.
