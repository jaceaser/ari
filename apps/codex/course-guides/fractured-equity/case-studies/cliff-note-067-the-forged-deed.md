## The Situation

A 2015 deed appeared in the chain of title purporting to convey the property from an elderly mother to a third party. The deed was recorded. It looked facially valid. A subsequent buyer purchased from that third party, and potentially another buyer after that.

The problem: the mother was in the hospital on the date the deed was executed. She was physically unable to appear before a notary, physically unable to write her signature, and medically incapacitated. She never signed the deed. Someone else signed it in her name.

The mother died without ever having intended to convey the property. Her actual heirs — the legitimate recipients of the property — had received nothing because the forged 2015 deed appeared to have already conveyed the property to a stranger. Two competing chains of title now existed: one tracing through the forgery, one tracing through the mother's actual intestate heirs.

## What the Operator Did

The operator was working with the legitimate heirs — the mother's actual children — and needed to establish that the forged chain was legally worthless.

Two evidentiary pillars went into the quiet title filing. First, a handwriting expert was retained to examine the 2015 deed and compare the signature against authenticated samples of the mother's actual handwriting. The expert confirmed the signature was not the mother's — the writing characteristics, pressure patterns, and letter formations were inconsistent with her genuine signature. Second, the mother's medical records from the hospital were subpoenaed. The records established that on the exact date the deed was executed, the mother was admitted as an inpatient, with documented cognitive and physical incapacity. She could not have signed the deed.

With that foundation in place, the operator filed a quiet title action. The forged-chain parties were named as defendants. The court's analysis was straightforward: a forged deed is void ab initio — void from its very inception, not merely voidable.

The distinction between void and voidable is everything in this context. A voidable deed is one that can be ratified, waived, or protected against by bona fide purchaser doctrine. A deed that is void ab initio cannot be ratified, cannot be waived, and cannot be cured by subsequent purchase. The bona fide purchaser doctrine — which normally protects innocent buyers who take title without notice of defects — does not apply to forgery. A forged deed conveys no title at all. You cannot purchase something from someone who had nothing to sell.

The court declared the 2015 forged deed void ab initio. All subsequent deeds tracing back to the forged conveyance — every deed in the forgery chain — were declared void as well. They fell with the root forgery because they derived from an instrument that never conveyed any title. The legitimate chain, running through the mother's actual death and descent to her heirs, was confirmed.

## The Outcome

The investor's acquisition from the legitimate heirs stands. Clean title confirmed through the mother's actual chain. The buyers in the forgery chain — potentially innocent people who paid real money for a property — lost everything they paid. They have remedies against the forger personally, but they have no title. The property was not theirs to acquire regardless of how innocent their purchase was.

Quiet title judgment recorded. Legitimate chain confirmed.

## The Lesson

The void/voidable line is one of the most important distinctions in Texas title law. Forgery sits firmly on the void side. Void means the deed never had legal effect, never transferred any title, and cannot be cured retroactively by any subsequent event. No recording, no reliance, no innocent payment of money creates rights downstream of a forgery.

The practical consequence: when you're acquiring from the legitimate heirs, trace the chain carefully. If a competing deed in the record turns out to trace to a forgery, your chain wins regardless of who else recorded what after the forgery date. But you must prove the forgery — handwriting experts and medical records are the standard tools. Get both, and the quiet title action is straightforward.

The cautionary note runs the other direction too: if you're looking at a deal and the chain has a suspicious deed — particularly one from an elderly or vulnerable grantor to a non-family stranger during a period when the grantor's health was declining — that's a red flag worth investigating before you acquire. Buying downstream of a forgery doesn't give you protection. It gives you a lawsuit you didn't need.
