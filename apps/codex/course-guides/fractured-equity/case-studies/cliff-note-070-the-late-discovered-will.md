## The Situation

The deal looked clean. The operator had identified a property in intestate succession, located all apparent heirs, executed the appropriate affidavit of heirship documentation, and acquired fee simple title. Three months after closing, a will surfaced — one that nobody in the family had mentioned and that had apparently been sitting in an attorney's file for years. The will named different beneficiaries than the intestate heirs who had sold to the investor.

The newly-discovered beneficiaries retained counsel and sent a demand letter. They claimed the intestate heirs had no right to sell because the decedent left a will, and therefore the investor had received no valid title. They wanted the property back.

This is a scenario that can genuinely alarm an operator who isn't familiar with the legal protections that apply. Let's look at what the law actually says.

## What the Operator Did

The operator responded with a legal memorandum laying out two separate and independent shields.

**Shield One: The four-year probate bar.** Texas Estates Code § 256.003 requires that a will be submitted for probate within four years of the testator's death, absent extraordinary circumstances. If the will was not timely probated within that window, probate is barred — the will cannot be admitted, cannot be administered, and cannot control the disposition of property. The operator verified the timeline: the decedent had been dead for more than four years. The will was legally non-probatable. The beneficiaries named in it had no path to enforce it through a Texas court.

**Shield Two: Bona fide purchaser doctrine.** Even if the probate bar hadn't applied — even if the will could theoretically have been probated — the bona fide purchaser doctrine protected the investor's acquisition. The BFP doctrine protects a buyer who acquires real property for value, in good faith, and without notice of competing claims. The investor had no notice of the will, acquired from heirs who held apparent title authority under the intestate succession laws, and paid fair consideration. Under Texas law, a BFP's title is protected against subsequently surfacing claims that the BFP had no notice of at the time of acquisition. The late-discovered will is precisely this scenario.

The memorandum made the conclusion explicit: the beneficiaries' remedy, if any, was a personal action against the intestate heirs who had received payment and then disbursed it. The property itself was not subject to any valid claim by the beneficiaries. The investor's title was clean.

The beneficiaries recognized the analysis was correct. They filed suit against the intestate heirs — the people who had actually received the sales proceeds — and settled that case for $15,000.

## The Outcome

Investor's title was unaffected from the moment the demand letter was received to the moment the resale closed. The resale proceeded on normal timeline. Beneficiaries received $15,000 from the intestate heirs through direct settlement — a recovery that had nothing to do with the investor's property.

## The Lesson

Two separate legal doctrines protect the investor in the late-will scenario, and understanding both is what turns a frightening demand letter into a confident legal memorandum.

The four-year probate bar under § 256.003 is the first line of defense. If the decedent has been dead for more than four years and the will was never filed, the will is a nullity. It cannot be probated. It controls nothing. This is a statute of limitations problem for the beneficiaries, not a title problem for the investor.

The BFP doctrine is the second line of defense, and it applies regardless of the probate bar. An investor who acquires from apparent intestate heirs for value and without notice of a competing claim has the protections of a bona fide purchaser. A late-discovered will is not notice — it's a document nobody knew about. An investor cannot be held to notice of a document they had no reasonable means of discovering.

The beneficiaries' remedy in that scenario is always against the people who received the money. Not against the person who received the property in good faith. That's the design of the doctrine, and it operates exactly as intended here.

When a late will surfaces after your closing, don't panic. Pull the probate bar analysis, confirm your BFP status, and respond in writing with the legal framework. In most cases, the beneficiaries' counsel will recognize the legal reality quickly.
