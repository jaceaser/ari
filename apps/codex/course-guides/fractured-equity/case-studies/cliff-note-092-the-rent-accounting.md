## The Situation

Cotenancy is a shared ownership structure with legal obligations that run among the co-owners. One of those obligations — one that many cotenants do not understand until someone presents them with the legal theory — is the duty to account for rent collected from the property.

When one cotenant collects rent and keeps all of it, that cotenant is enriching themselves at the other cotenants' expense. Their interest in the property entitles them to their proportionate share of the rent — no more. The other cotenants are entitled to their proportionate shares. A cotenant who collects 100% of the rent and distributes 0% is holding the other cotenants' money and has been for however long the collection has continued.

In this deal, one of four cotenants held a 25% interest and had been renting the property to tenants for six years. She collected every rent payment and kept every penny. The other three cotenants — each holding 25% — had received nothing and had apparently not known or not acted.

The investor, building a position in the property, had been acquiring the non-collecting cotenants' interests. Along the way, the investor recognized that those non-collecting cotenants held something more valuable than just their fractional property interests: they held accounting claims against the rent collector that had accumulated over six years.

## What the Operator Did

The investor used Template 8 — the Assignment of Rights and Claims instrument — to acquire the accounting claims from the three non-collecting cotenants at the time of purchasing their property interests, or in some cases as a separate transaction. Each non-collecting cotenant assigned to the investor their right to claim their proportionate share of the rent that had been collected and withheld.

The investor then assembled the accounting analysis. The starting point was the rent rate — documented through evidence that the investor gathered: tenant interviews, bank records the cotenants produced, and comparable rental rate analysis for the period in question. The investor calculated the total rent collected over the six-year period.

The statute of limitations analysis then trimmed the claim. Under Texas CPRC § 16.051, an action on an accounting claim must be brought within four years of when the cause of action accrued. The investor was not entitled to recover rent collected more than four years ago — only the most recent four years were within the limitations window. The calculation became: four years of documented rent at the documented rate, applied to the 75% that the rent-collecting cotenant owed to the three non-collecting cotenants (since 25% of the rent was legitimately the collector's own share).

The result: approximately $52,200 in assigned accounting claims — representing the four-year maximum recoverable rent withheld from the other cotenants.

The investor compared this figure to the fair market value of the rent-collecting cotenant's 25% interest in the property. The accounting claim exceeded the interest value. The rent collector owed more to the investor (through the assignments) than the investor owed to the rent collector for a market-rate purchase of the 25% interest.

The investor presented the accounting claim to the rent-collecting cotenant through counsel: you have a $52,200 accounting liability that we have acquired through assignment. We are prepared to sue on this claim. Alternatively, you convey your 25% interest to us in exchange for a full release of the claim — no cash changes hands, you resolve your liability, we acquire the interest.

## The Outcome

The settlement was reached. The rent-collecting cotenant signed a deed conveying her 25% interest to the investor. The investor, in exchange, delivered a full release of the $52,200 accounting claim. No cash exchanged hands in either direction.

The cotenant had avoided a lawsuit she would have lost — the accounting claim was well-documented and the liability was real. The investor acquired a 25% fractional interest for zero cash consideration, funded entirely by the accounting claim leverage that the assignment instrument had created.

With the rent collector's 25% added to the positions already accumulated, the investor achieved full consolidation.

## The Lesson

Rent-accounting leverage is one of the most underused tools in the fractured-equity operator's kit. Most operators look at a rent-collecting cotenant and see an obstacle. The operator who knows the accounting doctrine sees a liability — and a negotiating lever.

The mechanic has three parts:

**First:** Recognize when rent has been collected. Look at whether the property has tenants. Ask the cooperative cotenants whether rent has been collected and distributed. If the answer is that one person collected and didn't share, the accounting clock has been running.

**Second:** Assign the claim. Template 8 captures the non-collecting cotenants' right to make the accounting claim. Assign it at the time you purchase their interests, or in a separate transaction. The assignment transfers the legal right to make the claim from them to you.

**Third:** Calculate and present. Document the rent rate, apply the four-year limitations window, calculate the 75% (or whatever the non-collectors' combined interest is) of that rent. Compare to the collector's interest value. If the claim exceeds the interest value, you have a zero-cash acquisition available.

The four-year statute of limitations is not an obstacle — it is a forcing function. It means that the longer you wait, the older claims age out of the recoverable window. Move on accounting claims as soon as you identify them.
