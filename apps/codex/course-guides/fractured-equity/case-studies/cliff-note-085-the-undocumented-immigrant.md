## The Situation

The heir in question was undocumented. She lived in Texas, had done so for years, worked, paid taxes under an ITIN, and maintained a bank account. She held a fractional interest in an inherited property as one of several heirs. She was cooperative and willing to sell.

The operator faced the question that stops most investors cold when they hear the word "undocumented": can this person legally sign a deed? And if so, does FIRPTA create a withholding obligation that complicates the closing?

The correct answer to both questions requires understanding that they are separate questions, governed by separate bodies of law, that often produce different answers than operators expect.

## What the Operator Did

The operator separated the two questions before analyzing either.

**Question One: Conveyance Capacity**

Texas law does not condition the capacity to convey real property on citizenship or immigration status. The relevant capacity requirements are competence — the ability to understand the nature of the transaction — and majority, meaning the person is 18 years of age or older. The heir was an adult of sound mind. She met both requirements. Her immigration status was legally irrelevant to her ability to execute a deed.

This is not a loophole or an edge case. It is settled Texas property law. Real property conveyances in Texas are governed by the Texas Property Code and related common law — none of which impose immigration status requirements. The operator confirmed this with counsel and moved forward.

**Question Two: FIRPTA Analysis**

FIRPTA — the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act, codified at 26 USC § 1445 — imposes a 15% withholding obligation on proceeds paid to "foreign persons" in connection with US real property sales. The critical legal question is how FIRPTA defines "foreign person," and the answer has nothing to do with immigration status.

Under FIRPTA, the relevant definition is "non-resident alien" (NRA) — a term drawn from the Internal Revenue Code's tax residency framework, not from immigration law. An NRA is someone who fails the substantial presence test: 31 days of physical presence in the current year and at least 183 days when applying the three-year weighted formula (100% of current-year days, 33.3% of prior-year days, 16.6% of two-years-ago days).

The heir had lived continuously in Texas for multiple years. Her day count under the substantial presence formula far exceeded 183 days. She satisfied the substantial presence test. Under FIRPTA, she was a "US person" — not a non-resident alien. No withholding was required.

The heir signed the deed before a mobile notary who came to her home. Payment was made by check from the title company's escrow account. The heir deposited the check into her existing bank account — undocumented individuals can and do maintain bank accounts, particularly with ITINs rather than SSNs, under various bank account programs that do not require SSN.

## The Outcome

The full acquisition was completed. The heir received her payment, the title company issued its policy, and the deed was properly acknowledged and recorded. The operator documented the FIRPTA analysis in a file memo that was provided to the title company with the closing package — not because it was required, but to preempt any questions at the title company level from staff who might not have previously worked through a FIRPTA analysis on an undocumented seller.

Immigration status created no legal obstacle to either the conveyance or the closing mechanics.

## The Lesson

The instinct when an undocumented heir appears is to treat it as a disqualifying complication. That instinct is wrong on both of the questions it implies.

Texas conveyance law requires competence and majority. It does not require citizenship, a green card, a Social Security Number, or any particular immigration status. The law is concerned with whether the person can understand and intend the conveyance — not with the person's documentation.

FIRPTA is the more analytically interesting question, and the one where the analysis most often surprises operators. FIRPTA's definitions come from the tax code, not from immigration law. The IRS does not define "non-resident alien" the way the immigration system defines "undocumented." An undocumented person who has lived in the United States for years is almost certainly a US person under the IRS substantial presence test, and therefore outside FIRPTA's withholding requirements. An international heir who just arrived in the country to close a deal might be a non-resident alien for FIRPTA purposes even if they hold a valid visa.

Run each analysis independently. They use different definitions, reach different conclusions, and apply different legal frameworks. Do not assume that immigration status drives either answer — because it drives neither.
