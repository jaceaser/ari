## The Situation

The decedent had owned the property in full — both the surface estate and the mineral estate beneath it. At the time of death, the mineral interests were subject to an active oil and gas lease with an oil company. The lease was producing. Royalties were flowing. The heirs were receiving mineral royalty checks.

This created a more complex acquisition structure than a straightforward surface-only property. Two separate estates existed: the surface estate, which includes the right to use, improve, and sell the physical land and improvements above ground, and the mineral estate, which includes the right to extract subsurface resources and receive royalties from production.

The fractured equity situation involved the surface ownership. The heirs held the mineral estate through the active lease and had no interest in surrendering the royalty income. The question was whether the operator could acquire the surface estate alone, at a price appropriate for that half of the property bundle.

## What the Operator Did

Texas property law expressly permits the severance of surface and mineral estates. Once severed — either by conveyance or by retention at the time of a surface sale — the two estates are independently owned, independently transferable, and independently valued. This is a fundamental feature of Texas real property law and has been settled for over a century.

The operator reviewed the mineral deeds and the active oil and gas lease. Under Texas law, an oil and gas lease survives the death of the mineral interest owner. The lease continues in force according to its terms, binding successor owners of the mineral estate. The heirs had stepped into the decedent's position as lessors — they were receiving royalty payments and had no reason to want the mineral lease disturbed.

The negotiation was straightforward once the structure was understood. The heirs would convey the surface estate to the investor and retain the mineral estate. They kept the royalty income. The investor acquired the surface at a price discounted to reflect the absence of minerals — $72,000 against a $125,000 surface value.

The conveyance documents were drafted to clearly transfer only the surface estate, with a specific mineral reservation in favor of the grantor heirs. The oil and gas lease was unaffected and continued in place post-closing.

## The Outcome

Surface acquired at $72,000. The surface sold at $118,000. Net profit on the transaction: $38,000.

The heirs retained the mineral estate and continued receiving royalty payments from the active oil and gas lease. The oil company's lease continued on its terms regardless of the surface ownership change. Everyone got what they valued.

## The Lesson

The bifurcated acquisition — surface only, minerals retained — is an underused tool in Texas fractured equity practice. Many operators assume that buying the property means buying everything, and that severed mineral rights represent a complication that blocks the deal. Neither is true.

Texas law fully supports independent ownership of surface and mineral estates. When heirs are receiving mineral royalties and have no motivation to give them up, structuring the acquisition to convey only the surface eliminates that friction entirely. The heirs keep their royalty income. The investor acquires the surface at a price reflecting only what they're actually receiving. Both sides get the piece they actually want.

The documentation discipline is critical: the conveyance must clearly and specifically transfer only the surface estate, with an explicit mineral reservation clause. Ambiguity in the deed language can create disputes about what was and wasn't conveyed. Be precise. List the reservation explicitly. If the mineral estate has been previously severed and is separately titled, confirm the chain for both the surface and mineral sides before closing.

Also verify lease status before assuming anything about the mineral value. An active producing lease is different from a lease that's expired or from an unleased mineral estate. Know what the heirs actually have before you agree to price the surface accordingly.
