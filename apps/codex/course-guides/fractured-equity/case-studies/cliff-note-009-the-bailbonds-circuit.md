## The Situation

Lawrence Figueroa got arrested. To secure his bail, he deeded his home to a bail bond company. The company later deeded the property back to Lawrence alone — not to Lawrence and his wife Hortense jointly. Four weeks elapsed between the two deeds.

Lawrence later died intestate. He and Hortense had four children together. Neither had been married before. Looking at the deed chain, it appeared that Lawrence alone became the sole owner of record after the bail bond round-trip — which raised the question of whether the property had been converted to his separate property.

If it was his separate property, the children had inheritance rights under § 201.002 separate property rules. If it remained community property, the analysis was different entirely.

## What the Operator Did

The operator traced back to the original acquisition. Lawrence and Hortense were married when they originally purchased the home. Under Texas Family Code § 3.002, property acquired during marriage is presumed community property. That presumption attaches at acquisition.

The controlling authority is Cockerham v. Cockerham and the body of case law following it: community property character follows the source of acquisition funds, not the form of intervening deeds. Hortense never joined the deed to the bail bond company. She did not sign away her interest. Her half was never legally conveyed. The deed from Lawrence to the bail bond company conveyed only Lawrence's half — it could not convey what Hortense held. And when the bail bond company deeded back to Lawrence alone, the property did not thereby become his separate property; it remained community property because the community character was established at purchase and was never validly stripped.

The round-trip deed produced a misleading paper record but did not change the legal character of the property.

At Lawrence's death, the property was still community property. Under § 201.002, because all four children were children of both spouses with no children from outside the marriage, Hortense takes Lawrence's entire 50% plus retains her own 50%. Result: Hortense owns 100%.

The children were educated that they had no current claim. They would inherit from Hortense when she eventually died.

Single-seller transaction with Hortense at $115,000 on a $185,000 property.

## The Outcome

Hortense is the sole owner at 100%. Clean single-seller transaction at $115,000. Property valued at $185,000. Children educated and excluded from the current transaction.

## The Lesson

Never accept "the deed says just him" as proof of separate property. The threshold question is always: were they married when the original purchase happened? If yes, presume community regardless of what subsequent deeds look like. Intervening conveyances — even a round-trip through a third party like a bail bond company — do not strip community character when the original character was established at acquisition. You trace to the source, not to the paper trail.
