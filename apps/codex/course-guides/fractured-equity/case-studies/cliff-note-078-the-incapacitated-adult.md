## The Situation

One of the heirs — an elderly woman — had advanced dementia. Her daughter had been running her life for years in the practical sense: managing the bank accounts, making medical decisions, keeping the bills paid. It was a functional arrangement, built on family trust and necessity. But it was entirely informal. There was no court-appointed guardianship, no power of attorney, and no other legal instrument giving the daughter authority to act on her mother's behalf in legal or contractual matters.

Now the family needed to sell the inherited property. The daughter assumed she could sign documents on her mother's behalf, given that she had been handling everything for years. That assumption was legally incorrect.

Texas contractual capacity doctrine requires that any party executing a deed understand the nature of the transaction they are entering — what property is being conveyed, for what consideration, and what the legal effect of signing will be. An adult with severe dementia does not meet that threshold. A deed executed by an incapacitated person who lacks contractual capacity is void — not merely voidable, but void. A title company will not insure a transaction where one grantor lacked capacity at the time of signing.

The daughter's informal authority, regardless of how long it had been exercised, was not legally sufficient to substitute for the incapacitated heir's signature on a deed. The law requires formal, court-conferred authority for real property transactions involving incapacitated adults.

## What the Operator Did

The correct legal mechanism is a guardianship of the estate under Texas Estates Code Chapter 1101 et seq. A guardianship of the estate is a court proceeding in which a judge formally adjudicates a person's incapacity and appoints a guardian with legal authority to manage the incapacitated person's property and financial affairs, subject to ongoing court supervision.

The operator's counsel filed a guardianship petition in the appropriate county probate court. The petition identified the heir as the proposed ward — the person to be placed under guardianship — and proposed the daughter as the guardian of the estate.

The court ordered an independent geriatric examination conducted by a licensed physician with expertise in evaluating cognitive function and capacity in elderly patients. The geriatric examination confirmed that the heir suffered from severe dementia affecting her ability to understand and make decisions about her property and finances. The examining physician's report documented the nature and extent of the incapacity.

The court reviewed the petition, the medical evidence, and the proposed guardianship structure, and appointed the daughter as guardian of the estate. With the guardianship in place, the daughter had court-conferred legal authority to enter into transactions involving her mother's property — subject to court approval for significant matters like real estate sales.

The court required an independent appraisal of the incapacitated heir's interest in the property. The appraised value of her share was $19,500. The court approved the sale at that appraised value, with proceeds to be deposited in a court-supervised account to be used for the incapacitated heir's care and benefit. The guardian executed the deed on behalf of the ward.

## The Outcome

The incapacitated heir's interest sold at $19,500 — full appraised market value. Proceeds went to a supervised account for her care. The full acquisition of all interests in the property was completed. Title was clean and insurable.

## The Lesson

Informal family management is one of the most common landmines in fractured equity work involving elderly heirs. Families fall into practical arrangements — a capable adult child starts handling things, and the arrangement works fine until a real estate transaction requires legal authority that the family member simply doesn't have.

The moment a fractured equity analysis reveals that one heir may lack contractual capacity — whether from dementia, severe mental illness, or any other cognitive condition — the investor must treat that heir's interest as requiring formal guardianship before any conveyance can occur. No amount of family consensus, no signed family agreement, and no statement by other family members substitutes for the legal authority that guardianship provides.

The guardianship proceeding takes time — typically several months from petition to appointment — and has costs in attorney fees and court filings. Build both into the deal timeline and deal economics when you identify an incapacitated heir early in due diligence. Trying to rush or work around the process creates transactions that fall apart at closing when the title company flags the capacity issue.

Compare this situation to Cliff Note 80: if the heir had executed a statutory durable power of attorney before losing capacity, the daughter would have had legal authority to convey from the beginning, and no guardianship would have been needed. The difference between those two outcomes is whether the heir planned ahead. Most fractured equity situations involve heirs who did not plan ahead. Guardianship is the remedy when planning did not happen.
