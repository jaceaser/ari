## The Situation

A 25% interest in a Texas property was held by a Mexican citizen who had inherited the interest from her relative. She had never lived in the United States. She had no green card, no visa, no US presence. She was cooperative, wanted her money, and was willing to sign — the logistical question was how to execute a deed that would be valid for Texas recording when the signing had to happen in Mexico, and how to handle the FIRPTA withholding that her status would trigger.

## What the Operator Did

The operator worked two tracks simultaneously: the signing mechanics and the FIRPTA compliance.

**Track One: The Signing**

A deed for Texas real property must be acknowledged before a person authorized to take acknowledgments. In the United States, that means a notary public. In a foreign country, US law recognizes several alternatives — including acknowledgments taken by US consular officers at American embassies and consulates abroad. This is an established legal mechanism, not an improvisation. Consular officers are specifically authorized to perform notarial functions for US legal purposes.

The heir traveled to the US Consular Office in Monterrey, Mexico — the nearest US consular facility to her location. The consular officer witnessed her execution of the deed and provided an acknowledgment on the instrument. This acknowledgment was legally equivalent to a Texas notary acknowledgment for recording purposes.

To use a foreign-executed document in Texas, an additional authentication step was required: an apostille. The apostille is an international certification mechanism established by the Hague Convention Abolishing the Requirement of Legalisation for Foreign Public Documents. It certifies the authenticity of the signature and capacity of the official who executed the document — in this case, the consular officer. The apostille was obtained from the appropriate Mexican authority and attached to the deed instrument.

With the consular acknowledgment and apostille in place, the deed was complete and acceptable for Texas recording.

**Track Two: FIRPTA Compliance**

The operator confirmed the heir's FIRPTA status independently. The analysis was straightforward: the heir had never lived in the United States, did not satisfy the substantial presence test under any year's calculation, and held no lawful permanent residence. She was unambiguously a "foreign person" under 26 USC § 1445.

Under FIRPTA, the transferee — in this case, the operator acting through the closing entity — was required to withhold 15% of the amount realized by the foreign person in connection with the sale of US real property. The "amount realized" in this context was the heir's purchase price for her 25% interest. At closing, 15% of that amount was withheld and remitted to the IRS by the closing agent using the required withholding forms.

The heir received 85% of her agreed purchase price in net proceeds.

## The Outcome

The deed was accepted by the title company without objection. Consular acknowledgments with apostilles are known instruments in real estate transactions — not common, but not novel. The title underwriter was familiar with the procedure.

The 15% FIRPTA withholding was remitted to the IRS as required. The heir would file a US tax return for the year of the sale and claim either credit for the withholding or a refund if the withholding exceeded her actual tax liability — a process handled separately between the heir and her tax advisor, not the operator's concern.

Full closing was achieved. The operator consolidated the property across all four heirs — including one who was in another US state, two who were local, and this international heir in Mexico.

## The Lesson

International heirs introduce two procedural requirements — signing mechanics and FIRPTA compliance — neither of which is a deal-stopper.

For the signing, consular officers at US embassies and consulates worldwide are authorized to take deed acknowledgments for US legal purposes. Mexican heirs have convenient access through the US Consular network in Mexico — Monterrey, Mexico City, Guadalajara, Ciudad Juárez, and others. The apostille authenticates the consular signing for US court and recording purposes. Plan for lead time on the apostille — it is not instantaneous, but it is a predictable process.

For FIRPTA, the analysis is binary: is the heir a foreign person under the IRS definition? If yes, 15% withholding applies. If no — because the heir satisfies the substantial presence test as a long-term US resident, as seen in the prior case study — withholding does not apply. Run the analysis before closing. The closing agent needs to know whether withholding is required in order to prepare the correct disbursement.

One alternative worth noting: if the foreign heir can travel to the United States for the signing, the apostille step is eliminated and the signing can occur before a standard US notary. For some heirs, this is practical. For others, travel is not feasible. Understand your options and plan accordingly.
