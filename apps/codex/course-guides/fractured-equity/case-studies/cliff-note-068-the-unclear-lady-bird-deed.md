## The Situation

In 2015, an elderly grantor executed a Lady Bird Deed leaving the property to her daughter. A Lady Bird Deed — also called an enhanced life estate deed in Texas — allows the grantor to retain the right to use, sell, mortgage, or otherwise convey the property during her lifetime, with the property passing to the named beneficiary automatically at death only if the grantor did not exercise that retained power beforehand.

In 2019, without mentioning the 2015 Lady Bird Deed at all, the same grantor executed a regular warranty deed conveying the property to her son. The 2019 deed was recorded.

Grantor died in 2022. Now two deeds existed in the record pointing to two different children. Daughter pointed to the 2015 Lady Bird Deed. Son pointed to the 2019 warranty deed. Both claimed 100%.

## What the Operator Did

The legal analysis starts with the nature of the Lady Bird Deed itself. Unlike a regular life estate, which permanently divests the grantor of the right to convey the remainder interest, an enhanced life estate retains that power in the grantor. The grantor of a Lady Bird Deed can revoke the beneficiary designation, mortgage the property, sell the property, or otherwise convey the fee interest during her lifetime. That retained power is what makes the Lady Bird useful for estate planning — it provides Medicaid protection without permanently locking in the beneficiary.

The 2019 warranty deed to son was a full fee simple conveyance. It purported to convey the entire property — not a partial interest, not a life estate, not a contingent interest. A full fee simple conveyance is the ultimate exercise of the grantor's retained power under the enhanced life estate. By conveying a fee simple in 2019, the grantor exercised — and simultaneously exhausted — her retained power.

Texas enhanced life estate deed doctrine treats a subsequent full conveyance as an implicit revocation of the Lady Bird beneficiary designation. The grantor did not need to reference the 2015 Lady Bird Deed in the 2019 deed, nor did she need to execute a formal revocation instrument. The act of conveying the fee simple was itself the revocation. Son received the fee simple in 2019. Daughter received nothing at the grantor's death in 2022 because there was no remaining interest to pass — the grantor had already conveyed it away.

The analysis was clear as a legal matter. But two competing deeds in the county record created a title insurance problem. No title company would issue a commitment without resolution of the apparent conflict. The operator filed a quiet title action naming daughter as a defendant. Daughter received proper service and defaulted — she did not appear to contest the action. The court entered judgment quieting title in son, which was then recorded.

## The Outcome

The quiet title judgment recorded in son's favor. The surface ambiguity was resolved by court order. With the judgment on record, the title company issued a commitment, and the operator acquired from son.

## The Lesson

The Lady Bird Deed is powerful precisely because it preserves the grantor's retained power. But that retained power cuts both ways. The grantor can revoke the beneficiary designation at any time during life — expressly or, as here, implicitly through a subsequent full conveyance. The daughter in this case had no enforceable rights after 2019 because the grantor exercised and exhausted the retained power.

The tricky part is that both deeds were validly executed and properly recorded. Nothing in the record is fraudulent. The conflict arises from the interaction of Texas enhanced life estate doctrine with a subsequently executed deed that doesn't reference the earlier instrument. That ambiguity is real enough that title insurers require a quiet title judgment before they'll commit on the property.

When you encounter a Lady Bird Deed followed by a subsequent conveyance in the chain, the analysis is: did the subsequent conveyance exhaust the grantor's retained power? If it was a full fee simple conveyance, the answer is yes — the Lady Bird is implicitly revoked, and the subsequent grantee holds fee simple. Get the quiet title judgment to memorialize that analysis in the record, and you have a clean chain going forward.
