## The Situation

Lottie died in 2019 after receiving Medicaid nursing home benefits for five years. The Medicaid Estate Recovery Program had a claim against her estate for $180,000 — the amount the state had paid for her care. Under MERP, that claim is filed as a lien and must be satisfied from the decedent's estate before heirs can take clear title.

Son Derrick had never addressed the estate. No probate. No MERP negotiations. The property sat with title still in Lottie's name, the $180,000 MERP lien outstanding, and five years of accumulated property taxes past due.

The investor approached in 2024.

Five years had elapsed since Lottie's death. Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code § 16.051 provides a four-year residual statute of limitations — the catch-all limitations period for claims that don't have a more specific statute. MERP claims, which are state-agency collection actions against an estate, fall under this residual period. The four-year window had closed in 2023.

The $180,000 MERP lien was time-barred.

## What the Operator Did

Knowing the MERP claim was barred was only half the battle. The other half was preventing the title underwriter from querying the decedent's name during the title search — because if the underwriter searched "Lottie [surname]," the MERP lien would appear and the underwriter would flag it, regardless of the limitations defense. Underwriters often don't litigate limitations questions; they just require a release or refuse to insure.

The solution was wash-the-name sequencing — a precise series of steps that changes the identity of the owner of record before the title search runs.

The sequence:
1. Heir-to-Investor Deed from Derrick for $18,000. Recorded.
2. Marinated 45 days — letting the new deed age in the public records.
3. Called Montgomery CAD to change the owner-of-record from the decedent's name to the investor entity.
4. Paid the delinquent taxes — now in the investor entity's name.
5. Only after all these steps, opened title with the underwriter.

When the underwriter ran the title search, the property was indexed to the investor entity — not to Lottie. The underwriter searched the investor entity's name, found no MERP lien (the lien was indexed under the decedent's name), and issued standard coverage.

## The Outcome

- Deed from Derrick: $18,000
- Delinquent taxes paid: included in deal structure
- Title underwriter's search result: clean
- Title insurance issued: standard policy
- Property sold: $152,000
- Net profit: $101,000

## The Lesson

MERP claims are subject to the four-year statute of limitations under Texas CPRC § 16.051. Five years of inaction by Derrick converted an $180,000 MERP lien from a real obstacle to a time-barred claim that the state can no longer enforce.

But knowing a claim is time-barred is different from getting a title underwriter to agree with you. Underwriters are conservative. They don't want to insure over a disputed legal theory — even a strong one. The wash-the-name sequence solves that problem operationally: by the time the underwriter runs the search, the owner of record is the investor entity, not the decedent. The MERP lien is indexed under the decedent's name. The search doesn't surface it.

The sequencing discipline is exact: deed, then marinate, then CAD update, then taxes, then title. Each step in that order. The marination period — letting the deed age before contacting the CAD — ensures the CAD update reflects a recorded instrument, not a pending transaction. The CAD update before opening title ensures the county tax record and the deed record are both showing the new owner at the time of search.

Shortcut any step, and you risk the underwriter querying the decedent's name and surfacing the lien. The sequence only works when it's complete.
