## The Situation

Marlena Whitworth died in Tyler. She had originally had five adult children. The fourth child, Desmond, died seven years before his mother — in 2018. Desmond had been divorced in 2010 and had no children of his own. He died without a spouse and without descendants.

The remaining four siblings were fighting over their respective shares. The immediate question was what happened to Desmond's piece. Was there a fifth share floating around? Was his ex-wife entitled to something? Did the share pass to the other siblings, and if so, in what proportion?

## What the Operator Did

Texas Estates Code § 201.001(e) is direct on this point: a predeceased heir who leaves no descendants and no surviving spouse at the time of the decedent's death takes no share. The hypothetical share that Desmond would have held redistributes equally among the surviving heirs. This is distinct from per stirpes distribution — per stirpes applies when the predeceased heir left descendants. Desmond left none.

The AOH documented three critical facts: Desmond's 2018 death, his 2010 divorce, and the absence of any descendants. The timing of the divorce mattered enormously. The question under the statute is whether Desmond had a surviving spouse at the time Marlena died — not at the time he died. Since his ex-wife was not his spouse at Marlena's death, she takes nothing. Since he had no children, there are no descendants to step into his shoes.

Result: the estate divides equally among the four surviving siblings at 25% each, not five siblings at 20% each.

One sibling held out after the others agreed to sell. With 75% consolidated, the operator filed for partition, which resolved in 4 months.

## The Outcome

Four equal buyouts were acquired cleanly. No phantom share to chase. The holdout resolved through partition at 75% in 4 months.

## The Lesson

A childless predeceased sibling creates no phantom share. Divide among the living. The detail that catches people is the timing of the marital-status check. You verify the marital status at the decedent's death — not at the sibling's earlier death. Desmond's divorce in 2010 meant nothing by itself; what mattered was that no surviving spouse existed at the moment Marlena died. Get that timing right in the AOH and the share math is clean.
