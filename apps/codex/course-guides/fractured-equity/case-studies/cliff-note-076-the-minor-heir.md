## The Situation

A 10-year-old girl had inherited a 25% interest in the family property. The other three adult heirs — parent or other family members — held the remaining 75% among them and were ready to sell. The property was appraised at $192,000, making the minor's 25% share worth $48,000.

The problem was not the adult heirs. The problem was the minor. In Texas, a person under the age of 18 lacks legal capacity to execute a deed conveying real property. Even if the minor's parents wanted to sign on her behalf, they lacked authority to do so without a court appointment and court supervision. There is no informal workaround, no parental signature that substitutes for the legal process, and no way to take a deed from a minor outside the statutory guardianship framework.

An operator who attempted to acquire the minor's interest without going through proper guardianship would receive a deed that was legally void and uninsurable. A title company would reject it. The transaction would unwind. The operator would have a problem.

## What the Operator Did

The correct path ran through Texas Property Code § 29.002, which governs court-supervised transactions involving minor's real property interests exceeding $10,000 in value. The statutory framework requires a guardianship proceeding, an independent appraisal, and court approval of any sale — and it mandates that the sale occur at market value, not at a discounted price.

The operator's counsel filed a guardianship petition in the appropriate county court. The minor's parent or guardian was identified and submitted to the court. The court appointed a guardian ad litem — a court-appointed attorney whose job is to represent the minor's independent legal interests throughout the proceeding, separate from the interests of the family members who are selling. The guardian ad litem's role is protective: they review the proposed transaction, analyze whether it is in the minor's best interests, and report to the court with a recommendation.

An independent appraisal was ordered on the property. The appraiser determined the market value of the property at $192,000. The minor's 25% share translated to $48,000 at market value. The court required that the minor receive $48,000 — no discount, no fractured equity reduction, no negotiation below fair value. The court's obligation is to protect the minor from being disadvantaged in transactions where her legal incapacity prevents her from negotiating on her own behalf.

The court approved the sale at $48,000 for the minor's share, with proceeds to be deposited in a court-supervised savings account held for the minor's benefit until she reaches the age of majority.

The operator structured the profit around the adult heirs' shares. The adult heirs — with full legal capacity and no court oversight requirement — could and did sell at Fractured Equity discount pricing below market value. The investor's returns came from the discounts on those shares. The minor's share was acquired at full market value, treated as a cost of doing the deal rather than a discount opportunity.

## The Outcome

The minor received $48,000 — full market value for her 25% interest in the $192,000 property. The proceeds went into a court-supervised account for her benefit. The investor completed the acquisition of all four interests, including the minor's, and achieved the planned profit through the adult heirs' discounts. Clean transaction, properly documented, insurable title.

## The Lesson

Minor heirs are not a dead end, but they are a structural constraint. Texas law is explicit: a minor's real property interest worth more than $10,000 cannot be sold without court approval, and court approval will not be granted at less than market value. There are no exceptions for fractured equity investors, no family agreements that substitute for the process, and no title company that will insure around it.

The operator's job when a minor heir is present is to plan for the constraint from the beginning. Budget for full market value on the minor's share. Budget for the time and cost of the guardianship proceeding — which typically runs several months. Build those numbers into your deal analysis before you commit to a price with the adult heirs.

The good news is that the presence of a minor heir does not make the deal impossible. It makes it slower and more expensive on one share. If the adult heirs' discounts are sufficient to generate acceptable returns even with one share at full market value, the deal works. Many fractured equity deals have this structure — multiple heirs at discount, one heir at full price, the overall acquisition still profitable because the discounts on the other shares cover the model.

The discipline is knowing the rule before you get to the negotiation table.
