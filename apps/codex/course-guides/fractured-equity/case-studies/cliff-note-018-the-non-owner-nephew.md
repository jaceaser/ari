## The Situation

Emmaline Corrigan let her nephew Basil live in the family home rent-free for five years before she passed in 2022. There was no lease. There was nothing in the will about Basil. There was nothing in writing at all. When Emmaline died, her three actual heirs inherited the property and sold to the investor. Basil remained in the house and, when contacted, offered a single defense: "Aunt Emmaline wanted me to stay."

That claim has a name in Texas law. Basil's position when Emmaline was alive was a permissive occupancy — she allowed him to be there. That permission ended at her death because permission is personal to the grantor. The moment Emmaline died, Basil's lawful permission evaporated. He became a tenant at sufferance under Texas Property Code Chapter 24 — someone who was once lawfully in possession but whose right has expired. The heirs, and through them the investor, have every right to remove him.

The second issue is the "oral promise" defense itself. Texas Business and Commerce Code § 26.01, the Statute of Frauds, is explicit: promises to convey or grant interests in real property exceeding one year must be in writing to be enforceable. An alleged oral promise of a life tenancy — which by definition would last decades — is squarely barred. Basil's argument was legally dead before he even made it.

## What the Operator Did

The operator served a 3-day notice to vacate. Basil ignored it. The operator filed a forcible detainer action in JP Court — the correct venue for possession disputes. The trial was scheduled and held 21 days after filing.

At trial, Basil presented his "oral promise" defense. The court dismissed it under the Statute of Frauds. The argument had no legal foundation. The court issued a writ of possession, which authorized the constable to physically remove Basil if necessary. Basil, at that point, apparently grasped what was about to happen and vacated voluntarily before the constable arrived to execute the writ.

## The Outcome

The property was vacant 41 days from the date the 3-day notice was served. Total cost of the proceeding was $1,800, including attorney fees. This is about as clean as a contested occupancy situation gets. Basil had no legal ground, his attorney (if he consulted one) would have told him the same, and the result was predictable from the moment the notice was served.

## The Lesson

Non-owner occupants are tenants at sufferance regardless of their family relationship to the decedent. The fact that Basil was Emmaline's nephew does not grant him one additional day of lawful occupancy beyond what Texas law provides — and Texas law provides none, once the permissive occupancy is terminated. Oral promises do not create real property interests. That is the entire point of the Statute of Frauds, and Texas courts enforce it strictly. Eviction is the remedy. Do not negotiate a "will you please leave" arrangement with non-owners. Serve the notice, file the case, get the writ. The timeline is predictable and the cost is modest.
