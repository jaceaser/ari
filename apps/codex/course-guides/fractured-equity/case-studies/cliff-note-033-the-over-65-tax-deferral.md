## The Situation

Elderly homeowner Henrietta, age 78, hadn't paid property taxes in nine years. The accumulated balance was $62,000. Texas Tax Code § 33.06 provides that elderly and disabled homeowners who file for the over-65 exemption with tax deferral can stop paying property taxes while they remain alive in the home — the taxes accrue, but the taxing authority cannot collect or foreclose during the exemption period.

Henrietta had done exactly that. For nine years, the balance grew while the county stood down. Then she died.

At death, the deferral terminates. Texas Tax Code § 33.06 gives the heirs a 181-day grace period from the date of death before the deferred taxes become immediately due and enforceable. After that 181-day window, the taxing authority — in this case, the county's collection firm LINEBARGER — could proceed to foreclosure.

The heirs had no idea about the $62,000 deferred balance until collection activity began.

## What the Operator Did

The operator moved fast. The 181-day grace period under § 33.06 is the window of opportunity, and it closes whether you're ready or not.

Step one was consolidating heir interests. The heirs, facing a $62,000 tax bill they hadn't expected and couldn't pay, were motivated to exit. The operator structured a rapid acquisition: all heir interests consolidated — 100% ownership transferred — in 40 days from initiation, at a total cost of $18,000.

Step two was opening negotiations with LINEBARGER on the deferred balance. The firm represents the taxing authority on collection matters. Rather than demanding full immediate payment, the operator structured a payment plan agreement — deferred balance to be paid from sale proceeds at closing. LINEBARGER agreed.

The 181-day clock never expired because the operator had clear title and a structured resolution before the deadline arrived.

## The Outcome

- Heir acquisition (100%): $18,000 total
- Deferred tax balance paid at closing: $62,000
- Property sold at: $215,000 (90 days from acquisition)
- Net profit: $87,000

The sale funded the deferred balance payment directly. No out-of-pocket on the $62,000 before the closing.

## The Lesson

Over-65 tax deferrals create ticking time bombs that detonate at death. The balance accumulates invisibly while the homeowner lives — no demand letters, no court filings, no public pressure. The heirs inherit a property they think is valuable and discover within weeks that nine years of taxes are now immediately due.

The 181-day grace period under § 33.06 is the window. It sounds like plenty of time. It isn't — not if you need to locate heirs, negotiate, document, deed, and arrange the deferred-balance payment plan simultaneously. The operator who moves in the first 60 days has a cooperative negotiating environment. The one who shows up at day 160 is dealing with a taxing authority that has already filed its foreclosure petition.

The key discipline here is knowing the deferral exists before making your offer. When you pull county records and see nine years of zero tax payments on a property held by an elderly owner, that's not a skip — that's a § 33.06 deferral. Check the exemption filings. Know the balance. Price the deal accordingly, and structure the payment plan before you close.
