## The Situation

The tax auction is six days away. The property has $47,000 in delinquent taxes and is on the Tuesday sale list. The investor holds 33% of the ownership, acquired two weeks prior. Counsel, reviewing the underlying tax suit, finds a significant problem: two of the heirs were never served. The attorney ad litem appointed to locate unknown parties missed them entirely. Their names appear in title records and heirship documents but not in the service of process record.

This matters enormously under Texas and federal constitutional law. Texas Rules of Civil Procedure 117a governs service in tax suits, and due process under Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank (339 U.S. 306) and Mennonite Board of Missions v. Adams (462 U.S. 791) require constitutionally adequate notice before a judgment can bind a party's property rights. Parties who were never served are not bound by the judgment. Their interests survive the tax sale. A buyer at Tuesday's auction would acquire title still subject to those two heirs' interests — a substantial cloud.

LINEBARGER GOGGAN BLAIR & SAMPSON, the collection firm, holds the lawsuit. They want a clean auction. They do not want to defend a defective service record in front of a judge.

## What the Operator Did

Counsel filed a TRO petition in the district court where the tax suit was pending. The petition was supported by an affidavit of heirship identifying the two missed parties and documenting exactly how they should have been found and served. The TRO sought to halt the Tuesday auction on the grounds that the judgment was constitutionally infirm as to the non-served heirs.

The TRO was granted ex parte — meaning the court signed it without first hearing from the opposing parties, based on the documented emergency. That is appropriate when an irreversible action (a tax sale) is imminent and the legal defect is clear on the face of the record.

Counsel served LINEBARGER with the TRO the following morning. Within 72 hours — not weeks, not months, 72 hours — LINEBARGER's counsel contacted the investor's attorney and stipulated to a 60-day continuance of the auction sale. In exchange, the investor withdrew the TRO. The dynamic was straightforward: LINEBARGER knew the service defect was real. They preferred a brief delay to a contested hearing where their attorneys' diligence failures would be displayed in open court.

## The Outcome

The auction was postponed 60 days. The investor used that window to acquire the two missed heirs' interests at $1,500 each, and simultaneously negotiated a payment plan with LINEBARGER for the $47,000 delinquency. The payment plan was executed, the auction date came and went without a sale, and the property was never auctioned.

## The Lesson

TROs based on defective service in tax suits work because the collection firms — LINEBARGER, PERDUE, and their counterparts — have strong institutional incentives to avoid contested hearings about service failures. Their business model depends on efficient, predictable foreclosures. A known service defect on the record undermines the enforceability of everything they are doing. A 60-day continuance in exchange for withdrawing a well-founded TRO is a straightforward trade for them. The 60-90 day postponement gives the operator what is needed to resolve the underlying heir acquisition and tax issues. Always review the tax suit record before the auction.
