## The Situation

A minor heir held a fractional interest in a property that needed to move. The heir was six months from turning 18 under Texas Family Code § 101.003 — the state's age of majority. No one could deed the minor's interest without court authorization; a minor lacks legal capacity to convey real property. The question the operator faced was not whether to address the minor's interest — it was *when*.

The obvious answer to most operators is: wait. Six months is not that long. The heir turns 18, signs the deed, deal closes. Simple.

The operator did not accept obvious answers without checking the math first.

## What the Operator Did

Before making any procedural decision, the operator built a six-month carrying-cost analysis. The property was vacant and deteriorating. Deferred maintenance was accumulating at approximately $800 per month — small items compounding into larger ones as the building sat unoccupied and unmaintained. Property taxes were accruing at $400 per month. The local market was softening; comparable sales in the neighborhood were tracking down roughly $1,000 per month as inventory increased and buyer activity slowed.

Six months of waiting produced this ledger:

- Maintenance deterioration: $800 × 6 = $4,800
- Tax accrual: $400 × 6 = $2,400
- Market-value decline: $1,000 × 6 = $6,000
- Estimated wait-period value erosion: **$12,000**

The operator then priced the alternative. Guardianship of the estate in Texas is a court proceeding — the parent or another appropriate person petitions to be appointed guardian, the court authorizes them to act on the minor's behalf in property matters, and the guardian executes the conveyance. Total cost: approximately $4,500 in attorney fees and court costs. Estimated timeline: 60 to 75 days.

The math was not ambiguous. Guardianship cost $4,500. Waiting cost $12,000. The operator filed for guardianship immediately.

The minor's parent was appointed guardian of the estate. The guardian executed the deed conveying the minor's fractional interest as authorized by the court. The court's order specifically authorized the real estate transaction and confirmed the sale price was at fair market value for the minor's protection — standard Texas guardianship procedure.

## The Outcome

The deal closed in 90 days from the operator's decision to pursue guardianship. The minor's interest was sold at full market price. No value was lost to carrying-cost erosion because the operator moved instead of waiting.

The financial comparison was decisive: $4,500 in guardianship costs versus $12,000 in estimated carrying-cost erosion from a six-month wait. The operator saved approximately $7,500 in net deal value by treating the timing decision as an economic calculation rather than a procedural default.

## The Lesson

Every operator who encounters a minor heir asks the same question: do I file guardianship or do I wait? Most operators wait automatically because guardianship sounds complicated and expensive. That instinct costs money every time it is not tested against the actual numbers.

Waiting is a choice with a price. That price is carrying costs — taxes, maintenance, and market movement — multiplied by the number of months until the heir turns 18. Guardianship is a choice with a price. That price is attorney fees and court costs, typically $3,000 to $6,000 depending on the county and complexity.

Compare the two numbers. When the carrying-cost wait exceeds the guardianship cost, file immediately. When the minor is three weeks from turning 18 and the property is stable, waiting is the rational choice.

The operators who lose money on deals like this are the ones who never do this math. They assume waiting is free. Waiting is never free — it is just a cost that feels invisible until you add it up.
