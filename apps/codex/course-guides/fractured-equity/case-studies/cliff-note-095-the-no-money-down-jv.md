## The Situation

The heir held 100% of the inherited property in economic terms — she was the only child of the only child of the original owner. The property was hers by right. But the right meant nothing without clear title, and clear title was inaccessible to her without resources she did not have.

Three generations of inheritance had passed through the property without a single person filing probate, recording an affidavit of heirship, or otherwise documenting the transfer of ownership through the public records. The chain of title sat frozen at the original owner's name — a person who had been dead for decades. To sell the property, the heir would need:

- A comprehensive Affidavit of Heirship establishing three generations of succession
- Likely a formal Determination of Heirship proceeding to produce court-level documentation
- Delinquent taxes cleared
- Title company underwriting of a complex historical chain
- Attorney involvement at multiple stages

Total estimated cost: in the range of $14,000 to $20,000 in legal fees, court costs, and tax obligations. The heir had none of that. She had the property and no way to reach it.

## What the Operator Did

The operator recognized that a conventional acquisition structure would not work here. The heir was not going to convey a deed for a purchase price and trust the investor to clear title — she had no leverage once she transferred the interest, and she had no money to contribute to the closing process. A straight purchase would have required either paying her at closing (impossible with the title defects) or asking her to trust that the investor would do the work and pay her later (which is not a standard or enforceable structure in Texas).

The Joint Venture structure solved the alignment problem. Template 2 — the JV Agreement used in fractured-equity transactions with cooperative but resource-limited heirs — creates a documented, enforceable arrangement:

**The Heir-to-Investor Deed:** The heir conveys 100% of her interest to the investor immediately. This gives the investor control and the ability to act on the property without needing the heir's ongoing authorization. The heir is no longer a party whose signature is needed at every turn.

**The JV Agreement:** Simultaneously with the deed, the investor and heir execute a JV Agreement that documents the economic arrangement. The investor agrees to fund all costs — legal, title clearing, taxes, carrying costs, marketing, and closing. The agreement specifies that upon sale, net proceeds (gross sale price minus all documented costs) are split 50/50 between the investor and the heir.

**The Heir's Position:** The heir holds no deed but holds a contractual right to 50% of net proceeds under the JV Agreement. The JV Agreement is recorded or otherwise protected to prevent the investor from selling the property and ignoring the heir's share. The heir has exchanged the uncertainty of an untouchable property for a documented legal right to half of whatever the property produces.

The investor then executed the work. A comprehensive three-generation Affidavit of Heirship traced the chain from the original deceased owner through each subsequent generation to the current heir. Counsel evaluated whether an AOH alone would be sufficient or whether a formal Determination of Heirship was needed — in this case, a combination approach satisfied the underwriter. Delinquent taxes were paid. The property was marketed once clear title documentation was in place. Closing occurred with a standard title insurance policy.

## The Outcome

Gross sale price: $175,000.

Costs itemized:
- Legal fees (title clearing, heirship documentation, attorney review): approximately $18,000
- Delinquent taxes paid: approximately $12,000
- Carrying costs (insurance, utilities, maintenance during the period): approximately $8,000
- Marketing and closing costs: approximately $18,000
- Total costs: approximately $56,000

Net proceeds: $175,000 - $56,000 = $119,000

Split 50/50:
- Heir: $59,500
- Investor net (after costs funded upfront): approximately $119,000 ÷ 2 - $56,000 costs advanced = approximately $45,000 (since the costs had already been spent and the heir's 50% came out of gross before the investor recovered costs, effectively: investor received $59,500 of the $119,000 net minus the $14,500 they had fronted above what their 50% covered)

The heir received $59,500 — money she could never have accessed without the investor's capital and expertise. The property would have continued to sit deteriorating, with taxes accruing, with no path to sale, until a tax foreclosure eventually consumed it.

The investor received approximately $45,000 — a return achieved without any acquisition purchase price, funded entirely from the spread between the gross sale price and the documented costs.

## The Lesson

The No-Money-Down JV is not a trick. It is a structure that exists because certain heir-property situations cannot be solved any other way. The heir has the right but not the resources. The investor has the resources but not the right unless the heir conveys it. The JV structure aligns both parties' interests around a shared outcome.

Three conditions determine whether a No-Money-Down JV is appropriate:

**First:** The heir must hold clear economic ownership without any competing claim from other heirs or creditors. A sole heir with a three-generation title defect qualifies. A complex multi-heir situation does not — the JV structure works cleanly when there is one documented economic owner who simply cannot reach the asset.

**Second:** The deal economics must support the structure. The property must be valuable enough that a 50% share of net proceeds — after all costs — is worth doing for both parties. If the property would net $20,000 after costs, a $10,000 split is not worth the investor's time and risk. This deal's $119,000 net produced $59,500 and $45,000 — both parties had a meaningful win.

**Third:** The heir must understand the structure and accept it with eyes open. The heir is giving up the possibility of 100% of the proceeds in exchange for certainty that they will receive 50% through a process they could not execute alone. That is a rational trade when the alternative is 100% of nothing.

Document the JV Agreement thoroughly. Record it or take other steps to protect the heir's position contractually. The deal closes for both parties — and the heir's $59,500 is the proof that the structure works.
