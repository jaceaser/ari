## The Situation

The family home passed to four heirs as tenants in common. Cotenant Corinne, holding a 25% interest, executed a written three-year lease with a third-party tenant named Thierry — signing the lease herself, without the knowledge or joinder of the other three co-owners. Thierry paid $1,450 per month in rent. He paid it faithfully for 48 months. All of it went to Corinne.

Texas cotenancy law permits one cotenant to lease their own undivided interest and effectively lease the property itself — the tenant takes subject to the arrangement. But that permission comes with an obligation: when one cotenant collects rent from property co-owned by others, the collecting cotenant owes those other co-owners their proportional share of the rent received. The non-joining cotenants are entitled to a rent accounting. The lease may have been valid, but the rent was not Corinne's alone to keep.

The investor acquires the three non-Corinne interests. With that acquisition comes the right to demand the accounting — and the investor takes an assignment of that claim through Template 8.

## What the Operator Did

The first step was straightforward: the investor served a 30-day notice on Thierry as the 75% majority owner of the property. The written lease had already expired and the tenancy had converted to month-to-month, requiring only a 30-day termination notice. Thierry vacated in 28 days without dispute.

The second step was the accounting claim against Corinne. The math was clean: $1,450 per month times 48 months equals $69,600 in total rent collected. The non-joining cotenants held 75% of the property, so their proportional share of that rent was $52,200. Corinne owed $52,200 to the co-owners she had never disclosed the lease to — a claim now held by the investor as assignee.

The investor then presented Corinne with the accounting demand. Corinne's attorney did the arithmetic: her 25% interest in a property worth roughly $32,000 in fractured-equity acquisition terms was worth far less than $52,200. The accounting claim exceeded the value of her own interest. The global settlement was straightforward — Corinne conveyed her 25% interest to the investor in exchange for a full release of the accounting claim. No cash changed hands.

## The Outcome

The investor acquired 100% of the property. The total acquisition cost was $32,000 — the purchase price paid to the three cooperative cotenants. Corinne's 25% came at no additional cost; the accounting claim served as the consideration on both sides of the exchange. Thierry vacated in 28 days from notice. The entire resolution was complete.

## The Lesson

Cotenants who collect rent without sharing it with their co-owners do not get to keep it. Texas law is clear: proportional rent accounting is owed. The investor who acquires from the non-collecting co-owners steps into their shoes and inherits the right to demand that accounting. When the accounting liability exceeds the value of the collecting cotenant's own interest — as it did here — the leverage is extraordinary. The collecting cotenant is essentially paying for their own buyout through the money they already collected. Assign the accounting claim. Use it. It is often the most powerful tool you have.
