## The Situation

Truman Edgerton is a 25% heir who has turned the family home into his personal fortress. His deceased father's property sits with three other siblings who have cooperated and already sold their interests. Truman is different. He refuses to pay taxes. He refuses to contribute a dime toward maintenance. When the investor approached him with a buyout offer, Truman's response was unambiguous: "go to hell."

The investor holds 75% of the property through assignment from the other three siblings. Truman holds 25% and occupies the premises. He has no legal right to exclusive possession — cotenants do not get the whole house because they happen to be sleeping there — but he is acting as though he does, and no amount of reasonable conversation is going to change that.

The key law here is Texas Property Code Chapter 24 — forcible detainer against a cotenant holding over exclusively against majority owners — and Texas Property Code Chapter 23, which governs partition. The strategy is to apply leverage from both directions at the same time.

## What the Operator Did

The operator filed a forcible detainer action AND a partition petition on the same day. Both were served on Truman simultaneously. The message was unmistakable: there are now two separate legal proceedings pending, either of which will result in Truman losing the property.

Within two weeks, Truman had hired an attorney. That attorney did what competent attorneys do: he reviewed the facts, reviewed the law, and told his client the truth. The negotiation that followed was quick. Settlement terms were straightforward — $10,500 for Truman's 25% interest, plus $5,000 in relocation assistance, a 60-day vacate commitment, and a mutual release of all claims. Upon execution of the settlement agreement, the investor dismissed both the forcible detainer action and the partition petition.

## The Outcome

Truman vacated on day 52. The total payout was $15,500, which covered both his interest and his moving costs. His attorney explained the calculus clearly: the forcible detainer would remove him from the property without compensating him for his interest, and the partition would result in a forced sale at which the majority owner held all the leverage. Either path ended with Truman getting far less than $15,500 or nothing at all. He accepted the settlement.

## The Lesson

Hostile occupants respond to legal pressure, not reason. You have probably already tried reason — that is why you are reading this cliff note. The dual filing of eviction and partition simultaneously is the correct tool because it signals commitment. It tells the occupant and their attorney that there is no escape from losing the property, only a choice of how much cooperation yields in return. Attorneys engaged by occupants in these situations typically counsel acceptance of a reasonable settlement once they review the actual legal landscape. File both. Serve both. Then let the attorney do your negotiating for you.
