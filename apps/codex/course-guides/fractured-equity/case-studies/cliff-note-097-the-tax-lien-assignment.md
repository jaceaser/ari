## The Situation

The delinquent tax list is where fractured-equity operators find their leads. Most of the time, the delinquency reflects a property ownership situation that is fractured by heirs, estates, defunct entities, or other structural problems. But sometimes the delinquent tax list reveals a different situation: a living, solo owner with significant equity who simply has not paid their property taxes and is now at risk of losing a valuable asset to the taxing authority.

This property was that second situation. The owner was a single individual. The property had approximately $180,000 in fair market value. The encumbrances against it — aside from the tax delinquency — were modest. There was substantial equity. The owner had fallen behind on taxes due to a combination of circumstances that included an extended illness and a period of financial disruption.

The investor recognized that the conventional fractured-equity acquisition toolkit did not directly apply here — there were no heirs to negotiate with, no estate to navigate, no title defects from a chain-of-title problem. But the delinquent tax situation opened a different door: the tax lien transfer mechanism under Texas Tax Code § 32.06.

## What the Operator Did

**Understanding § 32.06**

Texas Tax Code § 32.06 permits a person to pay the delinquent property taxes on behalf of an owner and to receive, in return, a transfer of the taxing authority's tax lien. This is not the same as buying a tax lien certificate from a tax authority at a public sale — it is a direct payment to the taxing authority with a contractual and statutory right to receive the lien. The transferred lien is documented under § 32.065 and gives the investor a position as a secured creditor with a lien that carries the same priority as the original tax lien — which in Texas means the senior position.

Texas property taxes are the senior lien in all cases. They prime mortgages, judgment liens, and other encumbrances. When the investor steps into the taxing authority's lien position through a § 32.06 transfer, the investor inherits that senior priority.

**Executing the Transfer**

The investor calculated the full delinquent tax amount — including penalties and interest that had accrued — and paid it directly to the county taxing authority. The payment was made under § 32.06, with the appropriate documentation for the transferred lien filed under § 32.065.

The investor was now a secured creditor holding a lien with statutory priority against a property with approximately $180,000 in value. The investor's exposure was the amount paid in taxes plus whatever carrying charges and costs the statutory framework provided for.

**The Repayment Agreement**

The investor approached the property owner with a repayment structure rather than immediately pursuing foreclosure. The equity situation was strong enough that the owner should, in theory, have been able to refinance the property — the lenders would find adequate equity coverage even with the tax lien in place — and use the refinance proceeds to pay off the investor's transferred lien. The investor gave the owner a defined window to complete the refinance or establish an alternative repayment arrangement.

The owner's credit situation proved more complicated than the equity position suggested. Multiple credit issues that a mortgage lender would evaluate independently prevented the refinance from proceeding. The timeline agreed upon passed without repayment.

**The Foreclosure**

The investor exercised the foreclosure rights available under the transferred tax lien. Texas law provides a foreclosure process for transferred tax liens that differs from the non-judicial foreclosure process used for deed-of-trust liens. The investor followed the applicable statutory procedure — providing required notices, following the timeline, and proceeding to the foreclosure sale.

The investor bid at the foreclosure sale in an amount sufficient to acquire the property. As the lienholder conducting the sale, the investor's position was inherently advantaged: the investor knew the property, knew the equity, and was bidding against their own lien.

## The Outcome

Title was transferred to the investor through the statutory foreclosure. The investor had paid the delinquent taxes, held the senior lien position through the § 32.06 transfer, and executed the foreclosure process as provided by Texas law.

The equity in the property — approximately $180,000 in value above the investor's total costs, including taxes paid, carrying charges, and foreclosure costs — was captured through the acquisition and subsequent disposition.

## The Lesson

The tax lien transfer mechanism under § 32.06 is advanced. It is not appropriate for operators without solid understanding of the statutory framework, the lien priority implications, and the foreclosure procedures applicable to transferred tax liens. But for operators who understand it, § 32.06 creates an entry strategy that does not depend on heir cooperation, estate proceedings, or title defect remediation.

The mechanism works best in a specific factual profile:

- A property with significant equity (the senior lien position is most valuable when there is equity to capture)
- A living owner whose delinquency is circumstantial rather than structural (if the property is genuinely underwater, the lien position is less meaningful)
- A delinquency large enough that paying it creates a meaningful secured position
- An owner whose path to refinancing or alternative payment is genuinely blocked

When all four conditions are present, the investor who pays the taxes under § 32.06 converts a delinquent-tax situation into a secured creditor position with foreclosure rights that, if exercised, produce an acquisition at a cost equal to the taxes paid plus carrying charges — potentially well below the equity value of the property.

The senior lien priority is the key. Taxes prime everything else in Texas. Stepping into the taxing authority's position means stepping in front of every other creditor in the priority queue. That position is worth understanding and, when appropriate, deploying.
