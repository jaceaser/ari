## The Situation

Cotenancy creates a shared obligation: each cotenant owes a duty not to waste the common property. A cotenant in possession who allows the property to deteriorate through neglect, or who actively damages it through misconduct, has breached a duty to every other cotenant and is liable for the resulting diminution in value.

The occupant in this deal had been living in the property for years. The state of the property told a story: a roof that had been leaking for what appeared to be at least two years had soaked the ceiling joists and wall framing in two sections of the house. Fixtures — light switches, outlet covers, cabinet hardware, a bathroom exhaust fan — were missing, apparently removed and not replaced. A non-structural interior wall had been removed without permits and without proper structural reinforcement of the adjacent framing. The kitchen had been partially "remodeled" with materials and workmanship that would need to be removed and redone. General cleaning and remediation costs added to the total.

The other cotenants had not been watching. The investor, acquiring interests from them, discovered the damage condition during due diligence.

## What the Operator Did

Before making any claim, the investor needed documentation. A waste claim without supporting evidence is an assertion; a waste claim backed by a detailed professional damage assessment is leverage. The investor hired a licensed contractor with estimating experience to walk the property and prepare a written assessment that:

- Described each condition of damage observed
- Distinguished between normal wear and tear (not attributable to waste) and damage attributable to the occupant's conduct or neglect
- Estimated repair costs for each item to restore the property to condition comparable to neighboring properties of similar age and construction
- Noted which conditions were caused by active misconduct (the structural modification, the removed fixtures) versus neglect (the leaking roof allowed to continue)

The contractor's report documented total damages of $38,000. This number was not inflated — the contractor's methodology was defensible and based on market repair costs, not premium restoration pricing.

Counsel filed a waste claim against the occupant-cotenant in the district court. The petition alleged waste under Texas cotenancy law — the doctrine that a cotenant in possession who damages the common property is liable to the other cotenants for their proportionate loss — and claimed damages based on the contractor's assessment.

The occupant responded as expected: counterclaims alleging that she had made improvements to the property that should be credited against any waste recovery. This is a recognized doctrine — a cotenant who makes genuine improvements that increase the property's value may be entitled to a credit for those improvements in a waste or contribution accounting. Counsel evaluated the claimed improvements. Some were legitimate; most were not, because the "improvements" were either offset by the damage they accompanied or were below-standard workmanship that would need to be corrected anyway.

The litigation created the pressure for settlement. The occupant's attorney ran the same math the investor's attorney had run: a $38,000 documented waste claim against a cotenant holding an interest with a nominal value of $42,000 left very little room for the occupant to receive meaningful value in any negotiated resolution. Litigation would cost both sides; the occupant would lose on most of the waste issues; the improvements credit would not save her.

## The Outcome

Settlement was reached on terms that the economics dictated. The occupant conveyed her fractional interest to the investor for $15,000 — reflecting the nominal value of approximately $42,000 reduced by the waste offset and the practical reality that litigation would cost her more to continue. The waste claim was released in full as part of the settlement.

The occupant received $15,000 cash. The investor received the interest at a price that accurately reflected the economic damage the occupant had caused to the shared property. No further litigation.

The investor then factored the $38,000 in documented repairs into the post-acquisition rehab budget, knowing the scope of work that needed to be done before the property could be positioned for sale.

## The Lesson

Waste claims serve two purposes simultaneously: they are a direct path to damages recovery through litigation, and they are a negotiating tool that reduces the effective purchase price of a damaging cotenant's interest.

The key is documentation. A professional damage assessment, prepared before any legal claim is filed, anchors the waste claim to specific, itemized, defensible figures. It gives the investor credibility in negotiations and evidence in litigation. Without the assessment, the waste claim is a number pulled from the air — vulnerable to challenge and difficult to settle around.

The offset structure is elegant: instead of paying the occupant $42,000 for an interest that the occupant damaged the property to be worth less of, the investor credits the $38,000 waste claim against the purchase price and pays $15,000 for what is economically the correct purchase price after accounting for the occupant's conduct.

Document the damage. File the claim. Let the math do the negotiating.
