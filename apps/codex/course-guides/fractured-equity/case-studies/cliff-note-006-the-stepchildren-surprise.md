## The Situation

Harrison Pemberton died in Waco owning community property with his second wife Genevieve. Harrison had two children from his first marriage — Sierra and Darren. He and Genevieve had one child together, Matthew. Harrison died first. Genevieve died later. Neither left a will.

Genevieve had been living in the property and believed she owned it fully. Sierra was also involved with the property and paying taxes on it. Neither Sierra nor Genevieve understood what Texas law actually did with Harrison's half of the community estate when he died with children outside his current marriage.

## What the Operator Did

Texas Estates Code § 201.003 is one of the most counterintuitive rules in Texas intestate law. When a decedent dies with community property and has ANY child outside the current marriage, 100% of the decedent's community half passes to his children — not to the surviving spouse. Zero to the spouse. All to the children.

At Harrison's death: his 50% community interest split equally among Sierra, Darren, and Matthew — 16.67% each. Genevieve retained only her own 50%. She received nothing from his half.

At Genevieve's later death: her 50% passed entirely to Matthew as her only child. No stepchildren inherit from a stepparent under intestate succession.

Final ownership after both deaths: Matthew 66.67% (his 16.67% from Harrison plus Genevieve's full 50%), Sierra 16.67%, Darren 16.67%.

Sierra was occupying the property and paying taxes. The operator's pitch was direct: you are paying taxes on a property where your stepbrother owns two-thirds. She sold her 16.67% for $3,500 after understanding this. Darren sold his 16.67% for $4,200. Matthew, who held the dominant interest at 66.67%, declined a full buyout but agreed to a JV structure at 50/50 on his share.

## The Outcome

Sierra acquired at $3,500. Darren at $4,200. Matthew became a JV partner at 50/50 on his 66.67% rather than accepting a buyout. The transaction completed with Matthew as a participating partner.

## The Lesson

Children from prior marriages trigger § 201.003, which hands them ALL of the deceased parent's community half — zero to the surviving spouse. Catches everybody. Most families — most attorneys, even — do not think about this rule until they are sitting across from an investor who has done the math. Teaching the law is the deal strategy here. The revelation creates cooperation because it resolves a confusion that was simmering but had no resolution. Once Sierra understood she was subsidizing Matthew's majority stake, the decision to sell became obvious.
