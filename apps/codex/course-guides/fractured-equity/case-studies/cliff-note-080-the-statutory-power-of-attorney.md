## The Situation

An heir held a fractional interest in inherited property. By the time the operator reached her, she had advanced dementia and lacked contractual capacity to sign any legal documents. At first glance, this looked like the scenario from Cliff Note 78 — informal family management, no legal authority to convey, guardianship required.

But there was a critical difference: three years before the dementia had progressed to the point of incapacity, this heir had visited an estate planning attorney and executed a Texas statutory durable power of attorney. She had named her son as her agent. The document was properly executed, witnessed, and notarized.

That one piece of advance planning changed everything about the transaction.

## What the Operator Did

The first task was verifying the POA's legal validity and scope. Three questions governed the analysis.

**Question One: Is the POA durable?** Under Texas Estates Code Chapter 751 et seq., a power of attorney is durable only if it expressly states that it survives the principal's incapacity or disability. A non-durable power of attorney terminates automatically when the principal becomes incapacitated — which means a non-durable POA executed before dementia onset would have become ineffective the moment the heir lost capacity. The heir's instrument included the statutory durability language making it effective through incapacity. It was durable.

**Question Two: Does the POA cover real property transactions?** A power of attorney's grant of authority is defined by what the instrument says. A POA that covers only financial accounts or healthcare decisions does not confer authority to execute real estate deeds. The operator reviewed the instrument against Texas Estates Code Chapter 752, which defines the specific grants of authority available in statutory POAs. The heir's POA included the real property grant — it expressly authorized the agent to buy, sell, manage, and encumber real property on the principal's behalf. The authority was there.

**Question Three: Was the POA executed before incapacity?** A person must have capacity at the time of executing a POA — the same contractual capacity required for any legal instrument. A POA executed after dementia onset, when the principal lacks capacity, is void. The timing here was clear: the heir executed the POA three years before the dementia progressed to the point of incapacity. The execution was valid.

All three questions resolved in favor of the POA's validity and the agent's authority. The operator's counsel prepared the deed and conveyance documents. The son, acting as agent under the durable POA, executed the deed on behalf of his mother as principal. The deed identified him as "John Smith, agent for Jane Smith under Statutory Durable Power of Attorney dated [date]" — the standard form for agent-executed conveyances in Texas.

The title company reviewed the POA instrument, confirmed its validity and scope, and accepted the agent-executed deed. No guardianship petition was filed, no court proceeding was initiated, and no independent appraisal was ordered. The transaction moved on a normal timeline.

Because the agent had the authority to negotiate and agree to a price on the principal's behalf — unlike a court-supervised guardianship sale that requires market value — the operator was able to acquire the interest at Fractured Equity discount pricing. The agent negotiated the same way any other heir would negotiate.

## The Outcome

Agent-signed deed accepted. Transaction closed at Fractured Equity pricing — a discounted acquisition, not a market-value court-supervised sale. No guardianship required. No court proceeding required.

## The Lesson

The difference between this case and Cliff Note 78 is a single document executed years earlier. The heir who planned ahead — who sat with an estate planning attorney and executed a statutory durable POA before losing capacity — created a path for her family to handle her real property interests without court intervention. The heir who made no such plan left her family with a multi-month guardianship proceeding and a court-mandated market-value sale.

The statutory durable POA is the planning tool that eliminates the guardianship burden. In fractured equity practice, you will encounter both situations — heirs with POAs and heirs without. When a POA exists, verify the three elements (durable, real property scope, pre-incapacity execution) before you close without guardianship. If all three are satisfied, the agent has authority and the transaction can proceed.

One additional verification step that experienced operators build into POA transactions: confirm the POA has not been revoked. A durable POA remains in effect through incapacity, but it can be revoked by the principal while the principal still has capacity. Check with the agent and review the available records to ensure no revocation instrument was executed after the POA was signed. A revoked POA, regardless of what the original instrument says, is no longer effective.

The broader takeaway: advance planning by a property owner directly determines how difficult or easy that property's transfer will be years later. A statutory durable POA costs a few hundred dollars in attorney fees. A guardianship proceeding costs thousands and takes months. In fractured equity practice, you are cleaning up the consequences of the planning that did or did not happen years before you arrived.
