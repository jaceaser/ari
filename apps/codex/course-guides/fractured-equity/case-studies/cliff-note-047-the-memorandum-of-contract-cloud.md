## The Situation

A memorandum of contract from a 2022 purchase attempt appeared in the real property records and was flagged on the title commitment. Memorandums of contract are filed to put the world on notice that a specific buyer has a contract to purchase a specific property — it's a protective filing that prevents the seller from selling to someone else while the original deal is pending.

The 2022 deal, however, was not pending. It was dead. The buyer's financing had fallen through, the transaction had never closed, and both parties knew the deal was over. But when the deal collapsed, no one had bothered to file a release of the memorandum. The instrument sat in the real property records — still indexed against the property, still showing on title searches — even though the underlying contract it memorialized had expired and was completely unenforceable.

Texas Property Code § 12.007 governs memorandums of contract. When the underlying contract expires or terminates without closing, the memorandum should be released of record by a Memorandum of Contract Release instrument. Without a recorded release, the memorandum clouds title indefinitely.

## What the Operator Did

The path to clearing this cloud was direct: find the would-be buyer and get the release.

The investor's counsel identified and contacted the 2022 would-be buyer. The conversation was simple — the deal was dead, both parties knew it, and the memorandum needed to be formally released. The would-be buyer had no remaining interest in the property, no pending claim, and no reason to resist. They had simply never thought about the unreleased memorandum.

The resolution instrument was Template 11 — the Memorandum of Contract Release. The would-be buyer executed it. It was notarized and recorded in the real property records.

The recorded release cleared the title commitment. The cloud was gone.

## The Outcome

- Memorandum of contract: released
- Would-be buyer cooperation: immediate — no resistance
- Resolution instrument: Template 11 (Memorandum of Contract Release), executed and recorded
- Title cleared: clean commitment issued
- Investor closing: proceeded normally

## The Lesson

Stale memorandums of contract are common. Real estate deals fall apart at high rates — financing contingencies fail, due diligence issues surface, buyers change their minds. When those deals collapse, the paperwork for them doesn't always get cleaned up properly. The memorandum stays in the records because no one is paying attention to the title status of a property they're no longer buying.

This creates a straightforward mechanical problem with a straightforward mechanical solution. The cloud is not adversarial — the would-be buyer has no current interest in the property and no reason to maintain the cloud. They just need to be found and asked to sign the release.

The investigation is simple: pull the memorandum from the real property records, identify the buyer who signed it, and make contact. Usually cooperative on first contact, as in this case. Occasionally requires a little more persuasion — the would-be buyer might want the release as leverage for some unrelated dispute with the heir. Even then, a small accommodation is typically enough to get the signature. The release instrument itself — Template 11 — is simple and straightforward.

Document the contact and the release carefully. The recorded release is what clears the title commitment. Verbal acknowledgment that the deal is dead is not sufficient — the title company needs the recorded instrument. Execute the release, notarize it properly, and get it into the real property records before you open title for the investor's closing.

The lesson for deal pipeline: when you're reviewing title commitments, a stale memorandum of contract is one of the easier Schedule C items to clear. Identify the would-be buyer, make contact, use Template 11, and move on.
