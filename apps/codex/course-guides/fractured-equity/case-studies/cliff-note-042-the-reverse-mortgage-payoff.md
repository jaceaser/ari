## The Situation

The decedent had taken out a Home Equity Conversion Mortgage — a HECM, commonly called a reverse mortgage — on her property. At the time of her death, the HECM balance owed was $185,000. The property was worth $245,000. The heirs held the equity spread — but only if they acted.

Federal law governing HECMs (12 U.S.C. § 1715z-20 and HUD implementing regulations) gives heirs a structured timeline to resolve the loan after the borrower's death. Heirs have up to 12 months — extendable with HUD approval in certain circumstances — to choose one of three options: pay off the HECM balance and keep the property, sell the property and pay off the balance from proceeds, or execute a deed-in-lieu of foreclosure and walk away.

The critical HUD rule for sales: heirs can satisfy the HECM by paying 95% of the current appraised value, OR the full outstanding balance — whichever is less. This ceiling is protective in cases where the HECM balance has grown to exceed property value. In this case, the balance ($185,000) was less than 95% of appraised value ($232,750 at 95%), so the full balance of $185,000 applied.

The heirs faced a ticking 12-month clock and no cash to pay the $185,000 balance.

## What the Operator Did

The investor's offer to the heirs was structured to solve their problem: acquire the heirs' interests for $22,000 combined, with the investor taking responsibility for the HECM payoff at closing. The heirs received $22,000 they wouldn't otherwise see — the alternative was a deed-in-lieu or foreclosure, both of which produce nothing for the heirs.

The investor accepted the HECM payoff obligation at closing. The full $185,000 balance was paid from the investor's proceeds at the sale closing, with HUD's servicer handling the discharge of the reverse mortgage lien.

After acquisition, modest cleanup — no major rehabilitation — brought the property to market condition. Sold at $241,000.

## The Outcome

- Heir interest acquisition (combined): $22,000
- HECM payoff at closing: $185,000
- Total investor cost: $207,000
- Cleanup costs: modest
- Sale price: $241,000
- Net profit: approximately $28,000
- Timeline: 75 days from acquisition to close of sale

## The Lesson

Reverse mortgages create structured acquisition opportunities that most buyers don't know how to navigate. The HECM timeline — 12 months with extensions — feels long, but it's not. By the time heirs figure out what a HECM is, locate the servicer, understand their options, and realize they can't pay $185,000, several months have elapsed. The investor who shows up with a clear proposal — "I'll acquire your interests for cash and handle the HECM payoff" — is solving a real problem the heirs cannot solve themselves.

The 95%-of-appraised-value rule is the ceiling to understand when the HECM balance has grown beyond property value. In those cases — where the balance exceeds 95% of appraised value — the investor can pay 95% of appraised value and get clean title, even though the servicer's ledger shows more owed. The servicer absorbs the shortfall through FHA insurance. That ceiling makes severely over-leveraged HECM properties potentially viable acquisitions that look impossible at first glance.

In this case the math was tighter — the balance was well below value — making the profit margin slim. But the 75-day timeline and relatively clean execution demonstrate the model. The discipline is speed: HECM servicers work on strict HUD-mandated timelines, and delays in the transaction can trigger foreclosure proceedings even when the parties are cooperating. Move fast, communicate with the servicer proactively, and get the payoff demand letter before you close on the heir interests so you know the exact number you're working with.
