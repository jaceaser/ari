## The Situation

Most operators think about deceased-person estates exclusively in terms of real property. That instinct is natural because real property is the most visible asset — it shows up on delinquent tax lists, on county records, on title searches. But a decedent's estate can include anything the decedent owned at the time of death: pending legal claims, unclaimed property sitting with the state Comptroller, business interests, investment accounts, and a range of other assets that pass to the estate's heirs.

The decedent in this case had left a complicated estate. There was real property — the starting point of the investor's interest. But there was also a personal injury lawsuit that the decedent had been pursuing at the time of death and that had not been resolved. There was unclaimed property recorded with the Texas Comptroller under the decedent's name — funds that had been escheated to the state from a financial account the decedent had abandoned. And there was a business interest — a membership interest in a small company the decedent had helped found.

The heir — the sole heir, as it turned out — knew in general terms that the estate contained these things but had neither the time, the expertise, nor the resources to pursue each asset class independently. The heir wanted a single transaction: sell everything to one party, receive one payment, be done with it.

## What the Operator Did

The investor recognized that the estate's aggregate value across all asset classes was substantially higher than the real estate alone would have been worth. The heir's desire for simplicity created a pricing opportunity: the investor could offer a lump sum for all assets that was higher than the real estate alone justified, but lower than the aggregate of all the individual asset recoveries the investor expected to achieve.

**The Assignment Instrument**

The investor executed a comprehensive Assignment of Rights and Claims — Template 8 — covering all of the heir's inherited interests:

- The right to pursue and recover on the pending personal injury lawsuit
- The right to claim the unclaimed property from the Texas Comptroller
- The business interest (membership interest in the company)
- The real property interest

The assignment was drafted to be comprehensive. Texas property law treats claims as assignable unless the claim is strictly personal in nature — personal injury claims survive death in Texas and pass to the estate, and the estate's right to prosecute the claim is assignable to a third party who purchases it. The unclaimed property right, the business interest, and the real estate interest were each assignable through standard mechanisms.

**Pursuing Each Asset Class Independently**

Once the assignment was executed and documented, the investor engaged separate professionals to pursue each asset class:

**Personal Injury Lawsuit:** The investor retained a personal injury attorney who evaluated the pending claim, assessed its strengths and settlement value, and prosecuted it to resolution. The claim settled for an amount that, after the attorney's contingency fee, netted $62,000 to the investor.

**Unclaimed Property:** The Texas Comptroller's Unclaimed Property Division holds funds escheated from financial institutions when accounts are abandoned. The investor filed a claim with supporting documentation of the assignment and the deceased account holder's identity. The Comptroller processed the claim and released $38,000 in unclaimed property funds.

**Business Interest:** The investor engaged a business attorney and broker to evaluate the decedent's membership interest in the company. The company's other members were approached, the interest was valued through negotiation and review of the company's financials, and the interest was sold through a negotiated transaction at $142,000.

**Real Estate:** Standard fractured-equity methodology applied — title was cleared through the appropriate combination of heirship documentation and legal work, and the property was positioned for sale. It sold at $67,000 after closing.

## The Outcome

The recoveries by asset class:

| Asset | Recovery |
|-------|----------|
| Personal injury lawsuit (net of attorney fees) | $62,000 |
| Unclaimed property | $38,000 |
| Business interest | $142,000 |
| Real estate | $67,000 |
| **Total recoveries** | **$309,000** |

Acquisition cost (payment to heir under the assignment): $150,000.

Net profit after all professional fees, transaction costs, and expenses: approximately $131,000.

The heir received $150,000 — a single payment for an estate the heir was not equipped to administer independently. The investor received a $131,000 net profit on a transaction that required identifying, pursuing, and closing on four separate asset classes across a multi-month period.

## The Lesson

The scope of an estate is defined by everything the decedent owned at death — not just the real property. An operator who limits their attention to real estate is leaving money on the table whenever a decedent also had pending claims, unclaimed property, or business interests.

Template 8 — the Assignment of Rights and Claims — is the instrument that captures everything in a single conveyance. When a heir wants simplicity and is willing to assign all inherited interests for a single price, the investor who can value the aggregate of those interests and structure the acquisition appropriately is positioned to profit from the spread between the lump-sum price and the individually realized values.

The spread in this deal was substantial because four separate asset classes were involved, each pursued independently through professionals who knew their specific domain. The investor's role was to coordinate, fund, and manage the process across all four tracks simultaneously — not to be an expert in personal injury litigation, unclaimed property procedures, business valuation, and real estate all at once, but to engage the right professionals for each and capture the aggregate.

Look past the real estate when you analyze a deceased person's estate. What else did they own? What pending claims existed? Were there business interests? Is there unclaimed property in the Comptroller's database? The answers to those questions sometimes produce the most valuable parts of the deal.
