## The Situation

The estate the operator was working had multiple heirs — standard fractured-equity terrain. One of those heirs was a minor who had died before reaching the age of majority. The minor held a fractional interest in the inherited property, and that interest needed to be accounted for before the deal could close.

Most operators freeze at this point. A deceased minor sounds like a complicated mess — a sub-estate that needs its own probate, its own administrator, its own proceedings. The operator did not freeze. The operator traced the succession.

## What the Operator Did

Under Texas Estates Code § 201.001, when a person dies intestate, their property passes to their heirs according to the statutory scheme. The minor was a person. The minor died intestate — no will, obviously, since the minor was a child. Therefore the minor's own estate passed to the minor's heirs under the same rules that had governed the original decedent's estate.

The operator walked through the analysis:

- Did the minor have any descendants? No — the minor was a child.
- Did the minor have a surviving spouse? No.
- Therefore, under Texas intestate succession, the minor's estate passed to the minor's parents.

The critical discovery was what this meant practically: the minor's parents were already heirs of the original decedent. They were already parties the operator was dealing with. They already had interests in the same property. The minor's death had not introduced any new parties to the ownership picture — it had simply redirected the minor's fractional interest to people who were already at the table.

The family tree collapsed. Instead of a structure that included the parents' original shares plus a separate minor's share requiring independent disposition, the structure now consisted entirely of the parents holding their original shares plus the deceased minor's absorbed share. Two deeds from the parents would capture everything.

The operator documented the minor's succession through a supplemental affidavit of heirship establishing the minor's own intestate heirs and confirming that the minor's interest had passed to the parents by operation of law. No separate probate of the minor's estate was required because the interest passed automatically at the moment of death. The affidavit documented what the law had already done.

## The Outcome

The operator acquired the parents' combined interests through a single conveyance from each parent. Each parent conveyed both their original inherited fraction and their share of the deceased minor's redirected interest in a single deed instrument. No guardianship proceedings were needed — the minor was deceased, not living. No separate minor's estate proceeding was required — the interest had passed by intestate succession to parties who were already capable adults with legal authority to sign.

The deal closed on a normal timeline with normal documentation. The family tree had appeared complicated until the operator applied the succession analysis. Once the succession traced cleanly back to existing parties, what looked complicated became simple.

## The Lesson

Deceased minor heirs almost always simplify deals rather than complicate them. The instinct is to see "deceased minor" and expect a tangle — a separate estate, a separate proceeding, a new set of parties to find and negotiate with. That instinct is usually wrong.

A deceased minor passes their interest by intestate succession just like any other decedent. In most family structures, that succession runs upward to the parents, who are often already parties to the deal because they are themselves heirs of the original decedent. When the succession runs back to existing parties, the family tree collapses — fewer parties to deal with, not more.

The operator's job on any deceased-heir situation, including deceased minors, is to trace the succession before assuming complexity. Pull Texas Estates Code § 201.001. Walk through the analysis: descendants first, then surviving spouse, then parents and siblings. Find out where the interest actually went. Nine times out of ten, the answer is already sitting at your negotiating table.
