## The Situation

Seven months ago, a tax foreclosure sale was completed. An investor entity bought the property at the Harris County tax auction for $38,000. The property is worth $210,000 — a significant equity position at stake. The original fractured equity investor, reviewing the underlying tax suit record, discovers that heir Esperanza was never properly served. She appears in the title records and family history but not in the service of process documentation. The ad litem missed her.

Texas Rules of Civil Procedure 329b(f) provides for Bills of Review — equitable actions to set aside judgments — and allows them to be filed for up to four years after the challenged judgment becomes final. The elements from Alexander v. Hagedorn and Caldwell v. Barnes require showing a meritorious defense, which was not presented due to extrinsic fraud or mistake, and that no fault or negligence caused the failure to present the defense. For a party who was never served, the Peralta v. Heights Medical Center doctrine significantly lowers this burden — if you were not served, you did not have an opportunity to defend, and the judgment simply cannot bind you.

Esperanza's 25% interest survived the tax sale because the sale could not constitutionally extinguish an interest belonging to someone who was never notified. The tax sale purchaser bought a property with a live 25% interest still outstanding against their title.

## What the Operator Did

The investor acquired Esperanza's 25% interest for $4,500. This purchase did two things: it gave the investor a direct property interest in the subject property, and it put the investor in a position to pursue — or threaten to pursue — the Bill of Review on Esperanza's behalf as her assignee.

Counsel drafted a Bill of Review Threat Letter directed to the tax sale purchaser's attorney. The letter identified the service defect, cited TRCP 329b(f) and Peralta, and stated the demand clearly: $35,000 in exchange for a full release of the Bill of Review claim and a warranty that no such challenge would be filed. The purchaser's counsel was invited to verify the service defect independently — which they did.

The verification confirmed what the investor's counsel had already established: Esperanza had not been served. The service defect was real, not manufactured, and it created genuine legal exposure to the purchaser's entire title position. Negotiation followed. The purchaser's counsel countered at a lower number; the investor's counsel held. Settlement was reached at $28,000.

## The Outcome

The investor received $28,000 from the tax sale purchaser. From that, subtract $4,500 paid to acquire Esperanza's interest and $3,500 in attorney fees for the threat letter and negotiation. Net profit: $20,000 in approximately six weeks from first identifying the defect to receiving the settlement check.

## The Lesson

Tax sales are not automatically final and unchallengeable. TRCP 329b(f) provides a four-year window for Bills of Review, and missed heirs — parties who were never served — carry the lightest possible burden under Peralta because they had no opportunity to appear. Tax sale purchasers who discover a documented service defect face a straightforward risk calculation: litigate and potentially lose their entire $38,000 investment plus title to a $210,000 property, or settle the claim for a fraction of that exposure. They consistently choose to settle. The leverage here is asymmetric: the investor paid $4,500 for the ability to threaten a claim that the purchaser could not afford to lose. That asymmetry is the entire play.
