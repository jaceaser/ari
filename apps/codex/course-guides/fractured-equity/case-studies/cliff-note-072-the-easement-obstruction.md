## The Situation

The subject property had a driveway easement recorded in the county deed records — an express easement created by deed that gave the property owner the right to cross the neighbor's parcel to reach a public road. Without that driveway access, the property was effectively landlocked. There was no other means of ingress and egress.

The neighbor had parked vehicles on the easement strip and refused to move them. The neighbor's position: the easement had not been used in years and had therefore expired. Without a usable driveway, the property's market value dropped approximately 40% from its unencumbered value. A deal that made sense at full value was underwater at the 40% reduction.

The operator's analysis was that the neighbor was wrong on the law — but the encroachment needed to be resolved before the title would be clean enough for resale. That required litigation.

## What the Operator Did

Counsel filed an injunction action in the appropriate court, seeking an order requiring the neighbor to remove the parked vehicles and restore clear easement access.

The neighbor's legal argument rested on two related theories: non-use and abandonment. The argument was that because the easement had not been actively used for some period of time, it had either expired or been abandoned.

Texas property law rejected both theories in this context. An express easement created by deed does not expire through non-use. Unlike an easement implied by necessity or an easement by prescription, a deed-created express easement is a real property right that transfers with the land to every subsequent owner of the dominant estate. It persists unless and until it is affirmatively extinguished — through merger of the dominant and servient estates, through express written release, or through abandonment established by clear and unequivocal evidence.

Abandonment of an easement under Texas law requires more than non-use alone. The holder of the easement must have taken affirmative acts demonstrating a clear and unequivocal intent to abandon the easement right permanently — not merely a period of disuse, but a positive election to give up the right. No such acts existed here. The easement holder had simply not needed to use the driveway during the relevant period. Non-use is not abandonment.

With the expiration and abandonment arguments defeated, the neighbor had no legal basis for blocking the easement. The court ordered the vehicles removed and the easement strip cleared. Full driveway access was restored.

## The Outcome

Full property value restored. The property closed at $165,000 with clean, confirmed driveway easement access. The injunction order was recorded in the county records, establishing a permanent record of the easement's validity and the court's order enforcing it against the neighbor.

## The Lesson

Express easements by deed are durable property rights. They do not evaporate through non-use, and they are not subject to adverse possession by the servient estate owner who decides to park cars on the strip. An operator who walks away from a deal because a neighbor is obstructing an easement — without first analyzing whether the easement is legally valid and enjoinable — is leaving money on the table.

The 40% value reduction from an obstructed easement is exactly the kind of title complication that creates a Fractured Equity discount on an otherwise solid property. The neighbor's obstruction drove away every conventional buyer who wasn't willing to deal with the complexity. The operator who was willing to understand the easement law, hire counsel, and file the injunction action restored full value with a court order.

The practical discipline here is title review before acquisition. Understand every easement in the chain before you close. Confirm each easement's basis (deed, necessity, prescription), confirm it's currently valid, and identify any potential obstruction before you're committed to the deal. An easement you discover post-acquisition is still fixable — but it's easier and cheaper to underwrite it upfront.
