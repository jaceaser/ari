## The Situation

A routine title search on the property turned up a 1967 deed with a missing notary signature. At some point during the recording process more than five decades ago, a clerical error occurred — the deed was recorded without a complete notarial acknowledgment. Texas Property Code § 12.007 requires acknowledgment for a deed to be properly recorded. On its face, this deed was defective.

The title commitment flagged the instrument. The question for the operator was whether this 57-year-old technical error would require active remediation — a curative affidavit, a court proceeding, or some other intervention — or whether time had already cured the problem.

## What the Operator Did

The operator brought the issue to the title underwriter and walked through the applicable law.

Texas Property Code § 12.007 establishes acknowledgment as a requirement for proper recordation. A deed lacking a proper acknowledgment is technically defective in the recording sense — it may not provide constructive notice, and it may be facially vulnerable to challenge. But the Texas curative framework layered on top of § 12.007 substantially limits the practical consequences of these historical defects.

Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code § 16.033 is the curative statute for stale deed defects. Under § 16.033 and the curative doctrine developed in case law around it, a deed that has been recorded for more than 10 years, and against which no adverse claim has been successfully asserted, is presumed valid despite technical defects in the acknowledgment or other formal requirements. The policy rationale is stability: buyers and title companies need to be able to rely on long-recorded instruments without reopening chains to challenge every notarial imperfection from decades past.

The 1967 deed was 57 years old at the time of the title search. No adverse claim against the instrument had ever been filed. No competing claimant had ever surfaced to argue the missing notary signature invalidated the deed. Under the curative presumption, the deed was accepted as valid without any remedial filing.

The title underwriter closed the file on the defect and issued the commitment without an exception for the 1967 instrument.

## The Outcome

Closed normally. No curative affidavit was required. No court proceeding was necessary. Title insurance issued without exception for the defective acknowledgment.

## The Lesson

The 10-year threshold under the Texas curative statutes is the practical dividing line for technical deed defects. If you encounter a deed with a missing notary signature, an incomplete acknowledgment, or a similar clerical defect — and that deed is more than 10 years old with no adverse claims in the record — the curative presumption almost certainly applies. Bring it to the underwriter and walk through the analysis rather than assuming the worst.

If the deed is under 10 years old, the curative affidavit is the tool. A properly executed curative affidavit filed in the county deed records documents the circumstances of the technical defect and establishes the basis for treating the instrument as valid despite the formal error.

The critical thing not to do is confuse a technical acknowledgment defect with a substantive deed defect. A missing notary signature is a clerical error in the recording process — embarrassing and technically non-compliant, but curable by time. A forged deed is substantively void ab initio and cannot be cured by any passage of time or curative affidavit. Know the difference, and you won't waste money on litigation that isn't needed or — worse — walk into a deal with a void deed you treated as merely technical.
