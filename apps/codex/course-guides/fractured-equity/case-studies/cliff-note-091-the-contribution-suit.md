## The Situation

A property with four co-owners was drowning in delinquent taxes. The investor held 40%; three other heirs each held 20%. The taxes had accrued for years while the heirs ignored both the obligation and the approaching tax foreclosure deadline. If taxes were not paid, the county would eventually foreclose — and the entire property would be lost to tax sale, with the heirs receiving only their proportionate share of whatever the tax sale produced, after the taxing authority took what it was owed.

The investor paid the $18,000 in delinquent taxes to stop the tax foreclosure clock. This was not altruism — it was tactical. Texas cotenancy law is clear: a cotenant who pays a disproportionate share of common obligations against the property acquires contribution rights against the other cotenants for their proportionate share. The investor's 40% obligated the investor to $7,200 of the $18,000 total. The three other heirs owed their proportionate shares — $3,600 each — to the investor.

The investor had paid the taxes. Three heirs owed money. The question was what to do when they refused to pay it back.

## What the Operator Did

The investor sent formal contribution demand letters to all three cotenants. Each letter documented the tax payment with receipts, calculated the recipient's proportionate share of the obligation, and demanded reimbursement within 30 days. The letters were sent certified mail with return receipt requested — creating a documented record of delivery and the start of the demand period.

Two cotenants did not respond at all. The third sent a series of communications that alternated between partial-payment proposals and excuses, but never produced any actual payment. After the demand period expired, the investor filed contribution suits against all three in the appropriate court.

The two non-responding cotenants defaulted — they did not retain counsel, did not appear, and did not file any response. Default judgments were entered for the full contribution amounts owed, plus court costs. The investor then obtained abstracts of judgment for each default judgment and filed writs of execution against the defaulting cotenants' property — specifically, against their 20% interests in the subject property.

The sheriff's sale was scheduled and noticed. At the sale, the investor submitted bids of $1,200 per interest — nominal amounts calibrated to what the investor was willing to pay to acquire interests that nobody else in the marketplace would bid competitively for. A 20% fractional interest in a contested property with pending litigation, no control rights, and no independent exit path is not an attractive purchase for outside bidders. The investor was the only bidder at $1,200 per interest. Both interests were awarded to the investor.

The third cotenant — who had been watching the proceedings — understood at this point that the same outcome awaited them. They agreed to sell their 20% interest cooperatively rather than continue through the litigation-to-sheriff's-sale pipeline. A price was negotiated and the interest was purchased through a standard deed transaction.

## The Outcome

The investor's ownership consolidated from 40% to 100% through three transactions:

- Two 20% interests acquired at the sheriff's sale for $1,200 each = $2,400 total
- One 20% interest acquired cooperatively at negotiated price
- Total out-of-pocket across the full consolidation: $18,000 in delinquent taxes paid (preserving the property for all heirs), approximately $8,000 in legal fees for the contribution suits, $2,400 in sheriff's sale bids, plus the cooperative purchase price

The property, now fully consolidated and with cleared tax obligations, was positioned for a retail exit.

## The Lesson

Contribution foreclosure is not a last resort — it is a designed play. The investor who pays delinquent taxes on a co-owned property is not just solving a property preservation problem; the investor is acquiring contribution rights that, when combined with the legal enforcement mechanism, produce a pathway to acquiring additional fractional interests at costs far below any voluntary purchase price.

The mechanic is straightforward:

1. Pay the taxes (document everything)
2. Send formal demand letters with proof of payment and calculations (create the paper record)
3. File suit when they don't pay (contribution suits are well-established under Texas cotenancy law)
4. Obtain default judgments when they don't appear (most won't)
5. Execute on the judgments through writ and sheriff's sale (the cotenant's fractional interest is executable property)
6. Bid nominally at the sheriff's sale (you're the only bidder who can value the interest)

The nominal bid at sheriff's sale is the punchline of this entire sequence. A 20% interest in a property that the investor controls 60% of, that has pending litigation, and that no external buyer would touch competitively — that interest is worth $1,200 to the investor at the sheriff's sale precisely because the investor is the only party positioned to capture the value embedded in it. No outside buyer bids. The investor acquires the interest for the cost of a bid that covers the sheriff's paperwork.

This is the machinery behind the Fractured Equity playbook at its most powerful. Learn the contribution rights doctrine, document your tax payments, and know when to pull the trigger on the legal mechanism that turns an obstinate co-owner situation into a complete consolidation.
