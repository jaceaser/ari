## The Situation

The will named an executor, but the will language — or the court's determination at qualification — required court supervision for any sale of real property. This is the opposite of independent administration. Every significant action requires notice to beneficiaries, a court hearing, and a judge's approval.

The executor was cooperative and wanted to sell. The problem wasn't motivation — it was the legal framework she was operating within. Under Texas Estates Code Chapter 356, dependent executors and administrators must petition the court for permission to sell real property under § 356.251. The court then sets a hearing, requires notice to interested parties, and confirms the sale at a price it finds reasonable.

And "reasonable" has a definition: courts typically won't approve a sale at a price significantly below appraised value. That's where the Fractured Equity discount disappears.

## What the Operator Did

The investor negotiated a price with the executor that both parties could accept. The executor then filed a petition with the probate court to approve the sale at the negotiated price. Beneficiaries were formally notified of the proposed sale — giving them an opportunity to object or offer competing bids.

A hearing was held. The court examined the proposed price against the appraised value. The court approved the sale at a price within 5% of the appraised value.

That 5% constraint is the key number. Court-supervised sales in Texas track closely to appraisal — the judicial approval process is specifically designed to prevent estate assets from being sold at dramatically below-market prices.

## The Outcome

The transaction closed at the court-approved price. Total timeline: four months from first contact to funded close. The acquisition cost was meaningfully higher than what an independent-executor scenario would have yielded — the court process compressed the available discount.

## The Lesson

Dependent executors are not Fractured Equity counterparties in the traditional sense. The court supervision that constrains them also constrains the pricing. If a property is under dependent administration or dependent executor supervision, expect near-market pricing and a multi-month process.

This doesn't mean never pursue these transactions. Properties with structural characteristics that justify acquisition even at near-market prices — unusual lots, development potential, below-market rents that depress MLS appeal — can still be worth the effort. But the Fractured Equity discount that drives this practice largely evaporates once a court is setting the floor price.

The first diagnostic step before pursuing any probate property: confirm whether the executor or administrator is independent or dependent. That determination changes the entire transaction structure.
