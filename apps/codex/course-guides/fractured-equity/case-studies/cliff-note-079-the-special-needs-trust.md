## The Situation

One of the heirs in this fractured equity situation was severely disabled. At some point prior to his disability, or through estate planning by the family, his inherited property interest had been placed inside a special needs trust — a specific type of trust designed to hold assets for a disabled beneficiary without disqualifying that beneficiary from means-tested government benefits like Supplemental Security Income (SSI) and Medicaid.

The legal structure here is importantly different from the incapacitated adult cases in Cliff Notes 76, 77, and 78. The disabled heir did not personally own the real property interest. The trust owned it. The trustee — a professional trust company — was the legal entity with authority to act on behalf of the trust's assets.

This distinction is everything. The heir's personal incapacity was irrelevant to the transaction because the heir was not a party to the transaction. The trust company was the party, acting through its trustee.

## What the Operator Did

The first call went to the trust company. The operator explained the situation: the trust holds a fractional interest in a real property that all co-owners are seeking to sell, and the question is whether the trust has authority under its governing instrument to convey the interest.

The trustee conducted an internal review of the trust instrument — the document that created the special needs trust and defined the trustee's powers. Most well-drafted special needs trusts include explicit trustee authority to buy, sell, and otherwise deal in real property on behalf of the trust. Texas Property Code Chapter 111 et seq. also provides a default statutory framework for trustee powers, including the power to sell trust assets, even where the trust instrument doesn't enumerate the power explicitly.

The trust company confirmed authority to sell. The trustee's fiduciary standards applied to the transaction — specifically, the fiduciary duty to deal at arm's length and receive fair market value for trust assets. A trustee cannot sell trust assets at a distressed discount the way an individual heir might choose to do. The beneficiary's interests must be protected, and in a trustee context, that protection is enforced by the fiduciary standard.

The sale price was set at market value: $18,500 for the trust's fractional interest in the property. The other heirs — who had full legal capacity and were not operating under fiduciary constraints — sold at Fractured Equity discount pricing. The investor's profit came from those discounts, not from the trust's share.

Proceeds from the trust's sale were paid directly to the trust and remained inside the trust structure. This is the critical element for government benefit preservation. SSI and Medicaid are means-tested programs — they have asset limits that determine eligibility. A distribution of cash directly to the disabled beneficiary would count as a personal asset and could disqualify him from benefits. By keeping the proceeds inside the special needs trust, the assets remain available for the beneficiary's supplemental needs without being counted as personal resources for benefits eligibility purposes.

## The Outcome

$18,500 market-value sale for the trust's interest in the property. Proceeds deposited to the special needs trust. Government benefits preserved. Other heirs completed their sales at Fractured Equity discount. Full acquisition of all interests completed. Clean title, insurable.

## The Lesson

The special needs trust is a more streamlined path than guardianship for heirs with disabilities, precisely because the trust structure creates a ready-made legal actor — the trustee — with clear authority to transact. When the trustee has authority under the trust instrument (or under Chapter 111's default powers), no court proceeding is required to authorize the sale. The trustee acts, applies fiduciary standards, and the transaction proceeds.

The operator's job in this situation is to identify the trust structure early — before assuming the heir needs guardianship — and contact the trust company directly. Ask specifically about the trustee's authority to convey real property interests and what internal review process the trust company uses for transactions of this type. Most professional trust companies have established procedures for exactly this situation.

The government benefit preservation element is non-negotiable. Proceeds must remain inside the trust. Any deal structure that routes the trust's proceeds to the beneficiary personally would expose the beneficiary to a benefit disqualification — which is a serious harm that the trustee is legally obligated to prevent. Make sure the closing documents reflect payment to the trust entity, not to the individual beneficiary, and confirm with the trust company that this structure satisfies their requirements before closing.

The contrast with Cliff Note 78 is worth noting: when no trust or power of attorney exists for a disabled heir, guardianship is the only path. When a special needs trust is in place, the trustee handles it without a court proceeding. The lesson across both scenarios is to determine the legal structure of each heir's situation before assuming the worst case applies.
