## The Situation

The probate window had closed. The decedent had died more than four years earlier, there was no will, and the heirs had never taken any legal action to establish title. The only path to a clean conveyance was the Affidavit of Heirship — a document that, when properly executed and recorded, establishes the intestate family tree for future title purposes.

The title underwriter was initially hesitant. An AOH is not a court order. It's a sworn statement by witnesses who claim to know the family facts. It's inherently less authoritative than probate — which involves judicial scrutiny — and underwriters have seen plenty of defective AOHs that omitted heirs, misstated relationships, or were signed by interested parties.

## What the Operator Did

The investor's counsel prepared a comprehensive AOH — what experienced practitioners sometimes call a Monster AOH. The document went well beyond the bare minimum. It included:

- Complete family tree documentation tracing from the decedent through all children, grandchildren, and collateral relatives who might have any intestate claim
- Two disinterested witnesses — neither of whom was an heir, neither of whom had any financial interest in the outcome — who had known the decedent personally and could attest from personal knowledge to the family structure
- Specific recitations about the decedent's marital history, confirmed absence of adopted children not otherwise identified, and the absence of any pending probate proceedings
- Documentation supporting each heir's identity and relationship

The AOH was filed in the county deed records — the critical step. An AOH sitting in a file drawer does nothing. An AOH on record starts the clock.

The legal foundation the investor's counsel cited to the underwriter: Texas Estates Code § 203.002. Under that provision, an AOH that has been on record for five or more years becomes prima facie evidence of the facts recited in it. "Prima facie evidence" means the facts are presumed true unless disproven — a meaningful legal standard that shifts the burden of proof.

The underwriter confirmed that the five-year ripening period would apply (or would shortly apply given the estate's age), and accepted the AOH as sufficient foundation for title insurance subject to standard exceptions.

## The Outcome

Title insurance issued. The acquisition completed normally. No probate was required, no court was involved, and the transaction closed on the strength of the comprehensive documentary record the AOH represented.

## The Lesson

AOH-alone transactions work for older estates — specifically, estates where the death was more than four years ago (eliminating probate as an option) and where the intestate distribution is clear and supportable by documentary evidence.

The two keys to persuading a hesitant title underwriter:

First, the quality of the AOH. A bare-bones AOH signed by a neighbor and a cousin will not satisfy a careful underwriter. A Monster AOH with two truly disinterested witnesses who can attest from personal knowledge, full family tree documentation, and comprehensive recitations about marital history and collateral relationships presents a different document entirely.

Second, the § 203.002 ripening argument. Five years on record converts the AOH from a sworn statement into prima facie evidence. If the estate is old enough that the AOH will ripen quickly or has already ripened, that legal point changes the underwriter's risk calculation significantly.

Don't skip the comprehensive approach on the assumption that a quick AOH will do. The work you put into the AOH is the work that gets the deal insured.
