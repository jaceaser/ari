## The Situation

The decedent had spent most of her life in California, where her estate was being administered through the California probate system. But she had owned a piece of Texas real estate — perhaps acquired decades earlier, perhaps inherited, perhaps an investment that had sat undeveloped. That Texas property couldn't be transferred through the California probate alone.

Texas law requires that real property located in Texas be conveyed through a Texas-domiciled proceeding. A California executor's authority extends to California assets; Texas assets require separate Texas legal process.

## What the Operator Did

The California executor, through her California attorney, retained Texas probate counsel in the county where the Texas property was located. The Texas attorney filed for ancillary probate under Texas Estates Code Chapter 452, which governs the process by which foreign (out-of-state) wills and domiciliary determinations are recognized in Texas.

The process: file the California will and related California probate documents with the Texas county court, demonstrate that the California probate was properly opened and the executor properly appointed, and petition for ancillary letters testamentary. The Texas court reviews the foreign proceeding, confirms it was validly conducted, and issues Texas ancillary letters giving the executor authority to act regarding Texas real property.

With ancillary letters in hand, the California executor had full legal authority to sign a deed conveying the Texas property. The investor and the executor negotiated a price and executed a standard executor's deed.

## The Outcome

The ancillary probate process took 90 days from the Texas filing to the issuance of ancillary letters — straightforward but not fast. Once the letters issued, the closing moved quickly. The transaction closed at $125,000 against a $168,000 estimated market value.

## The Lesson

Out-of-state decedents who owned Texas real property require ancillary probate. There is no workaround — an affidavit of heirship alone will not substitute for a properly issued deed from an authorized party when there's a pending foreign probate. The Texas county clerk and title underwriters will require the Texas ancillary proceeding.

The practical implications: first, identify the out-of-state probate early and confirm what state it's in. Second, engage Texas counsel to file ancillary probate in the correct county (where the Texas property is located). Third, budget 60 to 90 days for the process — it's not fast, but it's predictable.

Coordinate with the out-of-state executor and their counsel from the first contact. They are the counterparty who will sign the deed. If they understand what you need and why, the ancillary process moves on a defined timeline without surprises.
