## The Situation

The property was in Texas. The heirs were everywhere else. Five heirs across five jurisdictions — California, Florida, Tennessee, Mexico, and one local to Texas. The legal question was simple: Texas law governs Texas real property, and each heir's deed conveying their Texas interest would be governed by Texas requirements regardless of where the heir lived or where the signing occurred. Out-of-state signings are not exotic or unusual — they happen on every multi-state estate.

The practical question was logistical: how do you coordinate five valid, acknowledged deed signings across five jurisdictions, on a timeline that keeps the deal moving and does not let any single heir's delay collapse the whole transaction?

## What the Operator Did

The investor broke the problem into components and attacked each one independently rather than trying to manage all five signings as a single coordinated event.

**The Four US Heirs**

For the California, Florida, Tennessee, and Texas heirs, the signing mechanism was mobile notary. Mobile notary services — professionals who travel to the signing location and witness and acknowledge document execution — are available in every state of the United States. A deed signed by a Texas property owner before a notary in California is a valid deed for Texas recording purposes, because Texas law accepts acknowledgments taken by notaries in the state where the signing occurs.

The investor contacted each of the four US-based heirs, explained the transaction, obtained their agreement, and scheduled mobile notary appointments in each location. The investor provided each mobile notary service with the pre-prepared deed instruments for their respective heir, along with instructions on the acknowledgment format required for Texas recording.

The California, Florida, and Tennessee mobile notary appointments were completed within the first two weeks. The Texas heir signed locally at the investor's request.

**The Mexican Heir and the FIRPTA Analysis**

The Mexican heir required more analysis before scheduling. Two signing paths existed:

- **Path One: Sign in Mexico** — Consular officer signing at a US consular office in Mexico, followed by apostille authentication for US recording purposes. This was the path described in Case Study 86. FIRPTA's 15% withholding would apply because the Mexican heir had never lived in the United States.

- **Path Two: Travel to Texas** — The Mexican heir flies to the United States, signs before a Texas notary in person. No apostille needed. But FIRPTA still applies — the heir's status as a non-resident alien is determined by US presence history and the substantial presence test, not by where the deed signing occurs. A single trip to Texas for a closing does not transform a Mexican citizen who has never lived in the United States into a US person under FIRPTA.

The investor evaluated both paths against two criteria: FIRPTA result and logistical simplicity. The FIRPTA result was identical — 15% withholding applied either way because the heir was unambiguously a non-resident alien under the IRS test. The logistical comparison favored travel: the apostille process in Mexico takes time and adds a layer of authentication that must be verified by the title company; travel eliminates that layer entirely and produces a standard acknowledged deed.

The investor arranged and paid for the Mexican heir's travel to Texas. The heir flew in, was met by the investor's representative, and signed the deed before a Texas notary. Same-day: FIRPTA withholding documented, closing package complete.

**FIRPTA at Closing**

The Mexican heir's proceeds at closing were subject to 15% FIRPTA withholding regardless of the signing path chosen. The closing agent withheld the required amount and remitted it to the IRS pursuant to 26 USC § 1445. The heir received 85% of her agreed purchase price in net disbursement.

**Coordination Mechanics**

The investor's coordination approach was sequential rather than simultaneous. Rather than trying to schedule all five signings for the same week and letting any single delay cascade, the investor scheduled in waves:

- Wave One: California, Florida, Tennessee mobile notaries in the first two weeks (earliest available slots, largest geographic spread)
- Wave Two: Texas local heir in week two (simple to schedule, no travel logistics)
- Wave Three: Mexican heir travel in week three (more complex to arrange, scheduled after the US signings were confirmed)

This sequencing built in buffer: if the Mexican heir's travel had been delayed, the other four signings would already be complete and waiting.

## The Outcome

All five deeds executed and delivered within 35 days of coordination beginning. The multi-state logistics that most operators would treat as a reason to pass on a deal were resolved through methodical scheduling, mobile notary services, and one plane ticket.

The deal closed with a full consolidation of all five fractional interests. The title company accepted all five deeds without issue — mobile notary acknowledgments from California, Florida, and Tennessee are standard practice, and the in-person Texas signing for the Mexican heir produced a straightforward Texas notarized instrument.

## The Lesson

Multi-state heir logistics are a logistics problem. They are not a legal barrier, an unusual procedure, or a reason to pass on a deal. Every heir's obligation under Texas law is the same regardless of where they live: execute a deed before a notary authorized in their jurisdiction. Texas accepts those acknowledgments. Title companies process them routinely.

The investor's job is coordination: contact each heir, schedule each signing, provide properly prepared instruments to each notary service, and track each completed signing to closing. Professional mobile notary services handle the field execution — the investor's contribution is the coordination infrastructure.

For international heirs, add the FIRPTA analysis before deciding on a signing path. The analysis determines whether the heir is a US person (no withholding) or a non-resident alien (15% withholding). That analysis is driven by the heir's US presence history, not by where the deed is signed. Once the FIRPTA status is determined, choose the signing path — apostille-in-country or travel-to-US — based on logistical simplicity, since the tax result is the same either way.

Thirty-five days from first contact to full consolidation across five jurisdictions. Logistically manageable. Legally straightforward. Operators who treat geography as a deal-breaker are leaving deals on the table.
