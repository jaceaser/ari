## The Situation

A Houston residential property had been vacant for eight years. In that time, the City of Houston Code Enforcement had been busy: $47,000 in accumulated code enforcement liens covering overgrown vegetation, unsecured structures, broken windows, and code violations that had compounded through multiple citation cycles. Beyond the lien balance, code enforcement had escalated to a pending demolition order — the city had determined the structure was a public nuisance and had the authority to demolish it.

The investor had already acquired 60% of the property from cooperative heirs. The demolition order was the immediate threat. If the city demolished the structure, the value of the acquisition dropped to land value only.

## What the Operator Did

Immediate contact with City of Houston Code Enforcement — the same day. Not a week later, not after doing more due diligence. The city was moving on a demolition timeline, and every day of delay narrowed the window for negotiation.

The operator presented two things to code enforcement: a rehabilitation plan and a contractor quote. The rehabilitation plan laid out exactly which violations would be cured and on what timeline. The contractor quote demonstrated that the work was real — a licensed contractor had agreed to perform the necessary remediation for a specific dollar amount. Cities don't grant demolition stays based on promises; they grant them based on documented plans with committed contractors.

The demolition stay was granted: 90 days to cure violations. This stopped the demolition clock and created the time needed to work through both the physical remediation and the lien negotiation simultaneously.

On the lien negotiation: the investor approached code enforcement with the rehabilitation plan already in motion. This matters because Texas Local Government Code Chapter 214 gives municipalities the authority to negotiate lien reductions with committed new owners who execute cure plans. The city's interest is compliance, not face-value collection. An investor who shows up with a signed contractor agreement and begins curing violations is exactly the outcome the municipality wants — and they know it.

Curing the violations was the first step: fence installed, property mowed and cleared, structure secured, windows boarded. Completed within the first 30 days of the 90-day stay.

Lien negotiation proceeded from a position of demonstrated good faith. The city reduced from $47,000 to $19,500 — a 59% reduction from face value.

Rehabilitation cost beyond the code-cure work: $38,000 total to bring the property to sale condition.

## The Outcome

- Code enforcement lien paid (negotiated): $19,500 (down from $47,000)
- Rehabilitation cost: $38,000
- Sale price: $128,000
- Net profit: $42,000

The demolition order was withdrawn upon completion of the cure. Clear title issued.

## The Lesson

Stop the demolition first. Everything else — lien negotiation, rehabilitation planning, acquisition of remaining heir interests — is secondary to halting the city's demolition timeline. A demolished building is a vacant lot, and vacant lots don't produce the profit margins that rehabbed structures do. Make the call to code enforcement the day you close on the first heir interest, or even the day before.

Municipal liens reduce 40 to 70 percent with documented cure plans. The range varies by city, by the age of the liens, and by how cooperative the new owner demonstrates itself to be. Houston code enforcement, like most city agencies, has discretion on negotiated settlements with new owners. They exercise that discretion in favor of owners who cure violations quickly and communicate proactively.

The contractor quote is not a formality — it's the credibility instrument. Code enforcement officials have seen every kind of stall and excuse. An investor who shows up with a licensed contractor's signed quote and begins the cure within the first week of the stay is a different class of counterparty than one who shows up with a verbal promise and a vague timeline.

Eighty percent of demolition stays on properties like this are granted when the new owner presents a credible, documented rehabilitation commitment. Show up prepared, move fast, and cure violations before the stay expires. The lien reduction follows naturally.
