## The Situation

A duplex passes to four heirs as tenants in common. Heir A is not passive — she lives in Unit 1 as her primary residence and has arranged for unrelated tenants to occupy Unit 2, collecting rent from them without sharing it with the other three co-owners. The month-to-month tenant arrangement in Unit 2 was made by Heir A alone; the other heirs never joined. The other three heirs want to sell.

Texas cotenancy law gives Heir A the right to lease her undivided interest — but it does not give her the right to unilaterally bind all cotenants to a tenancy that exceeds her own ownership share. More importantly for the resolution, the Unit 2 tenants are month-to-month, and month-to-month tenancies require only a 30-day notice to terminate. They are not protected by a multi-year lease. The investor acquires 75% from the three cooperative heirs and now holds majority ownership.

Two separate occupancy problems must be resolved simultaneously: Heir A in Unit 1 (a cotenant, requiring negotiation) and the tenants in Unit 2 (month-to-month occupants, requiring notice). Each demands a different remedy.

## What the Operator Did

Step one: acquire the 75% from the three cooperative heirs. Total cost: $24,000.

Step two, running in parallel: serve the 30-day notice on the Unit 2 tenants as the 75% majority owner. The tenants vacated. No eviction necessary — they were month-to-month and the notice was proper.

Step three, running simultaneously with step two: negotiate Heir A's 25% interest plus her vacate of Unit 1. The negotiation combined two things into one agreement — the acquisition of her ownership interest and her agreement to vacate — because they were inseparable. Heir A could not simply sell her interest and remain as an occupant; the vacate was part of the deal. Settlement: $12,000 for her 25% interest plus $4,500 in relocation assistance, totaling $16,500.

The parallel structure was intentional. Running the tenant notice and the heir negotiation at the same time compressed the total timeline to 90 days rather than sequencing them, which would have doubled it.

## The Outcome

Full possession and 100% ownership within 90 days. The duplex sold vacant for $195,000. A subject-to-occupancy sale — selling with tenants and heir in place — was estimated at approximately $145,000 because investors buying encumbered duplexes discount heavily. The vacant sale premium was $50,000. Against total acquisition costs of $40,500 ($24,000 for the 75% plus $16,500 for Heir A's buyout and relocation), the vacant sale premium substantially outweighed the cost of clearing the property.

## The Lesson

Mixed occupant situations require parallel tracks, not sequential ones. Identify each occupant, identify the applicable legal category — cotenant, month-to-month tenant, lease tenant, non-owner permissive occupant — and apply the correct remedy simultaneously to each. In this case: 30-day notice for the month-to-month tenants, and a negotiated buyout-plus-relocation package for the heir. Running both tracks at once is the right discipline. Sequential resolution wastes time and costs money you do not need to spend.
