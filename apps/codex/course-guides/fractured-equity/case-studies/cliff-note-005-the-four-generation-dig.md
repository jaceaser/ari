## The Situation

Gertrude Blackwell died in 1978 in rural Waller County. She was the original owner of the property. After her death, the property passed through four more generations of death without a single probate being opened.

Daughter Ophelia died in 1995. Granddaughter Estelle died in 2015. Great-grandson Marcus died in 2020. Forty-six years passed. Every generation came and went without touching the courthouse. The living heirs were scattered great-great-grandchildren plus a handful of adopted-in relatives who may or may not have had valid claims depending on the nature of those adoptions.

By the time the operator arrived, this was not a title question so much as an archaeological project.

## What the Operator Did

No probate was available. The four-year statute under Texas Estates Code § 256.003 had long expired — at Gertrude's death in 1978, at Ophelia's death in 1995, at Estelle's death in 2015, and at Marcus's death in 2020. The AOH was the only path.

The operator built a 14-page Monster AOH tracing the full chain: every death, every marriage, every birth, every adoption, every share calculation across all four generations. Each generational transfer was analyzed independently. At each death, the analysis asked the same questions: who was the decedent's surviving spouse at that moment? Did they have descendants? What were the shares under § 201.001 or § 201.002? How do those shares flow to the next generation?

Fractions accumulated. Some interests ended up as small as 1/48 of the whole — the result of repeated per stirpes division across multiple children across multiple generations. Nine separate fractional interests were identified, ranging from 1/48 to 1/6.

Under Texas Estates Code § 203.002, an AOH ripens to prima facie evidence after five years of recording. The operator recorded the AOH and acquired each fractional interest separately. Title accepted the AOH.

## The Outcome

Nine fractional interests were acquired for a cumulative total of $18,000. The property was worth $98,000. Title accepted the AOH. The property sold for $92,000. Net profit: $58,000.

## The Lesson

Multi-generation chains clear through one comprehensive AOH. The key discipline is analyzing each generation independently — you cannot treat the whole chain as one event. At each death, you re-run the marriage status, descendants, and share distribution analysis for that specific moment. Tiny fractional interests that look impossible to monetize — 1/48, 1/24, 1/12 — can be acquired for a few hundred dollars each and aggregate to complete consolidation. The Monster AOH is the instrument. Probate is neither available nor necessary when the statute has run.
