## The Situation

Reginald and Arlene Whitfield bought a house together in 2007 and divorced in 2014 after seven years of marriage. The divorce decree was thorough on bank accounts and retirement funds. The Arlington house was never mentioned.

Reginald moved out after the divorce. Arlene stayed and continued paying the bills. For seven years, from 2014 to his death in 2021, Arlene lived in the property under the assumption that it was hers — either by virtue of the divorce, or by virtue of Reginald's departure, or by some other logic that felt correct but had no legal basis. Reginald died in 2021 without opening any probate proceeding.

## What the Operator Did

Texas Family Code § 7.001 and the body of case law interpreting it are clear on this point: a divorce decree that fails to specifically address community real property converts that property to tenancy-in-common between the former spouses at the moment the divorce is finalized. Each party holds 50% as separately owned property from that point forward.

The operator obtained a certified copy of the 2014 divorce decree from the Tarrant County District Clerk. The Arlington property was not addressed anywhere in it. This meant that from 2014 forward, Reginald and Arlene held as tenants-in-common — each owning an undivided 50% with the rights and obligations of a TIC owner.

At Reginald's death in 2021, his 50% passed by intestate succession to his heirs: his brother and his niece and nephew. Arlene's 50% was already hers and was not affected.

The operator acquired the three Reginald-side interests through separate buyouts totaling $16,000. With 50% consolidated from Reginald's heirs, the operator approached Arlene with the analysis. She did not believe it at first. The operator paid for her independent counsel. Her attorney confirmed the conclusion. Arlene, now understanding her actual ownership situation, agreed to a cooperative sale. Her net: $82,000.

## The Outcome

Reginald's 50% was acquired through three buyouts totaling $16,000. Arlene was educated through independent counsel, agreed to cooperate, and netted $82,000 from the sale.

## The Lesson

Always obtain the divorce decree — the actual court document, not a summary, not what the family remembers, not what the CAD records show. Post-divorce TIC is extremely common and produces ownership configurations no one in the family recognizes. The decree is the governing instrument. A decree that forgets to address a piece of real property creates a TIC the moment it is signed, regardless of who moved out, who paid the bills, or what everyone assumed. The deed does not fix it. The passage of time does not fix it. Only a subsequent deed or court order fixes it.
