## The Situation

The decedent left a will naming a specific individual as independent executor. The executor had qualified — posted bond if required, filed the oath, received letters testamentary — and was actively managing the estate. He was competent, motivated to close, and understood that the property needed to be sold to wind up the estate.

When the investor approached, the conversation was direct. The executor had the legal authority to sell. He had the practical motivation to sell. He didn't need a committee decision, family consensus, or court approval.

## What the Operator Did

Under Texas Estates Code Chapter 402, an independent executor has broad powers to manage and sell estate assets without court supervision. Section 402.002 and the general power clause in most wills give independent executors explicit authority to sell real property at the prices and on the terms they determine appropriate.

The investor negotiated directly with the executor. No probate court involvement needed, no beneficiary consent required for the sale mechanics (though the executor owed fiduciary duties to the beneficiaries in terms of the price accepted). Standard purchase contract, standard closing process.

The documentation package was clean: an Executor's Deed conveying title from the estate to the investor, plus an Executor-Sale Supplemental Affidavit (Template 12) establishing the executor's authority, the independent administration status, and the absence of any court-supervised sale requirement.

Title insurance issued on the standard timeline. No unusual exceptions.

## The Outcome

Closed at $145,000 against a $185,000 estimated market value. The executor was efficient — motivated to close the estate and distribute to the beneficiaries. The transaction completed in 30 days from first contact to funded close.

## The Lesson

Independent executors are the cleanest probate counterparty in the Fractured Equity practice. They have unilateral authority to sell, they move on their own timeline without court approval, and they typically understand what they need: a credible buyer who can close quickly.

Two calibration points:

First, don't overprice the discount. An independent executor who is competent and motivated has options — they can list on the MLS, take offers from other buyers, or take time to shop the deal. The Fractured Equity discount is real, but it's not unlimited when the counterparty has unrestricted sale authority and alternatives.

Second, confirm the independence. Verify the letters testamentary and the will language. An executor who believes they're independent but whose will actually requires court approval for sales is a different — and much slower — transaction.
