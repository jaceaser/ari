## The Situation

Annika inherited her mother's home subject to the outstanding mortgage. She is the sole heir and holds 100% of the ownership interest. But she has fallen $8,500 behind on the mortgage payments. The servicer sent the 30-day notice of default, which triggered the clock on the substitute trustee sale notice. A 21-day notice of substitute trustee sale was posted. The sale is 51 days from now and Annika has no realistic way to come up with $8,500 on her own timeline.

Texas Property Code § 51.002 governs non-judicial foreclosure in Texas. The process is fast: 30-day notice of default, then 21-day notice of substitute trustee sale, then the foreclosure sale on the first Tuesday of the following month. No court involvement required. The lender does not file a lawsuit — they exercise the power of sale in the deed of trust. And critically: there is no statutory right of redemption after a Texas mortgage foreclosure, unlike the two-year window available after a tax sale. Once the substitute trustee sale is completed, Annika has nothing.

If the substitute trustee sale completes, Annika walks away with whatever equity survived the foreclosure bid — often nothing if the bid equals the loan balance — and loses the property. The investor has 51 days to close, cure the arrearage, and cancel the sale.

## What the Operator Did

The investor presented Annika with a standard Fractured Equity offer: $40,000 for her 100% ownership interest. The investor would acquire subject to the existing mortgage — meaning the mortgage stays in place, the investor does not pay it off at closing, but takes responsibility for it going forward. Annika gets $40,000 in cash and is relieved of the mortgage obligation through the conveyance.

The transaction closed in 8 days using a mobile notary — the investor arranged for a notary to come to Annika's home so she did not need to visit a title company office. Speed was the critical variable. Every day that passed was a day closer to a substitute trustee sale that would leave Annika with potentially nothing.

Immediately upon closing, the investor paid the $8,500 arrearage to the mortgage servicer. Payment of the arrearage cured the default and cancelled the scheduled substitute trustee sale. The foreclosure was stopped.

The investor then refinanced the mortgage into the investor's own name, clearing the subject-to structure, and held the property for 18 months before selling.

## The Outcome

The property sold 18 months after acquisition for $241,000. Net gain over the full hold period, after accounting for the $40,000 paid to Annika, the $8,500 arrearage cure, refinancing costs, and carrying costs over 18 months, was approximately $65,000.

Annika received $40,000 in a situation where the substitute trustee sale would likely have yielded her far less — or nothing at all.

## The Lesson

Mortgage foreclosures in Texas move at 51-day speed. That is not a long runway. An heir facing a substitute trustee sale needs a buyer who can close in 5-10 days — not 30, not 45, not the timeline of a conventional retail transaction. The Fractured Equity model, with its mobile notary protocol and subject-to structure, is often the only realistic option available to that heir within the timeline.

Know the difference between a tax sale and a mortgage foreclosure: tax sales have redemption rights; mortgage foreclosures do not. If you miss the closing window before the substitute trustee sale, there is no coming back. Speed is the entire product in this scenario.
