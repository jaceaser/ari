## The Situation

Oswald Bentley died in Fort Worth. He had three adult children: Dorothy, Penelope, and Eleanor. His middle daughter Penelope predeceased him, leaving two children of her own — Trevor and Delphine. Oswald's wife had died long before him, making him a widower.

The immediate questions: what happens to Penelope's share, who takes it, and does Penelope's surviving husband — the father of Trevor and Delphine — have any claim on the property?

## What the Operator Did

Texas Estates Code § 201.001(e) applies per stirpes distribution when a predeceased heir leaves descendants. Penelope's one-third share passes through her to Trevor and Delphine equally, so each takes one-sixth of the total estate. Penelope's husband takes nothing — he was her spouse, not Oswald's heir. Texas intestate succession does not give a son-in-law or daughter-in-law any claim on the parent-in-law's estate.

The AOH traced the family tree through two generations. It documented Penelope's death, identified Trevor and Delphine as her two children, confirmed each takes one-sixth, and explicitly established that Penelope's husband has no interest under Texas intestate rules.

Dorothy and Eleanor each retained their one-third shares as the surviving children. The operator negotiated each interest separately.

Trevor and Delphine were geographically scattered — no attachment to Fort Worth, no involvement with the property, no memory of Oswald's real estate. They were essentially receiving an unexpected inheritance check they did not know existed. Each sold their one-sixth for $3,500. Dorothy and Eleanor's one-third interests were negotiated independently.

## The Outcome

Full consolidation at $28,000 total. Trevor and Delphine each received $3,500 for interests they did not know they held. Dorothy and Eleanor were negotiated separately.

## The Lesson

Grandchildren taking per stirpes are typically the most profitable acquisitions in fractured equity. They are disconnected from the property, frequently unaware they even own anything, and genuinely grateful for unexpected cash. Their parent's surviving spouse takes nothing — the per stirpes rule passes the share through the deceased heir down to that heir's descendants, not across to that heir's spouse. Get the two-generation AOH right and you expand your acquisition pool while keeping your per-share cost to a minimum.
