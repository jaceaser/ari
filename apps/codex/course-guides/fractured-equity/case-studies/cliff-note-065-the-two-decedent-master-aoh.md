## The Situation

Dad died in 2015. Mom died in 2022. Community property marriage. Two adult children. Zero probates opened — on either death.

This is the single most common fractured equity pattern involving two sequential deaths. The family treats both as "already handled" because they kept the property occupied or simply assumed it passed automatically. Nothing passed. Two estates sat open simultaneously, both intestate, neither filed.

The question is how to document two separate ownership events in a single instrument when no court has ever touched either death.

## What the Operator Did

Texas Estates Code § 201.002 governs each death in sequence. The analysis runs in order:

Dad died first. He was married at the time of death, had no children outside the marriage. Under § 201.002, the community property passes 100% to the surviving spouse — Mom. Mom becomes the sole owner of the entire property.

Mom died second. At the time of her death, she was not married. She had two children, both of whom survived her. Under § 201.002, her 100% interest in the property passes equally to her two children — 50% each.

The operator executed a Two-Decedent AOH under Codex Template 17, which is specifically designed for the husband-then-wife sequence. The instrument documented both deaths, both dates, the community property character of the marriage, the absence of children outside the marriage, and the surviving-heir status of both adult children. Disinterested witnesses swore to the full marital and heirship history covering both events.

Both adult children signed at Fractured Equity discount pricing.

## The Outcome

Full consolidation for $32,000. Market value of the property: $195,000.

No probate was needed on either estate. The Two-Decedent AOH documented what Texas law already did — vested title in Dad's heirs (Mom), then re-vested in Mom's heirs (the two kids) — at the moment of each respective death.

## The Lesson

The Two-Decedent AOH is purpose-built for the most common sequence in Texas fractured equity: husband dies, wife inherits, wife dies, kids inherit. All of it occurs by operation of law at the moment of each death. The AOH doesn't create the transfer — it documents transfers that already happened. Most investors walk away from these situations because they see two deaths and assume two probates are required. They're not. The legal analysis runs cleanly through § 201.002 twice, and Template 17 captures both passes in a single instrument.

The pattern works especially cleanly when both spouses had children only with each other. The moment you introduce children from outside the marriage, the § 201.002 analysis splits differently, and you'll need to trace both the community and separate property shares on Dad's death before you can determine what Mom actually inherited. Keep that variable in mind before assuming the two-decedent form applies without modification.
