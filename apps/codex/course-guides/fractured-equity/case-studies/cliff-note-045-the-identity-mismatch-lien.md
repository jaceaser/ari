## The Situation

The title commitment came back with a $55,000 judgment on Schedule C — filed against "Robert Williams." One of the three heirs to the property was named Robert Williams. The title officer flagged it as a potential lien against the heir's interest.

This is a common-name false positive. Robert Williams is a frequently occurring name. The judgment debtor and the heir share a name but may have nothing else in common.

Texas CPRC § 34.001 governs abstracts of judgment. The statute requires that abstracts be indexed to the judgment-debtor's identifying information — but in practice, county real property records index abstracts by name only. A title search that returns a judgment against "Robert Williams" has identified a name match, not necessarily an identity match. The title officer's job is to flag it; the operator's job is to investigate whether the flag is real.

## What the Operator Did

The investigation had a clear objective: establish whether the "Robert Williams" named in the judgment was the same individual as the heir Robert Williams, or a different person who happened to share the name.

The starting point was the court record underlying the judgment. Judgments and court files are public records. From the court file, the investor's counsel obtained the judgment debtor's date of birth: 1958.

The heir's Robert Williams was born in 1972.

Fourteen years' age difference, different Social Security numbers, different address history. These were unambiguously different individuals who happened to share a common name.

The resolution instrument is Template 20 — the Identity Affidavit for Lien/Judgment Disambiguation. This is a sworn affidavit executed by the heir setting out his identifying information (full name, date of birth, Social Security number, address history) alongside the publicly available identifying information for the judgment debtor, and affirmatively stating that he is not the same individual as the judgment debtor.

The affidavit was prepared, executed, and submitted to the title company. The title officer reviewed the documentation — the DOB discrepancy alone was conclusive — and accepted the position. The $55,000 judgment was removed from Schedule C as a non-matching lien.

## The Outcome

- Judgment claimed: $55,000
- Actual attachment to heir's interest: none — different person
- Resolution instrument: Identity Affidavit (Template 20)
- Schedule C result: $55,000 judgment removed
- Acquisition: proceeded

## The Lesson

Common-name false positives are frequent. Robert Williams, James Johnson, Maria Garcia, David Smith — any judgment against a common name triggers flags in title searches, and those flags require investigation before closing. Most of the time, the flag is a false positive. The names match; the people don't.

The investigation path is always the same: get the judgment debtor's identifying information from the court file. Date of birth, Social Security number if available, address history. Compare to the heir's identifying information. If they don't match, prepare the Identity Affidavit documenting the discrepancy.

The Identity Affidavit is a sworn instrument — the heir attests under penalty of perjury that the judgment is against a different person. This protects the title company, which is relying on the affidavit to remove a flag from the commitment. Draft it carefully, include all relevant identifying information for both individuals, and make the discrepancy explicit and obvious.

Don't assume the lien attaches — but don't assume it doesn't either. The failure mode that's worse than a false positive is a false negative — assuming a common-name lien is a mismatch without investigating, closing the deal, and then discovering the lien was actually against your heir. Check the court file. Confirm the mismatch. Document it. Only then proceed.

Template 20 handles this efficiently when the investigation confirms a mismatch. It's one of the most frequently useful instruments in the toolkit precisely because common-name false positives appear in almost every deal that involves a common name heir.
