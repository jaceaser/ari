## The Situation

A property went to tax sale. The auction produced $45,000. The delinquent tax debt owed was $22,000. That left a $23,000 surplus — and under Texas Tax Code § 34.04, that surplus does not belong to the county. It belongs to the former owner, or, if the former owner is deceased, to that owner's successors-in-interest.

The former owner was deceased. His heirs were the successors-in-interest. The county held the $23,000 awaiting a claim.

The heirs didn't know. They thought the property had been lost entirely at the tax sale — which it had. What they didn't know was that the auction had produced more than the debt, and that the excess was sitting in a county account with their name on it.

## What the Operator Did

The operator's first move was documentation of the heirs and their standing as successors-in-interest. An Affidavit of Heirship establishing the family tree was prepared, along with an Assignment of Rights and Claims (Template 8) from each heir. The assignment transferred the heirs' interest in the overage claim — and any distribution that resulted — to the investor.

The operator paid the heirs a combined $6,500 for the assignments. The heirs, who believed they had nothing to show for the property, received money they weren't expecting for something they didn't know they had.

Counsel then filed a petition for distribution in district court under Texas Tax Code § 34.04. The court reviewed the claim, confirmed the assignment was valid and the heirs were the proper successors-in-interest, and ordered distribution of the $23,000 surplus to the investor as assignee.

## The Outcome

- Assignments purchased from heirs (combined): $6,500
- Attorney and filing fees: modest
- Distribution ordered by court: $23,000
- Net to investor: approximately $16,500

No property to acquire, no repairs, no carrying costs. This was a paper claim — research, documentation, and a court filing.

## The Lesson

Tax sale overages are an underutilized asset class. Most people who study fractured equity focus on acquiring ownership interests in the property itself. But when a property has already gone through tax sale, the former owner's rights don't vanish entirely — they shift. If the auction produced more than the debt, the surplus belongs to the former owner's estate, and that claim survives the sale.

Heirs almost never know about it. The county isn't required to hunt them down — it holds the funds and waits. Some states have holding periods after which unclaimed overage escheats to the state; Texas gives former owners and their heirs a meaningful window under § 34.04.

The mechanism is straightforward: document the heirs as successors-in-interest, get assignments of rights, and petition the district court for distribution. Template 8 covers the assignment instrument. The hearing is usually uncontested — there's no adverse party fighting for the money once the taxing authority has been paid.

The deal structure here is pure arbitrage: you pay heirs a fraction of what the claim is worth because they don't know the claim exists, and you recover the full amount through the court petition. The research that reveals the overage is the competitive advantage. County tax sale records are public. The surplus balance is on file. The investor who searches those records before anyone else contacts the heirs is the one who collects.
