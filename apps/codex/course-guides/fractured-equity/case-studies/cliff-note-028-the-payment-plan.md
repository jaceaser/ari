## The Situation

The property has $23,500 in delinquent taxes and is scheduled for auction in 45 days. The investor has $15,000 in available capital but is working multiple deals simultaneously and needs to preserve the remainder for other obligations in progress. A $23,500 lump-sum payoff is not feasible without disrupting other deals. The auction cannot simply be ignored.

Texas Tax Code § 33.02 authorizes installment agreements for delinquent tax obligations. The statute specifically empowers tax collection firms — PERDUE BRANDON FIELDER COLLINS & MOTT, LINEBARGER GOGGAN BLAIR & SAMPSON, and similar firms — to negotiate and execute installment arrangements on behalf of the taxing authorities they represent. This is an authorized, statutory process, not an informal accommodation.

One critical distinction: paying the county tax office directly online does not satisfy the collection firm's fees and does not cancel a pending tax auction. The collection firm holds the lawsuit and controls the sale date. The firm must be the party to the agreement. Any plan that excludes the firm leaves the auction on the schedule.

## What the Operator Did

The investor contacted PERDUE directly — not the county tax assessor-collector, not the online payment portal. The firm's collection attorney was presented with a specific, structured proposal: $12,000 at execution of the installment agreement, and $11,500 paid in installments over the following five months. In exchange, the auction would be cancelled and not rescheduled during the plan's performance period.

PERDUE accepted. Collection firms are incentivized to accept reasonable installment arrangements because they recover their attorney fees and collection costs regardless of whether the delinquency is resolved through a lump-sum payment or an installment plan. A performing installment plan is better for their recovery model than a chaotic auction with uncertain proceeds and contested distribution.

The investor executed the agreement, made the $12,000 initial payment, and the auction was cancelled.

## The Outcome

With the auction off the calendar, the investor had a working window to complete the heir consolidation — acquiring any remaining heir interests in the property. The consolidation was completed during the installment period. When the property sold, the remaining installment balance was satisfied from the sale proceeds at closing. Net profit from the deal: $68,000.

## The Lesson

Payment plans are the first-line tool for tax sale avoidance when a lump-sum payoff is not operationally feasible. The process is straightforward: contact the collection firm, propose specific terms, get the agreement in writing, make the first payment at execution. Texas Tax Code § 33.02 makes this a statutory option, not a favor. The firm is motivated to accept because their fees are recovered either way.

Two common mistakes to avoid: First, paying the county tax office directly. That satisfies the underlying tax obligation but not the firm's fees, which are a separate balance, and the auction may proceed regardless. Second, waiting too long. Firms are less flexible in the final days before an auction when the sale preparations are complete. Contact the firm immediately when you identify a tax delinquency issue.
