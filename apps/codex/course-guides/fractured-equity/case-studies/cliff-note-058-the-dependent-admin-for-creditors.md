## The Situation

The heirs hadn't probated the estate or addressed the property. Creditors — owed money by the decedent — grew tired of waiting and filed to open a dependent administration themselves. A creditor-initiated dependent administration is one of the most complicated estate situations because the administrator's primary obligation is to the creditors, not the heirs. The heirs are residual beneficiaries only — they get what's left after all claims are paid.

In this case, an attorney was appointed as administrator. His fees plus the creditor claims presented an immediate question: was there anything left for the heirs, and was there margin for an investor?

The math: $78,000 in documented debts plus $22,000 in administrator fees (attorney-administrators bill for their time) against a property worth $165,000. Gross residual to heirs: $65,000. That's not nothing — but the heirs were fragmented, unmotivated, and would have to navigate the administration process themselves to get there.

## What the Operator Did

The investor analyzed the full claims picture under Texas Estates Code Chapter 355. Administration costs under § 355.102 are first-priority claims — they come before the general creditors. The claims allowance process was already underway.

The investor then approached the attorney-administrator with a proposal. Administrator-attorneys in creditor-initiated administrations often welcome investor resolution: it closes the file, generates their fees, and avoids the uncertain timeline of a traditional estate sale. The administrator had economic incentive to cooperate.

The structure negotiated:
- $8,000 to acquire all heirs' residual interests (the heirs got something, which was better than fighting through the administration for the uncertain remainder)
- $82,000 to satisfy the creditor claims at negotiated amounts (not necessarily full face value — creditors often accept less than the claim amount to close)
- $18,000 to buy out the administration — paying the administrator-attorney's fees in full and closing the matter

Total investor outlay: $108,000.

## The Outcome

Administration closed. Total investor cost: $108,000. Sale price: $158,000. Net to investor: $45,000. The claims were satisfied, the administration fees were paid, the heirs received the $8,000 consideration, and the property transferred cleanly.

## The Lesson

Creditor-initiated administrations are not automatic write-offs. The key question is the math: do the claims plus administration costs leave enough room between total outlay and market value for meaningful margin?

In this case: $108,000 total cost against $165,000 value — $57,000 gross margin, $45,000 after transaction costs. That's a real number. Not every deal works, but this one did because the claims were negotiable, the administrator was cooperative, and the property's value held.

Administrator-attorneys in creditor administrations have a concrete incentive to cooperate with investor resolution: it pays their fees and ends a file that would otherwise grind on for months. Use that alignment. Come to the administrator with a structure that closes the administration on reasonable terms for everyone.

The critical skill is claims analysis. Know exactly what is owed, to whom, at what priority, and at what settlement discount. The gap between face-value claims and negotiated settlement is often where investor margin lives.
