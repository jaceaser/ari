## The Situation

A dependent administrator had been appointed by the court four years earlier to manage the estate. The administrator — a family member with no particular legal expertise — had qualified, taken the oath, and done essentially nothing since. No inventory filed, no final account submitted, no property sold, no administration advanced. The estate just sat open.

Meanwhile the property deteriorated. The sole heir was cooperative and motivated to sell — she simply wanted to be done with the whole situation. But when the investor approached a title company for title insurance, the underwriter balked. An open dependent administration clouds the ownership picture: technically, the administrator has court-supervised authority over estate assets. The title company wanted the administration closed before it would insure.

## What the Operator Did

The investor's counsel filed a motion to close the administration and submit a final account. Under Texas Estates Code §§ 356.251 and 401.001, a dependent administrator needs court approval for sales — but there's no reason an inactive administration can't be terminated when the work is done or the parties agree it should end.

Critically, the administrator had no economic interest to oppose the motion. He wasn't being paid (no compensation arrangement had been established), he had no claims against the estate, and he had no reason to want the administration to continue. He cooperated fully.

The court approved the closure in 45 days. With the administration formally terminated, the investor proceeded with the standard documentation: an Affidavit of Heirship establishing the family tree and the heir's interest, followed by a Heir-to-Investor Deed from the sole heir.

## The Outcome

Acquisition completed at $28,000. The title company accepted the documentation — closed administration, AOH, and direct deed — and issued title insurance. The property sold at $165,000.

The critical legal point: under Texas Estates Code § 201.001, a sole heir's individual interest vested at the moment of the decedent's death — regardless of the administration status. The administration didn't create or define the heir's interest; it was just an administrative layer that needed to be cleared.

## The Lesson

Inactive administrations close quickly when the administrator has no reason to resist. If the administrator is a family member with no fee arrangement and no claims, the motion to close is often unopposed and can clear in 30 to 60 days.

The broader principle: the sole heir's ownership interest exists independently of whether an administration is open. The administration is a procedural wrapper, not the source of title. Push for closure when the title company requires it — but understand the legal foundation well enough to negotiate with title underwriters when closure isn't necessary.
