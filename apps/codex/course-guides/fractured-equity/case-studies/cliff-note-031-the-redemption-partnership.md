## The Situation

Raoul's father died, and seven months later Raoul discovers that the property — his father's homestead — was sold at a tax auction while the estate was unsettled. The auction happened. The property now ostensibly belongs to the tax sale purchaser. But Texas Tax Code § 34.21(a) gives Raoul a lifeline: the former owner's successor in a homestead has two full years from the auction date to redeem the property.

Redemption means paying the sale price plus all subsequent taxes paid by the purchaser plus a premium — 25% of the total if redeemed in the first year, 50% if redeemed in the second year. Raoul runs the numbers. The auction price, subsequent taxes, and 25% premium total $54,500. The property is worth considerably more than that. But Raoul has no access to $54,500. He has the right, and he has the asset — if he exercises the right. He has 17 months left to do something.

Without the funding, the redemption right expires and the tax sale purchaser keeps the property. Raoul walks away with nothing from a property his father lived in for years. The investor sees a situation where a party with a valid legal right simply needs capital to exercise it.

## What the Operator Did

The investor proposed a partnership. The terms were simple: the investor would fund the full $54,500 redemption through Raoul's attorney, restoring the property to Raoul's ownership. In exchange, Raoul would convey 60% of the property to the investor as consideration for that funding. Raoul would retain 40%.

The redemption was executed through Raoul's counsel — proper legal form requires the redemption to be made by or on behalf of the person with the redemption right, not the investor directly. The payment was made, the redemption was completed, and the property was restored to Raoul's ownership as a matter of Texas law. Quiet title was then filed to confirm that the tax sale had been effectively undone and that title was clean of any competing claim.

Raoul then executed a deed conveying 60% to the investor. Both parties now held undivided interests in a free-and-clear property. The property was listed and sold 8 months after the redemption.

## The Outcome

The property sold. From the proceeds, the investor received 60% — which, after accounting for the $54,500 funded, yielded a net return to the investor of $17,800 above the capital deployed. Raoul received his 40% share: $48,200. Raoul walked away with $48,200 from a property he had been about to lose entirely. The investor deployed $54,500 and recovered it plus $17,800 in profit over approximately 8 months.

## The Lesson

Redemption partnerships are a distinct tool that monetizes rights that would otherwise expire unused. The heir has the right but not the capital; the investor has the capital but not the standing to redeem directly. Together, both parties extract value from a situation that would otherwise benefit only the tax sale purchaser.

Structure matters: the investor funds, the heir exercises the redemption through their own counsel, the heir conveys the agreed equity stake to the investor, and the property sells on the open market. The heir gets something real instead of nothing. The investor gets a return on capital deployed in a situation with limited downside — the property is already worth more than the redemption cost before the first brick is laid.
