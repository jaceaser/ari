## The Situation

The investor discovered a property that had been through mortgage foreclosure three years earlier — but something about the chain of title was wrong. The purchaser at the foreclosure sale was a name the investor didn't recognize. Within days of the auction, that purchaser had deeded the property to an LLC connected to the decedent's estranged son. The son's LLC had been collecting rent on the property for three years since.

The actual heirs — the decedent's other children — had received no notice of the foreclosure. They didn't know it had happened. They believed they still had rights in the property and were not receiving rent.

Two legal theories applied here. First, Texas Property Code § 51.002 requires specific notice procedures for non-judicial foreclosure. A defective notice process can render the foreclosure void or voidable. Second, Texas Business and Commerce Code Chapter 24 — the Uniform Voidable Transactions Act — provides that a transfer made to defraud creditors or to defeat other parties' rights can be avoided. The straw buyer at the foreclosure plus the immediate deed-out to a family-connected LLC fit the UVTA pattern.

## What the Operator Did

The operator acquired the actual heirs' rights first. Three children of the decedent each had an inheritance interest in the property. The operator purchased all three interests for a combined $18,000, with each assignment documented using an Assignment of Rights instrument that covered both the ownership interest and any legal claims arising from the estate.

With assignments in hand, counsel filed suit asserting two theories: defective foreclosure notice under § 51.002 (the actual heirs were known parties who should have received notice), and fraudulent transfer under TUVTA (the straw buyer and immediate deed-out to the son's LLC was a scheme to strip the estate).

The son's LLC and the straw buyer faced significant exposure. Litigation of either theory — particularly combined — was unlikely to end in their favor. They settled rather than litigate.

## The Outcome

Settlement terms:
- Son's LLC conveyed the property to the investor by deed
- Son's LLC paid $45,000 in damages
- Each of the three actual heirs received $8,000 from the settlement
- Investor netted $97,000

The $18,000 paid for heir assignments was recovered many times over. The heirs received more from the settlement damages than they had received from the initial assignments — a result the operator delivered that the heirs couldn't have produced on their own.

## The Lesson

Sham foreclosures happen. The pattern is consistent: a straw buyer — often a nominee with no real economic stake — purchases at foreclosure and immediately deeds out to a family-connected entity. The strip-and-deed separates the legal form of the transaction (a foreclosure sale, which is public record) from the economic reality (a family member engineering the foreclosure to take the property for himself).

Look for it in the chain of title. When you see a foreclosure purchaser who immediately deeds out — same week, same month — to an LLC connected to a family member, pull the notice documentation. Texas § 51.002 requirements are technical and specific. Defective notice is more common than you'd expect in owner-engineered foreclosures, because the person running the scheme often isn't careful about compliance.

Combined UVTA and defective-foreclosure claims are powerful precisely because they're redundant. The defendant can't win on both theories simultaneously. That dual exposure drives settlements even in cases where the defendant might win on either theory alone. Settlements in this pattern typically involve property conveyance plus cash — the son's LLC loses the property and pays to get out of the litigation.

The assignments of the actual heirs' rights are the predicate move. Without those assignments, you have no standing to bring the claim. With them, you become the party with both the right to sue and the resources to pursue it.
