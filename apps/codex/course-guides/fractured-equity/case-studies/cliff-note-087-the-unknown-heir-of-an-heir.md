## The Situation

An estate with multiple heirs had a wrinkle: one of the original heirs had himself died approximately 15 years earlier. His share of the inherited property would have passed to his own heirs by representation — but nobody knew who those heirs were, or whether they even existed. The family thought maybe he had children somewhere. Or maybe not. The uncertainty was total.

This is not a situation where an affidavit of heirship solves the problem. An AOH is sworn testimony about who the heirs are — it depends on the witnesses having actual knowledge. When the witnesses genuinely do not know whether the intermediate heir had children, an AOH cannot establish that fact. Worse, if you record an AOH stating he had no children and then a child surfaces, that child has a valid claim to challenge the AOH's conclusions. The AOH becomes a liability.

The operator needed a definitive legal determination — one that would bind even the unknown heirs who had not yet appeared.

## What the Operator Did

Counsel filed an Application to Determine Heirship for the deceased intermediate heir under Texas Estates Code Chapter 202. This is a formal probate court proceeding with specific statutory requirements:

- A hearing before a court with proper jurisdiction
- An attorney ad litem appointed by the court to represent the interests of unknown heirs
- Public notice given in accordance with the statutory requirements — typically citation by publication — to give any unknown heirs the opportunity to appear
- Sworn testimony or other evidence of the decedent's family history
- A court decree establishing the heirs and their fractional interests

The attorney ad litem is the key figure in this process. Unlike a guardian ad litem (who represents a known but absent party), the attorney ad litem in a heirship proceeding represents an entire class of people — all potential heirs who have not appeared — and whose existence may not even be known at the time of appointment. The ad litem's job is to investigate actively: conduct genealogical research, search public records, interview known family members, follow every thread that might reveal whether unknown heirs exist.

The attorney ad litem appointed by the court in this case conducted a thorough investigation. She pulled birth records, searched county records for the deceased intermediate heir's name, interviewed his siblings and cousins, and traced his known address history through the relevant databases. The investigation uncovered something the family had not known or had forgotten: the deceased intermediate heir had two children from a relationship that the main family had been unaware of. Those children were adults now, living in another state, and entirely uninformed that they had inherited a fractional interest in a Texas property through their father.

The public notice period ran. The two children, once identified, were served directly with notice of the proceeding. They appeared at the hearing — represented by counsel — and confirmed their identity as the deceased heir's children.

The court issued its decree naming the two children as the heirs of the deceased intermediate heir and establishing their respective fractional interests in the property by representation. The decree was recorded in the real property records of the county where the property was located.

## The Outcome

With the court decree in hand, the operator had a definitive, judicially binding establishment of the ownership picture. No future claimant could emerge and credibly challenge the chain of title on the basis of the deceased intermediate heir's line — the court had investigated, given notice, and adjudicated. The decree bound even heirs who had not appeared.

The operator then approached the two discovered children directly. They were willing to sell — they had not known about the property, had no attachment to it, and were happy to receive cash for an interest they had not expected to have. The operator acquired their interests through standard purchase transactions at negotiated prices.

Timeline from the initial Application to Determine Heirship to the recorded court decree: approximately six months. This is typical for the proceeding — the investigation, notice period, scheduling of the hearing, and post-hearing decree drafting collectively take time. It is not a fast process, but it is a definitive one.

## The Lesson

When a chain-of-title analysis reveals a deceased heir with unknown or potentially unknown descendants, the Determination of Heirship proceeding is the right tool. Not an AOH. Not a quiet title action that tries to extinguish claims without identifying them. The formal heirship proceeding — with its attorney ad litem, public notice, and court decree — is specifically designed for this situation.

The attorney ad litem is what makes the proceeding work. This is an independent investigator with legal authority and professional obligation to find what is there to be found. In this case, the ad litem found two children the family had not known about. That discovery was worth far more than the cost of the proceeding — had those children not been found and their interests not acquired, they would have remained cloud-on-title claimants indefinitely.

The recorded court decree is the strongest possible resolution of an unknown-heir situation. Title companies and underwriters recognize it as dispositive. It is what you want when the ownership question is genuinely uncertain — not the weakest tool that might get you to closing, but the strongest tool that definitively resolves the uncertainty.

Six months is the cost. A definitive chain of title is the result. When the alternative is a permanent cloud on title, six months and the associated legal fees are always worth it.
