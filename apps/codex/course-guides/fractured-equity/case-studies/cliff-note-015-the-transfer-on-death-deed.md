## The Situation

Hilda Vanderbilt recorded a Transfer-on-Death Deed in 2019. She named her nephew Atticus as the sole beneficiary. She died in 2023 — four years after recording the TODD. In those four years, she never revoked it.

Her two adult children had no idea the TODD existed. When she died, they assumed the property would pass to them through intestate succession as her natural heirs. They surfaced with an expectation of ownership.

## What the Operator Did

The operator searched the county deed records before approaching anyone. The TODD was there — recorded in 2019, clearly identifying the property and Atticus as the beneficiary, never revoked by any subsequent instrument.

Texas Estates Code § 114.001 et seq. governs Transfer-on-Death Deeds in Texas. The statute, enacted in 2015, allows a property owner to record a deed designating a beneficiary who takes the property at the owner's death outside of probate. The TODD must be recorded before death to be effective. It can be revoked at any time before death by recording a revocation instrument. If not revoked, it controls.

A valid, unrevoked TODD recorded before death passes the described property to the designated beneficiary at the moment of death by operation of law. It overrides intestate succession. It overrides contrary provisions in a will as to the described property. Probate is not required.

Atticus owns 100% of the property by operation of the TODD. The adult children have no claim on this property. Their mother made a deliberate legal choice in 2019 to direct this property to Atticus outside the probate system. The children were educated on this point. They may have claims to other assets in Hilda's estate — but not to this property.

The operator acquired Atticus's 100% for $38,000. Clean title.

## The Outcome

Atticus's 100% interest acquired for $38,000. Clean title. Adult children educated that the TODD controlled and their intestate claims do not apply to this property.

## The Lesson

TODDs have become increasingly common since Texas enacted the statute in 2015. Search the county deed records for a recorded TODD before you start building any assumption about intestate succession. A valid TODD that was recorded before death and never revoked is an absolute override — it controls over intestate defaults, it controls over wills, and it delivers clean title to the named beneficiary by operation of law with no probate required. If you miss the TODD, you are negotiating with the wrong people.
