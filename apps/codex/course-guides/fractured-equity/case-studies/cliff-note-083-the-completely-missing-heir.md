## The Situation

Five heirs shared ownership of an inherited property. Four were findable and willing to deal. The fifth had not been seen or heard from since 1994. The last known address was more than 25 years stale. No phone number existed. No social media presence could be identified. Every relative who was asked shrugged — nobody had seen him in decades, nobody knew if he was even still alive.

That fifth heir held 20% of the property. Twenty percent of a deal is not a rounding error. The operator could not convey clean title without either finding the heir, waiting for presumed-death proceedings, or proceeding through the judicial publication service process.

## What the Operator Did

The operator understood that courts do not authorize citation by publication based on a casual search. "I couldn't find him on Google" is not diligent search. Diligent search is a documented, professional-grade effort using multiple tools across multiple categories, all of it sworn to in affidavit form. The court evaluates the affidavit before authorizing publication — and courts reject thin affidavits.

The operator hired Sean Baker, a professional investigator the operator had used on previous hard cases. Baker's search protocol covered:

- TLOxp full person search with all known identifiers — name, date of birth, last known SSN fragment, last known address
- Bakers research platform cross-reference
- Criminal and civil court records in the last known county of residence
- Utility record inquiries at the last known address
- County tax assessment rolls under the heir's name
- Ancestry.com and public family tree databases for any recent documentation of the heir's status
- Obituary searches in the last known county and surrounding counties

Every search step was documented with the date run, the tool or source used, and the result returned. No stone was left undocumented. The investigator's report became the backbone of the Affidavit of Diligent Search that would go before the court.

Counsel reviewed the affidavit and confirmed it met the threshold for citation by publication under Texas Rules of Civil Procedure 109. The petition was filed. The court reviewed the diligent search documentation and authorized publication service. The court also appointed a guardian ad litem — an attorney whose job was to represent the unknown heir's interests during the proceeding, investigate whether any additional information could be found about the heir's whereabouts, and report to the court.

The citation was published in a newspaper of general circulation in the county where the property was located, as required by Rule 109. The publication period ran its statutory course.

## The Outcome

The missing heir did not appear during the publication period. The guardian ad litem conducted their own investigation, found no additional information on the heir's location or status, and filed a report confirming no appearance was anticipated. Default was entered against the missing heir.

The court approved the proceeding. The missing heir's 20% interest was addressed through the default judgment, allowing the deal to proceed with a clear title path. The title company was satisfied with the documented publication process and the court's default judgment.

Total costs broke down as follows:

- Professional investigator fee: approximately $1,200
- Publication costs (statutory newspaper publication): approximately $400
- Guardian ad litem attorney fee: approximately $1,100
- **Total: approximately $2,700**

Timeline from petition filing to default: 105 days — within the 90 to 120 day range typical for Texas publication-service defaults.

## The Lesson

Missing heirs are not dead ends — they are documented search problems. The law provides a mechanism to proceed without a missing party, but that mechanism requires demonstrated effort first. The diligent search affidavit is not a formality; it is the key that unlocks publication service.

Do the search properly. Use professional investigators with access to professional databases. Document every step. Date-stamp every query. Record every result — including negative results, because showing that you searched and came up empty is exactly what the affidavit needs to demonstrate. A court reviewing a petition for publication service is asking one question: did you really try to find this person? Your affidavit answers that question, and the quality of that answer determines whether the court authorizes the next step.

Budget $2,000 to $3,000 for the full publication process. Timeline runs 90 to 120 days. These are not small costs — but they are finite and predictable costs for a defined legal process. Compare them to the alternative: leaving 20% of the deal's ownership unresolved and untransferable. There is no comparison.
