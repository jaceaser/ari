## The Situation

Five cotenants owned 120 rural acres equally — 24 acres each in economic terms, though the title was undivided, meaning no specific acres were assigned to any specific owner. Three of the five wanted to sell. Two wanted to hold.

The two who wanted to hold were exercising a legitimate property right. You cannot force a person to sell their undivided interest in land simply because the majority wants to sell. But three cotenants cannot sell the whole property without all five signing — and two were refusing to sign.

Partition was the answer. The question was what kind of partition, and how to position before filing it.

## What the Operator Did

The investor recognized two things before taking any action. First, the three cooperative cotenants were the investor's entry point — they wanted out, and the investor could buy their interests at a price that reflected both the value of 20% fractional interests and the discount that comes from needing a third party to move your interest for you. Second, the character of the property — 120 acres of rural land — meant that partition in kind might be available and might actually be advantageous.

Partition in kind is the physical division of property among cotenants. It is preferred by Texas law when feasible because it gives each cotenant full ownership of an identified piece of land rather than a share of auction proceeds. Texas Property Code § 23.001 directs courts to consider partition in kind before ordering partition by sale. For residential homes and urban lots, partition in kind is rarely feasible — you cannot meaningfully divide a single-family house. For 120 acres of rural land with reasonable uniformity, partition in kind is often perfectly feasible.

**Step One: Pre-Partition Acquisition**

The investor approached all three cooperative cotenants and purchased their 20% interests. Each 20% interest was purchased separately, negotiated based on the individual seller's situation. The pricing reflected the fractional-interest discount — each owner was selling a piece of a property that could not be independently sold or used, so the price per acre was below retail. But the investor was committed to the acquisition and provided certainty and speed that the owners valued.

After completing three acquisitions, the investor held 60% of the property across three equal ownership positions.

**Step Two: Partition Filing**

With 60% consolidated, the investor filed a partition petition naming the two retaining cotenants as defendants. The petition requested partition in kind, consistent with § 23.001's preference. The two retaining cotenants did not oppose the petition — they wanted to keep their land, and partition in kind would achieve exactly that by assigning them identified parcels that they would own outright going forward.

The court appointed commissioners with survey and real estate expertise to design the division. The commissioners' mandate was equitable division — each parcel should represent 20% of the property's total value, accounting for access, water, terrain, and other value factors. The commissioners surveyed the property, analyzed the terrain variations, identified road access points, and confirmed water availability across different sections.

**Step Three: The Division**

The commissioners designed five 24-acre parcels. The parcels were not identical — no rural 120-acre tract is perfectly uniform — so the commissioners weighted the division to make each parcel economically equivalent in value even where the physical characteristics differed. The investor held three ownership positions (the three acquired shares), so the investor was assigned three parcels. The commissioners assigned the investor the three parcels with the best road access and the most consistent terrain — characteristics that translated directly into retail buyer appeal.

The two retaining cotenants each received a 24-acre parcel corresponding to their retained interests.

## The Outcome

The investor owned three 24-acre parcels — 72 acres — free and clear of any cotenant relationship. The former cotenants had been separated into independent landowners with no ongoing shared ownership.

The investor then sold the three parcels individually to retail buyers rather than as a single 72-acre bulk sale. The key economic insight was that 24-acre tracts in that market commanded a premium per-acre price compared to large bulk tracts. Buyers who wanted a small farm, a rural homesite, or a recreational property could absorb a 24-acre parcel that they could not absorb a 72-acre parcel. Individual parcel pricing produced aggregate proceeds that exceeded what 60% of the whole-tract value would have been at a bulk auction.

The two retaining cotenants received their 48 acres, became independent owners, and had no further involvement with the investor or with each other's properties.

## The Lesson

Partition in kind is a tool that most fractured-equity operators think about only for residential property — where they quickly conclude it is not applicable. That conclusion is correct for single-family homes. But it misses the powerful application of partition in kind to rural land.

Large rural tracts that can be physically divided into multiple viable parcels are excellent candidates for partition-in-kind strategy. The investor who pre-positions through pre-partition acquisitions and then files for partition in kind can receive multiple individually saleable parcels that, when sold separately at retail, produce more aggregate value than a single bulk auction would.

The pre-partition acquisition is essential to this strategy. The investor who files partition with only a 20% interest gets one parcel — their 24 acres — and the other four cotenants get theirs. That may still be profitable if the original acquisition price was low enough. But the investor who acquires three of the five interests before filing gets three parcels, controls the exit on 60% of the property, and can choose the best parcels through the commissioner's equitable assignment process.

Rural land. Large tracts. Physical division feasible. Pre-partition acquisition. Partition in kind. Individual retail parcel sales. This is the sequence that turns a fractured rural property into a premium retail exit.
