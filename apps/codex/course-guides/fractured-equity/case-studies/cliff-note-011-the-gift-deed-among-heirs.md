## The Situation

Three siblings inherited a home equally following their parent's death — one-third each. The oldest brother, Maurice, had an $80,000 child support lien recorded against him personally. Someone in the family was aware of the lien and understood that it could attach to Maurice's one-third interest and cloud the title if the property was ever sold.

To address this, Maurice gift-deeded his one-third interest to his sister Daphne before any investor got involved. Daphne had no liens. After the gift deed, the title record showed Daphne at 66.67% and Vernon at 33.33%. Maurice no longer appeared as an owner.

An investor approached the property.

## What the Operator Did

The operator acquired both interests: Daphne's 66.67% at $18,000 and Vernon's 33.33% at $9,000.

The title search flagged Maurice as a grantor in the chain — the intestate inheritance flowed to Maurice, and then Maurice gift-deeded to Daphne. The question was whether the $80,000 child support lien could reach through the gift deed to Daphne's interest, or from there to the investor.

Texas Family Code § 157.317 establishes that child support liens index to the obligor personally. The lien attaches to property in which the obligor holds an interest. After Maurice executed the gift deed, he held no interest in this specific property. His lien had nothing to attach to here.

The gift deed was a valid conveyance under Texas Property Code § 5.001. Recorded consideration was nominal, which was expected for a gift deed. The chain distance between Maurice-as-obligor and the investor — with Daphne as an intervening good-faith grantee — prevented the lien from traveling forward to impair the acquisition.

Standard title insurance was issued.

## The Outcome

Full consolidation for $27,000 total. Standard title insurance issued without exception for the Maurice lien.

## The Lesson

Gift deeds from lien-burdened heirs to clean heirs are legitimate chain-distance tools. Maurice got rid of the lien trap. Daphne got ownership. The investor got a clean chain and standard title insurance. Nobody lost except the child support creditor — who was unlikely to collect against this specific asset anyway, and who has other enforcement mechanisms available for a personal obligation. The deal works because the lien attaches to the person and their current property interests, not to a chain of prior title.
