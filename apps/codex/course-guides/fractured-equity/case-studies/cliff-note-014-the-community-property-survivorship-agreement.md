## The Situation

Nelson and Beatrice Caldwell were married and had signed a Texas Community Property Survivorship Agreement in 2008. Nelson died in 2021 without a will. Their adult children immediately surfaced with the assumption that they were entitled to inherit Nelson's half of the community estate under intestate succession — the default rule they had seen applied in other family situations.

The CPSA was not in the county deed records. It was found in Nelson's personal papers.

## What the Operator Did

Texas Estates Code § 112.001 et seq. authorizes spouses to sign a Community Property Survivorship Agreement directing that on the death of the first spouse, 100% of their community property passes to the surviving spouse. The instrument overrides intestate succession. It overrides contrary will provisions as to covered property. It does not require recording to be effective — a private signed and acknowledged document between the spouses is sufficient.

The operator located the CPSA in Nelson's personal papers and confirmed proper execution: signed by both Nelson and Beatrice, acknowledged before a notary. The instrument met the statutory requirements. Beatrice takes 100%.

The adult children were educated. Their intestate inheritance claim assumed no CPSA existed — but the CPSA did exist and it superseded intestate succession entirely. They have no current interest in Nelson's half of the estate. Their eventual inheritance comes from Beatrice when she dies, not from Nelson now. The CPSA simply changed the timing and sequence — it did not disinherit them permanently, it only deferred their share to Beatrice's death.

With Beatrice as the sole owner, the transaction was straightforward. Single-seller acquisition at $138,000.

## The Outcome

Single-seller transaction with Beatrice at $138,000. Adult children educated that their inheritance flows from Beatrice at her eventual death, not from Nelson now.

## The Lesson

Community Property Survivorship Agreements are uncommon but fully operative when present. The critical deal discipline is this: always search the decedent's personal papers for any estate-planning instrument that might override the intestate defaults you are assuming apply. CPSAs, transfer-on-death deeds, revocable trusts — any of these can redirect ownership in ways the public records do not reveal. Missing a CPSA means building your entire acquisition strategy on a false premise about who owns what and how many sellers you actually need.
