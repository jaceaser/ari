## The Situation

One of the heirs to the Texas property was the decedent's son, who had been living in Guatemala for years. He was cooperative and willing to sell — distance was not an attitude problem, it was a logistics problem. Texas recording law requires that a deed be acknowledged before a notary or other authorized officer. A deed signed in Guatemala before a Guatemalan notary doesn't automatically meet Texas recording requirements without additional authentication steps.

Texas Property Code § 12.007 requires valid acknowledgment for a deed to be recorded. For international signatures, the Hague Apostille Convention and consular acknowledgment procedures provide the path.

## What the Operator Did

The investor's counsel identified two options for the Guatemalan heir: either sign before a US consular officer at the US Embassy in Guatemala City, or obtain an apostille on a locally-acknowledged document through the Guatemalan foreign ministry. Either would satisfy Texas recording requirements.

The consular officer route was chosen as the cleaner path. The son traveled to the US Embassy in Guatemala City, presented proper identification, and signed the deed before a US consular officer. Consular officers are explicitly authorized under federal law to perform notarial acts for US legal purposes — their acknowledgment carries the same legal weight as a US notary's.

The apostille was obtained from the appropriate Guatemalan authority to authenticate the consular acknowledgment for international use. The completed deed package — deed plus apostille — was then sent to Texas for recording.

Separately, the closing team addressed FIRPTA. Because the son was a foreign person under US tax law, his share of the sales proceeds was subject to withholding under the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act. The investor withheld 15% of the heir's $15,000 proceeds — $2,250 — and remitted it to the IRS as required.

## The Outcome

The transaction closed. The son received $15,000 minus the FIRPTA withholding via international wire transfer. The deed recorded without issue.

## The Lesson

International heirs are a logistics challenge, not an insurmountable legal obstacle. Two tools address the acknowledgment problem: US consular officers at any US Embassy or Consulate can perform acknowledgments for US legal documents, and the Hague Apostille Convention provides authentication for documents acknowledged locally in member countries.

Two compliance items to address for every foreign-person heir:

First, the acknowledgment. Confirm which method — consular officer or apostille on local acknowledgment — is most practical given where the heir is located. Not every country is a Hague Convention member, which affects which path is available.

Second, FIRPTA. Any foreign person selling US real property is subject to 15% withholding on gross proceeds unless a withholding certificate from the IRS reduces it. Build this into the closing math from the beginning. The heir receives less than the gross purchase price — which affects negotiation — and the withheld amount goes to the IRS, not the buyer.

Don't let an overseas address derail a deal. The path exists. Budget extra lead time for the logistics.
