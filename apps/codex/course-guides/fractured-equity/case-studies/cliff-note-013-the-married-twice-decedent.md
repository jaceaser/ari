## The Situation

Augustus Kensington's marital history was complicated. He married a first wife, divorced her, and had two biological children from that marriage. He then married a second wife who had a child of her own — a stepchild he never formally adopted. His second wife predeceased him. He married a third time — Rebecca — and they had no children together. He died in 2022 while married to Rebecca. The property at issue was community property purchased during the third marriage. No will.

Rebecca was occupying the property. She believed she owned it fully. The stepchild from the second marriage had a vague sense of a possible claim. The two children from the first marriage were out there somewhere.

## What the Operator Did

Texas Estates Code § 201.003 operates on a single trigger: does the decedent have ANY child who is not a child of the current marriage? If yes, 100% of the decedent's community half goes to all of his children — none of it goes to the surviving spouse.

Augustus had two children from his first marriage. They were not children of Rebecca. The trigger was met. His entire 50% community interest passed to those two children equally — 25% each. Rebecca received zero of his community half. She retained her own 50%.

The stepchild from the second marriage has no claim under intestate succession. She was the child of Augustus's late second wife — not Augustus's biological or legally adopted child. Intestate succession does not pass through stepparent relationships.

The AOH documented the complete marital history: first marriage, divorce, two biological children. Second marriage, second wife's preexisting child, no adoption, second wife predeceased. Third marriage to Rebecca, no children, community property acquired during this marriage. Excluded parties listed with recitals: the divorced first wife (no surviving spouse claim), the stepchild from marriage two (no blood or adoption relationship to Augustus).

Final distribution: Rebecca 50%, Child from Marriage #1 at 25%, Child from Marriage #1 at 25%.

Rebecca was educated through the AOH process and her own counsel. Rather than a full buyout, she agreed to a JV structure at 50/50 on her 50%. The two children from marriage one each sold their 25% interests: $8,500 and $9,500 respectively. Property sold, Rebecca netted $72,000.

## The Outcome

Rebecca participated as JV partner on her 50% at 50/50. First child's 25% acquired for $8,500. Second child's 25% acquired for $9,500. Property sold. Rebecca netted $72,000.

## The Lesson

Multiple-marriage decedents require tracing every marriage and every child from every marriage — there is no shortcut. The § 201.003 rule is binary: the existence of ANY child outside the current marriage activates the rule and redirects 100% of the community half to the children. One child from a marriage three decades ago is sufficient. The stepchild of a prior spouse gets nothing regardless of how long the relationship lasted or how close they were. Get the marital history right and the distribution math follows.
