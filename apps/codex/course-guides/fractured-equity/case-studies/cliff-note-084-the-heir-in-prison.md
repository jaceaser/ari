## The Situation

A 25% fractional interest in an inherited property was held by an heir serving a 15-year sentence in a Texas Department of Criminal Justice facility. The heir could not attend a title company closing. Standard notary protocol requires the signing party to appear in person before the notary — and in-person at a prison means navigating TDCJ's facility-specific visitation and notary access rules, which are almost entirely unknown to conventional title professionals.

Most operators look at this situation and assume the heir's interest is inaccessible until release. That assumption is wrong. The heir is a competent adult with legal capacity to convey real property. The only logistical challenge is completing a valid notarized signing inside a correctional facility.

## What the Operator Did

The operator contacted Sean Baker, a professional investigator with documented experience navigating TDCJ facilities. The choice of the right investigator mattered here — this is not a task for a skip tracer who locates missing people via database. This is a task for someone who knows correctional facility procedures, has existing relationships with TDCJ administrative contacts, and understands the specific variation in notary access rules from one unit to another.

Baker contacted the facility to determine the notary access protocol for that specific unit. TDCJ facilities do not operate uniformly on this issue: some have notaries employed on staff who can witness signings by appointment, some permit credentialed mobile notaries to enter the facility under supervised conditions, and some require coordination through the warden's office. Baker identified the correct procedure for this facility and initiated the scheduling process.

In parallel, the operator's attorney sent a letter to the heir at the facility, explaining the transaction, outlining the proposed purchase price, and requesting the heir's agreement in writing before the signing appointment was scheduled. The heir wrote back confirming agreement. This advance coordination meant the signing appointment would not be wasted on a reluctant signer.

Baker arranged the in-prison notary signing through the facility's approved process, coordinated the appointment time, and ensured the deed instrument was properly prepared in advance. The heir appeared before the notary inside the facility and executed the deed. The notary acknowledged the signing. The deed was complete.

## The Outcome

The operator acquired the incarcerated heir's 25% interest for $4,000. Payment was deposited to the heir's commissary account — the payment method TDCJ permits and that the heir specifically requested because it provides immediate access to funds inside the facility. Commissary payments of this nature are standard and legally permissible.

The deed was accepted by the title company without objection. An in-prison notarization, properly conducted under Texas Government Code Chapter 406, carries the same legal weight as any other acknowledged instrument. There is no title company exception for prison signings — it is a valid deed.

The professional investigator's total fee for navigating the TDCJ signing: approximately $900. This included facility research, scheduling coordination, and on-site presence to ensure the logistics proceeded correctly.

The remaining heirs were acquired through standard mobile notary or in-office procedures. The deal closed in full.

## The Lesson

Incarcerated heirs are worth pursuing, not avoiding. Two facts work in the operator's favor: first, the logistical problem is solvable through professional investigators who know correctional facility procedures; second, incarcerated heirs are often among the most motivated sellers in a deal. A person serving a multi-year sentence has few legitimate income sources, limited ability to manage property interests, and immediate need for resources — in any form the facility permits.

The negotiating dynamic is often favorable. This heir accepted $4,000 for a 25% interest in a property worth significantly more. That is not exploitation — the heir had no ability to manage or monetize the interest during their sentence, no practical recourse to force a different outcome, and a genuine need for commissary funds. A fair transaction serves both parties.

The legal framework is straightforward. Incarceration does not affect legal capacity to convey real property in Texas. Texas Government Code Chapter 406 governs notary public procedures. An in-prison notarization, properly conducted, is a valid acknowledgment. Title companies understand this — it is not a novel situation.

Budget $800 to $1,200 for a professional investigator to handle TDCJ logistics. It is money well spent on a signing that otherwise cannot happen through conventional channels.
