## The Situation

A residential property in a gated subdivision had accumulated $22,000 in HOA liens — six years of unpaid assessments plus attorney fees from the HOA's collection proceedings. The homeowner's association was threatening foreclosure under Texas Property Code § 209.009, which grants Texas HOAs a statutory right to foreclose for unpaid assessments.

The original owner had died. The heirs had been ignoring the HOA demands. The HOA was done waiting.

Three heirs held interests in the property. All three were cooperative — they wanted out but weren't sure what to do about the HOA lien.

## What the Operator Did

The acquisition side was straightforward: three cooperative heirs, $12,000 combined payment to acquire all interests and take ownership. With the property in hand, the operator turned to the HOA negotiation.

HOA boards have discretion in settlement negotiations with new owners. The leverage in these negotiations is specific: the HOA's interest is not in maximizing past collections — it's in getting a current-paying owner in the property going forward. An HOA that forecloses a property often ends up owning it, which creates its own problems. An HOA that settles with a new investor who then sells to a homebuyer gets a paying owner and no more administrative headaches.

The operator approached the HOA board with two elements: a settlement proposal on the past-due balance, and a commitment to bring current assessments current immediately.

The HOA accepted $14,500 to release all accumulated liens — assessments plus attorney fees — a reduction from $22,000, or approximately 34% off face value. Current assessments were brought current.

## The Outcome

- Heir acquisition (three cooperative, combined): $12,000
- HOA lien settlement (release of all liens): $14,500 (down from $22,000)
- Current assessments: paid and current
- Sale price: $175,000
- Net profit: $42,000

## The Lesson

HOA liens negotiate at 60 to 75 percent of face value when the new owner commits to curing current assessments and demonstrates intent to sell quickly. The range varies by HOA, by the composition of the board, and by whether attorney fees are included in the balance — attorney fees are often the most negotiable component, since the HOA's attorneys charge them out to get paid regardless of what the HOA recovers.

The key principle is that HOAs want ongoing dues, not litigation. Every month the property sits delinquent, the HOA is absorbing the administrative and legal cost of a problem that isn't generating any revenue. A new owner who pays the settlement and brings current assessments up-to-date is the solution the HOA actually wants — even when they're not saying it explicitly.

Approach HOA negotiations after acquisition, not before. Once you control the property, you are the new owner dealing with the HOA going forward. The HOA's options are settle with you and get a paying member, or fight you and get more delays. That dynamic favors settlement.

Time the conversation right: don't approach the HOA until you're already the owner of record. Negotiate as a buyer trying to clear a lien before closing is possible — some HOAs will hold the line on full payment when they think they still have leverage over a pending sale. As the owner of record with a property you plan to sell and a credible buyer's market, you have leverage too.
