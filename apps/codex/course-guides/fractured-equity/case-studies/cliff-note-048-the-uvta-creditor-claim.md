## The Situation

The decedent had transferred the property to his son approximately six months before dying. The consideration for the transfer: nothing. No money changed hands. The deed recited nominal consideration, but the transaction was a gift — or more precisely, an apparent attempt to put the property beyond the reach of creditors before the decedent's death.

A creditor held an $85,000 judgment against the decedent's estate. That creditor was paying attention. Under Texas Business and Commerce Code Chapter 24 — the Texas Uniform Voidable Transactions Act (TUVTA) — a transfer of property without reasonably equivalent value that leaves the transferor insolvent can be avoided by creditors. The transfer here checked both boxes: no consideration (not reasonably equivalent value) and a decedent who was apparently insolvent or near-insolvent when he made it.

TUVTA § 24.010 provides a four-year statute of limitations on avoidance claims. The transfer was six months old. The creditor was well within the window.

If the creditor's TUVTA suit succeeded, the son would lose ownership of the property — the transfer would be avoided and the property would revert to the decedent's estate, where the creditor could reach it to satisfy the $85,000 judgment. The son faced losing everything.

## What the Operator Did

The operator identified the three-way settlement opportunity. The parties and their positions:

The son: owned the property through a voidable transfer, facing loss of ownership if the TUVTA suit succeeded. Motivated to exit with something rather than lose everything.

The creditor: had an $85,000 judgment and a viable TUVTA claim. Could pursue full judgment value through litigation, but litigation is expensive, takes years, and might produce a judgment against an insolvent estate that collects nothing even after winning.

The investor: wants the property at a discount that reflects the litigation risk and the cloud on title.

The three-way settlement structure resolved all three positions simultaneously:

The investor acquired the son's interest — the subject of the TUVTA dispute — for $8,000. This was a deeply discounted acquisition reflecting the cloud on the son's title.

From that acquisition, the investor paid the creditor $35,000 — a partial satisfaction of the $85,000 judgment. The creditor received immediate, certain cash rather than an uncertain litigation outcome.

In exchange, the creditor released both the TUVTA avoidance claim and the $85,000 judgment.

The result: clean title in the investor, the TUVTA claim extinguished, the creditor's judgment satisfied and released.

## The Outcome

- Son's interest acquisition: $8,000
- Creditor payment (from acquisition proceeds): $35,000
- TUVTA avoidance claim: released
- Creditor judgment ($85,000): released
- Sale price: $175,000
- Net profit: $95,000

## The Lesson

Fraudulent transfer claims — TUVTA avoidance suits — cloud title and create acquisition opportunities that most operators don't know how to navigate. The cloud exists because the son's ownership is legally vulnerable. But that vulnerability also creates a motivated seller: a transferee who faces losing the property entirely through litigation will sell at a discount that reflects that risk.

The three-way settlement is the mechanism. The investor sits in the middle of a dispute between the creditor and the son, structures a payment to the creditor that satisfies them below full judgment value, acquires the property at a below-market price, and clears all clouds simultaneously. No party gets everything they wanted — but every party gets something better than their litigation alternative.

The creditor's calculus: $35,000 certain versus pursuing a voidable-transfer claim through the courts, winning, reverting property to an estate, then trying to collect on an estate that may have other creditors and complications. The bird in hand is worth something. Most judgment creditors in this position will settle for 40 to 60 percent of face value for a quick, certain, cash resolution.

The four-year SOL under § 24.010 is the operator's enemy in one respect and friend in another. As enemy: a TUVTA claim that's still within the four-year window is a real legal threat that has to be resolved, not ignored. As friend: once the four-year window closes, an old voidable transfer becomes unassailable, and properties that passed through no-consideration transfers more than four years ago may have clean-enough title that the settlement mechanism isn't needed.

Know the transfer date. Know the consideration. Know where you are in the four-year clock. These three facts determine whether you're dealing with a live TUVTA threat or an expired one.
