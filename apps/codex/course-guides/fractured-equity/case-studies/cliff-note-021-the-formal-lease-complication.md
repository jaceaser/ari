## The Situation

Before the decedent died, they had rented the home to a tenant under a properly executed written two-year lease. The lease was signed, witnessed, and valid in every respect. The tenant pays $1,800 per month and has never missed a payment. Fourteen months remain on the term when the investor acquires the property from the heirs.

Texas Property Code Chapter 91 is unambiguous on this point: leases survive the landlord's death and bind successors-in-interest. The investor did not buy the property free of the lease. The investor bought the property subject to it, stepping into the decedent's position as landlord. The tenant has the right to remain in possession until the lease expires — fourteen months from now — paying the same $1,800 per month under the same terms.

There is no early termination clause in the lease that favors the landlord. The operator reviewed the document carefully and found none. That eliminates the contractual exit. Eviction is not available — a tenant in good standing on a valid lease cannot be removed. The operator now faces a choice: hold the property for fourteen more months collecting $1,800/month while waiting to list it, sell it subject to the existing lease (at a discount), or negotiate a voluntary vacate.

## What the Operator Did

The operator opened a conversation with the tenant. The tenant, it turned out, had been looking for a larger place anyway. The timing was actually convenient for them. A cash-for-keys arrangement was proposed: $3,000 for a voluntary vacate within 45 days. The tenant accepted.

The operator paid $3,000. The tenant vacated within 45 days as agreed. The property was then listed on the open market as a vacant, show-ready home.

## The Outcome

The property sold vacant for $165,000. The investor's analysis had estimated that a subject-to-lease sale — meaning a sale to another investor who would take the property with the tenant in place — would yield roughly $138,000. The difference is $27,000. Subtract the $3,000 buyout and the net gain from the cash-for-keys arrangement was approximately $24,000. The $3,000 investment returned roughly eight times its cost.

## The Lesson

Valid leases survive owner death. That is settled Texas law. You cannot evict a tenant who is current on rent and holding a valid written lease simply because you bought the property. Do not try. Review the lease before you close, understand what you are acquiring, and budget accordingly. If the lease has no landlord-side early termination clause and the tenant is performing, your options are hold, sell subject-to, or negotiate a buyout. A reasonable cash-for-keys arrangement — $3,000 here — is almost always cheaper than the alternative. Compare the buyout cost against the subject-to discount and against the carrying cost of waiting out the lease. In this case the math was decisive.
