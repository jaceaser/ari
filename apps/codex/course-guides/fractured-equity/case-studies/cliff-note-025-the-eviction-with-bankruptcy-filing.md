## The Situation

Three days before the scheduled eviction trial, the hostile occupant heir filed Chapter 7 bankruptcy. This was not coincidence. Under 11 U.S.C. § 362(a), the filing of a bankruptcy petition automatically stays virtually all proceedings against the debtor and their property. That automatic stay is immediate and self-executing — the eviction trial that was scheduled for three days later cannot proceed. The state court case is frozen the moment the bankruptcy petition is filed.

The hostile heir had done the math: the eviction trial was three days away, and a bankruptcy filing cost less than three days of stalling. The automatic stay was the weapon. The play was delay.

But the stay is not permanent, and it is not unchallengeable. Under 11 U.S.C. § 362(d), a party in interest can file a motion for relief from the stay with the bankruptcy court, and the court can grant that relief "for cause" — including the fact that the debtor has no equity in the property and the property is not necessary to an effective reorganization. The investor holds 75% of the ownership. The debtor-heir holds 25%. On a property worth, say, $200,000, the debtor's 25% is $50,000 — but the debtor also owes the estate's share of taxes, maintenance, and potentially contribution. The "no equity" argument is strong.

## What the Operator Did

The investor immediately retained bankruptcy counsel. Bankruptcy is federal law and requires practitioners admitted in federal court — it is a distinct specialization from real estate litigation. Counsel filed an emergency Motion for Relief from Stay in the bankruptcy court, with supporting documentation showing that the debtor-heir held no equity in the property (the investor held 75%), that the property was not the debtor's primary residence in any meaningful sense, and that there was no reorganization plan to which the property could possibly be necessary.

The bankruptcy court scheduled the hearing 21 days after the motion was filed — well within the timeline contemplated by the statute. At the hearing, the court reviewed the facts: the investor held 75%, the debtor-heir had no equity position worth protecting, and the Chapter 7 filing was a liquidation proceeding, not a reorganization. The court granted relief from the stay. The eviction proceedings in state court resumed. The property was vacated 60 days after the date of the bankruptcy filing.

## The Outcome

The bankruptcy delayed the eviction but did not defeat it. The additional delay was approximately 60 days — the time from filing to the lifted stay and resumed proceedings. The additional cost was approximately $2,800 in bankruptcy counsel fees. The hostile occupant's tactic consumed two months and cost the investor less than $3,000 to defeat.

## The Lesson

Bankruptcy filings by hostile occupants and holdout heirs are a recognizable and common delay tactic. They are not deal killers. The moment you see a bankruptcy filing, retain bankruptcy counsel and file the Motion for Relief from Stay. The motion is routinely granted when the debtor holds no equity in the property and has filed a Chapter 7 liquidation — not a reorganization. The timeline is 3-4 weeks to a hearing and decision, the cost is modest, and the outcome in these fact patterns is predictable. Never assume the bankruptcy filing ended the deal. It only started the clock on the federal proceeding that defeats it.
