## The Situation

Heir Sylvester had inherited a 33.33% interest in a property worth $195,000. His one-third share was worth approximately $65,000. The problem was a $32,000 federal tax lien filed against him personally.

Under 26 U.S.C. § 6321, a federal tax lien attaches to all property and rights to property belonging to the taxpayer — including after-acquired property. When Sylvester inherited his one-third interest, the lien attached to it automatically.

The lien didn't exceed his share's full market value, but it did substantially encumber it. No title company would insure over an outstanding federal tax lien. The $32,000 had to be resolved before closing.

The key statute here is 26 U.S.C. § 6325(b)(3): Certificate of Discharge. This provision allows the IRS to issue a certificate releasing a specific piece of property from a federal tax lien when the property's value is determined to be inadequate security for the lien — or, more practically, when the IRS negotiates a partial payment arrangement that it determines is an acceptable resolution given the equity available.

## What the Operator Did

Counsel applied for a Certificate of Discharge directly with the IRS. The application documented the property value, the allocation of interests, and the equity available to Sylvester's share after accounting for other closing costs and co-owner distributions.

The negotiation with the IRS centered on the practical question: Sylvester's one-third share produces some equity at closing. The IRS can either take a partial payment now, or it can refuse the discharge, block the sale, and continue pursuing a taxpayer who apparently lacks the resources to pay the full $32,000.

The IRS chose the practical path. They negotiated to receive $18,000 of Sylvester's share at closing. The remaining balance — $14,000 of the original $32,000 lien — was addressed separately in Sylvester's ongoing collection matter. The Certificate of Discharge was issued, releasing this specific property from the federal tax lien.

## The Outcome

- Property sale price: $195,000
- IRS payment from Sylvester's share at closing: $18,000
- Certificate of Discharge: issued and recorded
- Title insurance: clean commitment issued after discharge
- Net to investor after all allocations: $62,000

The sale proceeded with no title encumbrance. The IRS received their negotiated partial payment at the closing table. Sylvester received whatever remained of his share after the IRS allocation.

## The Lesson

The IRS negotiates. This surprises people who assume federal agencies operate rigidly, but the Certificate of Discharge process under § 6325 is designed to accommodate exactly these situations — properties where the equity available to the lienholder is less than the full debt, and where a sale and partial payment serves everyone better than a stalemate.

The IRS's calculus is straightforward: they have a $32,000 lien against a taxpayer who clearly doesn't have $32,000 readily available. The alternative to a discharge is blocking the sale — which means the taxpayer remains in the same financial position and the IRS collects nothing. Eighteen thousand dollars at closing is better than zero, and the IRS knows it.

The application for Certificate of Discharge is a formal process. Counsel handles it — it's not a phone negotiation, it's a documented submission with a property appraisal, an itemized account of interests and allocations, and a specific proposal for what the IRS will receive at closing. The IRS reviews and responds. The timeline is typically weeks, not months, when the deal is straightforward.

For deal underwriting purposes: when you see a federal tax lien on an heir, don't assume it kills the deal. Calculate the equity available to that heir's share. If there's any equity, the IRS has an incentive to negotiate. If the equity is inadequate relative to the lien — which is the specific § 6325(b)(3) standard — the discharge application becomes even stronger, because the statute was written precisely for that circumstance.
