## The Situation

A neighbor was using an 8-foot strip along one edge of the investor's property as if it belonged to him — maintaining it, parking on it, and claiming it as his. The neighbor's verbal claim was that the strip had "always been his" and that the property line was not where the deed said it was. He threatened to contest any sale that included the strip.

Without the strip, the property's dimensions and site utility were reduced. With the strip confirmed, the operator's resale value was $15,000 higher. The stakes were real enough to justify a boundary survey and quiet title litigation if necessary.

## What the Operator Did

The first step was factual, not legal: commission a boundary survey. No title theory, adverse possession analysis, or litigation strategy is worth anything if you don't first establish exactly where the deed boundary is on the ground.

The operator retained a licensed boundary surveyor to run the survey. The surveyor examined the deed descriptions, researched the relevant plat records, and established the physical location of the deed boundaries on the ground. The survey confirmed the 8-foot strip fell entirely within the investor's property as described in the deed. The neighbor's claim was not supported by the physical evidence.

With the survey in hand, the operator analyzed the neighbor's potential legal theories. In Texas, a neighbor who has been using a strip of land for years might assert adverse possession as a defense in quiet title litigation. The two primary adverse possession statutes in Texas relevant to this situation are the 10-year long statute (Texas CPRC § 16.024 — continuous, open, notorious, hostile possession for 10 years) and the 5-year short statute (Texas CPRC § 16.025 — continuous, open, notorious possession under a deed or color of title, with payment of taxes).

The neighbor's best argument was the 5-year short statute, which has a lower time threshold but requires two specific elements: first, that the possessor holds under deed or color of title giving some claim of right to the property; and second, that the possessor has paid all taxes assessed against the property for the entire 5-year period of possession.

The tax payment requirement is a strict statutory element under the 5-year statute — not an optional showing. The operator researched the county tax records and confirmed the neighbor had never paid taxes on the 8-foot strip. The strip was assessed as part of the investor's parcel, and the investor's predecessors had paid taxes on it. The neighbor's 5-year adverse possession claim failed on this element alone.

The 10-year statute doesn't require tax payment, but it does require proof of continuous, open, notorious possession hostile to the true owner for the full 10 years — with nothing interrupting or undercutting that possession. The specifics of this situation didn't support a 10-year claim either, but the failure of the 5-year claim was the cleaner resolution.

The operator filed a quiet title suit naming the neighbor as defendant. The court's analysis confirmed what the survey showed: the strip was within the deed description, the neighbor's adverse possession claim failed, and title was quieted in the investor.

## The Outcome

Boundary confirmed by court order. Quiet title judgment recorded. Property resold at full value including the 8-foot strip. The confirmed boundary produced $15,000 more in sale proceeds than the property would have generated without the strip.

## The Lesson

Boundary disputes require a two-step response: commission a survey, then evaluate the legal claims. The survey comes first because it tells you whether you actually own what you think you own. If the survey confirms your deed description, you have the foundation for a quiet title action. If the survey reveals the boundary is genuinely ambiguous, you have a more complicated legal question — but you need to know that before you file.

The statutory elements of adverse possession are mandatory. The 5-year short statute in Texas requires tax payment as an explicit element — a neighbor who has been occupying a strip of land but never paid taxes on it has no viable 5-year claim. Courts don't overlook missing statutory elements. The adverse possession framework is designed to reward claimants who have behaved like owners in every sense — including paying the government what it's owed on the parcel.

The broader principle: don't let neighbor disputes run you out of a deal. A neighbor making a claim about where the line sits is not evidence that the neighbor is right. Commission the survey, analyze the law, and use quiet title if needed. A court order confirming your boundary is permanent, recorded, and will not resurface when your buyer's title company runs the search.
