## The Situation

Bo Markham broke into a vacant property in mid-2023. He had no relationship to the family, no lease, no permission of any kind. He changed the utilities to his name, made himself comfortable, and began living there as though the property were his. Eighteen months later, when the investor acquired 100% of the property from the actual heirs and came to take possession, Bo had a theory: adverse possession.

The theory was wrong. Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code § 16.024 is unambiguous — adverse possession in Texas requires ten years of peaceable and adverse possession, without interruption, under a claim of right. Eighteen months is not ten years. The math does not change no matter how convincingly Bo claimed the property. He also had a parallel problem under Texas Penal Code § 30.05 — criminal trespass — though the civil remedy through forcible detainer was the operative tool.

Changing the utilities does not create a property interest. Living somewhere without permission does not create a property interest at eighteen months. Nothing Bo did in that period created any cognizable legal claim to the property.

## What the Operator Did

The operator served a 3-day notice to vacate. Bo ignored it. The operator filed a forcible detainer action in JP Court under Texas Property Code Chapter 24. At trial, Bo attempted to raise adverse possession as a defense. The court rightly dismissed the defense as legally frivolous — eighteen months satisfies none of the elements of the ten-year adverse possession statute.

Judgment for possession was granted in the investor's favor. Bo then attempted to appeal the judgment to county court. Appeals of JP Court eviction judgments require the appellant to post a bond equal to double the monthly rent under Texas Property Code § 24.0053. Bo could not post the bond. The appeal was dismissed for failure to post. The writ of possession was executed.

## The Outcome

The property was vacant 41 days from the date the 3-day notice was served. Total cost was $1,800. Bo's appeal attempt was defeated not by additional legal argument but by the straightforward bond requirement — he simply could not pay it.

## The Lesson

Squatters with less than ten years of possession have no adverse possession claim in Texas. Period. The statute says ten years. Eighteen months, three years, five years — none of it qualifies. Do not be intimidated by the claim. Do not negotiate with a squatter as though the claim has merit. Use forcible detainer, not self-help removal — never physically remove someone yourself when a writ of possession gives you the constable to do it lawfully. And know that the double-rent bond requirement under § 24.0053 is a powerful secondary check on frivolous appeals: squatters who cannot afford rent often cannot post a bond equal to twice the monthly rent, and the appeal dies there.
