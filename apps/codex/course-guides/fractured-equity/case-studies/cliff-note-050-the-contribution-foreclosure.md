## The Situation

The investor held 40% of a property as a cotenant alongside three other 20% heirs. The property had accumulated significant delinquency, and the investor stepped in and paid $18,000 in back taxes alone to prevent a tax foreclosure that would have wiped out everyone.

The investor then sent formal contribution demand letters to all three of the other heirs, each demanding reimbursement of their proportionate share of the tax payment. One heir responded and made partial reimbursement. Two heirs ignored the demands entirely.

## What the Operator Did

The investor filed a contribution lawsuit against the two non-responding heirs. Texas common law gives every cotenant who pays more than their share of carrying costs the right to recover that excess from the other cotenants. Texas CPRC § 31.001 governs execution on judgments; Texas Property Code § 52.004 covers judgment liens on real property.

Both non-responding heirs defaulted — no answer filed, no appearance. Default judgments entered. The investor obtained a writ of execution and directed the sheriff to levy on each defaulting heir's 20% interest in the property.

The sheriff scheduled a public auction of both 20% interests. The investor showed up. No competing bidders appeared. The investor bid $1,200 per interest — nominal amounts that satisfied the judgment liens.

## The Outcome

The investor acquired both sheriff's-sale interests at $1,200 each ($2,400 total). Combined with the original 40% ownership, the investor now held 80% of the property. The one cooperative heir who had made partial reimbursement retained her 20% interest.

From 80% control, the investor negotiated a clean sale of the property for $165,000. The cooperative 20% heir received her proportionate share of the sale proceeds.

The contribution mechanic didn't just recover the tax payment — it turned two defaulting heirs' non-response into a consolidation mechanism that more than doubled the investor's ownership stake.

## The Lesson

Contribution-to-foreclosure is a core Fractured Equity mechanic. When cotenants refuse to reimburse shared carrying costs, the legal system provides a structured path to acquire their interests at nominal prices through the sheriff's sale process.

The key sequence: pay the taxes or other shared costs, document everything, send formal demand letters, file the contribution lawsuit when demands are ignored, obtain default judgments when heirs don't respond, and bid at the sheriff's sale. Nominal bids of $500 to $2,000 per interest are realistic when no competing bidders exist.

This is not aggression — it's the natural consequence the law attaches to non-payment of legitimate shared obligations. Heirs who refuse to pay their share of carrying costs effectively choose the outcome that follows.
