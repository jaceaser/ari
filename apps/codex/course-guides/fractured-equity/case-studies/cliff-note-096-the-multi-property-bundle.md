## The Situation

One estate, five properties, seven heirs, and one heir who was very motivated to exit quickly. That combination of facts created a strategic opportunity that the investor recognized immediately: the motivated heir held 25% of every property in the portfolio, and the investor could acquire all five positions in one transaction.

The five properties ranged from a residential house to a vacant rural acreage parcel. Each had its own tax situation, its own physical condition, and its own set of heir dynamics. No single property was simple. But one heir was willing to sell all of his interests at once for a price that reflected his urgency.

## What the Operator Did

**Step One: The Bundle Transaction**

The investor structured the acquisition of the aggressive heir's 25% interest in all five properties as a single bundled transaction. The economic logic worked for both parties:

From the heir's perspective: one closing, one set of documents, one check, done. No need to negotiate five separate transactions with potentially different buyers at different times. The heir valued certainty and speed above maximizing per-property proceeds.

From the investor's perspective: immediate foothold in all five properties simultaneously, with the ability to leverage that foothold in every subsequent negotiation. The bundle price of $142,000 reflected both the fractional-interest discount (minority positions in contested properties trade below proportionate value) and a bundle discount for simplicity.

The transaction was documented with one Purchase and Sale Agreement, one closing package, and five separate deeds — one for each property. Each deed conveyed the heir's 25% interest in the identified property. The investor left the closing owning 25% of five properties simultaneously.

**Step Two: Foothold Leverage in Subsequent Negotiations**

The 25% foothold across all five properties changed the investor's position in every subsequent conversation with every other heir about every property. The investor was no longer a stranger approaching heirs about a property the heirs owned — the investor was a co-owner with legal standing, contribution rights, and partition rights in every property in the portfolio.

This changed negotiation dynamics in several ways:

First, heirs who were undecided about selling now faced the reality that they were co-owners with an investor who would eventually need to do something with the properties. Selling to the investor was one option; being dragged through contribution or partition proceedings was another. The investor's presence as a co-owner made the "do nothing" option less comfortable.

Second, the investor could intelligently prioritize which properties to pursue first. Properties with more cooperative remaining heirs, better economics, or more pressing tax situations were prioritized. The investor could sequence the portfolio based on where the best deals were available rather than being forced to work all five simultaneously.

Third, tax payments made by the investor on any property generated contribution rights against the non-paying heirs — an additional tool to add to the toolkit as needed.

**Step Three: Property-by-Property Sequential Acquisition**

The investor worked through each of the five properties on its own timeline:

- Properties with cooperative remaining heirs were resolved through straightforward direct purchase negotiations
- Properties with resistant heirs required contribution demands, partition threat letters, and in one case an actual partition petition
- Each property was closed when the conditions for acceptable economics and title clearance aligned

Total acquisitions across all five properties completed at various points over the 18-month period.

## The Outcome

The 18-month total:

- Bundle acquisition: $142,000 (the aggressive heir's 25% across all five properties)
- Subsequent acquisitions from other heirs across all five properties: approximately $173,000
- Total acquisition cost: approximately $315,000
- Legal fees, carrying costs, and transaction expenses across the portfolio: included in total cost analysis
- Gross sale proceeds from all five properties: approximately $895,000
- Net profit after all costs: approximately $392,000

Individual properties closed at different points in the 18-month window. Some properties sold within 4 months of the investor achieving full consolidation; others required more time to position and market. The portfolio approach allowed the investor to maintain cash flow across the project rather than having all capital tied up in a single extended transaction.

## The Lesson

The bundle acquisition from a single willing heir is one of the most efficient entry strategies in fractured-equity work. When one heir holds a meaningful percentage across a multi-property estate and wants to exit, the investor who executes the bundle correctly gains simultaneous foothold in every property for a single combined transaction cost.

The bundle premium paid to the willing heir — accepting less per-property to get simplicity and certainty — is the investor's cost of simultaneous access. It is almost always worth it. The alternative is approaching each property separately, which takes longer, costs more in attorney fees and due diligence per property, and allows other heirs to see what is happening and adjust their negotiating positions.

The foothold creates leverage. A 25% position in a contested property is not control — but it is standing. Standing means contribution rights, partition rights, and the legal status of a co-owner who is not going away. That status changes every heir's calculation about whether to cooperate or resist.

Sequencing matters after the bundle is acquired. Work the easiest properties first to generate cash flow and momentum. Use the experience and relationships built on the easier properties to inform your approach on the harder ones. The portfolio is a series of individual deals — execute them in the order that optimizes the overall outcome.
