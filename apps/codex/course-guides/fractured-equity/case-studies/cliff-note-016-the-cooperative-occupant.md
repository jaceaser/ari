## The Situation

Bernadette Cashman was living in her deceased mother's house. She owned a 15% fractional interest in the property — one of five heirs. She had recently gone through a divorce and was raising a toddler on modest income. She wanted to sell and move on, but she did not have the money to front a security deposit and moving costs at a new apartment.

She was not hostile. She was not a problem. She was a person in a financially constrained situation who needed the transaction structured in a way that solved her immediate practical problems, not just her ownership question.

## What the Operator Did

The operator recognized that two separate problems needed two separate instruments.

Problem one: the ownership interest. Bernadette held 15% of the property by inheritance. That needed to be acquired through a deed conveying her fractional ownership to the investor.

Problem two: the physical occupancy. Bernadette was living there with a child. Even after she signed over her 15%, she would still be a resident with a vacate obligation. The deed conveyance alone does not produce a vacant property.

Texas Property Code Chapter 24 governs tenancy-at-sufferance and cotenant ouster frameworks. For a hostile or uncooperative occupant, Chapter 24 eviction is the mechanism. For a cooperative occupant, a consensual Occupant Relocation Agreement supersedes the statutory eviction path and produces a faster, cleaner result.

The operator executed two instruments separately:

One — the Heir-to-Investor Deed (Template 3): Bernadette conveyed her 15% ownership interest to the investor for $6,500. This was the ownership transaction.

Two — the Occupant Relocation Agreement (Template 19): $4,000 in relocation assistance with a 45-day vacate window. Additionally, the investor's escrow paid Bernadette's first month's rent and security deposit at the new apartment she identified. This was the occupancy transaction.

The two instruments addressed two distinct legal relationships. Keeping them separate matters: the deed conveys ownership, the relocation agreement conveys the right to unobstructed possession. Conflating them in one document creates ambiguity about what was purchased.

Bernadette moved within 38 days of signing. She left on friendly terms.

## The Outcome

Bernadette received $10,500 total: $6,500 for her 15% ownership interest plus $4,000 in relocation assistance. First month's rent and deposit at her new apartment were covered by escrow. She vacated in 38 days. The investor acquired the 15% and eliminated the occupancy obstacle. The remaining heirs' acquisitions then proceeded smoothly with the property vacant.

## The Lesson

Cooperative occupants want to leave but need practical help — deposits, moving costs, help navigating rental applications. The job is not to negotiate them out; the job is to solve their practical problems so they can do what they already want to do. Structure the relocation agreement as concrete assistance. Keep the ownership conveyance and the occupancy agreement as separate documents. The combination resolves both the title problem and the operations problem in one coordinated transaction — and leaves the occupant with a positive experience that supports rather than undermines the rest of the acquisition.
