## The Situation

The decedent had left a will that disinherited one child — presumably intentionally, for reasons the family understood even if the disinherited child refused to accept them. After the will was admitted to probate, the disinherited child filed a will contest under Texas Estates Code Chapter 256, claiming the will was the product of undue influence by the siblings who benefited from it.

The contest had been pending for two years. The property — listed as an estate asset — couldn't be sold while the contest was active because the title was effectively clouded. Any buyer would need to wait for the contest to resolve, and the outcome of litigation is never certain.

The disinherited child had some facts in her favor: the decedent had been elderly, somewhat isolated in the final years, and the favored children had been deeply involved in managing affairs. The undue influence theory wasn't frivolous. It just wasn't strong.

## What the Operator Did

The investor's counsel analyzed both sides of the contest: the strength of the undue influence claim, the quality of the evidence, the likelihood of success at trial, and the cost and time to get there. The assessment: the contest was weakly supported. The disinherited child had circumstantial evidence — proximity and involvement by the beneficiaries — but lacked direct evidence of manipulation that overcame the decedent's presumed testamentary capacity.

That analysis informed the settlement structure. The investor approached all parties with a global resolution: the will would prevail (the beneficiaries received their inheritance per the will), and the disinherited child would be paid $25,000 in exchange for releasing the contest. The investor would fund the $25,000 settlement as part of the acquisition transaction.

The mechanics: the investor paid $38,000 to acquire the property from the will-beneficiaries, and separately funded the $25,000 settlement to the contesting child. The settlement was documented, signed, and filed with the probate court. The will was admitted without further challenge.

## The Outcome

Total investor cost: $63,000 ($38,000 acquisition plus $25,000 settlement). Sale price: $175,000. The administration closed after settlement. Clear title issued.

## The Lesson

Will contests are often weaker than they appear — and more importantly, they're often more costly to litigate than to settle. The contesting heir is usually fighting for a number, not a principle. Find that number.

The investor's job is not to win the contest — it's to resolve it. Analyze the contest the way a litigator would: what's the realistic outcome if this goes to trial, what's the timeline, what are the costs on both sides? A settlement that gives the contesting heir something real in exchange for releasing the cloud often costs far less than the litigation would.

Factor the settlement cost into acquisition math from the beginning. In this case, $25,000 was the price of a clean title. Without that investment, the property sat in limbo indefinitely. With it, the deal closed and generated the margin that made the whole exercise worthwhile.
