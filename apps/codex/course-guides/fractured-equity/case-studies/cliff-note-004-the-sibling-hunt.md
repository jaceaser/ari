## The Situation

Xavier and Rosalind Haverford were married for 40 years. They bought a house together in 1984. Neither had children. Neither left a will. Xavier's side of the family had two living siblings and three dead siblings, one of whom had left children behind. Rosalind's side had one living brother.

Rosalind died first. Xavier died later. No probate was ever opened for either death.

When the operator arrived, both families had opinions about who owned what. Rosalind's brother assumed he had a claim. Xavier's family was not unified. The property sat in limbo.

## What the Operator Did

The legal analysis required two steps, applied in the correct sequence.

Step one: Rosalind died first, married, with community property and no descendants. Under Texas Estates Code § 201.002, when a spouse dies intestate with community property and no descendants, the surviving spouse takes 100% of the community estate. Xavier got everything at Rosalind's death. Her family gets nothing at that point.

Step two: Xavier then died without a spouse and without descendants. Under § 201.001, his estate passes to his parents first — both dead — then to his siblings per stirpes under the collateral heir rules. Two living brothers take their shares directly. The predeceased sister's three children — the nieces and nephews — take their mother's share per stirpes.

Rosalind's brother is excluded. The AOH documented this explicitly with a recital: her share passed to Xavier at her death, and her family has no intestate claim on Xavier's estate because they are not related to Xavier by blood.

The operator acquired five separate Xavier-side interests at varied pricing: $2,500 to $8,000 each, depending on individual financial circumstances. Total: $26,000 for 100%.

Rosalind's brother was initially angry. He was educated through the AOH documentation and his own counsel confirmed he had no claim.

## The Outcome

The investor acquired 100% for $26,000 total. Five Xavier-side heirs were bought out at prices ranging from $2,500 to $8,000. Rosalind's brother was educated and excluded.

## The Lesson

When both spouses die with no kids, everything runs through the LATER-dying spouse's family line. The earlier-dying spouse's family takes nothing because their share fully transferred to the survivor at the first death. This is counterintuitive to most families. Document it in the AOH explicitly — a written legal recital that the excluded family member can show their own attorney is far more effective than a verbal explanation at the kitchen table.
