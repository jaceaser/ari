## The Situation

The last recorded deed on this property was dated 1952. The family had been in the house for 72 years — three generations — with nothing in the county deed records to connect the 1952 grantor to anyone currently living there. No intervening deeds. No probates. No affidavits of heirship. Just a visible gap starting 72 years ago and a family who had simply stayed.

This is a chain-of-title gap, not a fractured ownership problem. The ownership record doesn't follow what actually happened on the ground. Somewhere between 1952 and today, the property passed through death, informal family arrangement, or undocumented conveyance — and none of it got recorded.

The property could not be sold with a conventional title commitment. Any title company looking at a 1952 last-recorded-deed was going to decline coverage. The gap had to be cured by litigation.

## What the Operator Did

The legal remedy for a gap this deep is quiet title through adverse possession. Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code § 16.024 creates a 10-year adverse possession statute — a person in continuous, open, notorious, and hostile possession for 10 years can ripen a title claim against any competing interest.

The key tool for a multi-generational gap is tacking. Adverse possession doesn't require that one single person hold the property for 10 consecutive years. Successive occupants can tack their periods of possession together, as long as there is privity between them — typically established by family relationship, inheritance, or informal transfer. Here, the family lineage created privity across three generations of occupants. The combined possession: 72 years.

The operator filed a quiet title action naming all possible collateral claimants as defendants. That sweep included the heirs of the 1952 grantor who might still hold record title by descent, any unknown parties who might claim through the break in the chain, and anyone else who could surface with a competing claim. Proper service was accomplished on all named defendants. A guardian ad litem was appointed for any unknown or unlocatable parties.

The case went to a bench trial. The evidence of 72 years of continuous family occupancy — utility records, tax receipts, neighbor testimony, photographs, and family testimony tracing the generational handoffs — was overwhelming against a 10-year threshold. The court confirmed that adverse possession had ripened, quieted title in the current family, and issued a judgment that was immediately recorded.

## The Outcome

A quiet title judgment by court order replaced the broken chain. The judgment recorded in the county deed records as the new authoritative statement of ownership. With a recorded judgment in hand, the title company could issue a commitment. The operator acquired the property and completed the sale.

No dollar figures were preserved in the cliff note for this case. The mechanism is what matters.

## The Lesson

When the chain is broken this far back, don't try to paper over the gap with affidavits. A 72-year gap is not a curative affidavit situation. It requires a quiet title action with adverse possession, properly pled and properly proved.

The family tacking theory is the engine that makes these cases work. Every generation that held the property openly, continuously, and without permission from the paper-title holder adds to the adverse possession clock. By the time you've got three generations in place, you've got evidence that is nearly impossible to contest. The 10-year threshold exists on paper; the family has been there for 72 years. That's not a close call in front of a judge.

The discipline is naming all potential defendants. A quiet title judgment only binds the people who were served. An incomplete defendant list leaves holes in the chain that title companies will find. Cast wide, serve everyone you can locate, appoint a guardian ad litem for the unknowns, and get the judgment recorded. Done correctly, court order replaces a broken chain permanently.
