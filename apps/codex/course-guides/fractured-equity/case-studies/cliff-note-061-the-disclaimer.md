## The Situation

Rhonda was one of the heirs to the estate — her share of the property was a meaningful asset. The problem was that Rhonda had significant IRS liens outstanding against her. If Rhonda accepted the inheritance, those liens would attach immediately to her newly acquired property interest, and the IRS could potentially reach the asset.

Rhonda had a solution: disclaim. Under Texas Estates Code Chapter 122, an heir can file a qualified disclaimer of an inheritance, treating herself as having predeceased the decedent for purposes of distribution. The disclaimed share then passes to whoever would have inherited it if Rhonda had died before the decedent — in this case, her two children.

The critical requirement: the disclaimer must be filed within nine months of the decedent's date of death. Miss that window and the disclaimer is ineffective — the inheritance is treated as having been accepted.

## What the Operator Did

Rhonda filed a qualified disclaimer within the 9-month window. The disclaimer was formal, in writing, and complied with the requirements of Texas Estates Code Chapter 122 and the federal qualified disclaimer rules under Internal Revenue Code § 2518 (which governs whether a disclaimer is effective for gift tax and creditor purposes under federal law).

With the effective disclaimer filed, Rhonda's share of the property passed as if she had predeceased the decedent. Her two children — the next takers in the line of succession — each received a 12.5% interest in the property.

The investor then negotiated directly with the two children. They were cooperative, understood the situation, and sold their interests at Fractured Equity discounts. The IRS liens that burdened Rhonda could not reach the inheritance because Rhonda had never legally accepted it — she was treated as having predeceased, and what you never own cannot be seized to satisfy your debts.

## The Outcome

The investor acquired both children's 12.5% interests and completed full consolidation of the property. Rhonda avoided creditor exposure on these specific assets. The children received consideration for interests they had unexpectedly inherited.

## The Lesson

The disclaimer is a powerful tool in the fractured equity context — not for tax planning (though it has tax applications), but for estate distribution mechanics. Heirs with creditor problems often want to get assets to the next generation without those assets passing through the creditor-exposed layer.

Two things to know:

First, the nine-month window is absolute. If an heir is considering a disclaimer, identify the death date, calculate the deadline, and act. There is no extension, no exception for ignorance, no equitable relief from a missed window.

Second, the heir cannot have accepted the inheritance — even informally — before disclaiming. If Rhonda had taken any action inconsistent with an intent to disclaim (accepting a rent check from the property, authorizing any transaction, directing anything), the disclaimer may be ineffective.

When you encounter an heir with creditor issues, ask the disclaimer question early. It may redirect the interest to motivated next-generation sellers who are easier to work with.
