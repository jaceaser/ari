## The Situation

A hostile 20% heir refused a $5,000 buyout offer. She had no particular plan for the property — no desire to live there, no plan to manage it — but she wasn't going to accept what she considered an insult. The investor had already acquired the other 80% of the property and needed this final interest to complete consolidation.

The investor filed a partition action under Texas Property Code Chapter 23. Every cotenant has an absolute right to compel partition — that's not a threat, it's the law. The question was whether the heir understood what partition actually meant for her net proceeds.

## What the Operator Did

The Partition Demand Letter did the work. The letter didn't just cite the legal right — it ran the specific numbers that her attorney would eventually run anyway:

- 20% of $155,000 gross property value = $31,000 gross heir share
- Commissioner-supervised auction discount to approximately 75% of fair market value
- $31,000 × 75% = $23,250 commissioner-sale proceeds to the heir
- Less $1,200 allocated legal fee share
- Less the heir's own attorney's fees to fight through the process
- **Net to heir through partition: approximately $3,200**

Alternative offered: **$4,500 now**, no attorneys, no waiting six months, no auction uncertainty.

The investor served this letter and waited. The heir's counsel received it, ran their own version of the same math, and reached the same conclusion.

## The Outcome

The heir settled at $4,500 within two weeks of receiving the demand letter. The partition petition was dismissed. Full consolidation achieved.

The critical numbers: commissioner-supervised sales in Texas typically clear 70–80% of fair market value after fees. The 75% discount assumption in the demand letter was not aggressive — it was realistic, which is exactly what made it persuasive.

## The Lesson

Run the partition math for the holdout before you file. Most holdouts refuse buyout offers not because the offers are truly insufficient — but because they don't understand what the alternative actually yields. They imagine partition as some kind of auction where they walk away with market value. They don't understand commissioner fees, auction discounts, and the six-month timeline of uncertainty.

A Partition Demand Letter with real numbers changes the conversation. You're not threatening — you're educating. The heir's own attorney will verify your math, and when they do, they'll give the same advice: take the money.

The $4,500 settlement was $500 below the original $5,000 offer the heir had refused. The partition filing scared her into taking less than she was offered to begin with.
