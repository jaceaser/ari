## The Situation

Months of work had produced a dominant ownership position: 95% of the property, consolidated through a combination of direct purchases from cooperative heirs and contribution procedures against non-paying ones. Four great-grandchildren of the original decedent held the remaining 5% collectively — each holding approximately 1.25% of the property.

The investor had made repeated buyout offers. The most recent was $600 per person for each 1.25% interest — a price that reflected the actual economic value of a tiny fractional interest in a property that could not be sold without the investor's cooperation. The four great-grandchildren refused. They refused not because $600 was demonstrably inadequate — it is very difficult to argue that $600 is inadequate for a 1.25% fractional interest that cannot independently be monetized — but because they had decided on principle that they were not going to cooperate with the investor.

This is the classic holdout scenario in its most extreme form: four small-fraction owners refusing a rational offer based on emotion rather than economics. The investor had one remaining tool.

## What the Operator Did

The investor filed a partition action under Texas Property Code Chapter 23. This is not a discretionary legal maneuver — it is an absolute statutory right of any cotenant. No court can deny a cotenant the right to partition. The question is only whether partition occurs by division of the property (partition in kind, which is almost never available for improved residential property because physical division is impractical) or by sale (partition by sale, which is the default for single residential properties).

The petition was filed in the district court for the county where the property was located. The four great-grandchildren were served through standard legal process. All four defaulted — they did not retain counsel, did not file a response, did not appear at any hearing. This is typical of small-fraction holdouts who refuse on principle but do not actually have the resources or commitment to litigate.

The court entered default against the four defendants and proceeded with the partition. Commissioners were appointed to supervise the sale, as required by the Texas partition statutes. The commissioners set the sale terms, advertised the property, and conducted the auction.

The property sold at the commissioner-supervised auction. Proceeds were distributed according to ownership percentages: 95% to the investor, 5% divided among the four great-grandchildren.

## The Outcome

Each of the four great-grandchildren received approximately $1,400 from the commissioner-supervised sale — more than double the $600 pre-partition offers they had refused. This outcome is not uncommon in commissioner-supervised auctions: competitive bidding sometimes pushes the auction price above the 70–80% of market value that partition demand letters typically cite as the commissioner-sale discount. In this case, competitive bidding produced a result closer to market value, which translated into larger holdout proceeds than anticipated.

The investor consolidated 100% through the partition process, completed the sale, and captured the full value of the consolidation strategy. The additional months of delay and legal expense from the partition process reduced but did not eliminate the investor's profit.

The four holdouts' irony was complete: they received more money than they were offered pre-partition, but they also caused themselves months of uncertainty and legal proceedings that a simple agreement would have avoided.

## The Lesson

Partition always works. The absolute statutory right of any cotenant to compel partition means that no holdout strategy can ultimately block the sale of a property when the other cotenants want to sell. The only questions are timing and cost.

The lesson here is actually bilateral. For the investor: partition is the backstop that makes the entire fractured-equity strategy viable. When cooperation fails, partition forces a resolution. The investor with 95% has overwhelming leverage — but even the investor with 30% can file partition and ultimately force a sale.

For holdouts: refusing reasonable pre-partition offers is almost always a bad economic decision. The commissioner auction does not guarantee market value — it guarantees some value, in a process the holdouts cannot control and did not choose. In this case, the holdouts happened to receive more than the offered $600. That is not typical. More often, commissioner sales produce results below the pre-partition offer, because the auction process, commissioner fees, and legal costs all reduce the net proceeds available for distribution.

The takeaway for operators is this: partition your way to resolution when cooperation fails. Price your pre-partition offers honestly. And never let a small fraction of hold-out percentage stop a deal that the economics support.
