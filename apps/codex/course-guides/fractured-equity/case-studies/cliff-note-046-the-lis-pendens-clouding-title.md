## The Situation

A lis pendens — a notice of pending litigation — had been filed in the real property records against the property the investor was attempting to acquire from the heirs. The filer was a would-be buyer who claimed he had entered an oral contract with one of the heirs to purchase the property and alleged breach of that oral agreement.

Texas Property Code § 12.007 authorizes the filing of a lis pendens in the real property records when litigation affecting title to real property is pending. The effect is significant: a lis pendens clouds title, and no title company will insure over an outstanding lis pendens. The investor could not close on the heirs' interests until the lis pendens was resolved.

The problem with the would-be buyer's claim was fundamental: Texas follows the Statute of Frauds for real property transactions. Texas Business and Commerce Code § 26.01 requires contracts for the sale of real estate to be in writing and signed by the party to be charged. An oral agreement to purchase real property is unenforceable as a matter of law in Texas. The would-be buyer had no written contract — he had a verbal conversation with an heir, which entitled him to nothing.

A lis pendens filed in support of a lawsuit claiming rights under an unenforceable oral contract is an improper lis pendens. Texas CPRC § 11.001 et seq. requires a proper purpose for the filing.

## What the Operator Did

Counsel filed a Motion to Cancel the Lis Pendens in the court where the underlying lawsuit was pending. The grounds were straightforward and legally decisive:

First: the alleged contract was oral. No written agreement existed. An oral contract for the sale of real property is unenforceable under the Texas Statute of Frauds — it creates no enforceable legal rights in the property.

Second: because the underlying claim was legally invalid, the lis pendens had been filed without a proper purpose. Texas CPRC § 11.001 et seq. authorizes courts to cancel improperly filed lis pendens instruments.

The hearing was scheduled 30 days after filing. The court heard argument and granted the motion. The lis pendens was ordered canceled.

## The Outcome

- Lis pendens: canceled by court order
- Would-be buyer's claim against the property: defeated — no enforceable contract
- Would-be buyer's remaining options: personal damages claim against the heir (if any) for the broken oral promise — not an interest in the property
- Investor closing: resumed and completed

## The Lesson

Improper lis pendens can be attacked and removed. The lis pendens is not the end of the deal — it's an obstacle with a procedural solution. The moment a lis pendens appears in the real property records, the response is to pull the underlying lawsuit, evaluate the legal basis for the claim, and determine whether a Motion to Cancel is appropriate.

The Statute of Frauds defeats most lis pendens filed on the basis of informal real property agreements. Texas has clear and long-standing authority that oral contracts for real property sales are unenforceable. A would-be buyer who had a handshake deal with an heir has a personal grievance — not a property right. Without a property right, there is no proper basis for a lis pendens, and the courts cancel them when presented with the motion.

The 30-day hearing timeline is fast by litigation standards. A Motion to Cancel a lis pendens is not a complex proceeding — it's a targeted motion on a discrete legal question, and courts understand that improperly clouded title causes immediate harm to parties trying to close transactions. Courts move these motions to hearing promptly.

The lesson for would-be buyers who file lis pendens on weak claims: it doesn't hold the property hostage for long, and it may expose the filer to sanctions and attorney's fees for the improper filing. The lesson for operators: a lis pendens in the records is not a show-stopper — evaluate it, attack it if the underlying claim is weak, and move through it.
