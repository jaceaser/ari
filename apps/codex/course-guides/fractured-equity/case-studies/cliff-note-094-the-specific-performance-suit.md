## The Situation

The deal had been moving on track. The heir had signed the Purchase and Sale Agreement. The price was $8,500 for her fractional interest. Closing was scheduled. Title work had been ordered, completed, and reviewed. The closing package was being prepared.

Three days before the closing date, the heir called and said she was not going to sign. She had changed her mind. She wanted to keep the interest. That was it — no negotiation, no counter-proposal, no explanation beyond "I've decided not to sell."

The PSA was a valid, signed contract for the sale of a real property interest. In Texas, as in most states, a party to a valid real property contract does not have a unilateral right to back out simply by saying so. The operator had rights. The question was how to enforce them quickly and efficiently.

## What the Operator Did

The operator's response unfolded in two stages: the demand letter and the legal mechanism.

**Stage One: The Specific Performance Demand Letter**

The operator had counsel send a Specific Performance Demand Letter within 24 hours of the heir's call. The letter cited:

- The signed PSA by date, parties, and agreed purchase price
- The heir's breach — her stated refusal to close as agreed
- Texas law on specific performance of real property contracts: because each parcel of real property is legally unique, courts in Texas enforce real property contracts through specific performance orders requiring actual performance, not just monetary damages
- The operator's election to pursue specific performance rather than accepting the breach and seeking only monetary damages
- A final deadline for the heir to confirm her intent to close at the agreed price and time

The letter was intended to give the heir and her attorney — if she retained one — a clear statement of the legal consequences before the lawsuit was filed. Many heirs who back out of signed PSAs do so without understanding that a signed real property contract is not a casual commitment they can walk away from by changing their mind.

No response was received within the deadline.

**Stage Two: Lawsuit and Lis Pendens**

The operator filed a specific performance suit in the district court for the county where the property was located. The petition sought an order requiring the heir to specifically perform the PSA — to actually sign and deliver the deed — along with attorney fees and costs as permitted by the contract.

Simultaneously, the operator filed a lis pendens in the real property records of the county. A lis pendens (Latin: "suit pending") is a notice recorded in the public land records that the property described is the subject of pending litigation affecting title or the right to possession. Its practical effect is decisive: any title company asked to insure a sale or mortgage involving that property will see the lis pendens and refuse to issue a policy until the litigation is resolved. The heir's interest was frozen. She could not sell it to anyone. She could not mortgage it. She could not do anything with it that required title insurance while the lawsuit was pending.

The lis pendens was the instrument that converted the heir's theoretical right to breach the contract into an actual problem she could not ignore.

## The Outcome

Within days of the lis pendens being recorded, the heir's newly retained attorney called the operator's counsel. The conversation was brief: the heir's attorney acknowledged the lis pendens, acknowledged the viable specific performance claim, and opened settlement discussions.

The settlement reached was straightforward: the heir would close at the original $8,500 purchase price, and the operator would pay an additional $1,200 to the heir's attorney as a contribution to the heir's legal fees incurred as a result of the breach situation. This was a practical resolution — the operator's exposure to litigation costs exceeded $1,200, and the deal got done at essentially the original terms.

The closing occurred. The lis pendens was released simultaneously with the recording of the deed. The deal that had almost fallen apart three days before closing ultimately closed at the terms the parties had originally agreed upon.

## The Lesson

Specific performance is the right tool when an heir breaches a signed real property contract. Two legal principles combine to make it powerful:

**The Uniqueness Doctrine:** Texas law treats each parcel of real property as unique — legally distinct from every other parcel in a way that makes monetary damages an inadequate remedy for breach of a real property contract. This is not a modern innovation; it is an old equity doctrine embedded in Texas law. Because real property is unique, courts have authority to order specific performance — to order the breaching party to actually sign the deed — rather than simply awarding damages that the operator would use to find substitute property.

**The Lis Pendens:** The lis pendens is the mechanism that gives specific performance its practical teeth. Filing the lawsuit creates the right to record the lis pendens. Recording the lis pendens freezes the property in the public record. A frozen property cannot be sold, mortgaged, or otherwise transferred without resolving the pending litigation. The heir who backed out to sell to someone else finds that she cannot — the lis pendens blocks any other closing. The heir who simply changed her mind finds that she cannot do anything else with the property until she resolves the lawsuit, which means either defending a specific performance suit she will likely lose, or settling on the plaintiff's terms.

Most specific performance cases settle quickly. The legal and practical exposure to the breaching party is clear, the remedy is available as a matter of law, and the lis pendens creates immediate practical pressure. Settlement terms typically hover around the original deal terms plus attorney fee contributions — cheaper than litigation to conclusion for both parties, and the deal closes either way.

The $1,200 in attorney fees paid above the original $8,500 purchase price was not a defeat — it was the cost of enforcing a contract that the heir had signed and then tried to walk away from. The deal got done.
