Missing heirs are the most significant title risk in fractured equity work. An heir who doesn't know they exist as a legal owner of a property, or who can't be found, still has a legal ownership interest. If they surface after you've acquired and resold the property, they can assert that claim — potentially unraveling the transaction.

The goal of missing heir research is not to find a perfect solution — sometimes that's not possible. The goal is to take reasonable steps to identify and locate all potential heirs, document those steps rigorously, and structure the acquisition in a way that manages the residual risk from heirs you couldn't locate.

## The Research Stack

Finding missing heirs requires layering multiple research approaches:

**Genealogy databases** — Ancestry.com, FamilySearch, MyHeritage, and similar platforms contain billions of historical records: birth certificates, death certificates, marriage records, census data, military records. These are your primary tools for reconstructing the decedent's family tree and identifying all descendants.

**Public records** — County deed records, probate filings, court records, and voter registration data can identify heirs and their current addresses. If an heir inherited property in another county, there may be a recorded instrument showing their address at the time.

**Obituaries** — The decedent's obituary and the obituaries of any deceased heirs often list surviving family members by name. Local newspaper archives and funeral home websites are searchable.

**Social media** — LinkedIn, Facebook, and other platforms can locate living heirs. Searching the heir's name combined with their last known location often surfaces current contact information.

## When You Can't Find Them

When a thorough search fails to locate a potential heir, the legal system provides a backstop: service by publication. This is the process of publishing notice of a pending lawsuit in a local newspaper for a statutory period, creating a legal fiction that the unknown heir has been notified.

Service by publication, done properly and documented carefully, allows a court to proceed with a quiet title action even when some potential heirs couldn't be personally served. The resulting judgment binds those heirs — meaning they can't later come back and claim the property without setting aside the judgment on procedural grounds.

The strength of your publication service depends entirely on how thoroughly you documented the search that preceded it. Courts look at whether you made genuine, diligent efforts to find the missing heirs before resorting to publication. Document everything.
