When a property owner dies without a will, and no one ever opens a formal probate, the legal ownership of their property is in limbo. The deed still shows the deceased person as the owner. The title company won't insure it. A buyer can't get a mortgage on it. The property sits — sometimes for decades — because nobody took the steps to formally transfer ownership to the heirs.

The affidavit of heirship is the most commonly used tool to resolve this problem without the time and expense of a full probate proceeding. It's a sworn statement, signed by two disinterested witnesses, that recites the essential facts about the decedent's life that determine who their legal heirs are: their name and date of death, their marital history, their children and whether any predeceased them, and the heirs' relationship to the decedent.

## How It Works

The affidavit is not filed in court. It's recorded in the county property records — the same deed records where deeds and liens are recorded. Once it's on file, it puts the world on notice of who the heirs are and gives the heirs a recorded basis for their ownership claim.

The two witnesses must be disinterested — meaning they have no financial stake in the property and are not heirs themselves. Neighbors, clergy, longtime family friends, and coworkers are common choices. They must have personal knowledge of the facts they're attesting to: they actually knew the decedent, knew the family, can speak to the marital history and children from personal knowledge.

## The Four-Year Rule in Texas

In Texas, an affidavit of heirship recorded for more than four years creates a presumption that the facts stated in it are correct. That presumption is the basis on which a title company will insure the property. Before four years, most title companies won't insure based on the affidavit alone — they'll require additional evidence. After four years, it becomes a reliable foundation for title insurance.

This timing creates an interesting strategy: record the affidavit as soon as you identify the property, even if you're years away from closing. Let the four-year clock start running while you work the deal.
