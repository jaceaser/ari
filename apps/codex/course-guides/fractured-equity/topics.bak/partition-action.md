A partition action is the legal proceeding that resolves the fundamental problem of co-ownership: what happens when the co-owners can't agree on what to do with the property?

The Texas Property Code is explicit about the right: "A joint owner or claimant of real property or an interest in real property or a joint owner of personal property may compel a partition of the interest or the property among the joint owners or claimants." This right is nearly absolute. It doesn't matter if the other co-owners oppose the partition. It doesn't matter if they have a larger ownership percentage. Any co-owner can compel a partition.

## Partition in Kind vs. Partition by Sale

When a partition action is filed, the court has two tools at its disposal:

**Partition in kind** — The court physically divides the property among the co-owners. Each owner receives a specific, legally described parcel of land corresponding to their ownership percentage. If you own 25% of a 40-acre tract, you might receive 10 acres. This is the preferred form of partition when the property is large, undeveloped land that can be divided without destroying its value.

**Partition by sale** — The court orders the property sold as a whole, and the proceeds are divided among the co-owners according to their ownership percentages. This is what happens with most residential and commercial properties, where a physical division would destroy the value of the asset. A single-family house cannot be meaningfully divided into four pieces — it gets sold, and the proceeds get split.

Partition by sale is almost always the result in fractured equity deals. The properties we work with are predominantly residential or small commercial — structures that cannot be divided. The court appoints commissioners to oversee the sale, which is typically conducted as a public auction.

## The Operator's Advantage at the Partition Sale

Here's the part most people miss: you can bid at the partition sale. As the operator who filed the action, you know the property better than anyone else at the auction. You've done the title research, you've inspected the property, you've built a relationship with at least some of the co-owners. Other bidders are walking in cold.

More importantly, you can credit your contribution claims and court costs against your bid. If other co-owners owe you $15,000 in contribution reimbursements that were awarded by the court, that $15,000 offsets what you need to pay at the auction. Your effective acquisition cost is lower than any competitor who has to bid with clean cash.

The partition action often prompts negotiated resolution before trial. Many co-owners who refused to discuss a sale become cooperative once they realize the alternative is a court-ordered auction where they lose control of the outcome. The filing itself creates leverage that often gets you to a negotiated buyout at better terms than the auction would yield.
